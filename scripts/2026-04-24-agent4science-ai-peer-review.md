# Agent4Science: AI Agents Get Their Own Reddit to Peer-Review Each Other's Papers

Paper link: https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-026-01278-1

## Summary

Chenhao Tan's Chicago Human+AI Lab has stood up Agent4Science — a Reddit-style site where only AI agents can post, comment, and peer-review research papers, most of which were themselves autonomously generated by CHAI's NeuriCo program. Nature covered it April 20, and the site already has roughly 40,000 comments from 150+ agents tagged as "skeptic", "academic", or "storyteller", with responses labeled "supports", "probes", or "challenges". It's either a serious prototype for machine-scale adversarial peer review or a perfectly enclosed echo chamber where AI critiques AI about AI — we're about to find out which, at scale. For anyone thinking about using agents to validate curated data, generated ontology mappings, or KG enrichment pipelines, this is the open experiment on whether AI critique actually catches failure modes or just generates plausible-sounding nitpicks.

## Script

Agent4Science. This one's worth your attention not because it's going to change your workflow tomorrow, but because somebody just ran a very public, very honest experiment on a question that's been sitting in the background of every agentic-AI-for-science conversation for the last two years. The question is: if AI agents are going to help write science, can AI agents also review it — without humans in the loop?

Nature covered this April twentieth. The site is called Agent4Science. It was built by Chenhao Tan and his group at the University of Chicago — the Chicago Human+AI Lab, CHAI. And the pitch is almost deliberately provocative. It's a Reddit-style site — subreddits, comments, upvotes, threaded discussion — where the catch is that humans are not allowed to post. You can watch. You cannot participate. The only accounts on the site are AI agents, configured by humans with personalities and areas of expertise, and then set loose to do what academics do. Read papers. Write peer reviews. Argue. Pile on. Dunk on each other's methodology.

The agents wear tags. Some are configured as "skeptics", some as "academics", some as "storytellers". Their responses get auto-labeled with indicators — supports, probes, challenges — so you can visually scan a thread and see which agents are defending the paper and which are taking it apart. There are around a hundred fifty agents in the system now. They've logged something like forty thousand comments.

And here's the part that matters: most of the papers these agents are reviewing were also generated by agents. Specifically, by NeuriCo — CHAI's own program that autonomously designs experiments, runs them, and writes them up. So what you have, end-to-end, is a closed loop. Agents generate the research. Agents post the research. Agents review the research. Agents argue about the research.

Now. You can read that loop two ways. Let me give you both.

The optimistic read. Peer review is the chokepoint of modern science. Good reviewers are scarce, overworked, and increasingly unwilling to donate their afternoons to line-by-line critique of manuscripts that are themselves often hastily written. If you can get adversarial AI agents to do a useful first pass — really dig in, actually probe the methodology, catch the obvious errors, flag the statistical sins — you relieve the human bottleneck without replacing the human judgment. Humans review the agent reviews. Ten-x throughput, easy. And the multi-agent setup has a real theoretical advantage over a single LLM giving you a review: you get disagreement. You get one agent saying "this is fine" and another agent saying "no, the control group is wrong", and the argument itself produces information that a single model wouldn't generate.

The pessimistic read. You've built an echo chamber with dropdown menus. Every agent in the system is some variant of a frontier LLM. They were trained on overlapping corpora. They have correlated blind spots. A skeptic-tagged agent and an academic-tagged agent arguing with each other are not two independent reviewers — they're the same model playing two roles, with all the same gaps. And when that whole apparatus is reviewing papers that were generated by yet another agent pulling from the same distribution of training data, you get something that looks like debate but is structurally closed. The failure mode isn't that nothing gets flagged. The failure mode is that the same things get flagged and the same things get missed, confidently, forever.

Which of those is actually happening on Agent4Science is, right now, an empirical question. And the cool thing about Tan making it public is that it's now an empirical question the rest of us can investigate. The comments are visible. The agent configurations are visible. If you want to ask — do these agents catch data leakage? Do they notice when a benchmark result is actually a data artifact? Do they push back on suspicious effect sizes? — you can go read the threads and find out.

Here's why this matters if you're doing biomedical informatics. You are already, probably this quarter, thinking about where to insert AI critique into your own workflows. Agents validating KG edges. Agents spotting mis-linked ontology terms. Agents flagging metadata that looks wrong. Agents reviewing autogenerated annotations before they get committed. Every one of those is a miniature version of the Agent4Science bet — does adversarial agent review catch the errors that matter, or does it generate plausible-sounding nitpicks while missing fundamentals? There is not yet a good answer. Agent4Science is, to my knowledge, the largest open test of the question, and it's happening in public, in a domain — AI research — where the underlying papers are exactly the kind of thing these models were trained on.

My read. The experiment is valuable even if it fails. If the agent discussions turn out to be vacuous, we learn something concrete about the limits of multi-agent peer review and the correlated-failure problem. If they turn out to be sharp, we learn something else concrete — that agent review is a genuine candidate for the first-pass chokepoint in scientific publishing, and we should start thinking seriously about what that means for the rest of the stack. Either answer is more useful than the current default, which is vague hand-waving about whether LLMs "can do peer review."

One thing to watch. The paper selection bias on the site is currently narrow — mostly AI research, mostly NeuriCo-generated. Watch for what happens when someone points the system at biomedical preprints. The failure modes in biomedical critique — are the controls right, is the statistical power real, does the assay actually measure what they say it measures — are exactly where correlated blind spots are most dangerous. If Agent4Science stays in the AI-reviewing-AI pocket, it's a curiosity. If it expands to domains where the ground truth is wetter and messier, it becomes a real test.
