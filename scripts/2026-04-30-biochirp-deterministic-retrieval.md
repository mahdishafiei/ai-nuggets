# BioChirp — Deterministic Graphs Catch What LLMs Miss in Biomedical Retrieval

Paper link: https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.64898/2026.04.25.720782v1

## Summary

A team out of IIIT Delhi quantified what biomedical KG builders have been muttering about for two years — LLM-based retrieval systems silently lose biomedical associations through output truncation, synonym mismatch, and run-to-run variability — and shipped BioChirp, an open-source framework that puts LLMs back where they belong: as query interpreters and candidate filters on top of deterministic graph retrieval against four major biomedical databases. The hybrid recovers more associations with higher reproducibility than pure LLM retrieval. The architectural lesson maps almost exactly to what Su Lab is doing with BioThings Explorer: stop asking the LLM to be the database, ask it to drive the database.

## Script

BioChirp. Today's nugget is a preprint that landed yesterday on bioRxiv, from Debarka Sengupta's lab at IIIT Delhi, and it's the kind of paper that confirms what you've been suspecting but couldn't prove with numbers. The title is, quote, "Deterministic retrieval recovers biomedical associations lost by language models." If you build or query biomedical knowledge graphs for a living, that subtitle is the entire thesis — and the paper's contribution is to actually quantify the loss and ship a framework that fixes it.

Here's the setup. Over the last two years, an enormous amount of biomedical retrieval has quietly moved from deterministic, structured queries against curated databases — your DrugBank lookups, your UniProt joins, your ClinicalTrials filters — to LLM-mediated retrieval. You ask a chatbot, it goes off and does some combination of vector search, web browsing, and freeform reasoning, and it hands you back a list of drug-target associations or gene-disease links. It feels great. It's fast. It's conversational. And it is missing things, in ways that are very hard to see unless you go looking.

The Sengupta group went looking. They identified three specific failure modes. First, output truncation — LLMs stop generating before they've enumerated everything they actually know, especially for queries with long answer lists. Ask for all known protein interactors of TP53 and you get a confident-looking list that is silently incomplete. Second, synonym mismatch — biomedical terminology is famously redundant, and LLM retrieval that doesn't aggressively normalize synonyms misses associations indexed under a different label. Third — and this one is the killer for any reproducible science workflow — run-to-run variability. Same query, same model, two different days, two different answer sets. Until now, the magnitude of all three losses was, in their words, unclear.

So they built BioChirp. The architecture is a hybrid that I think is the right shape, and it's worth describing carefully. Rather than throwing the LLM out, they keep it for the things LLMs are actually good at — interpreting a messy natural-language query into a structured intent, and filtering candidate results against the user's question. The retrieval itself, the part where you fetch associations from a database, is fully deterministic. They walk a graph. The graph is built on top of multi-source consensus entity resolution — meaning when DrugBank calls something one thing and ChEMBL calls it another, BioChirp resolves them to the same node before the query runs, instead of hoping the LLM remembers the alias. They tested this across four major biomedical databases, and the headline result is that the deterministic-graph approach recovers more associations and produces the same answer every time you run it.

Now let me tell you why this matters beyond the obvious. There's a real architectural argument in the AI-for-biomedicine community right now about who owns retrieval. One camp says LLMs will eventually subsume curated databases entirely — just train a bigger model on more biomedical text and stop maintaining graphs. The other camp — which includes pretty much everyone who actually maintains a biomedical knowledge graph — says no, the curated structure is doing real work, the LLMs should orchestrate retrieval over it, not replace it. BioChirp is a clean, quantified data point for the second camp. And the pattern it lands on — LLM as query interpreter, deterministic graph as the source of truth — is exactly the architecture that BioThings Explorer at Scripps has been pushing for years. The vocabulary is converging.

A few honest caveats. The benchmark detail in the abstract is thin — we know it's four databases and we know BioChirp wins on association recall and reproducibility, but the absolute numbers will matter, and you'll want to read the methods carefully when the full text drops. Multi-source consensus entity resolution is an old, hard problem, and how aggressive their normalization is will determine how much the result generalizes outside the four databases they tested. And as always with retrieval frameworks, the tooling is only as good as the tools you've wired up — if a question requires an operation BioChirp doesn't have a primitive for, you fall back to the LLM and inherit its failure modes again.

But the central claim — that pure-LLM biomedical retrieval is silently lossy and a small amount of structured retrieval recovers most of the loss — is almost certainly correct. And it's now in print. If you're building anything that wraps an LLM around a biomedical database, this is the prior you should be operating under from now on.

That's your nugget for today.
