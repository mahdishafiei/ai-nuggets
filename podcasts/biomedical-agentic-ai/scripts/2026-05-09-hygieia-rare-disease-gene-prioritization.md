# 2026-05-09 — Hygieia: a versatile AI agent for rare disease diagnosis with risk gene prioritization

Paper link: https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.06226

## Script

Welcome to Biomedical Agentic AI for Friday, May ninth — and this is the third nugget of the day. The first two were on the technical side: a Pareto-guided diffusion-plus-search method for protein design, and the Open-Rosalind paper on tool-first biomedical agents with process-aware benchmarking. Today's third item moves the lens from method papers to a deployed clinical agent — and it's one that Andrew, given your group's history with gene prioritization, will want to look at directly. The paper is called "A Versatile AI Agent for Rare Disease Diagnosis and Risk Gene Prioritization," and the system is called Hygieia, after the Greek goddess of health. It went up on arXiv this past Thursday, out of a Yale-led group with collaborators at Duke-NUS Medical School.

The setup will be familiar to anyone who's spent time around clinical genomics. Rare disease diagnosis is famously the diagnostic odyssey: average time to diagnosis is years, accuracy is poor, and the bottleneck isn't lack of data — it's that the data is fragmented across phenotype lists, exome and genome sequencing reports, and longitudinal clinical records, and weaving them together is genuinely hard cognitive work. Hygieia is a multi-modal agent that ingests all three of those streams — phenotypic features, genetic profiles, and clinical records — and outputs a ranked differential, including a prioritized list of risk-related genomic factors, with confidence scores attached.

What's interesting architecturally is that it's a router-based, knowledge-enhanced framework rather than one big LLM doing everything. The router decides what kind of disease category the case looks like and dispatches to a strategy tailored for that category — which is sensible, because the diagnostic logic for, say, an inborn error of metabolism is just structurally different from the logic for a skeletal dysplasia, and a single chain-of-thought prompt won't naturally handle both well. The "knowledge-enhanced" piece means external structured knowledge — not just whatever's been baked into the model's weights — is being pulled into the reasoning loop, which is the standard mitigation for hallucination on long-tail genetic variants where pretraining coverage is genuinely thin.

Now the headline result, and this is where I'd urge healthy skepticism even as I think it's probably real: in clinical validation runs done with experts at Yale and Duke-NUS, Hygieia outperformed physicians by between twelve and sixty percent across diagnostic benchmarks, and improved both interpretability and turnaround on real clinical cases. The wide range — twelve to sixty — tells you the gain depends a lot on which benchmark and which task. Probably the small number is pure clinician versus the model on routine cases where physicians are already strong, and the big number is on the long tail where the rate-limiter is breadth of knowledge rather than judgment. That's exactly the regime where you'd expect a well-tuned multi-modal agent with structured knowledge access to shine, and exactly the regime where the diagnostic odyssey actually happens.

A few caveats worth being honest about. First, "outperforming physicians on benchmarks" and "outperforming physicians in clinic" are not the same thing, and the paper is explicit that this is benchmark plus a real-case validation study, not a prospective trial. Second, the specific gene prioritization machinery sits in a crowded field — Phen2Gene, AMELIE, LIRICAL, Exomiser, and several agentic descendants are all here — and the paper's contribution is best read as "here's how to wire all of those signals together inside a router-based agent" rather than "here's a fundamentally new prioritizer." Third, confidence scores are great in principle, but their value depends on calibration on out-of-distribution cases, and that's the place I'd want to see more than benchmark numbers. The bigger-picture question this paper is gesturing at, though — can you build an agent that actually compresses the diagnostic odyssey for a rare-disease family — is one of the most important applied questions in agentic biomedical AI, and Hygieia is a serious entry. The full paper, the prioritization details, and the human-in-the-loop design choices are in the link in the show notes. That's it for Friday — see you Monday.
