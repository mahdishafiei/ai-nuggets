# MedCTA — clinical tool agents are brittle, and the bottleneck is planning, not perception

Paper link: https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.11702
Project page: https://ivul-kaust.github.io/MedCTA/

## Script

Welcome back to Biomedical Agentic AI. Today's pick is MedCTA, a new benchmark out of KAUST and MIT that tries to answer a question the field has been quietly avoiding. Frontier multimodal models keep climbing leaderboards on isolated medical imaging tasks and single-turn medical QA. But the thing we actually want to deploy in a clinic is an agent — something that looks at a real patient's chest CT, reads the report, decides which tools to call, runs them, integrates the results, and lands on a defensible answer. MedCTA asks how close any of these models actually get to that, and the answer is sobering.

Here's the setup. The authors built one hundred and seven real-world clinical tasks, every one of them with a clinician-verified executable trajectory — meaning a human expert has already worked out exactly which tools should be called, in what order, with what arguments, to reach the correct answer. The cases span CT, MRI, X-ray, histopathology, fundus imaging, and free-text reports, weighted roughly the way a generalist clinical workload would be. Then they gave the agents a deliberately minimal toolbox — five tools — text extraction from a region, a region-attribute describer, an image-level describer, a calculator, and a generic web search. Nothing exotic. And they ran eighteen multimodal models against it — the latest GPT, Claude, and Gemini families on the closed-source side, and Qwen, Llama, DeepSeek, Phi, and Mistral on the open side.

The headline number is bad in a way that's worth dwelling on. The best autonomous outcome accuracy across all eighteen models was thirty-one and a half percent, from GPT five-point-four. The best open-source model, Qwen three-point-five, came in just under twenty-eight percent. The worst was about three percent. So even the strongest frontier system gets the right answer to fewer than one in three clinician-validated clinical reasoning problems when it has to actually plan and use tools end-to-end.

The really interesting finding is in the failure mode breakdown. The benchmark scores agents on five things — whether they pick the right tool, whether they pass it valid arguments, whether execution actually completes, whether their overall trajectory matches a sensible clinical workflow, and whether the final answer is correct. And across the board, the agents are not failing because they can't read a CT scan or interpret a pathology slide. They're failing because they don't call the tools they should call. The under-call rate is ninety-nine percent — meaning across nearly every task, the agent skips at least one tool call that a clinician judged necessary. Protocol failure — wrong tool, wrong order, wrong stopping point — runs at fifty-eight percent. Models also confidently terminate trajectories early, returning answers before they've gathered enough evidence to actually justify them.

The punchline is the gold-routing experiment. When the authors hand-fix the planning problem — tell the model exactly which tool to call at each step, and just let the backbone do the perception and integration — Claude Opus's outcome accuracy jumps by thirty-five percentage points. That is an enormous delta. What it tells you is that the bottleneck right now is not the backbone vision-language model. The vision-language models can see the lesion. The vision-language models can read the report. The vision-language models can do the math. What they can't do reliably yet is decide, on their own, that they need to look at the lesion, then read the report, then do the math, in that order, for this patient. The reasoning is there; the agency is not.

For people building biomedical agents, this benchmark is a useful corrective. It is very easy to demo an impressive single-turn clinical QA result and call it agentic. MedCTA is a public, multimodal, clinician-validated yardstick that forces the harder question — does your scaffolding actually plan and execute, or does it just look smart in a screenshot. Code, data, and the evaluation suite are all openly released under a permissive license, so anyone can drop a new model or a new orchestration framework into it and get a process-aware score back, not just an end-to-end accuracy number.

And the broader take I'd offer is that benchmarks like this are where the agentic AI for medicine field gets honest. We've spent eighteen months celebrating models that can pass licensing exams. The thing that actually matters — autonomous multi-step tool use under real clinical complexity — is, by the numbers in this paper, still very much an open research problem. Worth tracking. That's it for today.
