# MDForge — agentic molecular-dynamics pipeline design that discovers a picomolar cucurbit[7]uril binder

Paper link: https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.12916

## Script

Welcome back to Biomedical Agentic AI. Today's pick is a fresh arXiv preprint from groups at the University of Notre Dame and the University of Connecticut, and it is one of the more substantive agentic-science papers we have seen this month. The system is called MDForge, and the title is "MDForge: Agentic Molecular Dynamics Pipeline Design under Sparse Simulator Feedback."

Let me set up why this is interesting before getting into how it works. Molecular dynamics is the workhorse simulation method in structural biology, drug discovery, and physical chemistry. You take a molecular system, integrate Newton's equations under a physics-based force field, and you get atomistic trajectories that tell you about binding affinities, conformational landscapes, reaction pathways — quantities that are either expensive or outright impossible to measure in a wet lab. The catch is that designing the pipeline for a new system is an expert job. You have to pick the force field, the equilibration schedule, the sampling protocol, the restraint scheme, the free-energy estimator, and these choices interact in nonobvious ways. A pipeline that works for one host-guest system does not transfer to another. And running even one trial on one molecule is GPU-hours of compute, so you cannot just brute-force search. Throughput of new pipelines, across the field, is correspondingly modest.

There have already been several attempts at LLM agents for molecular dynamics — MDCrow, MDAgent, DynaMate — and they all converge on the same pattern. The agent gets a fixed toolbox of force-field setup, simulation, and analysis routines, and it picks which tool to call when. MDForge argues that this is the wrong action space. A real expert is not selecting from a registered toolbox; they are writing whatever code the new system asks for. So MDForge frames pipeline design as open-ended code generation rather than tool calls. The agent emits the whole pipeline as an executable program.

That move opens up flexibility, but it creates the central technical problem of the paper. The supervisory signal in molecular dynamics is one terminal reward per trial — how close did the predicted binding free energy come to experiment — and each trial costs GPU-hours, so the trial budget is tiny. You cannot train your way out with reinforcement learning. The agent has to learn from a handful of feedback signals.

The solution, which is the methodological contribution worth paying attention to, they call PRISM — Process-Reward Interpretation via Subsystem Mediation. PRISM densifies that sparse terminal reward in two ways. First, it exploits the fact that an MD pipeline has stages — preparation, equilibration, production sampling, analysis — and it pulls diagnostics out at every stage boundary, so the agent gets feedback throughout the run, not just at the end. Second, and this is the cute part, it stands up a panel of physics-expert sub-agents — one specialist for force fields, one for sampling, one for analysis — and has them debate the diagnostics from each stage. The output is a typed, subsystem-attributable critique that tells the code-generating agent not just that something went wrong, but which subsystem broke and what kind of edit is called for. There is also a reputation loop: experts whose pre-trial predictions are confirmed by post-trial evidence accumulate weight within the task, so the panel slowly de-biases itself.

The benchmark results land where you would hope. On three SAMPL host-guest binding free-energy benchmarks — cucurbit seven uril, octa-acid, and a clip host called CBClip — MDForge produces runnable pipelines on all five trials per host, while LLM-critic and no-feedback baselines fail to compile on any host. On ranking accuracy, MDForge hits a Kendall tau of 0.56 on the cucurbit seven uril benchmark and 0.47 on CBClip, more than double the trial-level feedback baseline. Compared against the canonical paprika reference pipeline from the Gilson lab, which is the human expert standard for cucurbit-uril free-energy work, MDForge recovers 78 to 82 percent of the expert ranking utility, with no human in the loop. It also beats a pipeline assembled by a chemistry non-expert with general LLM coding assistance — the right baseline to ask whether the structured expert debate is doing real work, and it is.

But here is what makes this paper actually stand out. The team did not stop at retrospective benchmarks. They took the best cucurbit seven uril pipeline, ran it prospectively on ten unseen guest candidates pulled from ChEMBL and DrugBank, ranked them by predicted binding free energy, and sent the top hit — a small molecule called Bromantane — out for wet-lab validation. Because the affinity range was suspected to be picomolar, beyond what isothermal titration calorimetry can resolve directly, they used a competition NMR assay against a known picomolar reference ligand. The measured association constant came in at roughly eight times ten to the twelfth per molar — about four-fold tighter than the reference, firmly in the picomolar high-affinity regime, comparable to the best deliberately-engineered cucurbit-uril binders that took human labs years to produce.

That is a genuine prospective scientific discovery from an agent, not a benchmark win. The honest caveats. They tested only the top-1 candidate, not the other nine, so this is a single confirmed binder, not a hit-rate calibration. The benchmarks are host-guest systems, which are the standard tractable proxy for protein-ligand binding but are not protein-ligand binding. And the same multi-agent debate architecture has not yet been pushed to membrane systems or conformational free-energy surfaces, both of which the authors flag as next.

Two things to take away. One, the methodological move — open-ended code generation plus a per-stage, typed expert critique — is the right architectural answer for scientific agents working under expensive, sparse feedback, and it is going to generalize beyond molecular dynamics. Two, this is one of the small but growing number of agentic-science papers where the loop closes in a wet lab, not just in a benchmark table. That is the bar the field needs to meet, and Notre Dame plus UConn just cleared it. Paper link is in the show notes. See you tomorrow.
