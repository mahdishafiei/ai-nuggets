# BiomniBench — Process-Level Evaluation of Biomedical Research Agents

Paper link: https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.1101/2026.05.12.724604v1

## Script

Welcome back to Biomedical Agentic AI. Today's nugget comes from the Stanford group behind Biomni, the general-purpose biomedical AI agent that's been making the rounds for a year now. The same team — with Jure Leskovec and Kexin Huang as senior authors — has just dropped a companion piece on bioRxiv called BiomniBench, and if you build, or evaluate, or even just trust biomedical research agents, this one's worth your attention.

Here's the problem they're going after. We are now in a world where LLM agents genuinely do biomedical data analysis end to end. They load datasets, run pipelines, interpret results, write up findings. But the way we score them, almost universally, is by comparing the agent's final answer to a reference answer. Outcome-only evaluation. And the authors argue, pretty convincingly, that that approach is broken in two opposite directions at the same time. On one side, you get false positives: a correct final number can come from memorizing the original paper, from reward hacking the rubric, or from a chain of bad reasoning that happens to land on the right answer by coincidence. On the other side, you get false negatives: an agent that performs a perfectly valid alternative analysis gets marked wrong because its answer doesn't match the reference. Neither failure mode tells you anything useful about whether the agent is actually competent at science.

BiomniBench's pitch is to score the trajectory, not just the destination. They evaluate the agent's full sequence of choices — did it load the right data, did it filter it correctly, did it pick a sensible method, did it interpret the output in a way a domain expert would endorse — against rubrics designed task by task. The first instantiation, BiomniBench-DataAnalysis, has a hundred tasks across seventeen analytical task types and five disease areas plus general biology. And here's the part I find most interesting from a benchmark-design standpoint: every task is grounded in a published paper from Nature, Cell, or Science, and every rubric is co-developed with one of the original paper's authors or another experienced domain expert. That is genuinely expensive to do. You can't crowd-source it, you can't scrape it off Hugging Face. It's exactly the kind of rigor that separates a serious eval from yet another leaderboard.

Three findings stand out from the initial run. First, frontier models lead but there is substantial headroom remaining — nobody is saturating this benchmark. Second, and this one surprised me, the agent harness shifts the score as much as the base model does. Same Claude or GPT underneath, different scaffolding around it, and you get a different effective scientist. That's a strong empirical argument that "which model" is only half the question for anyone building these systems; the harness — the tool layer, the planning loop, the memory — is the other half. Third, the agents recurrently fail on three specific things: method selection, biological interpretation, and scientific reasoning. Not data wrangling, not code execution. The harder, more judgement-laden parts of being a scientist.

The framing here pairs nicely with a benchmark we covered just a few days ago, BiomedArena, which scored agents head-to-head on deep-research toolkit tasks but ultimately judged on outcome. BiomniBench is the process-level counterpart. The field will need both, but my prediction is that as agents get better at gaming outcome benchmarks — and they will — trajectory-level rubrics co-authored with the people who actually did the work are going to be where real progress gets measured. That's all for today. See you tomorrow.
