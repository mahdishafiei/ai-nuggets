# LLM-orchestrated PET/CT agent — end-to-end DICOM-to-draft-report on 170 real-world lung-staging scans

Paper link: https://doi.org/10.2967/jnumed.126.272362

## Script

Today's pick is a proof-of-concept that just came out in the Journal of Nuclear Medicine, from Hongyoon Choi's group at Seoul National University in collaboration with their spinout Portrai. It's an autonomous agent that takes raw PET/CT data — the actual DICOM tree coming off a scanner, not a curated benchmark — and produces a structured draft report at the other end, with the only human involvement being a one-line text prompt. They ran it retrospectively on a hundred and seventy baseline FDG PET/CTs done for lung cancer staging. The read I want to give you isn't really about nuclear medicine — it's about what the agent architecture does and, just as interestingly, where it stops being able to help.

The setup. A clinical PET/CT read is a chain of jobs, not a single perception task. The reader has to pick the right series out of a messy DICOM tree, register PET to CT, convert raw counts to standardized uptake values using injection metadata and patient weight, segment lesions, segment organs as reference regions, look at maximum-intensity projection images, and finally synthesize a staging report. Every link in that chain has had task-specific deep learning thrown at it over the last few years. What hasn't existed is a system that strings the whole chain together without a human babysitting each step. That's the gap this paper closes, and the way it closes it is the part worth carrying.

The architecture is three layers. At the bottom is an execution layer of Python modules — DICOM parsing, registration, SUV math, plus the two segmentation models they wire in: AutoPET's nnU-Net for whole-body lesion segmentation, and TotalSegmentator for organ-level masks. In the middle is a tool abstraction layer that exposes each of those as a schema — tool name, required inputs, expected output type, success criteria. And at the top sits a cognitive control layer: a text-based large language model, in their main configuration Gemini three Flash Preview, running a thought-action-observation loop and deciding which tool to call next based on the prompt and the intermediate state. When a tool fails or returns an empty mask, the orchestrator catches it and falls back. If whole-body lesion segmentation fails, it routes the case to the vision LLM working on the projection image and writes a qualitative report instead. The point worth pausing on is that the LLM is not doing the perception. It is doing the workflow.

The robustness result was, honestly, the part that surprised me. Across one-seventy examinations from three different Siemens Biograph scanners, with varying voxel sizes, reconstruction protocols, and inconsistent series naming, the agent completed the full end-to-end pipeline without human intervention in essentially every case — only one example failed, because the SUV calculation hit missing metadata. That kind of variability is what has historically killed automated PET pipelines, and the LLM control layer absorbed it.

The diagnostic results are more interesting once you look past the headline. Primary tumor detection was a hundred percent sensitive — every single primary lung lesion, found and reported. Nodal involvement is where it gets honest: sensitivity eighty-five percent, specificity thirty-nine. The agent overcalls hilar and mediastinal nodes with low-grade uptake that an expert would dismiss as reactive. Distant metastasis came in at seventy percent sensitivity and sixty-five percent specificity. And in the failure breakdown, you see exactly what's going wrong — false positives clustered on physiologic bowel and pelvic uptake, benign bone changes; false negatives on adrenal lesions, where the agent caught only two out of eleven, and brain metastases, where it caught zero out of three. The orchestrator can dispatch tools, but it cannot rescue you from a vision LLM that hasn't seen enough adrenal disease, and it cannot rescue you from the fact that FDG has lousy contrast against the avid background of normal brain.

There's a small supplementary experiment that is worth more than several of the headline metrics. They swapped Gemini three Flash for Claude Sonnet four point six and for Gemma four twenty-six billion as the orchestrator, holding everything else fixed, and the performance pattern was broadly similar across all three. In other words, the orchestration framework — the schema-defined tools, the thought-action-observation loop, the fallback logic — is doing the load-bearing work. The specific LLM is interchangeable.

The Su Lab read. Two things. First, the right abstraction for many real biomedical AI products is going to look more like this paper than like a medical foundation model. An LLM as a thin cognitive layer that knows when to call a deep specialist tool, knows how to read the tool's structured output, and knows what to do when the tool fails. The LLM does not have to be the world expert; it has to be a reliable middle manager. Second, and this is the more important read — orchestration is not a magic eraser. The agent inherited every limitation of AutoPET on small bone lesions, and every limitation of the vision LLM on intracranial uptake. Where the headline number is impressive, look down one layer at the tools the orchestrator is calling. That is where the real ceiling lives. That's it for today — talk to you tomorrow.
