## Script

Today's nugget is a bioRxiv preprint from the Kuan-lin Huang group at the Icahn School of Medicine at Mount Sinai. The title is "An Agentic Platform for Drug Repurposing Unified across Molecular, Phenotypic, and Clinical Scales." This is version two of a paper that first posted in April. The April version introduced the prediction layers; the version that posted yesterday adds the agentic orchestrator on top, expands the validation, and surfaces a population-scale clinical signal that I want to spend most of this episode on.

Quick context on why this paper landed on today's list. The Huang lab appeared on this show on May fifth, when their ADA Consortium preprint ran an agent against thirty-one published oncology target claims and showed that retraction status of the source paper predicted whether the agent could replicate the claim. That work was agent-as-auditor. Today's paper is agent-as-multi-scale-repurposing-orchestrator. Same group, different role for the agent, both papers worth knowing about together.

Here is what LinkD does. The platform has four layers, and the layers are independently useful, but the architectural bet is that orchestrating them together is more useful than any one of them. The first layer, LinkD-Bind, predicts binding affinity across about fifteen thousand drugs and twenty thousand human targets using a diffusion-based model. On BindingDB, Davis, and KIBA — the three standard binding-prediction benchmarks — LinkD-Bind ranks first in eight of nine comparisons. The interesting part of that result is where the gains concentrate. The biggest improvements are under cold-start conditions, where either the drug or the target was not seen during training. Cold-start is the regime that matters for repurposing, because the whole point is to find new uses for drugs against targets they have not been characterized against. An interpolation gain on already-known drug-target pairs would be much less interesting than the cold-start gain that the authors report.

The second layer, LinkD-Select, layers selectivity scoring on top of molecular docking and recovers ninety-five point three percent of known drug-target pairs. The third layer, LinkD-Pheno, brings in phenotypic data — drug-sensitivity profiles and CRISPR dependency screens across nine hundred sixty cancer cell lines from the public dependency-map and drug-screening atlases. LinkD-Pheno identifies thirty-four novel drug-gene pairs and recovers about eighty-five percent of known targets among its top fifty candidates. The fourth layer, LinkD-Agent, is the orchestrator. It takes a natural-language query, walks the binding layer, the selectivity layer, and the phenotypic layer, integrates the evidence, and returns a repurposing recommendation. The agent is served on a public web platform.

Now the part that elevates this from a competent integration paper to a top-of-corpus pick. The authors took LinkD's prioritized recommendations and validated them at population scale across eleven and a half million individuals — about ten million from the Mount Sinai electronic health record system and about one and a half million from UK Biobank. The LinkD pipeline prioritized two beta-blockers, propranolol and carvedilol, for prostate cancer prevention. The clinical analysis compared propranolol and carvedilol users against metoprolol users — metoprolol is the reference because all three are beta-blockers with similar indications, but propranolol and carvedilol have non-selective and mixed pharmacology while metoprolol is beta-one selective. Propranolol users had a hazard ratio of zero point eight two for five-year prostate cancer incidence. Carvedilol users had a hazard ratio of zero point nine two. Both protective, both statistically robust across the eleven-and-a-half-million-person comparator pool.

The mechanistic backing closes the loop. The agentic pipeline had flagged adrenergic-receptor-beta-two — A-D-R-B-2 — as the relevant target. Docking simulations supported propranolol and carvedilol binding at adrenergic-receptor-beta-two more avidly than metoprolol. And wet-lab growth-inhibition assays in LNCaP prostate cancer cells confirmed that propranolol and carvedilol suppressed growth in a way that metoprolol did not. So the architecture is: agent prioritizes from multi-modal integration, retrospective clinical analysis confirms the population-scale effect, mechanistic docking explains why those particular beta-blockers and not others, and wet-lab assays confirm the mechanism in the relevant cell line. Four layers of agreement on a single repurposing hypothesis.

For this show, the through-line is the orchestration architecture. There are a lot of biomedical AI papers right now that do one layer well — better binding prediction, better phenotypic screen analysis, better clinical-record mining — but the agentic value is in walking the layers together and surfacing hypotheses that would not have surfaced from any single layer. LinkD is one of the cleaner demonstrations I have seen this year that the integration is where the value lives, especially when the orchestrator can hand a researcher a hypothesis that comes with mechanistic, phenotypic, and population-scale evidence in the same answer. The public web platform is the kind of thing worth trying with a natural-language query on a target you actually care about, to see whether the orchestration buys you something or whether the agent just gives you what each individual layer would have given you separately. The Huang lab has shown twice in five weeks that they can stand up agent infrastructure with the methodological rigor to make claims that hold up, and that is a track record worth watching.

That is the nugget for June eighth. LinkD and LinkD-Agent, Mount Sinai, four-layer agentic drug repurposing, binding plus selectivity plus phenotype plus eleven-and-a-half-million-patient clinical validation, with the beta-blocker prostate-cancer signal as the worked example. Link in the message.
