# Huang's ADA Consortium: An Agent Audits 31 Oncology Target Claims, and the Retraction Status of the Source Paper Predicts Whether the Agent Replicates

Paper link: https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.64898/2026.04.29.721634v1

More headlines:

## Summary

Kuan-lin Huang's group at Mount Sinai, with the new Accelerated Discovery with Agents (ADA) Consortium, posted a bioRxiv preprint on May 3 that does something this whole field has been circling but few have actually executed — they ran an agent on a set of published oncology target claims and asked whether the agent could replicate them, with retraction status of the source paper as a quasi-orthogonal label. Thirty-one gene-target-disease hypotheses were each translated into a zero-shot validation prompt and executed in a single round by a biomedical research agent, then expert-scored. Non-retracted target claims validated at 64.3 percent — nine out of fourteen — while retracted target claims validated at 11.8 percent — two out of seventeen. That is a seventeen-fold differential, and the replicated set includes both the well-established hits — WRN in microsatellite-stable cancer, PRMT5 in MTAP-deleted cancer — and more recent discoveries like PTGES3, HASPIN, SLC5A3, PKMYT1, FAM126B, and PAPSS1. The framing matters more than any single number: this is agent-as-replicator, applied at the bench of target prioritization where translational risk is enormous and where agents are already getting deployed in pharma whether or not anyone has run this kind of audit. Huang's group has run it, and the result is that publication-quality matters — agents are not equal-opportunity validators of every claim that passes peer review.

## Script

Today's nugget is a bioRxiv preprint that posted on Sunday from Kuan-lin Huang's group at the Icahn School of Medicine at Mount Sinai. The title is Agent-Driven Validation of Oncology Therapeutic Targets, and the author line reads Huang and the Accelerated Discovery with Agents Consortium. The ADA Consortium is itself worth a sentence — this is one of the first preprints I've seen that lists a multi-institution agentic-AI consortium as a co-author block on biomedical research, which is a small institutional signal that the agent-for-science work is starting to coalesce into named collaborations rather than single-lab efforts. I flagged this paper as a headline in yesterday's OptimusKG episode. Today it is the main course, because after a closer read the methodology is sharper than I gave it credit for in a one-line summary.

Let me lay out what they did. The team identified thirty-one published gene-target-disease hypotheses, all in oncology, all involving context-specific dependencies — meaning the claim is not just gene X is a target but gene X is a target specifically in the genomic context of mutation Y or biomarker Z. WRN in microsatellite-stable cancer. PRMT5 in MTAP-deleted cancer. The kind of synthetic-lethal or context-conditional target that has been the dominant frame of cancer drug discovery for the last five years. They split the thirty-one hypotheses into two groups by the publication status of the source paper. Fourteen came from non-retracted papers. Seventeen came from retracted papers. They then translated each target claim into a zero-shot validation prompt, executed it in one round with a biomedical research agent, and had a domain expert score whether the agent's data-driven analysis supported the claim or not.

The numbers. Non-retracted target claims, validated at sixty-four point three percent — nine out of fourteen. Retracted target claims, validated at eleven point eight percent — two out of seventeen. That is a seventeen-fold higher likelihood that a non-retracted target shows context-specific dependency in the agent's analysis. The replicated targets include WRN and PRMT5, which we already knew, but also more recent and less canonical hits — PTGES3, HASPIN, SLC5A3, PKMYT1, FAM126B, PAPSS1.

Now — why this is methodologically interesting, and where the caveats are.

The interesting move is treating retraction status as a quasi-ground-truth label for whether a target claim is real. That is not a perfect ground truth. Papers get retracted for many reasons — image manipulation, statistical errors, authorship disputes, sometimes things that have nothing to do with the central biological claim. So retraction is a noisy signal of whether the target itself is correct. But it is a label that exists, in volume, generated by a process that is broadly orthogonal to whatever data the agent uses to validate. That orthogonality is the whole point. If your agent validation methodology agrees with the retraction process on a strong majority of cases, you have evidence that the agent is doing something like content-aware target assessment rather than pattern-matching the literature. If they had used positive controls only, you couldn't separate "agent confirms because the data are real" from "agent confirms because the agent is sycophantic toward published claims."

The seventeen-fold differential is the headline number, but the way I would read the paper is that the eleven point eight percent on retracted targets is actually the more informative datapoint. It is the false positive rate of an agent that is being asked to validate claims that, on average, should not validate. Eleven percent is a meaningful false-positive rate. It is too high to deploy an agent like this as the sole gate on a target prioritization decision. It is low enough that it could plausibly serve as one of three or four signals in a portfolio. And the two retracted targets that did validate are interesting cases — the biology may have been right even when the paper had problems, which Huang's group flags honestly.

The sixty-four percent on non-retracted targets is, conversely, the recall ceiling. A third of validated, non-retracted target claims do not replicate under agent-driven analysis. Some of that is agent miss rate, some is the difficulty of context-specific dependency itself — these are claims that depend on the right cell-line panel, the right perturbation, the right comparison. The honest framing is that if you point this agent at thirty new target claims tomorrow, you should expect it to confirm roughly two thirds of the real ones and roughly one in eight of the false ones. That is genuinely useful but it is not magic.

What the paper does not say in detail — and this is where I would want to read the methods more carefully — is which underlying LLM is driving the agent, what tools it has access to, and what data it queries. The abstract says zero-shot validation prompt executed by a biomedical research agent in one round. That is enough to see the methodology but it leaves open whether the agent is doing CCLE and DepMap dependency-screen analysis, whether it is querying gene-expression atlases, whether it is reading literature, or some combination. For the audience of this show, the answer matters — if the agent is calling MyGene, OpenTargets, DepMap, and similar curated open-data resources, then this becomes a direct demonstration that the open-biomedical-data infrastructure is the inference substrate that makes agent-driven target validation possible. I will read the methods later today and will note in tomorrow's episode if the architecture turns out to be the boring web-search-plus-reasoning loop versus the more interesting structured-resource-plus-tools pattern.

Two reasons this matters for the Su Lab and for anyone in this audience.

First — agents are already being deployed inside pharma R and D for target prioritization, and the field has lacked a serious external benchmark. The ADA paper is exactly the kind of external audit that a pharma agentic AI deployment should be measured against before anyone trusts its output. The retraction-status framing is portable. You could run it against any agent, any LLM backbone, any tool stack. There is a paper to write in someone running this exact protocol against three or four different commercial agentic systems and reporting the differential. That is a publishable comparison.

Second — the open-data biomedical resources are the load-bearing infrastructure underneath any version of this. Whatever the agent is doing under the hood, the cell-line panels, the dependency screens, the gene-expression data, the disease-context annotations all come from somewhere. If the somewhere is DepMap and CCLE and ClinVar and Open Targets and the Wikidata-bound biomedical entity layer that the Su Lab and adjacent groups have spent the last decade building, then this paper is another data point for why those resources need stable funding. The agent that gets sixty-four percent recall on real oncology targets is not a pure language model — it is a language model plus the curated structured data that someone has to keep alive.
