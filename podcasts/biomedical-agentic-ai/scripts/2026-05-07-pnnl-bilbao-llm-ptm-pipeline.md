# PNNL's PTMdiscoverer: An LLM Walks Into Proteomics' Hardest Annotation Problem

Paper link: https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.64898/2026.05.01.722279v1

## Summary

Aivett Bilbao's group at Pacific Northwest National Laboratory, with co-authors at PNNL's Environmental Molecular Sciences Laboratory, posted a bioRxiv preprint yesterday — May sixth — describing a generative-AI pipeline that uses large language models to take the output of an open-search proteomics run and turn it into annotated, biologically interpretable post-translational modification calls, then hands those calls off to a second tool, PTM-Psi, that models the structural and functional consequences on protein dynamics. The interesting move is not that LLMs are showing up in proteomics — it is where in the pipeline they show up. PTM identification has always been bottlenecked not at the mass spectrometer but at the interpretation step, where an analyst stares at thousands of candidate mass shifts and tries to figure out which ones are real biology, which are artefacts, and what they mean. That step is exactly the kind of unstructured, knowledge-heavy reasoning that LLMs are now starting to do well. For an audience that builds biomedical knowledge graphs and integrates open data, this paper is a clear example of language models doing the data-integration job that bioinformatics analysts have been doing by hand — and doing it inside a pipeline that connects mass spec, AI annotation, and structural modeling end to end.

## Script

Today's nugget is a fresh bioRxiv preprint that posted yesterday, May sixth, out of Pacific Northwest National Laboratory. The title is, An LLM-driven pipeline for proteomics-based detection and structural modeling of post-translational modifications. The corresponding author is Aivett Bilbao at the Environmental Molecular Sciences Laboratory at PNNL, with first author Anu George and co-authors Daniel Mejia-Rodriguez, Xueyun Li, Paul Rigor, and Margaret Cheung. The DOI is 10.64898/2026.05.01.722279.

To explain why this matters, I need to spend thirty seconds on what the pipeline is for, because the bottleneck this paper is targeting is a real one and most people outside proteomics do not appreciate it.

Post-translational modifications — PTMs — are the chemical decorations on proteins that switch them on, switch them off, change where they go in the cell, and change who they bind. Phosphorylation, acetylation, ubiquitination, oxidation, glycosylation. Almost every biological process you care about is gated by them. Mass spectrometry can detect them — modern instruments routinely produce candidate PTM lists with thousands of mass shifts per experiment. The problem is what comes next. An open-search proteomics run hands you a flood of candidate mass shifts on candidate residues, and somebody has to look at each one and decide — is this a real biological modification, is it a known artefact of sample handling, is it a chemistry of the experiment rather than the cell, and if it is real, what does it mean. That is a knowledge-integration problem. It requires reading the literature, cross-referencing modification databases, understanding the protein in question, and exercising judgment. Conventional proteomics tools do not do that. They flag and they leave the interpretation to the analyst.

What Bilbao's group built is a two-stage pipeline. The first stage is called PTMdiscoverer, and it is the LLM piece. It takes the raw output from an open-search proteomics workflow — the candidate mass shifts and the residues they sit on — and uses large language models to identify, annotate, and interpret which candidates are likely to be biologically meaningful PTMs. The LLM is essentially being asked to do the analyst's job. Read the candidate. Connect it to known modification chemistries. Connect it to the protein's biology. Decide whether the modification call is plausible, and if so, annotate it with biological context. The second stage, PTM-Psi, takes the high-confidence calls coming out of PTMdiscoverer and models what those modifications do — how they change the protein's structure, its dynamics, and its interactions. PTM-Psi was already in the literature from this same group; what is new here is bolting an LLM front end onto the discovery side so that the structural modeling actually has high-quality, biologically interpreted PTM calls to chew on.

They demonstrate the pipeline on cyanobacterial proteomics data. The biology they go after is redox-regulated dark complex formation in carbon metabolism — a real, open biological question about how cyanobacteria switch their carbon-fixing machinery in the dark via reversible thiol modifications. The pipeline connects the mass spec data, the LLM-driven PTM calls, and the structural model of the resulting complex. The point is not the cyanobacterial result on its own — it is that the same pipeline architecture should generalize to any system where you want to go from an open-search PTM hit list to a mechanistic structural hypothesis without losing a postdoc to manual annotation for six months.

A few things worth saying for this audience.

First — this is a particularly clean example of the pattern we keep seeing across biomedical AI. The LLM is not replacing the instrument. The mass spectrometer still runs. The structural modeling tool still runs. The LLM is doing the annotation and interpretation step in between, which is the step that has historically been done by domain experts reading literature and cross-referencing databases. That is a specific kind of work — knowledge integration over heterogeneous unstructured biological knowledge — and it is exactly the kind of work language models are well suited to. The lesson is not that the LLM is the whole pipeline. The lesson is that there is a particular bottleneck in almost every omics pipeline that is annotation- and interpretation-heavy, and inserting an LLM at that bottleneck unlocks the rest of the workflow. If you build biomedical pipelines for a living, the question to ask now is, where is the analyst-in-the-loop step in your pipeline, and what would it look like if a language model did that step.

Second — for the open biomedical data crowd. A pipeline like PTMdiscoverer only works because the LLM can ground its annotations in something. PTM databases, the literature, modification chemistry references, protein function annotations. The performance of this kind of system is going to depend, directly and measurably, on the quality and completeness of the open knowledge resources the LLM can draw on. Curated PTM databases, structured protein-function annotations, accessible literature. The same pattern from yesterday's SPARK pathology paper and the OptimusKG knowledge graph paper from earlier in the week. Agentic and LLM-driven biomedical AI is making the open knowledge substrate more valuable, not less.

Third — a caveat worth naming. The paper is a bioRxiv preprint demonstrating the pipeline on one biological system. It is not a head-to-head benchmark against expert analysts on a standardized PTM identification task, and it does not give us a number for how many of the LLM's PTM calls hold up when followed up experimentally. That benchmark will need to come — the field will need a clean evaluation of LLM-driven PTM annotation against expert calls before the community trusts these pipelines to drive downstream biology. But the architecture is right, the bottleneck is real, and the integration with structural modeling on the back end is the kind of end-to-end framing that a lot of single-stage AI tools in proteomics have been missing.

That's the nugget for May seventh. PTMdiscoverer plus PTM-Psi out of PNNL, an LLM-driven proteomics pipeline that turns open-search mass shift candidates into biologically annotated, structurally modeled PTMs, demonstrated on cyanobacterial carbon metabolism. The pattern — language models inserted at the annotation and interpretation step of an omics pipeline, sitting between the instrument and the downstream model — is one this show keeps surfacing, and it is the part of the agentic AI story that the open biomedical infrastructure community is best positioned to shape.
