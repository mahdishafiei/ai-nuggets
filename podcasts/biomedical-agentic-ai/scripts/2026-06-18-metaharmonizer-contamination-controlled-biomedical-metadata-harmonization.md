# MetaHarmonizer — biomedical metadata harmonization with controlled-vocabulary grounding and a contamination protocol that inverts the LLM-only advantage on the GDC benchmark

Paper link: https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.64898/2026.06.13.732088v1

## Script

Today's pick is a bioRxiv preprint out of the City University of New York's School of Public Health — corresponding author Sehyun Oh, with co-authors spanning the Bioconductor-adjacent multi-omics-curation community. Title: MetaHarmonizer — robust biomedical metadata harmonization and a contamination control for inflated LLM performance on public benchmarks. Same caveat as the last few episodes — bioRxiv's full text is gated behind a Cloudflare challenge from my host — but the abstract is unusually concrete, it carries the architecture, the numbers, the baselines, the AUC range, and the deployment story, and the headline finding is methodological and stands cleanly from the abstract alone.

Setup. Public biomedical repositories — GEO, the GDC, the SRA, ArrayExpress, dbGaP — hold enormous reuse potential, but inconsistent metadata is the wall that integration runs into. Standard answer for the last two years has been to throw an LLM at it: prompt a frontier model to map this sample's "tissue" field to an Experimental Factor Ontology term, or to align a column called "primary_diagnosis" in one schema to "histology" in another. There's a healthy publication stream on this — most of the top benchmarks involve the Genomic Data Commons schema and EFO term mapping — and recent LLM-based methods report eye-catching numbers. The MetaHarmonizer paper argues that those numbers are partially fictitious, and then it builds the system you'd want once you accept that.

The contamination finding is the part to land first because it changes how you should read the entire recent literature. The authors designed an evaluation protocol that probes whether the LLM has actually seen the benchmark's answer key. The probe is what they call zero-schema context — strip out the schema entirely, ask the model to recover GDC field identifiers from nothing, and see what comes back. Three out of five frontier LLMs recover eighty to one hundred percent of the GDC identifiers under that condition. That's not generalization. That's the model regurgitating the benchmark out of its training data. Once you control for that, the apparent LLM-only advantage on the GDC schema-mapping benchmark — the benchmark that essentially defines this subfield — inverts. The leaderboard the field has been chasing is, on its most-cited benchmark, partly measuring how much of the answer key the model has memorized. That's a result the bioinformatics community needs to internalize before it commits more infrastructure to LLM-only metadata pipelines.

The system they propose is the architectural response. Two modules. SchemaMapper aligns attribute names across schemas — "primary_diagnosis" to "histology," that sort of thing. OntologyMapper standardizes the values inside those columns to controlled vocabularies, the EFO and its neighbors. Both modules are multi-stage cascades — cheap exact and near-exact matching first, increasingly resource-intensive methods only when earlier stages don't resolve, and crucially every candidate the cascade can return is drawn from a pre-defined controlled vocabulary. Hallucinated ontology terms are excluded by construction; the system literally cannot return a term that isn't in the source vocabulary. LLMs are used, but as bounded preprocessing components — alias generation, paraphrase expansion of the controlled vocabulary's lexicon — not as inference-time decision-makers. The smart model isn't in the middle. It's offline, generating dictionaries that the deterministic matcher then consults at runtime.

The numbers track. On the GDC schema-matching benchmark, SchemaMapper with a deployment-optimized alias dictionary hits seventy-one point six percent Top-1 accuracy and beats the Magneto bipartite baselines on recall against ground truth. With the best alias dictionary, it tops Top-1, Top-5, and Recall, and matches the best Magneto reranker — which is itself a fine-tuned LLM-reranker — on mean reciprocal rank. And it beats LLM-only systems under the contamination-controlled protocol the same paper introduces — which is the comparison that actually matters now. On the EFO benchmarks where memorization isn't a viable shortcut, OntologyMapper lands seventy-seven point nine to ninety-five point five percent Top-1 across four benchmarks, beating text2term by up to sixteen point four percentage points and direct LLM inference by nineteen point two. Calibrated confidence scores across both modules separate correct from incorrect predictions with AUC between zero point seven three and zero point nine four — which is what lets you triage and put a human only on the uncertain cases. The whole thing runs locally, deterministically, in seconds for schema mapping and under a minute for ontology mapping against a thirty-three-thousand-term pre-indexed corpus. It's released as a Python package, domain-agnostic — you can point it at non-biomedical metadata too.

The Su Lab read. This is the paper for anyone who actually integrates biomedical data across studies for a living. The contamination protocol is generalizable — the authors explicitly position it as applicable to any LLM-augmented bioinformatics benchmark built on public data, which is most of them. That protocol should become a standard checkbox before any new LLM-metadata paper gets published. The architectural pattern is the right inversion of the default — LLMs as bounded preprocessing rather than inference engines, controlled-vocabulary grounding rather than free generation, multi-stage cascades that escalate only when needed, calibrated confidence rather than blind output. It's the same architectural lesson that the last several episodes have been converging on — versioned skill libraries for pharmacogenomics, OmicOS's registry-grounded execution, OmicsNavigator's pre-registered blueprints — applied to the metadata layer that all of those systems quietly depend on. And the package is open, local, and deterministic, which is the difference between a methodology paper and a tool people will actually use. If you've been building knowledge graphs over public biomedical data, the upstream FAIRness step you've been wishing existed just got a credible candidate.
