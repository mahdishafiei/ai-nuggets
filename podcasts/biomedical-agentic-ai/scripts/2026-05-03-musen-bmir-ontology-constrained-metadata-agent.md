## Summary

Josef Hardi, Martin O'Connor, Marcos Martinez-Romero, Jean Rosario, Stephen Fisher, and Mark Musen — Stanford BMIR, the group that built BioPortal and CEDAR — posted an arXiv preprint in March that I want to surface today as the second nugget because it is the literal embodiment of the morning episode's thesis. They built an LLM agent that does biomedical metadata standardization by querying authoritative terminology services in real time, rather than asking the model to recall ontology terms from training data. Tested on 839 legacy metadata records from HuBMAP with an expert-curated gold standard, real-time tool access consistently improves prediction accuracy across both ontology-constrained and non-ontology-constrained fields. The novelty isn't the architecture in isolation — it's the explicit positioning of authoritative ontology services as load-bearing inference infrastructure that the agent reaches into at every step. This is the BioPortal-as-tool pattern, and the fact that Musen's group is the one writing it down is the signal — the people who built the ontology serving infrastructure for biomedicine are now telling you, with HuBMAP numbers, that LLMs should be calling that infrastructure in real time, not paraphrasing it from a training-data shadow.

Why this paper for this audience today. Mark Musen's group built BioPortal — the canonical biomedical ontology repository — and CEDAR, the Center for Expanded Data Annotation and Retrieval. These are the people who have spent the better part of two decades building the infrastructure that turns "this is a tissue type" into a stable, resolvable, machine-readable identifier. When that group writes a paper about how LLMs should interact with biomedical ontologies, you should read it. They're not theorizing — they're reporting on the architecture they've concluded actually works, after watching every other approach fail.

Here's the paradigm contrast that makes this paper worth surfacing. Most LLM-for-metadata work — including some that's gotten more press — treats ontology grounding as a post-processing step. The model emits a candidate string, and a separate normalizer maps it to an identifier. MetaMuse, which we covered on April twenty-third, is in this lineage — Curator agents extract, an Arbitrator agent reconciles, a Normalizer agent maps to ontology IDs. That works. The Hardi paper points at a different architecture. The agent itself queries authoritative terminology services in real time, during reasoning, retrieves canonically correct vocabulary terms, and uses them as constraints on what it generates. The model isn't asked to remember the ontology. It's asked to use it. And the ontology is the live one running on a server right now, not the version frozen into the training corpus eighteen months ago.

The evaluation. Eight hundred thirty nine legacy metadata records from the Human BioMolecular Atlas Program — HuBMAP — an NIH consortium building a comprehensive cellular atlas of the healthy human body. Expert-curated gold standard, exact-match assessment. The reported finding is that real-time tool access consistently improved prediction accuracy across both ontology-constrained fields — where you'd obviously expect it to help — and, importantly, non-ontology-constrained fields, where the model is reasoning about contextual or free-text metadata. That second result is the more interesting one. It says the gain isn't just about looking up the right term; it's about the agent operating in a world where authoritative knowledge is one tool call away, and that changes the quality of the reasoning even on fields the ontology doesn't directly cover.

I want to flag the limitations honestly, because Andrew and people like Andrew are going to ask. HuBMAP is one consortium with a CEDAR-developed annotation template. The metadata is messy compared to a curated database, but it's clean compared to, say, GEO, ArrayExpress, or arbitrary supplementary tables from a thousand individual papers. The harder test is what happens when the agent is given metadata that has no template and was filled in by a graduate student in twenty fourteen who used "tissue" to mean three different things in three different fields. We don't have that benchmark in this paper. What we do have is a clean, well-evaluated demonstration of the paradigm, on a dataset that real biomedical data infrastructure people care about, from the group that ought to know.
