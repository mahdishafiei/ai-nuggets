## Script

Today's nugget is a portable, semantic-evidence-graph approach to opening up the U.S. EPA's ToxCast database to natural-language querying, posted to bioRxiv yesterday from the Leibniz Research Institute for Environmental Medicine in Düsseldorf. The system is called ToxCastLite, and it does something Andrew's audience will recognize as the right shape of problem. The full ToxCast distribution is roughly a hundred gigabytes of MySQL, which is essentially unusable for the kind of cross-domain evidence mining a working toxicologist actually wants to do. The team built a streaming converter that re-projects ToxCast into assay-scoped SQLite profiles, getting the footprint down to about three gigabytes for a focused use case like developmental neurotoxicity, and then exposed a compact RDF layer over those SQLite tables so the same evidence can be queried as a knowledge graph in GraphDB.

The interesting agentic-AI piece sits on top of that graph. The team built a Streamlit prototype where a locally deployed large language model translates plain-English toxicology questions into SPARQL queries against the RDF projection. The hallucination-mitigation strategy is the bit that's worth pausing on: rather than letting the model invent predicate names, the SPARQL generation is grounded in a versioned RDF schema, so the model can only emit queries that reference predicates and classes the schema actually declares. This is the same school of thought as Wikidata's property-and-class scaffolding for structured queries, and it sits closer to the controlled-vocabulary discipline of biomedical knowledge graphs than to free-form text-to-SQL — which is exactly where the hallucination rate tends to be lowest.

What ToxCastLite actually integrates is also worth calling out, because it lives at the intersection that Andrew works in. The base layer is ToxCast in-vitro bioactivity — concentration–response curves, half-maximal activity concentrations, and the MC6 quality flags. On top of that they pull in ToxRefDB version three, which is the in-vivo animal toxicology evidence with curated points of departure, effect summaries, and observation summaries. And then they pull in CPDat version four, which is the chemical product and functional-use database, so each chemical also carries an exposure-relevant context — what consumer products it shows up in, what function it serves. The connective tissue across all three sources is the DSSTox substance identifier, which means a single SPARQL query can ask, in effect: which chemicals have high-confidence in-vitro developmental-neurotoxicity activity, have positive apical evidence in animal studies, and appear in consumer-relevant product categories? That kind of question is genuinely hard to answer against ToxCast alone, and was effectively impossible against the hundred-gigabyte distribution without writing custom joins.

The case studies focus on developmental neurotoxicity. The team show three patterns that the system makes tractable. The first is concordance — where high-confidence in-vitro signals line up with positive animal evidence. The second is the gap — where in-vitro activity is present but no in-vivo developmental-neurotoxicity study exists, which is the new-approach-methodology argument that the field has been making for a decade now. And the third is prioritization — chemicals where in-vitro activity, in-vivo evidence, and exposure context all intersect. And critically, the user can drill from a semantic-graph hit all the way down to the raw concentration–response rows in the underlying SQLite without ever writing SQL or SPARQL by hand.

A couple of caveats worth flagging. The LLM is local, which is great for sovereignty and reproducibility, but the paper doesn't yet stress-test the natural-language-to-SPARQL accuracy at scale — the schema grounding limits the failure mode but doesn't quantify it. The integration is also focused on developmental neurotoxicity for now; broadening to other endpoint families will require additional ToxRefDB scoping. And the RDF layer is descriptive — it isn't yet pulling in mechanistic ontologies like AOP-Wiki to let the LLM reason about adverse outcome pathways across the graph. But these are extensions, not gaps in the core idea.

The reason this matters for the agentic-AI biomedical audience is that it's an honest, minimal example of the recipe that's going to define a lot of the next two years: package a sprawling open-data resource as a portable SQLite plus a versioned RDF projection, then put a schema-grounded LLM in front of it. ToxCast is one of the largest open computational-toxicology resources in the world. Making it queryable by a working scientist with no SPARQL background, and doing it in a way that resists hallucination by construction rather than by post-hoc filtering, is the kind of infrastructure work that pays back compound interest. Recommended read for anyone building agentic systems over biomedical knowledge graphs this year.
