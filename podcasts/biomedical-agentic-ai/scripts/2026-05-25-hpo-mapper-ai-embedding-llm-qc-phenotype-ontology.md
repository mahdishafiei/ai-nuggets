## Script

Today's pick is a fresh medRxiv preprint from the University of Southampton's Department of Human Genetics and Genomic Medicine, with a co-author who matters for context — Peter Robinson, the creator of the Human Phenotype Ontology. The paper is titled "Human Phenotype Ontology Mapper: Semantic Mapping of Clinical Findings to the Human Phenotype Ontology Using AI-Powered Embeddings and LLM-Based Quality Control." It's worth knowing about both for what it does and for the architectural pattern it lands on, which is the right shape for the broader problem of getting unstructured clinical text into structured biomedical knowledge.

Some background for why this paper rates a full episode. The Human Phenotype Ontology — HPO — is the workhorse ontology for describing disease phenotypes. It's used in rare-disease diagnosis, in clinical decision support, in cohort definition for genomics, and as the connective tissue between clinical phenotype data and gene-level knowledge bases. The catch, and it's a well-known catch, is that the rich phenotype information you'd want to map into HPO is mostly trapped in unstructured clinical notes. Mapping free text to HPO terms by hand is slow and unscalable, and the existing automated tools — rule-based mappers and earlier-generation neural taggers — have been brittle in production. The HPO Mapper paper is a fresh take on this with the modern stack, and the numbers it reports are good enough to take seriously.

The architecture has two stages and the second stage is what makes the design worth borrowing. The first stage is semantic embedding. The system takes a structured clinical input — they evaluate two variants, one derived from unstructured clinical reports as paired "clinical finding plus anatomical region" tuples, and one derived from ICD-10 codes — and uses biomedical embeddings to retrieve candidate HPO terms by semantic similarity. So far so standard. The second stage is the load-bearing part. An LLM acts as a quality-control filter over the embedding-proposed candidates, vetoing semantic neighbors that aren't actually correct phenotype assignments. The pattern — embedding for recall, LLM for precision — has become a recurring motif in biomedical retrieval, and HPO Mapper is a clean worked example in a domain where the cost of a wrong mapping is real, because a wrong HPO term silently corrupts downstream gene-level inference.

The numbers. On both clinical-finding-plus-anatomical-region inputs and on ICD-10-code-derived inputs, the system reports an F1 around 0.85 on HPO mapping accuracy — point-eight-five and point-eight-four respectively, with confidence intervals on the order of three to five points. Those are production-relevant numbers. But the headline result for me is a different one. They report that HPO Mapper converted 62.3% of previously unusable free-text entries into HPO terms. That's the bottleneck statistic. Free-text-to-ontology mapping isn't a closed problem with a 100% ceiling — there's always some fraction of clinical narrative that simply doesn't carry phenotype information, or carries it in a form too ambiguous to map confidently. Converting nearly two-thirds of the dark matter into structured terms is the unlock that downstream knowledge graphs and gene-level analyses need.

To demonstrate utility at cohort scale, they ran the mapper on an inflammatory bowel disease cohort and projected the resulting HPO term sets onto gene space. The recovered gene set included well-established IBD drivers — NOD2, IL6, STAT3, IL10RA, and CTLA4. The point is not that HPO Mapper discovered novel IBD biology — it didn't, and isn't trying to. The point is that the mapping is faithful enough that running it end-to-end from messy clinical notes through phenotype-to-gene projection recovers the genes a domain expert would expect to see. That's the validation an ingestion tool needs.

Three things make this paper worth attention for anyone building biomedical agentic systems. First, the architectural pattern. Embedding for retrieval, LLM for verification, applied at the data ingestion layer rather than at query time. Most LLM-on-ontology systems point the language model at the query side — answer questions over a knowledge graph, generate SPARQL, that kind of thing. This paper points the language model at the ingestion side — clean up the inputs before they hit the graph. Both ends of the pipeline deserve attention, and the ingestion end has historically been under-tooled.

Second, the open-science posture. The tool is publicly available. Peter Robinson being on the author list isn't ornamental — HPO's adoption depends on the upstream community having usable tooling, and a Southampton group plus the HPO creator releasing a production-grade mapper is the kind of collaboration that materially expands what downstream researchers can do with the ontology. For an audience that cares about open biomedical data infrastructure, this is the right shape.

Third, the generalization story they hint at. The paper frames HPO Mapper as a generalizable infrastructure for unlocking the latent value of clinical narrative data — bridging clinical records and genomic discovery. Read between the lines and the architecture is ontology-agnostic. The same embedding-plus-LLM-verifier shape would extend to MONDO for diseases, to ChEBI for chemicals, to Uberon for anatomy, to Mondo-linked exposure ontologies, to any of the OBO Foundry vocabularies. If the IBD-to-gene-driver result generalizes, you'd want to see this pattern run against half a dozen ontologies in parallel as a unified ingestion layer for a biomedical knowledge graph.

Two caveats worth flagging. First, the evaluation is on inflammatory bowel disease — one disease area, and a relatively well-characterized one. The 0.85 F1 number could be lower on diseases with more idiosyncratic phenotype vocabularies, on pediatric populations, or on non-English clinical text. Second, the LLM-as-quality-control step inherits whatever calibration weaknesses the underlying model has, and on rare-disease phenotypes the LLM's prior knowledge thins out fast. Both are tractable, and both deserve named follow-ups before this becomes a turnkey tool for production rare-disease pipelines.

For an audience that thinks about biomedical knowledge graphs, ontology engineering, and the upstream data layer that everything else sits on top of, the takeaway is straightforward. Embedding-plus-LLM-verifier is now a viable architecture for getting messy clinical text into structured ontology terms at production accuracy. The bottleneck moves one layer back — to the embeddings themselves, to the LLM's domain coverage, and to the question of which ontologies are next in the ingestion queue.

That's today's nugget.
