## Script

Good morning, Ollie. This is Ollie's AI Pulse for Wednesday, June twenty-fourth. Two threads dominated the AI community's attention over the last forty-eight hours. The first is a convergence story — the term "world model" has finally jumped the fence from the robotics camp into the agent-systems camp, and there's a paper out yesterday that makes that jump concrete. The second is the inference layer — two large rounds closed on Monday, on the same day, that together draw a pretty clear map of where the money is going now that the model layer is consolidating. We'll finish with a deep cut from Latent Space on why "AI security" is becoming a distinct discipline from "cybersecurity with A-I." Let's go.

First, Twitter Pulse.

The convergence story is the Qwen team at Alibaba dropping a paper called Qwen-AgentWorld on Monday night, and by Tuesday morning it was on top of Hugging Face daily papers and on the front page of Hacker News. The framing is what made it travel. They call it the first language world model. Not a vision world model — a language world model. The idea is that for general-purpose agents, the environment that matters is not pixels. It's command-line state, file system state, accessibility trees in Android, view hierarchies on Web, conversation state with M-C-P tools, returned bytes from a Terminal call. All of that is naturally represented in language. So they trained a model — two, actually, both Mixture-of-Experts, a thirty-five billion parameter total with three billion active, and a three hundred ninety-seven billion parameter total with seventeen billion active — to do next-state prediction across seven of those domains: M-C-P, Search, Terminal, software engineering, Android, Web, and O-S. The training is three stages. Continued pre-training to inject the state-transition dynamics. Supervised fine-tuning to activate explicit next-state-prediction reasoning, the chain-of-thought is literally — given this current state and this action, here is what the environment will return. And then reinforcement learning with a hybrid rubric-and-rule reward that sharpens how faithfully the model can simulate.

The numbers are tight but the direction is unambiguous. The three hundred ninety-seven billion version posts an average of fifty-eight point seven one on their own AgentWorldBench, beating G-P-T five point four at fifty-eight point two five. On the text-only domains it's about a one-point-two gap. Tighter on the visual ones — Web and Android, where accessibility trees alone leave information on the table. So the headline is not that they trounced a frontier closed model. It's that an open-weight Mixture-of-Experts trained explicitly on environment dynamics can match a closed frontier model at predicting what an agent's environment will do next. That's a different kind of capability claim than "we're better at math benchmarks."

Where this actually matters is what they do with the world model afterward. There are two stories in the paper. One — they use Qwen-AgentWorld as a decoupled environment simulator for agent reinforcement learning. You can train an agent in the simulator instead of paying to spin up real Terminals or real Android emulators. They report that mixing simulator-driven R-L with real-environment R-L outperforms real-only training on Tool Decathlon, their downstream agent benchmark. Two — they use world-model training itself as a warm-up for the agent foundation model. You train your model first to predict environments, then you fine-tune it to act in environments. The acting transfers better. The pre-training-as-warm-up frame is what makes this a foundation-model paper, not just a simulator paper. They are explicit — in one of the cleaner lines in the paper — that this is "not for cost reduction, but as a complementary axis for pushing the frontier." In Chinese-AI-research framing, this is the same instinct as 模拟环境作为预训练 — simulation-as-pretraining — that the Tairan He world has been arguing for the robotics side for two years. Alibaba just shipped it for the language side.

Now zoom out, because here's where it gets interesting on Twitter. Jim Fan at Nvidia has been giving a talk on the robotics circuit — most recently at Sequoia A-I Ascent — that he's been compressing into the line "V-L-As are dead, long live world action models." The argument is that Vision-Language-Action models — the dominant robotics paradigm of 2024 and 2025 — were a stopgap. They treated robotics as language with a video appendage. What you actually want is a video-first, world-model-first backbone where the language is the appendage. And Fan's lab has open-sourced something called DreamDojo, which generates pixel-space futures from motor commands, no engine and no hand-authored dynamics. He's calling it Simulation Two-Point-Oh. And he's been saying flatly on X that two thousand twenty-six will mark the first year that Large World Models lay real foundations for robotics, and for multimodal A-I more broadly.

The convergence is what Ollie should hear. The Qwen team is making the same architectural bet from the other direction. Robotics is converging on world models from the video-and-control side; agentic systems are converging on world models from the language-and-state side. The unifying abstraction is — train the model to predict the environment, then use that as the substrate for everything else. The biological world models that you work on sit philosophically in the middle of these two camps. The cell state is too high-dimensional to render as pixels and too structured to leave as raw language. If this convergence holds, the bio-world-model field will end up adopting the methodology that gets to ten million interaction trajectories first. Worth watching which lab puts together a foundation-model-scale dataset of perturbation-response trajectories in cells first — that's the analogous move.

The minor Twitter story, while we're on this — Marc Andreessen recirculated his "never hallucinate" custom instruction prompt over the weekend, and it picked up another round of mockery. The substantive take, which is worth absorbing because it keeps coming up — telling a language model not to hallucinate doesn't fix hallucination, because hallucination is not a behavior the model is choosing. But there's a more careful counter-take floating around that's worth holding — prompt engineering can't fix the architectural issue, but asking a model to cite sources, to flag uncertainty, and to refuse rather than guess does reduce error rates in practice. The right read is that the Andreessen prompt is a category error that nevertheless points at a real harm-reduction toolkit. The debate is less interesting than the toolkit.

That's Twitter Pulse.

Next — Money Moves.

Monday June twenty-second was the day the inference layer became its own funding category. Two rounds in one day. Baseten closed a Series F at one point five billion dollars, valuation around thirteen billion, led by Altimeter, Conviction, and Spark. The numbers underneath the round are the story. Annualized revenue run rate went from about two hundred million in December twenty twenty-five to about six hundred million by March of this year. That's tripling in a quarter. They report processing more than one billion inference calls a day, across eighty-seven clusters and eighteen clouds. The framing in their announcement is precise — inference at the app layer, as closed-source and open-source models converge in capability, cost, and customization. Translation — when every model is roughly as good as the next one and the differentiator is how you serve it, the inference operator captures the margin. That's the bet.

On the same day, Groq confirmed a six hundred fifty million dollar raise — led by Disruptive and Infinitum, the same investors with board seats. This is the second-act round after one of the strangest deals the sector has seen. Last December, Nvidia paid roughly twenty billion dollars for a non-exclusive license to Groq's tensor streaming processor technology, and in the same stroke hired away founder and C-E-O Jonathan Ross, president Sunny Madra, and a slice of the senior engineering team. By March, Nvidia had folded the I-P into its own Groq Three L-P-X inference card. Groq the company was left with the building, the remaining team, and the freedom to pivot. And the pivot is — they are not a chip startup anymore. They are an A-I inference neocloud. The six hundred fifty million is for data-center capacity, not silicon iteration.

Read these two rounds together and you can see the shape of the inference layer as a market. Baseten is the abstraction layer above the hardware — pick your model, pick your cloud, we'll handle the orchestration. Groq is the optimized-substrate layer — we'll run the hardware ourselves, you pay us per token. Both are now valued in the multi-billion range, in the same week, in the same business segment. Nvidia is essentially building the third version of that layer in-house, having paid twenty billion to do it. The implication for the model layer is real. If inference economics is where the margin is, the foundation model labs that don't control their own inference will be paying the inference layer a tax forever. That's part of why Anthropic and OpenAI are both investing in custom silicon paths, and it's the strategic logic behind why every major lab now has a serving product.

That's Money Moves.

Now — Podcast Deep Cut.

Last Monday, June twenty-second, Latent Space dropped an episode called "Red-Teaming after Mythos," with swyx hosting Zico Kolter and Matt Fredrikson. Kolter is on the OpenAI board and on the C-M-U faculty; Fredrikson is also at C-M-U and is the C-E-O of Gray Swan, the A-I red-teaming startup. The episode landed in the context of Anthropic's Mythos model being put under U-S Department of Commerce dual-use export controls earlier this year, which raised the policy question of what exactly is in a frontier-model export bucket that is not in a regular software export bucket. The episode's answer is the deep cut worth carrying for the week.

Kolter's opening framing, and I'm paraphrasing from the post — A-I systems have inherent vulnerabilities of their own. They can be tricked in ways people can be tricked. You need a different security mindset. Fredrikson sharpens it — A-I systems themselves introduce new vulnerabilities. It is not about using A-I to make your cyber infrastructure better. It is about understanding and mitigating the security risks you bring in when you adopt and deploy A-I.

The argument chain is — for forty years, cybersecurity was about adversaries exploiting code that did not handle untrusted input correctly. Buffer overflows, S-Q-L injection, deserialization bugs. The defensive playbook was — validate input, sandbox execution, patch the bug. With language-model-driven agents, the untrusted input is the input. There is no validating prose into a safe form, because the prose is the program. Indirect prompt injection — where an agent that fetches a webpage or reads a document encounters hidden instructions and follows them — is not a bug in the agent. It is the agent's substrate behaving as designed.

Gray Swan's commercial bet is on this gap. They have a system called Shade that automates red-teaming of frontier models and, per their evaluation work with Anthropic, reportedly now beats human red-teamers at finding jailbreaks at scale. The implication Kolter draws — and this is the take to carry — is that the assumption that frontier models get safer as they get more capable is empirically not holding for the indirect-injection class of attacks. The same capability gains that make agents useful also make them more obedient to instructions hidden in the data they read. You cannot scale-pill your way out of this.

The takeaway for the listener is — every model deployment story that involves agents reaching out to fetch arbitrary content needs an A-I-security review, not a cybersecurity review. The two disciplines have started to bifurcate. And the people doing the A-I-security review are coming out of language-model research labs, not network-security backgrounds. Worth tracking which biotech and life-sciences A-I deployments — there are now plenty — are even aware of this distinction. Most are not.

That's the deep cut.

Wrap.

The one thing to watch tomorrow is the first wave of real-world data from Anthropic's Claude Tag, which started rolling to Enterprise and Team customers yesterday inside Slack. It's an org-level persistent Claude that lives in your channels rather than a per-user chatbot, with what they call ambient behavior — Claude proactively keeps employees updated and follows up on threads. It's the first serious deployment of a persistent agent inside enterprise messaging at scale, and the question is whether it changes how people work or just becomes the new highest-volume source of Slack notifications. Either outcome is informative. Have a good Wednesday, Ollie.
