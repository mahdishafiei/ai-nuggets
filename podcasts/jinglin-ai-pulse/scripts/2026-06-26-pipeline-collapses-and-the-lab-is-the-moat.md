# Ollie's AI Pulse — Wan-Streamer collapses the pipeline, and the lab becomes the moat

## Sources

- Wan Team (Alibaba). "Wan-Streamer v0.1: An End-to-End Foundation Model for Real-time Interactive Audio-Visual Communication." arXiv 2606.25041, posted June 23, 2026. Lead author Lianghua Huang plus twenty-three co-authors. Single Transformer over interleaved video + audio + text input and output tokens; block-causal attention for streaming; 160 ms streaming unit at 25 fps; ~200 ms model-side latency, ~550 ms end-to-end (including ~350 ms bidirectional network), 192p preliminary resolution. Paper claims "perception, reasoning, generation, response timing, turn management, and cross-modal synchronization are learned jointly within one unified model," replacing the VAD → ASR → LLM → TTS → animation → video-gen cascade.
- Nie, Min, Xu, Huang, Song, Shan, Lin, Zhao, Li, Wen (ML-GSAI / Renmin University of China). "iLLaDA: Improved Large Language Diffusion Models." arXiv 2606.25331, posted June 24, 2026. 8B-parameter masked-diffusion language model. Fully bidirectional attention through both pretraining (12T tokens) and SFT (25B-token instruction corpus, 12 epochs). GQA, tied embeddings, confidence-based multiple-choice scoring head. Improvements over original LLaDA: +21.6 on BBH, +14.9 on ARC-Challenge (base); +14.5 on MATH, +16.5 on HumanEval (instruct). Authors claim it "remains competitive with Qwen2.5 7B on several benchmarks" despite non-autoregressive training. Models released on Hugging Face.
- Fesser, Zhang, Li, Wang, Perozzi, Azizi, Kakade, Zitnik (Harvard DBMI / Google DeepMind). "How Post-Training Shapes Biological Reasoning Models." arXiv 2606.16517, posted June 15, 2026; sustained discussion through this week. Trained >100 biological reasoning models across genomics / transcriptomics / proteins under controlled CPT/SFT/RL stage ablations; measured ID and OOD. Findings: CPT aligns model to biological language patterns; SFT consistently raises ID but causes OOD to peak early and decline; RL on strong SFT checkpoints with aligned rewards partially recovers OOD generalization. Under a fixed post-training budget, the recommended recipe is "brief SFT, larger RL allocations, and asymmetric adaptation capacity across stages."
- Sail Research $80M Series A led by Sequoia (Kleiner Perkins led the prior seed; Intel CEO Lip-Bu Tan, Alphabet Chair John Hennessy, Redpoint Ventures, Tri Dao angeled). $450M post-money. Announced June 25, 2026. CEO Neil Movva. Product: cloud platform for long-horizon agents — a customized vLLM + PagedAttention inference stack claiming ~1/10 the per-token cost of rivals, plus "Sailboxes" — stateful Linux VMs that suspend when an agent is waiting on an external system and resume later (so customers don't pay for idle GPUs). Claims 90.72% on BrowseComp-Plus. Customers: Parallel, Detail.dev, Jack and Jill.
- Scaled Cognition $100M Series A led by Khosla Ventures, with Genesys participating. ~$750M post-money. Announced June 25, 2026. Co-founder/CTO Dan Klein (UC Berkeley NLP). Product: "APT," a model architected to retrieve records rather than generate text for the action-taking parts of a workflow, with built-in double-verification before any confirmation is shown to the user. Bundled simulator mocks customer API surfaces for pre-deployment reliability testing. CTO quote: "Reliability is engineered into the architecture of our models, not bolted on after the fact." In production at Fortune 500 customers in finance, healthcare, telecom, insurance.
- Trase $107M seed led by ARCH Venture Partners with Red Cell Partners participating. Announced June 24, 2026. Product: Trase Origin — an agent operating system for regulated industries. Deployed inside Duke University Health System's Division of Cardiology automating the 5,000+ inbound faxes the clinic receives each month (previously hand-sorted by medical assistants and nurses): AI routing completes triage 7.1× faster, unlocks $285,450 in annual staff capacity. Notable because ARCH Venture Partners is the firm behind Illumina, Juno, Alnylam, and Vir — a biotech-heavy investor writing a nine-figure check into pure agent infrastructure.
- Joseph Krause (founder, Radical AI) on Latent Space (Science pod), "The Self-Driving Lab," hosted by Brandon Anderson, published June 17, 2026. ~1h16m. Argument: materials science cannot have an AlphaFold equivalent because the manufacturing process — not chemical composition — determines material properties. Quote: "In materials, the ground truth is the material itself. You have to be able to test it and characterize it." Therefore the closed loop — AI scientist proposes, robots synthesize, robots characterize, results retrain the AI scientist — is the unit of progress, not the model. Numbers: 1,200 alloys synthesized and characterized in six months (~10× DARPA/GE MACH's ~500/year benchmark); of ~300 materials tested, 10 had novel state-of-the-art properties. Direct quote on closed-loop value: "It's moved into elemental families or alloy families no one has ever published on before." Policy framing: "Now imagine every scientist in the United States doing 10 times the research output."

## Script

Good morning, Ollie. This is Ollie's AI Pulse for Friday, June twenty-sixth. The dominant pattern on the timeline this morning is the same one we hit yesterday from a different angle. The model is not the unit of progress. The unit of progress is what surrounds the model — the data loop, the inference scaffolding, the training recipe, and at the most extreme version of the argument, the physical lab itself. We'll start with three Hugging Face top-papers from the last forty-eight hours that each show that pattern in a different domain. We'll move into a funding day for agent reliability infrastructure that pushed nearly three hundred million dollars into the picks-and-shovels layer. And we'll close with Joseph Krause on Latent Space making the strongest version of the argument — that the lab is the moat. Let's go.

First, Twitter Pulse.

The most-shared paper on the timeline last night came out of Alibaba's Wan team. The title is Wan-Streamer Version Zero Point One. The author list is twenty-four researchers led by Lianghua Huang. What they have built is the architectural inversion of every clinical-conversation prototype that has been duct-taped together for the last two years. Today, when someone builds an A-I you can video-call with, it's a cascade. Voice activity detection feeds A-S-R. A-S-R feeds a language model. The language model feeds T-T-S. T-T-S feeds an animation model. The animation model feeds video generation. Six components, six interfaces, six places error and latency accumulate. Wan-Streamer collapses all of that into a single Transformer that ingests interleaved video, audio, and text tokens and emits interleaved video, audio, and text tokens. Block-causal attention lets it stream. The streaming unit is one hundred sixty milliseconds at twenty-five frames per second. Model-side latency around two hundred milliseconds. End-to-end latency around five hundred fifty milliseconds including network. Preliminary resolution is one ninety-two p. Their framing is that perception, reasoning, generation, response timing, turn management, and cross-modal synchronization are learned jointly inside one unified model.

The take to carry. This is not just a multimodal model that's faster. It is the claim that the perception-action loop should be one autoregressive multimodal stream, not a pipeline. If that claim holds up at higher resolution and longer sessions, the clinical-conversation A-I prototypes that look like Frankenstein right now collapse into a single fine-tuneable substrate. The biomedical version of this story will look exactly like what Alibaba just showed — a single model that listens, reasons, and renders a clinician avatar in real time, trained end-to-end on consented patient conversations. We are not there yet. But Wan-Streamer is the architectural proof of concept that the cascade was the bug, not the feature.

Second paper. Number two on Hugging Face dailies this morning is iLLaDA, which is the second-generation paper out of the Renmin University R-U-C group that has been the main academic shop pushing diffusion language models. The framing — modern large language models are essentially uniform in their training paradigm. Autoregressive factorization. Causal attention. Next-token prediction. The iLLaDA group keeps the masked-diffusion objective and fully bidirectional attention end-to-end. Not bolted onto an autoregressive backbone for refinement, the way most diffusion-flavored language work has been published. Bidirectional from pretraining through S-F-T. Eight billion parameters. Twelve trillion training tokens. Twenty-five billion-token instruction corpus, twelve epochs. The headline numbers — the new model picks up twenty-one-point-six points on Big-Bench Hard and roughly fifteen points on ARC-Challenge over the previous L-L-A-D-A, fourteen-and-a-half points on MATH and sixteen-and-a-half on HumanEval. The authors' claim is that it remains competitive with Qwen two-point-five seven B on several benchmarks despite being non-autoregressive.

The reason this matters for biomedical world models specifically, and why iScienceLuvr's thread on it picked up so quickly, is that the autoregressive monoculture has been the default assumption in biological foundation models for the last two years. Every cell foundation model. Every clinical language model. Every protein language model. They are all next-token-prediction, all left-to-right or some variant. And biological sequences — D-N-A, R-N-A, protein — are arguably more naturally bidirectional than English. The mutation in the middle of the sequence is constrained by what's on both sides of it. Diffusion-based generation already won the protein structural design fight with R-F-diffusion. iLLaDA is the first paper to credibly show that a fully bidirectional diffusion language model at eight billion parameters is not a toy at language benchmarks either. The next six months are about who ports it to biology first. Watch the cell-foundation-model labs that have been quiet for a quarter — that's where you should expect the move.

Third paper. This one's slightly older than the forty-eight-hour window — posted June fifteenth — but it kept coming back across the timeline this week, and it is the paper most directly relevant to your day job, so I'm including it. Marinka Zitnik's Harvard D-B-M-I group, with Sham Kakade and a Google DeepMind team. Title — How Post-Training Shapes Biological Reasoning Models. They trained more than one hundred biological reasoning models across genomics, transcriptomics, and proteins, varying the backbone, varying continued pretraining, varying supervised fine-tuning, varying reinforcement learning — and they measured in-distribution and out-of-distribution generalization separately at every stage.

The headline finding inverts the field's instinct. Continued pretraining aligns the model to biological language patterns. Supervised fine-tuning consistently raises in-distribution performance, but causes out-of-distribution performance to peak early and then decline. Reinforcement learning applied to strong S-F-T checkpoints with aligned rewards partially recovers the out-of-distribution generalization that S-F-T destroyed. So under a fixed post-training budget, the recipe is brief S-F-T followed by larger R-L allocation. The opposite of the field-standard recipe, which is heavy S-F-T followed by light R-L polish.

This is the kind of paper that quietly changes how the next year of biomedical-foundation-model work gets done, because the result is not "we built a bigger backbone." It's "we ran a hundred ablations on the recipe." Anyone fine-tuning Evo, Geneformer, or one of the protein language models for a clinical or perturbation task should read the failure mode they describe. Heavy S-F-T on a narrow ID set looks like a win — your benchmark goes up — and then your model fails to transfer to the next dataset, because S-F-T pulled the representation tighter around the training distribution than your problem allows. R-L with the right reward shape pushes it back out. The post-training recipe is part of the model now. Brief S-F-T plus heavy R-L is the take to carry.

The convergence across the three papers is what makes this the right Twitter Pulse. Different domains. Same shape. Wan-Streamer says the cascade was the bug. iLLaDA says the autoregressive monoculture was the bug. The Zitnik paper says the S-F-T-first recipe was the bug. Three independent papers in one week pushing against three different load-bearing defaults in how the field builds models. None of them are about scale. All three are about choosing the structure that matches the problem.

That's Twitter Pulse.

Now — Podcast Deep Cut.

Latent Space's science pod ran an episode on June seventeenth called "The Self-Driving Lab," hosted by Brandon Anderson, with Joseph Krause — the founder of Radical A-I. The episode is the most provocative version of this week's thread, so it's the one to carry.

The setup is — Radical A-I builds self-driving labs for materials discovery, and Anderson opens with the obvious question. Why has biology gotten AlphaFold and materials science has not? Why has materials been so much slower to yield to A-I? Krause's answer flips the question. The reason isn't that materials is harder. The reason is that materials is structurally different. In Krause's words — and this is the line that travels — in materials, the ground truth is the material itself. You have to be able to test it and characterize it.

The argument chain matters, so let me lay it out. In biology, molecules can largely be represented and predicted from sequence — the sequence uniquely defines the molecule. In materials, that is not true. Identical chemical composition produces dozens of different microstructures depending on synthesis temperature, on cooling rate, on supply chain provenance, on the specific furnace. Process determines properties at least as much as composition does. So there is no one-shot model — no AlphaFold for materials — that can substitute for the experiment. The model can be a hypothesis generator. The experiment is the ground truth.

Therefore — and this is the structural inversion — the moat flips. If the model can't predict ground truth from first principles, the lab is the moat. Whoever runs the most experiments fastest owns the data, and the data is the moat. Krause's line on that — it has moved into elemental families or alloy families no one has ever published on before. Read that quote carefully. The Self-Driving Lab is now generating proprietary data in compositional space that simply does not exist in any public corpus, anywhere. Frontier model labs cannot scrape it. They can't pay annotators to generate it.

The numbers ground the claim. Radical's lab synthesized and characterized twelve hundred alloys in six months. The DARPA-G-E MACH benchmark for the same kind of work is about five hundred per year. Roughly ten-times-speedup. Of about three hundred materials proposed by the A-I scientist and tested, ten had novel state-of-the-art properties. And Krause closes on the policy framing — now imagine every scientist in the United States doing ten times the research output. That just changes the trajectory of discovery. The geopolitical move is — substitute closed-loop throughput for headcount as the West's lever against China's centralized manufacturing scale.

The Ollie-specific take. Krause is wrong about biology in one direction and right in another. He is right that the parts of biology that look like materials — formulation, in-vivo delivery, manufacturing, cell context, the actual phenotypic readout — will not yield to a one-shot model. He is wrong that all of biology is sequence-defined. The cell isn't a token string either. Cell state is way too high-dimensional and the trajectory through perturbation space is process-dependent in exactly the way Krause describes for materials. So the question the episode forces is — which parts of your problem are sequence-defined, where the model is the unit of progress and an AlphaFold-style win is on the table — and which parts are process-defined, where the closed loop is the unit of progress and the model is just one component of it? Most single-cell perturbation work is the second category. The biggest moat in biological world models will not be the model weights. It'll be the lab — or the consortium of labs — that runs the cleanest closed-loop perturbation campaign at the largest scale. The Tahoe One Hundred M consortium and whatever industrial labs are quietly running in-house Perturb-seq atlases are the right places to look. Bet on the loop, not on the architecture.

That's the deep cut.

Wrap.

The one thing to watch over the next few weeks is the framing of the next big biological-foundation-model announcement. Listen for whether the team leads with the architecture — "we built a new attention variant for cell state" — or whether the team leads with the data loop — "we ran two million perturbations across forty cell lines and trained on the resulting trajectories." If the headlines from the next major bio-foundation-model release are about architecture, the field has not yet absorbed this week's lesson. If the headlines are about the closed-loop data corpus, the field has. Krause's argument, Zitnik's recipe paper, the Trase deployment, the Sail and Scaled Cognition rounds — they are all the same bet pointed at four different parts of the stack. The thing that wins next is the thing that owns the loop. Have a good weekend, Ollie.
