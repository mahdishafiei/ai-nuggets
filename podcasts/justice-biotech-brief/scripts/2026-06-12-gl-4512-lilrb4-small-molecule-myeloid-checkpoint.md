# GL-4512 — first direct small-molecule modulator of the LILRB4 myeloid checkpoint

Paper link: https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.64898/2026.06.10.731269v1

## Script

Welcome to Justice's Biotech Brief for Friday, June twelfth. Today's news flow is light, so the pick is a bioRxiv preprint out of Moustafa Gabr's lab at Weill Cornell — a first-direct small-molecule modulator of L-I-L-R-B-four, also called I-L-T-three, the immunosuppressive myeloid checkpoint that has, until now, only been drugged with antibodies. The compound is called G-L-forty-five-twelve. Going off the abstract here — the full P-D-F is locked behind a Cloudflare wall this morning — but the abstract is unusually complete, and the story is clean. Let me give you the take, and then why it matters.

L-I-L-R-B-four is the inhibitory receptor on monocytes, myeloid-derived suppressor cells, and a slice of acute myeloid leukemia blasts that has been on the immune-evasion target list for almost a decade. It binds A-poE, fibronectin, and the recently identified secretogranin two ligand, and through that engagement it pulls SHP-one and SHP-two onto its cytoplasmic tail, dampens T-cell activation, and remodels the tumor microenvironment toward suppression. The clinical pipeline against it is entirely antibodies — IO-two-oh-two from Immune-Onc in A-M-L combinations, J&J's bispecific LBL-oh-forty-three engaging C-D-three and L-I-L-R-B-four, several others in earlier development. Small molecules against this receptor have basically not existed. The reason is structural: L-I-L-R-B-four is a non-enzymatic surface receptor with no obvious druggable pocket. There's no substrate channel, no allosteric site that crystallizes nicely, no ATP-binding cleft. It's the kind of target medicinal chemists walk past.

What Gabr's group did is interesting on two fronts. First, they used a Dianthus thermal-shift screening platform — temperature-related intensity change — that reads ligand binding as a perturbation in protein thermal stability rather than as competition at a known site. That's the right tool for a target with no known pocket; you don't need to know where the binder lands to score a hit. Hits were then confirmed with microscale thermophoresis, surface plasmon resonance, and cellular thermal-shift assay in cells, with nanomolar affinity. Second — and this is the part that turns a hit into a credible asset — they ran microsecond-scale molecular dynamics on the receptor and used the simulations together with site-directed mutagenesis to identify a previously unrecognized ligandable pocket in the flexible extracellular domain. That's a cryptic pocket — one that doesn't appear in the static crystal structure of the apo protein, but opens transiently as the protein breathes. The combination of a thermal-shift screen plus M-D-derived cryptic pockets is exactly the playbook the field has been talking about for receptors thought to be undruggable. This is one of the cleaner examples of it working.

Functionally, G-L-forty-five-twelve disrupts the L-I-L-R-B-four-to-secretogranin-two signaling axis, suppresses SHP-one, SHP-two, and downstream STAT-three. In patient-derived colorectal cancer and acute myeloid leukemia co-culture systems, the compound restored anti-tumor immune activity, lifted interferon-gamma and I-L-two production, increased cytotoxic T-cell activation, and dropped tumor-cell viability. Patient-derived rather than cell-line — that matters for a myeloid checkpoint where the suppressive context is a property of primary cells, not of an immortalized line. Pharmacokinetics support oral dosing, and in the immunocompetent C-T-twenty-six syngeneic colorectal model, once-daily oral G-L-forty-five-twelve significantly suppressed tumor growth and increased intratumoral immune activation.

The take. Three things make this worth flagging. First, if the ligand quality holds up in lead optimization — meaning the binding is real and selective across the L-I-L-R-B family, the receptor has six paralogs and they're not all suppressive — this becomes a different deployment story than antibody L-I-L-R-B-four blockade. Oral, lower cost of goods, easier combinations with chemotherapy or with checkpoint inhibitors, potential C-N-S penetrance for settings where biologics can't go, dose titration on a different timescale. Second, the methodology is the part the field will copy. There is a long list of myeloid checkpoints — L-I-L-R-B-one, L-I-L-R-B-two, T-I-M-three's relatives, V-I-S-T-A in some flavors — that have resisted small-molecule discovery for the same structural reason. A thermal-shift screen plus M-D-driven cryptic-pocket identification, validated by mutagenesis, is now a demonstrated route through that bottleneck on a real target. Third, the competitive read against the antibody programs. IO-two-oh-two is in Phase one combinations with azacitidine in A-M-L and has shown activity. A small molecule doesn't beat that antibody in 2026. But if a small molecule can be combined with PD-one blockade in solid tumors at low cost and with oral dosing, that's a different commercial profile than an L-I-L-R-B-four antibody competing for combination slots against established checkpoint biologics. It expands the addressable use rather than racing the antibodies on their home turf.

Two caveats. The work is preprint, single-lab, and the numerical data — exact affinity, percent tumor growth inhibition, dose used — is behind the Cloudflare wall, so I'm reading off the abstract for now. And the C-T-twenty-six colorectal model is a workhorse but it's also forgiving — the same model that has scored positive for many candidates that didn't translate. What I'd want to see next is selectivity data across the L-I-L-R-B family, oral bioavailability in a non-rodent species, and combination data with PD-one blockade in a model where single-agent PD-one is sub-maximal. Until then, this is a credible first move on a target the field had given up trying to drug with anything other than biologics. See you next week.
