# Justice's Biotech Brief — 2026-06-20

Paper link: https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.64898/2026.06.15.732449v1

## Script

Good morning, Justice. One pick today, from bioRxiv overnight — a paper out of Ginkgo Bioworks that does something the bispecific antibody field has wanted for a decade and has never quite gotten at this scale. The Ginkgo group built one hundred sixty bispecifics from sixty-five parental antibody arms, on a uniform knobs-into-holes CrossMab I-G-G-one scaffold, ran ten developability assays on every parent and every bispecific, and asked one clean question. Given the two parents' biophysical readouts, how well can you predict the bispecific's behavior? The answer turns out to be sharp, quantitative, and immediately actionable for any group running a T cell engager, A-D-C, or trispecific discovery campaign.

Their P-R-O-P-H-E-T-A-b platform — Ginkgo's high-throughput biophysics rig — generates the matched parental-versus-bispecific dataset. The ten assays cover hydrophobicity, surface charge, self-association, polyreactivity, thermostability, aggregation, and the usual developability panel. Everything sits on one fixed scaffold and one fixed format. That fixed-format design is what makes the inheritance question tractable; mix in scaffold variation and you can no longer attribute outcomes cleanly to the parental arms.

Three classes of inheritance come out of the data, and each one carries a different platform-engineering implication.

First class — clean inheritance. Hydrophobicity and surface charge come straight through from the parents. Spearman rho is roughly point-eight-five to point-nine-five. That number is high enough that, on this scaffold, the bispecific's hydrophobicity is essentially a deterministic function of the two arms' hydrophobicities — same for surface charge. The practical consequence is that you can, and should, triage these two liabilities at the parental level. If both arms score poorly on hydrophobicity, you do not need to express and purify the bispecific to know it will score poorly. The authors push this one step further: avoid pairing two arms that simultaneously sit high on H-I-C and high on H-A-C — the hydrophobic-by-surface-charge plane. There is a parental biophysical ceiling on that plane, and pairing two arms that both push against it is a reliable way to manufacture a developability disaster.

Second class — partial inheritance. Self-association and polyreactivity come through from the parents at rho roughly point-six to point-nine, but with real outliers. The outliers are the interesting part because they are mechanistically interpretable. Many of them are driven by F-V to F-V charge complementarity between the two arms. Two variable regions whose surface charges are complementary — positive patch meets negative patch — stick to each other across the bispecific molecule, and the assembled bispecific is suddenly far stickier than either parent predicts. The mitigation is the inverse design rule. Pair F-V regions whose net charges are the same sign rather than opposite, and you suppress emergent self-association. The caveat the authors flag is that this rule generalizes only up to the formulation buffer; ionic strength changes the picture, so you re-validate the lead pairings in the buffer you actually plan to use.

Third class — does not inherit. Thermostability. Spearman rho is below point-four. There is essentially no information about the bispecific's melting behavior in the parental arms' melting behavior on this scaffold. You build the bispecific, you measure. There is no shortcut, no parental triage, no in-silico inference good enough to substitute. This is the rule the field has implicitly known but has not had quantified at this sample size before.

Why this matters for your group. Bispecific developability screening is expensive because every molecule has to be expressed, purified, and assayed before you can score it. If hydrophobicity and surface charge can be filtered upstream from the parental monoclonal data, that's a meaningful reduction in the bispecific-stage workload — depending on how aggressive the upstream cut is, it can be most of an order of magnitude. The F-V to F-V charge rule is the kind of design heuristic that is easy to overlook because monoclonal optimization rewards binding-pocket complementarity, and the same intuition that produces a good antigen-binder can produce a stickier bispecific when you pair it with the wrong partner arm. Reading the H-I-C-times-H-A-C ceiling and the F-V charge-sign rule together gives a platform team a defensible upstream filter before the construction stage. The thermostability finding is the inverse — don't expect parental melting temperatures to bail you out. Build it and test it.

Two important caveats. One — the scaffold is fixed. Everything in this paper is knobs-into-holes CrossMab I-G-G-one. Other bispecific architectures — D-V-D-I-g, B-i-T-E, dual-variable-domain, tandem scFv on a half-life-extending chassis, the appended-Fab formats — may inherit differently, and the authors are explicit that the rules need re-validation per format. So this is a starting point for a CrossMab platform; it is a hypothesis to test for anything else. Two — the work is authored and funded by Ginkgo Bioworks employees, and P-R-O-P-H-E-T-A-b is their commercial high-throughput platform. This is not external critique of the platform; this is the platform's own benchmark on its own scaffold. That is not a reason to discount the numbers — the experimental design is sound and the dataset is large — but it is a reason to want a second-site replication on an unrelated scaffold before betting a clinical program on the selection rules.

Net take. This is the kind of paper that should change how a translational platform group sets up the first three months of a new bispecific or trispecific campaign. Filter parental arms on hydrophobicity and charge before pairing. Design partner pairings against F-V to F-V charge complementarity rather than for it. And budget bispecific-stage testing for thermostability, because the parental signal really is below useful. Paper link is in the show notes.

That's the brief. Back tomorrow.
