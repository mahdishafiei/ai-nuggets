# CD8-iTreg CAR — bifunctional regulatory–cytolytic CD19 CAR T cell controls Nalm-6 leukemia and xenogeneic GVHD via Granzyme-K supramolecular attack particles, reframing the CRS dilemma for autoimmune CAR-T

Paper link: https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.64898/2026.06.18.731665v1

## Script

Good morning, Justice. One pick today, from bioRxiv overnight, and it lands directly in the middle of the conversation you've been having on your bench all year — how to keep the deep B-cell depletion of CD19 CAR T-cell therapy while losing the cytokine release storm. The paper says the answer might not be a different CAR, a different scaffold, or a different patient-prep regimen. It might be a different T-cell starting population.

The work is out of the Blazar lab in Minnesota in collaboration with Michael Dustin's group at Oxford, plus contributions from the Kean lab at Boston Children's and Harvey Cantor at Dana-Farber. That is essentially the consortium that has shaped human regulatory T-cell therapy and supramolecular-attack-particle biology for the last fifteen years, so when this group puts a CD8 regulatory T-cell paper out, it's worth reading carefully.

Set up what they did. They take human CD8 positive, CD25 negative T cells out of peripheral blood and induce them into a regulatory phenotype using anti-CD3-epsilon antibody loaded onto artificial antigen presenting cells, plus interleukin-two, plus TGF-beta, plus rapamycin. Call this population CD8-iTreg — induced CD8 regulatory T-cells. So far this is a known recipe for pushing CD8 cells toward a regulatory program, but the field's previous attempts have run into two problems. The cells lose their phenotype after a few divisions, and they lose their ability to kill anything because the regulatory program suppresses the cytolytic machinery. Both problems are the reason CD4-derived induced Tregs have been the dominant cell-therapy substrate for autoimmune indications, not CD8-iTregs.

The first claim of this paper is that their recipe produces CD8-iTreg cells that are simultaneously regulatory and cytolytic. The phenotype work — multi-parameter spectral flow plus single-cell RNA sequencing — shows a population that is stable, highly proliferative, and bifunctional. On the regulatory side, they express the canonical Treg markers — FoxP3, HELIOS, CD25, CD39, CTLA-4, the CCR4 trafficking receptor, IL-10 — and they downregulate the pro-inflammatory cytokines that conventional CD8 effectors produce. They also express CD103, which is the tissue-residency marker that lets them home into tissue rather than recirculate in blood. On the cytolytic side, they retain perforin, they retain multiple granzymes, and they show suppressive activity in functional assays comparable to CD4-iTregs — same suppression magnitude, just from a CD8 starting cell.

The second claim is the mechanism question, and this is where the Dustin lab fingerprint shows up. The killing isn't happening through the conventional cytotoxic-T-lymphocyte synapse model where perforin punches a hole and granzymes flow in. It's happening through supramolecular attack particles — SMAPs — which are these hundred-and-twenty-nanometer thrombospondin-shelled particles that pack perforin and granzymes into a discrete deliverable unit that the T cell releases at the target. The shell here is thrombospondin-4 rather than the thrombospondin-one that's been characterized in the CTL SMAP literature, and the particle is enriched in Granzyme-K rather than the Granzyme-B that dominates conventional CTL killing. The interesting biology is that Granzyme-K appears to do double duty — it contributes to the cytotoxic function on the target, and it contributes to the immune-suppressive function on bystander effector T cells. So one molecule, packaged into one delivery vehicle, gives you both arms of the bifunctional phenotype. That's a tidier mechanistic explanation for the regulatory-plus-cytolytic dichotomy than anything I've seen in the CD4-iTreg literature.

The third claim is the in vivo translation, and this is the experiment that should make you sit up. They transduce the CD8-iTreg cells with an anti-CD19 single-chain CAR — call it CAR19-iTreg — and they run it head-to-head against a conventional CAR19-loaded cytotoxic T lymphocyte in a CD19-positive Nalm-6 human leukemia xenograft model in immunodeficient mice. The CAR19-iTreg cells were not equivalent to CAR19-CTLs — they were superior. Better reduction in tumor burden. Longer survival. And critically, lower pro-inflammatory cytokine production at every measured time point. The conventional reading of an iTreg-derived CAR-T would predict the opposite — that the regulatory program would dampen anti-tumor activity. What seems to be happening is that the SMAP-packaged Granzyme-K is doing the killing through a delivery modality that doesn't require the full inflammatory storm of conventional CTL degranulation.

The fourth experiment is the one that should land hardest for the autoimmune CAR-T thesis. They take the CAR19-iTreg cells into a xenogeneic graft-versus-host disease model — human T cells transferred into immunodeficient mice get systemic inflammation and lethality — with residual human leukemia simultaneously present. The CAR19-iTreg cells suppress the GVHD lethality and control the leukemic growth at the same time, without increasing systemic inflammation. That's the combination the autoimmune CAR-T field has been chasing for three years — durable B-cell depletion plus controlled toxicity in a setting where pre-existing T-cell-driven inflammation is the disease driver. Kyverna's KYV-101 program, Cabaletta's CABA-201 program, Cartesian's Descartes-08, the various academic CD19 CAR-T programs out of Erlangen, Stanford, NIH — every one of them is having the cytokine release syndrome conversation as the gating clinical issue for moving this modality into lupus, myositis, scleroderma, neuromyelitis optica, all the rest. A CAR-T whose CAR-bearing cells are themselves anti-inflammatory by design rewrites that conversation.

A few caveats worth keeping straight. One — this is xenograft data in immunodeficient mice, not autologous human data. The CD8-iTreg phenotype stability in the absence of supporting cytokines, and the persistence of the regulatory program over months rather than weeks, are both open questions that the manuscript only partially addresses. Two — the paper is less than thirty-six hours old, the body figures aren't yet rendered on the bioRxiv server, and I'm working off the abstract plus author backgrounds. The quantitative size of the CAR19-iTreg versus CAR19-CTL effect — was tumor reduction two-fold or twenty-fold, did survival extend by a week or by months — those are in the figures, not the abstract, and the take should be calibrated when the full body lands. Three — autologous CD8-iTreg manufacturing is a real engineering problem. Anti-CD3-loaded aAPCs plus four cytokines is a more complex differentiation than the standard CD3-CD28 bead activation that most clinical CAR-T manufacturers run. Whoever takes this into the clinic has to industrialize the differentiation step before they get to the CAR transduction step. Four — the paper is allogeneic-style biology with autologous-style application, and translating CAR19-iTreg into a clinical program almost certainly demands the same gene-editing or allogeneic platform work that the Cabaletta and Kyverna programs have been doing in parallel for their conventional CAR-T constructs.

Net take. This is a sharp piece of work that takes the CD8-iTreg substrate that the field had quietly written off and shows that with the right differentiation recipe you get a cell that does what the CD4-iTreg programs can't — namely, deliver durable target-cell killing through a delivery modality that doesn't carry the inflammatory tax. The supramolecular attack particle as a payload-delivery vehicle, with Granzyme-K as the bifunctional molecule that does both cytolysis and bystander suppression, is the kind of mechanistic insight that should be portable to T-cell engager constructs as well — if you can build a TCE that biases its target-T-cell engagement toward SMAP release rather than free degranulation, you may get the same toxicity dampening from a more deployable modality. For your translational interest list — CAR-T, T cell engagers, autoimmune biologics — this is one of the more consequential preprints of the month and the one to watch through full-text-and-figures release and then through whatever cell therapy startup picks it up first.
