# Justice's Biotech Brief — 2026-06-17

Paper link: https://www.globenewswire.com/news-release/2026/06/15/3311621/0/en/Context-Therapeutics-Announces-Positive-Interim-Efficacy-and-Safety-Results-from-Ongoing-Phase-1-Clinical-Trial-for-CTIM-76.html

## Script

Good morning, Justice. Today's brief is Context Therapeutics' first efficacy peek at C-TIM seventy-six, a Claudin-six by C-D-three bispecific T cell engager, in platinum-resistant ovarian cancer. The interim Phase 1 readout dropped Monday from a May 29 data cut. The headline numbers are small, but the shape of them is what makes this interesting.

The trial is a standard Phase 1a dose escalation. Twenty-one patients enrolled across three Claudin-six positive solid tumors: fourteen with platinum-resistant ovarian cancer, four testicular, three endometrial. Weekly infusion, doses from twenty-two and a half micrograms up to five hundred sixty. The five-sixty dose blew through the target exposure window and was abandoned. The active range Context is calling out is one-forty to two-eighty micrograms weekly, and the efficacy numbers come from ten evaluable patients in that band, seven of them ovarian.

In those seven heavily pretreated ovarian patients, two confirmed partial responses, four with disease control. That's twenty-nine percent objective response, fifty-seven percent disease control. Three patients past the six-month mark on treatment. Safety: cytokine release syndrome in one patient, grade one. No I-CANS. No dose-limiting toxicities. Adverse events mostly grade one or two, reversible, clustered around the first or second dose. FDA Fast Track in platinum-resistant ovarian.

Now the take. Two things make this worth your attention beyond just another Phase 1 ORR line.

First, the patient context. The ovarian cohort had a median of seven prior lines of therapy. Eighty-nine percent had prior antibody-drug conjugates — which in practice means most of these patients had already exhausted mirvetuximab, the folate-receptor-alpha ADC that's the current standard in second and third line platinum-resistant disease. One hundred percent had prior anti-VEGF. Seventy-eight percent had a DNA repair agent on board at some point — PARPs, mostly. Fifty-five percent had a checkpoint inhibitor. These are patients out of options. Mirvetuximab in earlier-line platinum-resistant ovarian runs about a thirty-one percent response rate. Getting twenty-nine percent in a post-mirvetuximab population is not a smaller number — it's the same number on a much sicker denominator. The asset clearly has activity past ADC failure, which is the real translational question for any new ovarian agent right now.

Second, and this is the bigger story for your group: Claudin-six is the hottest "fetal antigen re-expressed in tumors" target in solid-tumor immunotherapy, and until now the main proof-of-concept asset has been BioNTech's B-N-T two-eleven — an autologous Claudin-six CAR-T paired with a CAR-Vac m-R-N-A booster. Phase 1/2 ORR in the low thirties, but with the full CAR-T operational stack: leukapheresis, lymphodepleting chemotherapy, manufacturing window, hospitalization, meaningful CRS. C-TIM seventy-six is going after the same target with an off-the-shelf bispecific T cell engager — no cell collection, no conditioning, no manufacturing. And the safety asymmetry is exactly what you'd want it to be. Grade one CRS in one out of nine patients at active dose, no I-CANS at all, no D-L-Ts. If those numbers hold through expansion, you have a Claudin-six asset that delivers cell-therapy-grade efficacy on an outpatient antibody footprint. That's the same shape of argument that's been playing out in B-cell malignancies — bispecifics catching CAR-T on depth of response while undercutting it on operational burden — now extending into a solid tumor where CAR-T has had a much harder time.

The caveats are real. Seven evaluable ovarian patients at active doses is a thin denominator. Open-label, single-arm, dose-escalation cohort. No biomarker selection disclosed — they're not pre-screening for Claudin-six expression by I-H-C, which means future expansion cohorts could get diluted by Claudin-six-low patients and the response rate could drift down. The early durability signal of three patients past six months is encouraging but tiny. And Context is a microcap; the financing path to a Phase 1b expansion in 2027 isn't a sure thing.

Next data update: the company plans to explore a Q-three-weekly dosing schedule in the second half of this year, leaning on early P-K that suggests weekly is overkill. If Q-three-W holds the efficacy and the safety, this asset shifts from "interesting solid-tumor T-cell-engager" to "outpatient injection schedule competitive with the ADC standard of care" — which is when the field starts paying real attention.

That's today's brief.
