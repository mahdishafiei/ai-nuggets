# LB2501 — Legend Biotech's in vivo CD19/CD20 CAR-T hits 100% ORR and 83% CR with no ICANS at top dose in r/r B-NHL, the first clean clinical proof-of-concept that a single IV infusion can replace ex vivo manufacturing and lymphodepletion conditioning

## Script

Today's pick is the Phase 1 readout for Legend Biotech's LB-twenty-five-oh-one, presented in a late-breaking oral session at the European Hematology Association meeting in Stockholm on Saturday. This is the first clean clinical proof-of-concept for an in-vivo CAR-T cell therapy — meaning the CAR-T cells are generated inside the patient from a single intravenous infusion of a vector, with no ex-vivo manufacturing, no apheresis-collection-edit-expand-reinfuse loop, and no lymphodepletion conditioning chemotherapy. For a translational immunologist running a CAR-T program, this is the data point that the field has been waiting on for three years.

The mechanism is what makes the result interesting. LB-twenty-five-oh-one uses Legend's TaVec platform — a lentiviral vector engineered with a surface targeting domain that restricts transduction to T cells. You infuse the vector intravenously. It finds endogenous T cells in vivo, delivers a CAR cassette, and the patient's own T cells express the CAR on their surface and start expanding in response to tumor engagement. The CAR is dual-targeting — CD-nineteen and CD-twenty — designed to close the antigen-escape door that single-target CD-nineteen products have run into. The whole pipeline collapses from a multi-week vein-to-vein process into a single infusion at the bedside, and the patient never sees fludarabine-cyclophosphamide conditioning. Those two changes alone would be a major operational shift; the question was whether the in-vivo route could deliver the depth of response that autologous CAR-T puts up. Until this readout, the field had a handful of in-vivo programs in clinic — Capstan's lipid-nanoparticle messenger-RNA approach, Umoja's lentiviral approach, Interius's lentiviral approach — but no data showing convincing complete responses at the top dose.

The numbers. Twelve patients with relapsed-or-refractory B-cell non-Hodgkin lymphoma dosed across two dose levels, data cut April first. Dose level one in six patients produced a fifty-percent overall response rate and a thirty-three-percent complete response rate. Dose level two in another six patients produced a one-hundred-percent overall response rate — six of six — with an eighty-three-percent complete response rate — five of six. All responses ongoing at the data cut, with median follow-up at dose level two of two-point-two months. CAR-T cells were detected in peripheral blood for up to one hundred sixteen days — the in-vivo-generated CAR-T population is real, expanding, and persisting.

Safety is where the readout breaks open. No dose-limiting toxicities. No serious adverse events. No deaths. No I-CANS — zero — across all twelve patients at both dose levels. Cytokine release syndrome in two-thirds of patients, but every event grade one to two, median duration four-and-a-half days, no steroids required, and only four patients of the twelve needed tocilizumab. Infusion-related reactions in seventy-five percent, also all grade one to two, median resolution under nineteen hours. Grade-three-or-higher cytopenias were limited to lymphocyte and neutrophil decreases — and importantly, there is no lymphodepleting chemotherapy administered before the infusion, so the cytopenias that did appear are biology-driven from the CAR-T expansion itself, not from added pre-treatment marrow suppression. This is the safety profile that the in-vivo concept was supposed to deliver: a CAR-T-style efficacy ceiling without the autologous CAR-T-style neurotoxicity floor.

The translational read. The two operational barriers that have throttled CAR-T from becoming a standard-of-care modality in earlier lines are manufacturing — the ex-vivo edit-expand cycle costs hundreds of thousands of dollars, takes weeks, and fails in a meaningful fraction of patients — and conditioning, where the fludarabine-cyclophosphamide regimen excludes elderly and cytopenia-fragile patients and limits how aggressively you can re-dose. LB-twenty-five-oh-one removes both. If the platform holds up across larger patient cohorts and longer follow-up, the addressable population for CD-nineteen-targeted therapy expands meaningfully — not just earlier-line non-Hodgkin lymphoma, but the patient cohorts that currently get bispecific antibody salvage because they cannot tolerate conditioning. The read-through to autoimmune CAR-T is bigger. The whole autoimmune CAR-T thesis — lupus, scleroderma, multiple sclerosis, refractory rheumatoid arthritis — has been gated by the safety asymmetry between a non-malignant patient and chemotherapy conditioning. If you can dose a CAR-T product without lymphodepletion and without I-CANS, the risk-benefit math for autoimmune indications shifts substantially.

The caveats are real and worth naming. Six patients at the top dose, two-point-two-month median follow-up, and B-cell non-Hodgkin lymphoma is the most CAR-T-tractable indication in oncology, so the durability and the cross-disease generalizability questions are both open. Lentiviral vector platforms carry their own regulatory and surveillance burden — replication-competent lentivirus testing, integration site monitoring, long-term follow-up — that ex-vivo products have a decade of experience navigating. The complete-response curve at one year is what tells you whether in-vivo CAR-T is autologous-CAR-T-equivalent or merely autologous-CAR-T-shaped. And the head-to-head against autologous lisocabtagene-maraleucel and axicabtagene-ciloleucel that would settle the durability question is still years away.

The competitive picture. The most useful comparison is to the other in-vivo CAR-T programs that have read out smaller pieces — Capstan's LNP-mRNA platform delivers transient, redoseable CAR-T with a fundamentally different persistence profile; Umoja and Interius use lentiviral approaches that look more directly similar to Legend's. Legend now sits in the lead position with the cleanest efficacy data and the cleanest safety profile across the field. The other read-through, back to yesterday's MonumenTAL-three talquetamab data, is the convergence — bispecific T-cell engagers and in-vivo CAR-Ts are now both trying to deliver CAR-T-grade depth-of-response on an off-the-shelf, no-lymphodepletion administration footprint. The same competitive pressure is going to land on every late-line and second-line indication in B-cell oncology over the next two years.
