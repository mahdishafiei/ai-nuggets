# Talquetamab — GPRC5D×CD3 bispecific T-cell engager cuts PFS hazard 72% in second-line myeloma in MonumenTAL-3 phase 3, NEJM publication moves GPRC5D from post-BCMA salvage into the earlier-line franchise

Paper link: https://www.nejm.org/doi/full/10.1056/NEJMoa2604657

## Script

Today's pick is the MonumenTAL-three phase three readout for Johnson and Johnson's GPRC-five-D-targeting bispecific T-cell engager talquetamab — brand name Talvey — presented at the European Hematology Association meeting in Stockholm on Saturday and simultaneously published in the New England Journal of Medicine. This is the first phase three to establish a GPRC-five-D bispecific as a progression-free-survival winner in earlier-line relapsed myeloma, and the numbers move the drug decisively out of its current post-BCMA-salvage positioning and into the second-line slot. For a translational immunologist tracking the T-cell engager franchise, this is the most consequential bispecific readout of the year so far.

The architecture is the place to start, because it's what makes the result interesting. Talquetamab is an off-the-shelf intravenously-administered bispecific antibody — one arm binds GPRC-five-D, the G-protein-coupled receptor family C group five member D, which is selectively expressed on malignant plasma cells and a narrow set of normal keratinized tissues — skin, hair, and tongue. The other arm binds CD-three, redirecting cytotoxic T cells onto the GPRC-five-D-expressing myeloma cell. The strategic significance of GPRC-five-D as a target is that it's the second non-overlapping surface antigen on the myeloma plasma cell that supports a clinical program of similar scale to the BCMA franchise. The BCMA-directed bispecifics — Tecvayli and Elrexfio — and the BCMA CAR-Ts — CARVYKTI and Abecma — all share one antigen and one resistance mechanism, antigen loss or BCMA shedding. A second target with non-overlapping expression means you get a sequencing pathway that retains activity after BCMA-targeted therapy fails, plus a parallel franchise in patients who are anti-BCMA-naive. MonumenTAL-three is the proof that the parallel-franchise positioning works in second-line disease.

The numbers. Eight hundred sixty-four patients with relapsed or refractory myeloma after at least one prior line of therapy including lenalidomide and a proteasome inhibitor. Eighty-five percent of them were lenalidomide-refractory and ninety-three percent refractory to their last line of therapy — a heavily pretreated population for second-line study. Three arms — talquetamab plus subcutaneous daratumumab plus pomalidomide, talquetamab plus subcutaneous daratumumab alone, or the control regimen of daratumumab plus pomalidomide plus dexamethasone. Both talquetamab-containing arms beat the control on the progression-free-survival primary endpoint. The triplet produced a hazard ratio of zero-point-two-eight — a seventy-two-percent reduction in the risk of progression or death. The doublet produced a hazard ratio of zero-point-three-three, a sixty-seven-percent reduction. Twenty-four-month progression-free survival hit eighty-one percent on the triplet versus fifty-one percent on the control. Overall survival also tracked — hazard ratio zero-point-four-seven on the triplet and zero-point-five-one on the doublet, with twenty-four-month overall survival landing at eighty-nine percent versus seventy-nine percent. Overall response rate was eighty-eight percent on both talquetamab arms versus seventy-eight percent on control. Complete response or better hit seventy-one percent on the triplet versus thirty-five percent on the control — more than double. And the M-R-D-negative complete response rate at the ten-to-the-minus-five threshold was fifty-two percent on the triplet versus sixteen percent on control. Across every depth-of-response metric the talquetamab arms outperform.

Safety is where the differentiation from BCMA-targeted therapy actually shows up, and it's the most translationally interesting part of the readout. Cytokine release syndrome ran sixty-eight percent on the triplet, mostly grade one to two, which is what you expect from any T-cell engager. The unique on-target toxicity pattern is what GPRC-five-D's expression on keratinized tissue produces — taste alteration in seventy-three percent of triplet patients versus four percent on control, weight loss in forty-six percent versus seven percent, plus skin and nail toxicity. These are quality-of-life-disrupting effects, not infection risk. The contrast with BCMA bispecifics is what matters here. Grade-three-or-higher infections on the talquetamab triplet were thirty-eight percent — actually lower than the forty-two percent on the control regimen and substantially lower than the rates you see with Tecvayli or Elrexfio. The BCMA bispecifics deplete normal plasma cells, including the ones making protective immunoglobulins, and the infection risk that comes with that has been the rate-limiting safety signal for the class. Talquetamab does not deplete normal plasma cells, so it sidesteps that risk and substitutes a different on-target-but-off-tumor signal that's easier to manage clinically. Treatment discontinuations for adverse events were ten-and-a-half percent on the triplet versus six-and-a-half percent on the control, so the safety overhead is real but tolerable.

The take. MonumenTAL-three lands GPRC-five-D as a credible second antigen in the myeloma plasma-cell franchise, and the off-the-shelf, no-lymphodepletion administration profile means it competes directly against the BCMA CAR-T products on access and convenience while delivering hazard ratios in the same zero-point-two-six-to-zero-point-three-three band that CARVYKTI has produced in its own second-line studies. For the bispecific T-cell engager platform broadly, this is the second major proof-of-concept after the Tecvayli phase three a year ago that the class is durable enough to support a multi-antigen franchise — not just a single hit-and-stop program. The competitive read on the in-vivo CAR-T programs chasing the myeloma indication is harder. The second-line slot that an in-vivo BCMA CAR-T was hoping to take is now a much harder commercial target — the bispecific is sitting in it with an off-the-shelf delivery footprint and infection-light safety profile. The next inflection point for talquetamab is the MonumenTAL-six readout that pushes it into the frontline setting on top of daratumumab and lenalidomide, and that's where the bispecific franchise either keeps moving earlier or hits the ceiling. The broader signal worth tracking: GPRC-five-D is the proof that the multi-antigen story works in myeloma. The third antigen — FcRH-five, or one of the others in development — is the next program to watch, because the sequencing logic only deepens from here.
