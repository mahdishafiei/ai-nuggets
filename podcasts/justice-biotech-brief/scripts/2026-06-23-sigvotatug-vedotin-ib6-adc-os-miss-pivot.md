# Sigvotatug vedotin — integrin-beta-6 ADC misses Phase 3 overall survival in non-squamous NSCLC, pivots to first-line pembro and PD-1×VEGF combos

Paper link: https://www.pfizer.com/news/press-release/press-release-detail/pfizer-announces-topline-phase-3-results-sigvotatug-vedotin

## Script

Good morning, Justice. One pick today, the readout you most need to read carefully — Pfizer announced topline Phase three results yesterday for sigvotatug vedotin in previously treated non-squamous N-S-C-L-C, and the headline is that the drug missed its primary endpoint of overall survival. But the more interesting story is what they're going to do with it, because the salvage plan lands directly on two of your bench's interests at once.

Set up what this drug is, because the target is the part that matters. Sigvotatug vedotin is a first-in-class antibody-drug conjugate against integrin beta six — I-T-G-B six, often written I-B six — with the vedotin payload, M-M-A-E, the same tubulin disruptor used in brentuximab and enfortumab and the Seagen ADC platform that Pfizer inherited. Integrin beta six is an adhesion integrin, overexpressed in non-squamous N-S-C-L-C, head and neck, gastric, colorectal, cervical. The first-in-human read-out from earlier in the year showed a confirmed response rate of nineteen percent as monotherapy in N-S-C-L-C, which is real but not spectacular — about where Trop-two ADCs sat at the equivalent stage. The Phase three thesis was, take it head to head against docetaxel in the second-line-and-beyond setting where docetaxel is the standard you have to beat to register an N-S-C-L-C ADC.

The trial is SigVie zero-zero-two, seven hundred and three patients, open label, randomized against docetaxel, non-squamous histology, mixed lines of prior therapy. The headline is the overall-survival miss in the full population. Pfizer didn't disclose hazard ratios or median values yet — that's for a future congress. But here's where it gets interesting. Two thirds of the trial were patients who'd had only one prior line — true second line. In that subgroup Pfizer says they saw a, quote, stronger trend on overall survival and progression-free survival, and outside commentary on the readout is calling it clinically meaningful in the second-line slice. They didn't release the second-line hazard ratio either but the phrasing is pointed.

The mechanistic intuition for why second line and third-line-plus split is worth saying out loud, because it's the same lesson the Trop-two ADCs have been learning. M-M-A-E is a tubulin disruptor. Docetaxel is a tubulin stabilizer. They hit the same machinery, opposite direction — and once a patient has had a taxane, the residual tumor population is enriched for cells that survive tubulin perturbation in general. By the third or fourth line, you're stacking an M-M-A-E ADC on top of patients who've already failed a taxane plus everything else, and the payload mechanism overlap drags the curve. The second-line patients in this trial — pure post-checkpoint-blockade, taxane-naive — are the population where the payload has a real biological window. That's the population the next trial should be powered around, not the broader pretreated mix the registration design captured.

Here's the part that matters for your bench. Pfizer's stated next moves for this asset are exactly the combinations you care about. One — a Phase three with pembrolizumab in treatment-naive N-S-C-L-C with P-D-L-one tumor proportion score fifty percent or higher. The rationale is that vedotin payloads induce immunogenic cell death — release of tumor antigens and damage signals from M-M-A-E-killed cells — so the ADC primes the tumor for checkpoint blockade. There's preclinical and Phase one combination data already supporting this. Two — and this is the one I want you to clock — a Phase one slash two combining sigvotatug vedotin with P-F zero-eight-six-three-four-four-zero-four, which is Pfizer's PD-one cross VEGF bispecific, the one they acquired from 3-S-Bio last year for up to six billion. In earlier-stage lung cancers and across I-B six expressing tumors. That's an ADC plus a PD-one VEGF bispecific in a controlled-trial setting, which is the exact intersection of two pillars on your interest list — and the rationale is right. Immunogenic cell death from the ADC, checkpoint release plus anti-angiogenic vascular normalization from the bispecific, the kind of stack the Akeso ivonescimab plus chemotherapy story has been making the case for, except here the chemotherapy slot is filled by a targeted payload.

A few caveats worth holding the take honest. One — we don't have the actual numbers. A stronger trend in two thirds of the trial sometimes means a hazard ratio of zero point seven five with a wide confidence interval that barely clears the line, and sometimes it means zero point six and a clean separation. Until the congress, you can't tell which. Two — registering an ADC on a subgroup defined by line of therapy is a thing the F-D-A increasingly does not love, especially after the recent enfortumab and TROP-two line-of-therapy refinements; the path forward is probably a fresh Phase three with prospective second-line stratification, not a subgroup-driven label expansion off this trial. Three — the combination programs are still early — Phase one slash two — and the asset's monotherapy bar is now visibly compromised, so the combinations have to do real lifting to justify the molecule, not just confirm tolerability. Four — integrin beta six expression varies a lot across histology and across patients; the trial was unselected by I-B six level, and a biomarker-selected re-cut is something Pfizer should be running on the existing tissue right now if they aren't already.

Net take. Headline reads as a Phase three failure, and on a strict registration basis it is. But the operational story is a payload-mechanism trap that recommends moving up a line, not killing the program — and the combination plans are the exact ADC-plus-checkpoint and ADC-plus-PD-one-cross-VEGF combinations that sit in the middle of your translational interests. The thing to watch is whether the second-line monotherapy curve survives the congress presentation, and whether the early combination data with the PD-one VEGF bispecific shows the ADC and the bispecific are actually synergistic versus additive. If both hold, this becomes a story about an ADC that got out of the wrong patient population and into the right one. If neither holds, it's a Seagen-era asset that the field will redirect attention from. Paper link's in the show notes.

That's the brief. Back tomorrow.
