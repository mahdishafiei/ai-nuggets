# Justice's Biotech Brief — 2026-06-21

Paper link: https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.64898/2026.06.18.733241v1

## Script

Good morning, Justice. One pick today, from bioRxiv overnight — a glioblastoma immunotherapy preprint out of the N-C-I Center for Cancer Research and the Ludwig Institute at Oxford, with Masaki Terabe corresponding and Benoit Van den Eynde and Carol Leung on the patent. Two reasons it's worth your five minutes. One, it reframes why checkpoint blockade has consistently failed in glioblastoma. Two, the vehicle is a clinical-grade vaccine platform you already know from the Oxford-AstraZeneca COVID shot — chad-ox-one, the simian adenovirus, prime, modified vaccinia Ankara, M-V-A, boost. Quick heads-up — the preprint posted within the last twenty-four hours, so bioRxiv hasn't rendered the full body yet and this take is built off the abstract. I'll be explicit where that limits the read.

Here's the reframe. The field has spent a decade trying to rescue checkpoint blockade in glioblastoma — anti-P-D-one monotherapy, anti-P-D-one plus anti-C-T-L-A-four, neoadjuvant designs, intratumoral delivery — and the dominant story has been that the brain tumor microenvironment is too immune-suppressed for effector T cells to function. This paper argues the bottleneck is upstream of that. It is not that effectors fail at the tumor. It is that you never get enough tumor-specific C-D-8 T cells primed in the first place, so there is nothing for checkpoint blockade to unleash. That hypothesis is testable, and the test is the rest of the paper.

The test is a strong heterologous prime-boost. Chad-ox-one delivers the antigen first — same chimp adenovirus backbone as the Oxford-AZ COVID vaccine, large insert capacity, single-dose immunogenic. Then M-V-A delivers the same antigen in a poxvirus context — different innate-immune signature, different neutralizing-antibody pool, so you don't waste the boost on anti-vector responses. Heterologous prime-boost is the canonical way to drive C-D-8 T cell magnitude in the textbooks; the question this paper asks is whether the magnitude is enough to do work against a brain tumor that has actively defeated checkpoint blockade. They picked the S-B 28 orthotopic murine glioblastoma model — explicitly because it is checkpoint-refractory in mouse hands, the way human G-B-M is refractory in patients. So this is the right adversary, not a softball.

The results, going off the abstract. The chad-ox-one slash M-V-A vaccine drives therapeutic efficacy against orthotopic S-B 28. It works against the known mouse tumor antigen P1A and also against a newly identified glioblastoma-associated antigen called G-P-R 149 — and the G-P-R 149 finding is the part of the story that I'd most want to see the methods for, because a new T cell antigen in glioblastoma is exactly the kind of thing that, if it holds up in human tissue, becomes a target nomination for a C-A-R-T or a T cell engager. Two, when they look at what's in the brain after vaccination, the antigen-specific C-D-8 T cells are dominantly C-D one-oh-three positive, C-D-69 positive — tissue-resident memory phenotype. They are polyfunctional. They are durable in the brains of mice that maintain tumor control. And the kicker — adoptive transfer of those brain T-R-M cells into naive recipients is sufficient to protect against subsequent tumor challenge. That's a sufficiency experiment, not a correlation. The T-R-M-like population is doing the work.

Now the part that should actually catch your eye as a translational immunologist. Adding checkpoint blockade on top of the vaccine provided no additional benefit. Read that carefully. If effectors at the tumor were the limiting step, anti-P-D-one on top of a strong priming vaccine should still extract more activity. It didn't. The simplest interpretation, and the one consistent with the rest of the paper, is that when you actually do prime an effective C-D-8 response peripherally and deliver it into the brain as T-R-M, the checkpoint axis is no longer the rate-limiter. That doesn't mean checkpoint blockade is useless in G-B-M forever — it does mean the order-of-operations matters, and the field's habit of layering I-O modalities onto checkpoint blockade may have the priority backwards in this disease.

A few caveats worth saying out loud. One, this is mouse. S-B 28 is a hard model and the right one to use, but it's still mouse. Two, this is abstract-only — I do not have the methods, the dosing intervals, the T cell magnitudes, the survival curves, the question of whether vaccination is therapeutic when started after tumor implantation versus prophylactic, or how durable the survival benefit is past the experimental endpoint. The clinical translation case rests on those numbers and I can't read them yet; the body should be up in a couple of days. Three, the M-V-A in this regimen is patent-encumbered — Van den Eynde and Leung are listed inventors on the viral-vector cancer vaccine patent — so whoever commercializes a clinical chad-ox-one slash M-V-A G-B-M program will be partnered or licensed in, not building from scratch. Four, the G-P-R 149 antigen — I have nothing on whether the human ortholog is expressed in human glioblastoma tissue. That's the first thing to check; until it is, treat G-P-R 149 as a mouse-model antigen, not a target nomination.

Translational read for your group. Chad-ox-one slash M-V-A is not hypothetical infrastructure — it's the same vector pair behind clinical cancer vaccine programs in H-B-V-driven hepatocellular carcinoma and prostate cancer, and the platform is manufacturable at scale. If the priming-is-the-bottleneck thesis travels from this paper into the clinic, the implication is that for cold, checkpoint-refractory tumors, you may want to think of viral-vector vaccination as the upstream half of an immunotherapy program — drive the antigen-specific repertoire peripherally, let T-R-M biology park those cells in the tissue — and reserve the cell engagers, A-D-Cs, and checkpoint inhibitors for the tumors that are actually antigen-rich and effector-limited. That's a different sequencing logic than the layered combinations the field defaults to right now, and it's the part of this paper I'd want to hear how Calibr's translational team would design around.

Net take. Mouse data, abstract-only, but the hypothesis is sharp, the model is the right adversary, the adoptive-transfer sufficiency proof is in, and the platform is clinical-grade. Worth tracking when the body lands. Paper link is in the show notes.
