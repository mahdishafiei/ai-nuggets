# B7-H3 × 4-1BB bispecific — Southampton architecture study sets the design rules for tumor-localized costim bispecifics: receptor-side bivalency is the dominant determinant, 4-1BB beats the rest of the TNFRSF, and the principles are conserved in human T cells

Paper link: https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.64898/2026.06.19.732642v1

## Script

Good morning, Justice. One pick today, and it lands directly on the architectural question you've been hearing about for the last week — when a costimulatory arm gets added to a bispecific T-cell engager, what format actually works, and which T-N-F-receptor-superfamily member should you point it at. The paper is out of Aymen Al-Shamkhani's group at the University of Southampton, with Mark Cragg and Stephen Beers' antibody-engineering operation alongside him, and it went up on bioRxiv yesterday.

The setup is the question every next-gen T-cell-engager program is now asking. First-generation T-cell engagers — blinatumomab and its descendants — pair a C-D-3-binding arm with a tumor-antigen-binding arm, the cell gets activated through signal one only, and a couple of years into that regimen you see the consequences. The cells exhaust, the activity in solid tumors stalls, and the on-target off-tumor toxicity is hard to dose around. The field's consensus repair has been to add a second co-stimulatory engagement — signal two — through a T-cell costimulatory receptor that's normally engaged by professional antigen-presenting cells. That receptor sits in the T-N-F-receptor superfamily — 4-1-B-B, O-X-40, C-D-27, G-I-T-R, C-D-40 are the canonical candidates. Pair that costim arm with a tumor antigen binder and you get tumor-localized co-stimulation — the signal-two engagement only happens where the tumor is, the systemic toxicity that ruined T-G-N-1-4-1-2 stays bounded, and the T cells get the cytokine support that lets them persist and differentiate properly in solid stroma. That is the architecture you heard underwriting the AbCellera-Jazz deal Justice's brief covered six days ago — Jazz paid fifty-six million dollars upfront for AbCellera's costim trispecific platform, and the contractual asset wasn't a molecule, it was the design space.

This paper is the rigorous architectural answer to two of the three open questions in that design space. Question one — what valency on which arm matters most. Question two — which T-N-F-receptor-superfamily member is the best signal-two arm to pick.

How they go after it. They build a panel of bispecific antibodies on a uniform scaffold using B7-H3 — the cancer-associated antigen Justice's group cares about for solid-tumor delivery — as the tumor-antigen anchor. Across the panel they vary two parameters orthogonally. First, format. They build a dual-bivalent 2-by-2 — two arms binding B7-H3, two arms binding the costim receptor. They build a fully monovalent 1-by-1 — one arm each. And they build a 2-by-1 — bivalent on the costim receptor side, monovalent on the tumor-antigen side. Second, the T-N-F-receptor-superfamily target — they swap which costim receptor the costim arms bind across multiple members of the family, and they hold everything else constant.

The findings. First the format result. The dual-bivalent 2-by-2 is maximally active. The fully monovalent 1-by-1 is the least active. And the asymmetric 2-by-1 — bivalent on the costim receptor, monovalent on the tumor antigen — retains substantial activity. That asymmetric data point is what makes this paper land. It says the bivalency that matters is on the T-cell side, not on the tumor side. The reason it matters mechanistically is what every T-N-F-receptor agonist program has been chasing — these receptors signal by trimerization or higher-order clustering, monovalent engagement just doesn't deliver the receptor crosslinking the downstream pathway needs. Putting two arms on the costim side gives you the crosslinking even when the tumor-side engagement is monovalent. The implication for a costim trispecific T-cell engager — the kind AbCellera is selling — is that the third arm has to be at least bivalent on the costim receptor, even if the C-D-3 and tumor-antigen arms each stay monovalent. That's a constraint on protein engineering that wasn't obvious before this kind of head-to-head was done.

The second finding. Across the T-N-F-receptor-superfamily targets they tested, 4-1-B-B drives the strongest cytotoxic CD8 T-cell differentiation and the strongest anti-tumor response. That's the rank order, not a marginal difference. The control they ran is the one that should matter to you — the hierarchy holds in human T cells, and it holds when they substitute the cognate natural ligand for the agonist antibody. That means the 4-1-B-B-on-top result isn't an artifact of antibody affinity or epitope geometry. It reflects something receptor-intrinsic — 4-1-B-B's signaling output, downstream of equivalent engagement, just delivers more cytotoxic differentiation than O-X-40, C-D-27, G-I-T-R, or C-D-40 do. That's a clean piece of biology that resolves a debate the field has been having for at least five years about whether the choice of costim arm matters much in practice.

The mechanistic controls. Activity requires the tumor antigen — bispecifics targeting B7-H3-negative cells don't drive the response, so this is genuinely tumor-localized rather than systemic 4-1-B-B agonism dressed in a tumor-antigen costume. And the 4-1-B-B signaling has to be T-cell-intrinsic — they show that with cell-specific depletion or signaling knockouts. So the architecture is doing what it's supposed to do — bringing signal two onto the T cell only where the tumor antigen is, through a 4-1-B-B engagement that's strong enough to drive the cytotoxic program.

Net take, with the right calibration. This is the architecture-rules paper the costim T-cell-engager space has been waiting for. Three points that should land for your translational work. One — receptor-side bivalency is the load-bearing design constraint, not tumor-side bivalency, which is the opposite of what you'd default to if you were optimizing tumor specificity. You can keep the tumor-antigen arm monovalent and recover a clean tumor-versus-normal-tissue window, as long as the costim arm is bivalent enough to crosslink the T-N-F-receptor on the T cell. Two — 4-1-B-B versus the other T-N-F-receptor-superfamily members is no longer a coin flip. There is a real hierarchy and 4-1-B-B sits on top, conserved between mouse and human and between antibody and natural ligand. If you're designing a trispecific T-cell engager with a costim arm and you're picking the receptor, this is the experiment that says 4-1-B-B first, others second. Three — B7-H3 as the tumor anchor is a deliberate choice in this paper, not a constraint of the biology, but it's worth flagging because B7-H3 is one of the more promiscuous tumor-associated antigens currently in clinical development across solid tumors, and the architecture this paper lays out is portable to other antigens at the same valency rules.

A few caveats. This is mouse-and-human-cell biology, not in-human clinical efficacy — the closest clinical comparators are the various 4-1-B-B-targeting tumor-localized bispecific programs that AstraZeneca, Pieris, F-star, and Inhibrx have moved into early-phase trials, and the read-across is the design-principles level rather than the activity level. The paper is roughly thirty-six hours old, so the body figures aren't yet rendered on the bioRxiv server and I'm working off the abstract here — the quantitative size of the 2-by-2-versus-1-by-1 differential, the absolute magnitude of the 4-1-B-B-versus-O-X-40 gap, and the in vivo survival curves are in the figures, and the take should be calibrated when the full body lands. Two of the senior authors have disclosed Talix Therapeutics and F-star consulting positions, which means there are commercial programs downstream of this work and the architectural rules being published are the ones the authors expect to deploy. And the receptor hierarchy was established without the inflammatory context that 4-1-B-B-targeting drugs run into when they reach the clinic — the systemic dosing that limited urelumab is downstream of the tumor-localization architecture, so this paper is the design-rule layer underneath the molecule-by-molecule clinical story.

For your interest list — bispecifics, trispecific T-cell engagers, A-D-C-costim combinations, autoimmune biologics — this is the principles paper that should sit at the front of the next protein-engineering review you do, and it's the one to point at when somebody at Calibr is debating receptor choice for a costim arm.
