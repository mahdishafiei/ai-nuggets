# mRNA-2808 — Moderna's mRNA-encoded trispecific T-cell engager posts first clinical signal in relapsed/refractory myeloma; sister TCE mRNA-2151 advances into ovarian and an in vivo CAR-T joins the autoimmune pipeline

Paper link: https://www.modernatx.com/science-day

## Script

Good morning, Justice. One pick today, and it lands squarely on your trispecific T-cell-engager interest from an angle the field hasn't really had to take seriously until now. Moderna held its Science Day yesterday, and the headline that came out of it is the first clinical signal — early, but real — for an mRNA-encoded multiplexed T-cell engager in relapsed and refractory multiple myeloma. The program is mRNA-2808, and the design of it is the part that should make you sit up.

The setup. Multiple myeloma has three antigens the field is now genuinely confident about — B-C-M-A, G-P-R-C-5-D, and F-c-R-H-5. B-C-M-A is the workhorse, with teclistamab and elranatamab as the approved bispecifics and the C-A-R T cells idecabtagene and ciltacabtagene driving the longer responses. G-P-R-C-5-D is the second-wave target — talquetamab is approved, and the data Justice's brief covered ten days ago out of MonumenTAL-3 confirmed it pulls competitive depth-of-response numbers head-to-head against B-C-M-A bispecifics. F-c-R-H-5 is the third — cevostamab from Genentech is the most advanced asset there, with phase one and phase two activity that has been clean enough that the antigen now belongs in the validated bucket. What every clinician treating myeloma will tell you is that the resistance pattern is the problem. Patients relapse off B-C-M-A by losing B-C-M-A expression or by editing the epitope. They relapse off G-P-R-C-5-D the same way. The standard sequential approach — B-C-M-A first, then G-P-R-C-5-D, then F-c-R-H-5 — is durable for some patients and quickly exhausted for others, and there is no clean answer on whether you'd be better off hitting all three simultaneously, because protein engineering hasn't given the field a credible trispecific T-cell engager that hits all three with comparable potency. The trispecific antibodies that have made it into the clinic have all been compromise molecules — engineering for three binding domains plus a C-D-3 arm in a single chain has cost affinity, manufacturability, or both.

What mRNA-2808 does is route around that protein-engineering ceiling. It is not a single trispecific antibody. It is a lipid-nanoparticle drug that delivers a multiplexed cocktail of messenger R-N-A — three separate transcripts, each encoding a different bispecific T-cell engager. One encodes a B-C-M-A-by-C-D-3 engager. One encodes a G-P-R-C-5-D-by-C-D-3 engager. One encodes an F-c-R-H-5-by-C-D-3 engager. The patient's own cells — primarily hepatocytes after I-V infusion — translate the messages and secrete the three bispecific antibodies into circulation in vivo. The result is that you co-administer three engagers simultaneously, each one a clean head-to-head bispecific against its target, without ever needing to engineer a single molecule that has to fold around three tumor-binding domains.

The intuition here is what should land for your work. The mRNA encoding is solving two problems the protein TCE field has lived with. First, you stop fighting the molecule. Each encoded engager is just a bispecific, with the affinity and the geometry the medicinal chemists already know how to tune. The combinatorial complexity moves out of the protein and into the formulation — you titrate the relative ratio of the three mRNAs, you don't titrate epitope-paratope tradeoffs in a four-way single-chain construct. Second, the in vivo translation gives you a pharmacology window the field has been chasing — you don't have to dose protein engagers continuously to keep coverage, because the encoded program drives ongoing secretion for as long as the transcripts persist, which Moderna's preclinical work has been advertising as roughly a week per dose in non-human primate models. That's not bispecific-half-life biology; it's mRNA-vaccine pharmacokinetics applied to a cytotoxic effector. And that's what makes the trispecific architecture practical — you're not stacking three protein infusions with three half-lives and three immunogenicity profiles. You're delivering one L-N-P that produces three engagers from inside the patient.

Now the data Moderna actually disclosed. The phase one and phase two study started enrolling in September of last year. It's an I-V dose-escalation in patients with relapsed and refractory myeloma, and it explicitly allows prior B-C-M-A, G-P-R-C-5-D, or F-c-R-H-5 exposure — so the trial is set up to read out exactly the patients who failed sequential bispecifics. What Moderna said yesterday at Science Day is that they have an encouraging early clinical signal — that's the language they used — and it's strong enough that they are accelerating the sister program, mRNA-2151, into the clinic. mRNA-2151 is the same modality applied to ovarian cancer. They did not publish patient counts, dose levels, or response rates. There is no O-R-R, no C-R, no minimum-residual-disease number disclosed. There is no cytokine-release-syndrome profile, which is the obvious concern for a modality that is producing three C-D-3 bispecifics simultaneously from a single infusion — the C-R-S profile of mRNA-encoded engagers is the question the field needs answered, because if the in vivo secretion kinetics produce a more gradual onset than a step-function protein infusion, that could meaningfully change the dose-limiting toxicity story. That data is what needs to come out at the next medical meeting, and the read-across from the trial design — three validated targets, prior-bispecific-allowed population — is what makes the eventual numbers diagnostic for the modality, not just for the molecule.

Two other items from Science Day worth flagging, both adjacent to your interests. First, mRNA-2151 — the ovarian engager — is described as a multiplexed T-cell engager in the same architecture as 2808, meaning the field's first non-hematologic readout of mRNA-encoded multispecific T-cell engagement is coming on the back of the myeloma signal. Solid-tumor T-cell-engager programs have a much harder time clinically than hematologic ones — the tumor-microenvironment problems, the on-target off-tumor toxicity windows, all the things that make Calibr's solid-tumor work hard. If 2151 reads out, it'll be the first credible test of whether the mRNA-encoded modality changes those numbers, and the targets aren't yet public. Second, the program that should sit on your autoimmune side of the house is mRNA-6007 — Moderna's first in vivo C-A-R T-cell program, also disclosed yesterday. It uses the same architecture — a targeted L-N-P delivering multiplexed mRNA — but the mRNA here encodes two C-A-Rs and the L-N-P is targeted to T cells, not hepatocytes. The intended use is deep B-cell depletion for lupus and other B-cell-mediated autoimmune diseases, putting it in direct competition with Capstan's C-T-X-1-1-3-1, the in vivo C-A-R T programs that AstraZeneca acquired from EsoBiotec, and Eli Lilly's Kelonia acquisition. Moderna placed it in their horizon-three bucket — first-in-human by end of 2027 — so it's not data-ready, but the architectural choice of multiplexing the C-A-Rs is the one that should ring a bell for you. Single-target C-A-R-T autoimmune programs have the same antigen-loss escape problem that myeloma is teaching the field about, and Moderna's bet is the same in both arms of the platform — encode more than one effector, deliver them simultaneously, route around the resistance pattern at the formulation layer.

Net take. The reason this is the pick today and not the headline news from B-I-O — the iBio obesity move, the Boehringer-Immunai deal, the BIO close-out — is that mRNA-2808 is the first clinical-grade evidence that mRNA-encoded multispecific T-cell engagers are a real modality, not a preclinical curiosity. If the eventual phase one data lands clean — and the C-R-S profile is the load-bearing question — then the trispecific T-cell-engager design space you've been watching shifts. The protein-engineering trispecifics, the AbCellera-Jazz costim trispecifics, the Janux conditionally activated formats — they're competing with each other and now with an mRNA-encoded option whose engineering constraints are entirely different. The cocktail-of-encoded-bispecifics architecture is what makes that an open competition rather than a parallel track. Worth keeping an eye on for the Rodgers Lab work and worth flagging for anyone at Calibr building solid-tumor T-cell engagers, because the same logic that worked for myeloma is what 2151 is going to test in ovarian.
