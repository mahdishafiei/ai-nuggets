## Script

Good morning, Matt — this is Sourcing Radar for Friday, June twenty-sixth. The lead today is a Lieber Institute paper out of Giulio Pergola's group — Oliver Howes and Mitul Mehta at King's College London on the imaging side — that I want Hemaka and Esther to log against the G-P-R fifty-two NewCo file before the weekend. They have built and replicated, across five cohorts and twelve hundred and fifty-one subjects, a functional M-R-I biomarker that stratifies who responds to D-two-blocking antipsychotics and who does not. The same paper hands you the rationale and the patient-selection tool for a non-dopaminergic mechanism. That is the conversation we have been waiting to have with Lieber on the program.

Let me walk the lead.

The framework comes out of rodent behavioral psychology. When you pair a cue with a reward, two stable phenotypes emerge in rats. Sign-trackers — call them S-T — fixate on the cue itself; they treat the cue as if it were the reward. Goal-trackers — G-T — orient to the reward delivery location and ignore the cue. The two phenotypes have very different dopamine biology. Sign-tracking is dopamine-dependent in a way goal-tracking is not. The framework has been around for years and has been a real frustration for translational psychiatry because the human analog has been hard to pin down — prior attempts used behavioral proxies that did not replicate cleanly.

The Pergola group does it with imaging instead of behavior. They take BOLD signal during the Monetary Incentive Delay task — a workhorse reward paradigm — across five independent cohorts, and they cluster on the anticipation-versus-outcome activation profile. The discovery cohort is eight hundred and ninety subjects from the IMAGEN consortium. The replication cohort is two hundred and forty-five subjects collected at Bari. Two pharmacological cohorts — thirty-four healthy males with single-dose challenges and forty-eight healthy adults with seven-day repeated-dose challenges — plus a clinical cohort of thirty-four first-episode and chronic psychosis patients at Bari. Twelve hundred and fifty-one subjects total. The two-cluster solution recovers cleanly in every single cohort, with replication accuracies ranging from seventy-seven to ninety-four percent. S-T individuals show heightened ventral striatal BOLD during reward anticipation. G-T individuals show the inverse — heightened BOLD during reward outcome. That is the phenotype. It is reproducible, it is data-driven, and it falls out without any task learning required.

Then the pharmacology, and this is where it becomes a Blackbird artifact rather than a neuroscience artifact.

Single-dose risperidone and haloperidol — full D-two and D-three antagonists — selectively suppress anticipatory ventral striatal BOLD in the S-T cluster. No effect in G-T. The effect is dose-dependent in the risperidone subsample with a clean linear gradient, with a significant Bayes-factor null in G-T. And in S-T only, the anticipatory BOLD reduction tracks a parallel drop in self-reported energy on the visual analog scale — the subjective sedation that drives a lot of antipsychotic non-adherence in the real world. Seven-day amisulpride, also a full D-two-three antagonist, replicates this in the repeated-dose cohort. The detail that should grab Avi's attention here is that the original group-level analysis of that amisulpride dataset — published by Osugo and colleagues — reported no significant effect on reward anticipation. The phenotype split rescues a real pharmacological effect that the conventional analysis missed. That is a textbook biomarker-enabled drug-effect-recovery story, and it is exactly the kind of thing pharma companion-diagnostic teams pay attention to.

Seven-day aripiprazole — the D-two partial agonist, mechanistically distinct — produces a qualitatively different pattern: it increases anticipatory BOLD in G-T and decreases outcome-phase BOLD in G-T, with S-T largely unchanged. The authors flag this as preliminary because it is a single cohort, but the cross-compound divergence — antagonists and partial agonists producing opposite-sign effects in the same placebo-defined clusters — kills the obvious regression-to-the-mean objection and tells you the phenotypes are tracking real drug mechanism, not statistical noise.

Then the clinical cohort, and this is the part that pulls directly through to G-P-R fifty-two. Thirty-four patients on standing antipsychotic therapy at Bari. The ST-GT split still recovers. S-T patients show blunted anticipatory ventral striatum compared to healthy S-T controls — consistent with on-treatment suppression. They construct a patient-specific D-two affinity index — basically a weighted sum of each patient's medication regimen by published D-two binding affinity — and find that it correlates negatively with anticipatory ventral striatum BOLD at r equals minus point four-six, and positively with negative-symptom burden on the PANSS negative scale at r equals plus point four-seven. Critically, when they run the same correlations against D-one, D-three, D-four, and D-five affinities, nothing — the signal is specifically D-two. And the G-T patients, who did not show the on-drug anticipatory blunting, have significantly higher negative symptoms than the S-T patients on the same medications.

Translate that into operator language. The patients who respond on the positive-symptom side to D-two blockade — the S-T phenotype — pay for that response with anticipatory reward circuit suppression that the patient experiences as anhedonia, low energy, and worsening negative symptoms. The G-T patients are not getting the same anticipatory suppression but are dragging higher negative-symptom burden anyway. In both groups, the more D-two-affine your drug regimen, the worse your negative symptoms. The paper is essentially the imaging-and-pharmacology defense of the clinical fact that the field has been wrestling with for thirty years — that D-two-driven control of positive symptoms is bought at the cost of negative-symptom worsening — and it gives you a single-scan biomarker to identify it patient by patient.

The Blackbird angle. Three reads.

First, the direct pull-through to the schizophrenia NewCo with Lieber and Third Rock. The G-P-R fifty-two agonist program is built on the thesis that you can address positive, negative, and cognitive symptoms together by going around D-two rather than through it. This paper is the cleanest published rationale to date for why that thesis is correct, and it comes out of the Lieber Institute. More usefully, it hands you a forward-looking trial design tool. A G-P-R fifty-two development candidate going into proof-of-concept in 2027 has a hard time differentiating on negative symptoms in a small early study against placebo — negative symptoms are noisy and slow. But a study that enriches on G-T patients identified by this imaging phenotype — patients with high baseline negative-symptom burden whose anticipatory reward circuitry is not driven by D-two — gives you a population where a non-dopaminergic mechanism should produce a clean signal and a comparator-class signal that is muted. That is a study power story and a trial-cost story, and it is one Esther should be running past Third Rock as a strategic frame on the clinical plan.

Second, the platform read. Pergola has now published two adjacent infrastructure assets out of Lieber inside a month — the trans-T-WAS engine I covered Thursday on the target-discovery side, and this fMRI patient-stratification tool on the development side. Sourcing one platform from a lab is a paper. Sourcing two is a program. The question for Hemaka in the next Tom Hyde conversation is whether the Lieber neuropsychiatry platform stack — postmortem transcriptomics for target ID, MRI-derived phenotyping for trial enrichment, the IPSCs and astrocyte work that has been coming out of Weinberger's lab — adds up to a licensable platform-layer asset that could underwrite multiple single-asset spinouts the way Blackbird is positioning Aletira's SELEXON underneath multiple gene-therapy programs. That is a different conversation than the one-program-at-a-time deal flow.

Third, the candor flag. This is a biomarker correlation paper, not a prospective trial. The S-T enrichment story for a G-P-R fifty-two POC is a hypothesis you would still have to validate in a feasibility cohort before you commit to it in a registrational design — and the clinical sample here is small (twenty-six concordant patients after exclusions) and Bari-only. None of that changes the lead recommendation; it sharpens it. Hemaka should ask Pergola directly whether the group is open to running a prospective biomarker-validation arm alongside a Lieber-Blackbird industry partner's clinical program. That is a paid collaboration with a downstream license, not a one-off paper conversation.

Second item. Steven Farber's lab at Johns Hopkins put out a version-four revision this week of the LipoGlo whole-animal phenotypic screen — the paper finally locked in its peer-review form on Tuesday. The headline is straightforward but the platform is the asset. They take a zebrafish reporter line that puts NanoLuciferase on Apolipoprotein-B — apo-B is the structural scaffold of every atherogenic lipoprotein in human blood — and dose three thousand compounds from the Johns Hopkins Drug Library, four doses each, eight replicates each, fifty-five thousand individual animal measurements through automated robotics, with M-T-P inhibitor lomitapide as positive control on every plate. They identify forty-nine unique B-lipoprotein-lowering hits at stringent statistical cutoffs. Nineteen validate orthogonally. They then take one of those hits — enoxolone, a licorice root component — and demonstrate in two clean genetics-and-pharmacology experiments that it lowers apo-B through inhibition of the nuclear receptor H-N-F-four alpha, an effect that is lost in H-N-F-four-alpha mutants and reproduced by independent H-N-F-four-alpha inhibitors.

Why this matters commercially. The atherogenic lipoprotein market is the largest therapeutic market in the world and is still dominated by mechanisms that target apo-B synthesis (lomitapide, mipomersen) or clearance (statins, P-C-S-K-nine therapeutics, bempedoic acid). Statins on top of dietary intervention leave roughly thirty percent residual cardiovascular mortality. The newer mechanisms are priced for severe dyslipidemia only. The field genuinely needs new mechanism. And vertebrate phenotypic screens at scale are rare — the discovery infrastructure is what is missing. The Farber lab has now demonstrated, with this paper, three things a pharma deal team cares about. One, the screening platform works at scale — three thousand compounds, fifty-five thousand measurements, with quantified hit-call statistics that recover the positive control with reasonable hit rate. Two, mechanism-of-action triage is possible directly in the vertebrate system using genetic-rescue logic, which compresses the medicinal-chemistry-to-target loop. Three, the hits include a previously unannotated nuclear-receptor mechanism — H-N-F-four alpha — that nobody is currently developing on, with a small-molecule starting point in hand.

The author list is the other half of the read. Steve Farber is the senior. Joseph Thierer recently spun out of Farber's lab into independent leadership on the apo-B program; Jeff Mumm built the ARQiv high-throughput zebrafish screening platform that the entire screen runs on; Jun O. Liu curates the Johns Hopkins Drug Library and has a long track record of high-throughput drug repositioning hits going into companies. That is a Hopkins translational stack with a built-in tech-transfer instinct.

The Blackbird angle for Avi and Hemaka. The natural lead is not the apo-B program itself — there are three or four credible companies already in the cardiometabolic apo-B space, and Hopkins will be testing the waters with strategic partners. The lead is the LipoGlo platform as a discovery service or platform spinout — vertebrate phenotypic screening for lipid biology generalizes to N-A-S-H, lipodystrophy, familial chylomicronemia, fatty liver, and the cardiometabolic-renal axis where SGLT-two and G-L-P-one franchises are now sitting on the largest budgets in industry. The candor caveat is that platform spinouts in screening are notoriously hard to value — every pharma has internal screening — but the differentiation here is the whole-animal vertebrate readout for a class of biology where cell-based screens systematically miss tissue-level lipid trafficking. Worth a J-H-T-V-routed conversation with the Farber group, with Avi probing whether Joseph Thierer is interested in operator leadership and whether the H-N-F-four-alpha small molecule has filed composition-of-matter coverage independent of the platform claims.

That is the radar this morning.
