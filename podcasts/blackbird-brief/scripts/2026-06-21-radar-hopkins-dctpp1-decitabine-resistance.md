# Sourcing Radar — Hopkins drug discovery cracks DCTPP1, unlocking decitabine in prostate cancer

## Sources

- Hauk, Liu, Nelson, Yegnasubramanian, Berger. "Structural and cellular insights into DCTPP1 antagonists and their synergistic action with DNMT inhibitors." PNAS, published June 15, 2026. DOI 10.1073/pnas.2534029123. PMID 42296362.
- Dhar et al. (corresponding: Pieper, Case Western; co-authors at Johns Hopkins and Lieber Institute including Bindu D. Paul). "Endothelial KLF4 depletion drives age-related neurovascular dysfunction and neuropsychiatric impairment." PNAS, published June 18, 2026. DOI 10.1073/pnas.2426990123. PMID 42313933.
- Background on DCTPP1 / triptolide precursor work: bioRxiv 10.1101/2024.05.19.594134 (May 2024) — same Hopkins group; the PNAS paper extends that work with chemically distinct scaffolds and X-ray crystal structures.

## Script

Good morning, Matt — this is Sourcing Radar for Sunday, June twenty-first. One lead pick this morning out of Hopkins oncology, plus a target-identification paper out of the Lieber side that's worth keeping on the watch list.

The lead is a new PNAS paper from Vasan Yegnasubramanian, Bill Nelson, and Jim Berger at the Sidney Kimmel Comprehensive Cancer Center, published Monday. The first author is Glenn Hauk in Berger's structural biology lab. They have done two things that, together, look licensable.

First, the target. The enzyme is D-C-T-P-P-one. It's a nucleotide pyrophosphatase whose day job is to hydrolyze methylated cytidine triphosphates and keep them out of the genome — basically a quality-control filter on the deoxycytidine triphosphate pool. The clinically interesting wrinkle is that the same active site also chews up the activated form of decitabine, the FDA-approved DNA methyltransferase inhibitor. That's the cell-intrinsic resistance mechanism that has kept decitabine and azacitidine boxed into M-D-S and leukemia for twenty years and shut them out of basically every solid tumor people have tried them in. Yegnasubramanian and Nelson had previously shown this — with triptolide, in a bioRxiv preprint back in 2024 — that if you inhibit D-C-T-P-P-one, decitabine actually gets incorporated, D-N-M-T-one gets degraded, you get genome-wide demethylation, and the cancer cells die. The triptolide piece told you the biology was real. It didn't give you a drug — triptolide is a natural-product warhead with selectivity and tox issues that make it a tool compound, not a clinical candidate.

What's new in this paper is that the team ran a high-throughput chemical screen against purified D-C-T-P-P-one, pulled out previously unreported inhibitor classes with submicromolar potency, solved X-ray crystal structures of the lead compounds bound in the nucleotide-binding pocket, and mapped the key interactions — a pair of tryptophans and two histidines that mimic the natural substrate. That's the medicinal-chemistry starting point. Then they confirmed cellularly that the new scaffolds synergize with decitabine in prostate cancer cells, and used loss- and gain-of-function studies to show the synergy is on-target for D-C-T-P-P-one. So you have a novel chemical class, atomic-resolution S-A-R, and a clear pharmacology hypothesis.

The Blackbird angle is, I think, pretty clean. One — novel target with no clinically validated competition. Two — defensible chemistry, because the crystal structures and the new scaffolds presumably anchor a fresh composition-of-matter family that Hopkins technology ventures can wrap I-P around. Three — the commercial framing writes itself. You aren't competing with the next-generation antibody-drug conjugate or the next CAR-T. You are running a small-molecule combination program that potentially turns a sixty-five-dollar-a-day generic — decitabine — into a real solid-tumor agent, starting with castrate-resistant prostate cancer and then asking which other tumors have high D-C-T-P-P-one. Four — the senior team is the right team. Nelson runs the Sidney Kimmel. Yegnasubramanian directs the next-gen sequencing center and the prostate cancer SPORE. Jim Berger is one of the better structural-biology PIs in the world for hit-to-lead enablement. You don't have to teach these people how to drive a program.

The honest read on what's still open. This is a cell-based result, not in-vivo yet in this paper, and not in patients. The activity is submicromolar, not picomolar — there is medchem optimization work to do. And triptolide-class molecules have given the field a lot of false starts, so the bar for the new chemotypes is that they genuinely do not behave like covalent natural-product hammers. But the structural-biology hook is exactly what de-risks that part. I'd want a meeting on this with the Yegnasubramanian and Berger groups. It looks like the kind of program where a translational grant to do in-vivo prostate cancer xenografts and develop a couple of analog series would set up a co-investment-ready data package inside twelve months.

The second item is a watch-list pick, not a license target — but it's on the Lieber side, so worth flagging. Andy Pieper's group at Case Western, with Bindu Paul at Hopkins and the Lieber Institute as a key collaborator, has a PNAS paper out Wednesday on endothelial K-L-F-four. The story is that K-L-F-four is a transcription factor in brain microvascular endothelial cells that constrains pro-inflammatory and cellular senescence programs at the chromatin level. K-L-F-four falls with age in endothelial cells. The team made an endothelial-specific knockout and showed it phenocopies advanced aging in middle-aged mice — blood-brain barrier leak, loss of neurovascular coupling, microvessel rarefaction, neuroinflammation, neurodegeneration, and on the behavioral side, anxiety and cognitive deficits.

For Blackbird, this is a target-identification paper. It does not come with a chemical lead, and the corresponding-author center is Case Western. The reason it's worth tracking is the framing — endothelial K-L-F-four as a tractable lever on neuropsychiatric phenotypes. That sits next to the Lieber thesis where we already have a small-molecule G-P-R fifty-two program, and it sits next to a broader story about non-neuronal targets in psychiatry that has not been mined commercially yet. The Bindu Paul lab at Hopkins has chemistry, the Lieber tissue resource is unique in the world, and if a small molecule or biologic that engages this pathway shows up out of any of those benches in the next year, we'll want to know about it early. So I'd ask the Lieber team whether they have, or are about to have, a chemical probe for K-L-F-four induction or for the downstream senescence program in endothelial cells.

That's the radar today. One actionable lead — the D-C-T-P-P-one program at the Sidney Kimmel — and one watch-list signal on the Lieber side. Have a good Sunday.
