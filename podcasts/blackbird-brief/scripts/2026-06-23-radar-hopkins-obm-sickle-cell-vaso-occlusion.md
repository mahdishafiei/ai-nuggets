## Script

Good morning, Matt — this is Sourcing Radar for Tuesday, June twenty-third. The lead today is a Hopkins B-M-E paper that turns a label-free imaging technique into the first in-patient view of sickle cell vaso-occlusion forming in real time, and the second item is a Nature Genetics paper out of the Lieber Institute that drops six hundred and forty-one new schizophrenia gene calls onto the table — relevant for the G-P-R fifty-two NewCo thesis.

The lead is out of Nick Durr's biomedical engineering lab at Johns Hopkins, with Lydia Pecker on the hematology side at the Hopkins School of Medicine. First author is Marisa Morakis; the paper is just out in Blood Advances. They used a technique called oblique back-illumination microscopy — O-B-M for short — to image sublingual capillaries in ten people with sickle cell disease, before and after transfusion, against ten unaffected controls. About ninety capillaries per participant. What they see, in vivo and label-free, is individual red blood cells adhering to the endothelium and then physically obstructing the vessel. Often those red cells are visibly sickled. They classify every vessel as fast-flow, slow-flow, or no-flow, and they count adhered red blood cells per vessel. Sickle cell patients had forty-nine percent fast-flow vessels versus seventy-eight percent in controls, sixteen percent no-flow versus two percent, and well over a hundredfold more adhered red blood cells per vessel — one-point-three-seven versus zero-point-zero-one. After a single transfusion, fast-flow vessels jumped from forty-nine to sixty-six percent, no-flow vessels dropped from sixteen to six percent, and adhered red cell counts fell by about half. All on tight p-values with a small cohort. This is the first time a method has put a quantitative number on vaso-occlusion in a human patient that responds, in days, to a known disease-modifying intervention.

The Blackbird angle is, I think, the most interesting sourcing lead we've seen out of Hopkins B-M-E in a while. Let me walk it.

One — the unmet need is concrete and the pharma buyers are real. Sickle cell disease is now a four to five billion dollar market on the back of crizanlizumab, voxelotor, and the two approved CRISPR and lentiviral gene therapies, exa-cel and lovo-cel. Every one of those programs and every Phase two and Phase three in the indication has the same problem — the primary endpoint is a patient-reported vaso-occlusive crisis rate, which is noisy, slow, and confounded by hospital access. The field has been begging for a measurable, mechanistic pharmacodynamic biomarker for fifteen years. This paper presents one — visible, quantifiable, and responsive at the days-to-weeks timescale that a Phase one or two trial can act on. That is exactly the asset that gene-therapy and small-molecule sickle players would pay to embed in their pivotal protocols and label-extension studies.

Two — the I-P is defensible in the right way. The molecule story isn't where the value is — this is a device, an instrument, and a deep-learning-based analytic stack for vessel classification and adhered-cell counting. Durr's group has been building the O-B-M platform for several years; this paper is the clinical proof. The composition-of-matter equivalent is the instrument geometry plus the algorithm. That's defensible, especially with a clinical use case under it. The closest analog as a business is something like the Cytalux or Spectral M-D playbook — sell the instrument plus the workflow, charge per scan in trial, then expand into routine clinical monitoring of severely affected patients.

Three — the commercial wedge writes itself. You don't have to displace anything. You partner with the sickle cell developers — Vertex and CRISPR Therapeutics on Casgevy, Bluebird on Lyfgenia, Pfizer on the post-acquisition Global Blood Therapeutics franchise, Novartis on crizanlizumab — and embed O-B-M as a pharmacodynamic readout in their next round of trials. Each one of those deals is a recurring-revenue contract. Then second wave you go after the centers of excellence as a clinical monitoring tool for high-utilizer sickle patients. The total addressable market is small relative to a therapeutic, but the path to first revenue is fast and the deal terms are favorable because every one of those pharma counterparties has a hole in their endpoint package.

Four — the team is the right team. Nick Durr trained as a B-M-E with Andy Yun at Wellman, runs a fluorescence-and-photonics translation lab at Hopkins, and has shipped clinical-grade imaging hardware before with companies like PhotoSound. Lydia Pecker is one of the leading younger sickle cell hematologists in the country and runs the Hopkins adult sickle program. Sophie Lanzkron, who used to run that program and is now at Jefferson, is on the paper. That is the operating team you'd want — a B-M-E lead with translation pedigree and a clinical co-investigator who can open every sickle-disease pharma door in the country.

Five — and this is the platform-extension point — O-B-M is fundamentally a label-free hemorheology imaging technology. The same instrument, with different analytic pipelines, should read out on any disease with microvascular adhesion or flow disturbance. Diabetic microvascular complications. Cerebral small-vessel disease. Microcirculation collapse in sepsis or critical care. CAR-T-associated cytokine release syndrome. So the first product is the sickle cell biomarker, but the platform claim is "in vivo, label-free, quantitative human microvascular imaging at single-cell resolution," and that opens out into a much bigger pipeline.

The honest read on what's still open. The cohort here is ten patients. The transfusion comparison is the strongest within-paper signal, but Phase three pharma deployment will require multi-site reproducibility and reader-agnostic analytics — both of which are tractable engineering questions, not science questions. There is a comparable Israeli group, Sight Diagnostics, working on label-free hematology, but they're a single-drop microfluidic readout, not an in vivo platform. Defensibility against follow-on photonics groups depends on how broad the Hopkins filings are around the optical geometry, the depth-resolution approach, and the analytic pipeline. And on the team side — Durr has not yet been the founding C-E-O of a venture-backed device company, which is the standard de-risking step we'd want before writing a co-investment check.

What I'd want to do is set up a call through Johns Hopkins Technology Ventures with Durr, Pecker, and the Doloff-Wilson-T-T-R-E-C neighbors to scope the program. The questions are — is the O-B-M platform already a filed I-P family with breadth beyond sickle cell, are there active sponsored-research conversations with any of the sickle cell pharma names, and what does the eighteen-month plan look like to a multi-site clinical biomarker package. If the I-P is broad and there's daylight against the gene-therapy developers' endpoint problem, this is the kind of program where Blackbird Laboratories should run a translational grant into the multi-site validation study, and Blackbird BioHub becomes the obvious incubation home for the company that comes out of it.

The second item is the Nature Genetics paper from the Lieber Institute that hit yesterday. Pergola is the corresponding author, with Tom Hyde, Joel Kleinman, and Dan Weinberger all senior on it. What they've done is take the Lieber BrainSeq R-N-A-sequencing resource — six brain regions, hundreds of donors — and build two new computational models, called INGENE and MODULE, that capture the distal regulatory effects of trans-acting variants within gene co-expression networks. The standard transcriptome-wide association study, T-WAS, captures only the local, cis effect of each variant on gene expression. Most of the regulatory signal in non-coding G-W-A-S hits is distal. INGENE and MODULE jointly model both. Layered onto the Psychiatric Genomics Consortium wave three schizophrenia G-W-A-S, the framework calls seven hundred and sixty-six schizophrenia-associated genes — six hundred and forty-one of them new to T-WAS analyses.

For Blackbird this is a platform-leverage story, not a chemical-matter pick. The compound interest here is that the Lieber post-mortem brain bank — the same asset we co-built the G-P-R fifty-two program around — is now being turned into a target-discovery engine that materially expands what's on the schizophrenia menu. Six hundred and forty-one new gene calls is too many to act on at once, but the right move is to ask Pergola, Weinberger, and Hyde for the rank-ordered shortlist of nominated genes that are druggable — small-molecule-tractable G-P-C-Rs, ion channels, kinases, deubiquitinases — and that don't overlap the genes the major neuropsych shops at Bristol Myers Squibb, AbbVie, and Boehringer are already running. That's the Blackbird question. Then the second question is whether the INGENE-MODULE method itself is a licensable asset — either as a software platform to the consortium-genomics community, or as the analytical engine inside a Lieber-anchored target-discovery vehicle. Worth an explicit conversation with the Lieber side about both threads.

That's the radar today. One actionable Hopkins B-M-E lead on label-free microvascular imaging in sickle cell, and one Lieber platform paper that adds six hundred and forty-one schizophrenia gene calls to the menu adjacent to the G-P-R fifty-two thesis. Have a good Tuesday.
