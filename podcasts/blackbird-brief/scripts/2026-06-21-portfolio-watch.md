## Script

Good morning again, Matt — this is Portfolio Watch for the week ending Sunday, June twenty-first. Five items this week, three of which actually move the thesis.

The biggest item for us is at home, not in the deal sheet. Hopkins, on Wednesday the tenth, announced its Life Sciences Research Initiative — eighty million dollars a year, for two years, of internal funding into translational and basic life-sciences research at the partner institution that supplies most of our deal flow. Application periods opened Monday the fifteenth. The structure has two tracks. The big one is Transformational Science Team Awards — up to forty million a year, awards starting at ten million per project, four years of support, multi-P-I, milestone-driven. The other forty million a year goes to High-Impact Individual Awards. Ashani Weeraratna, the Bloomberg Distinguished Professor in cancer biology, chairs the oversight committee. The school is being explicit that this is partly a response to the cuts in federal research funding — they're using a hundred-and-twenty-plus alumni gifts under the Research Saves Lives campaign to keep the science moving.

For Blackbird, this is straightforwardly a tailwind. Two reasons. One — the work this fund is structured to support is exactly the work that produces licensable I-P. Multi-P-I, milestone-driven, up to four years — that is the program length where preclinical packages mature to where Blackbird Laboratories can actually grant into them and where Blackbird BioVentures can write a seed check. Two — there is now a much shorter path inside Hopkins from "interesting biology" to "fundable program," because the principal investigators have a real internal grant to chase and a real oversight committee to please. That tightens the pipeline of investor-ready academic projects on a six-to-eighteen-month horizon. The action item for the team is to figure out, ideally with Esther and Hemaka, how to be looped in to that oversight committee's review docket — not to influence funding, but to see the longlist of submitted projects early, before they hit Hopkins Technology Ventures.

The second item is GSK acquiring Nuvalent for ten point six billion dollars in cash, announced Monday the ninth. Nuvalent is the precision-oncology shop with next-generation R-O-S-one and A-L-K kinase inhibitors for non-small-cell lung cancer — zidesamtinib with a September P-D-U-F-A date and neladalkib with a November P-D-U-F-A date. GSK is paying a forty percent premium on the thirty-day average. Two implications for us. The first is validation. Big pharma will pay ten billion dollars for a novel-target oncology kinase asset that is still pending F-D-A review. That's the comp set that matters when we're thinking about exit value on a Hopkins drug discovery program — the D-C-T-P-P-one work we covered on the Radar this morning, or any of the broader Sidney Kimmel platform. The second is competitive — and this one cuts both ways for aSKY. Nuvalent's late-stage drugs are dropping into the same non-small-cell lung cancer market where aSKY and Sasha Krupnick's lung-immunology work eventually lands. Targeted kinase therapy and N-K-cell immunotherapy are not direct competitors mechanism-for-mechanism, but they're competing for the same patient flow and the same K-O-L attention. Worth a conversation with the aSKY team about positioning narrative.

Third item — and this one matters for the schizophrenia NewCo with Lieber and Third Rock. On Monday the fifteenth, Neumora reported that navacaprant, their kappa-opioid antagonist, failed both K-O-A-S-T-A-L-two and K-O-A-S-T-A-L-three Phase three trials in major depressive disorder. Two consecutive late-stage flops. They are cutting roughly thirty-five percent of staff, killing the depression program, and refocusing on Alzheimer's agitation, schizophrenia, and obesity. The read here is twofold. One — another novel-mechanism neuropsych asset has died in Phase three, which thins the field of credible competitors in mood-and-psychosis adjacent indications, and pushes the center of gravity in the space back toward muscarinic and orphan-G-P-C-R mechanisms — exactly where our G-P-R fifty-two program lives. Two — Neumora itself is now a louder competitor in schizophrenia specifically. They have a portfolio asset in that indication, they have cash from the I-P-O, and they need a win. It's the kind of restructuring that produces aggressive partnering on whatever schizophrenia compound they have left. Net for us: the macro signal is helpful — the field is converging on non-dopaminergic mechanisms with all-three-domain ambition, which is the G-P-R fifty-two pitch. The micro signal is a reminder to track Neumora's schizophrenia pipeline disclosure at the next quarterly call.

Fourth, briefly. Tuesday the sixteenth, Lilly bought 4E Therapeutics out of Austin. 4E has oral M-N-K-eIF4E pathway inhibitors targeting peripherally restricted sensory neurons for chronic pain — non-opioid, no C-N-S exposure. Their lead, 4-E-T-eleven-oh-three, has finished Phase one with a clean safety profile. Terms undisclosed. This isn't directly on-thesis for us, but it continues the pattern Lilly has been running for a year now of aggressively buying small, mechanism-first early companies and continuing in genetic-medicine, in pain, and in hearing loss with the Akouos and Seamless work. It's the macro deal pattern that we and other early-stage incubators are positioned to feed.

Fifth, and quickly. Incyte announced Monday the eighth that it's acquiring Vega Therapeutics — a Star Therapeutics subsidiary — for one point two five billion up front, up to two billion total with milestones. They get V-G-A-zero-three-nine, a Phase three subcutaneous monoclonal for von Willebrand disease. Off our thesis directly, but worth flagging as another bleeding-disorder Phase three asset trading at over a billion. Hematology is a smaller market than oncology but it keeps pricing reasonably.

To pull all of that together — the most important thing this week for the firm is Hopkins putting eighty million dollars a year into a structured translational-and-basic fund that we should be plugged into. The GSK-Nuvalent deal is a useful comp for what novel-target oncology is worth right now. The Neumora wipeout is a small tailwind for the G-P-R fifty-two thesis and a reminder that the schizophrenia competitive set is reshuffling. Lilly and Incyte show big pharma is still spending, mid-cap to mega-cap, on early and Phase three assets — the M-and-A backstop is intact.

That's the watch. Have a good Sunday, Matt. Sourcing Radar will be back tomorrow.
