## Script

Good morning, Matt — this is Sourcing Radar for Wednesday, June twenty-fourth. Quiet window on bioRxiv for clean Hopkins-led sourcing, but one paper crossed the desk overnight that is worth pushing to the top of the queue, and a method tool I want on your team's radar. The lead is a Simonetti and Siliciano preprint out of Johns Hopkins infectious diseases on antigen-driven reactivation of latent H-I-V reservoirs — the kind of paper that reframes the H-I-V cure roadmap and lands squarely in a sourcing space Hopkins owns.

Let me walk the lead.

The premise is the question that has stalled H-I-V cure for the last decade. Antiretroviral therapy controls replicating virus, but it cannot touch the latent reservoir — the small population of resting C-D-four T cells carrying integrated, intact proviruses that wait silently for treatment to stop. Two decades of work has established that the surviving reservoir is not random. Intact proviruses progressively accumulate in transcriptionally repressive parts of the genome — pericentromeric heterochromatin, zinc finger genes, lamina-associated domains — because the immune system has gradually killed off the cells whose proviruses were expressed and visible. So-called deeper latency. The dominant interpretation has been that these proviruses are essentially inert and not the source of viral rebound when therapy stops — and therefore not the bottleneck on cure.

The Siliciano lab has been the institution on this question for thirty years. Bob Siliciano invented the quantitative viral outgrowth assay — the q-VOA — that the entire reservoir field uses to measure inducible virus. The standard q-VOA stimulates T cells with a mitogen, p-h-a, which is the maximally-activating signal. The new paper from Francesco Simonetti, Joel Blankson, and the Siliciano group adds the missing piece. They built an antigen-restricted version of the q-VOA — they call it the ag q-VOA — that swaps the brute-force mitogen for cognate antigen presentation through the natural T-cell receptor pathway. The thinking is that real-world reactivation in a patient does not happen through p-h-a; it happens because a C-D-four T cell carrying a provirus encounters its specific antigen — a fungal protein, a herpesvirus peptide, a self-antigen — and goes into normal activation mode.

They take two extensively-characterized study participants. The first is a long-term-treated patient with a dominant intact provirus integrated into a pericentromeric region of chromosome fourteen, in an expanded clone that responds to Candida albicans antigen — a common commensal yeast. The second is an elite controller who has suppressed H-I-V without therapy for over a decade, who has a dominant intact provirus integrated into a zinc finger gene that sits in an H-I-V-Gag-reactive T-cell clone. Both proviruses live in regions where the dominant model says they should be transcriptionally silent.

The results. When they hit the long-term-treated patient's cells with C-albicans lysate, exponential viral outgrowth — driven exclusively by the pericentromeric provirus. When they hit the elite controller's cells with the cognate Gag peptide, exponential outgrowth — and every single sequence recovered matches the zinc-finger provirus. For the elite controller, standard p-h-a stimulation produced no outgrowth at all. Cognate antigen gave a ten-fold higher inducibility than the maximally-activating mitogen. And critically, they show the pericentromeric provirus is detectable in residual plasma viremia in vivo — meaning these so-called deep-latency reservoirs are not just an ex vivo curiosity. They contribute to ongoing virus production in patients on full suppressive therapy.

The implication is significant. The H-I-V cure field has spent fifteen years and most of the public and venture money on shock-and-kill — pharmacological latency-reversing agents like H-D-A-C inhibitors and P-K-C agonists that try to wake the entire reservoir at once. Every clinical trial in that class has been a disappointment, partly because the L-R-As are blunt and partly because the post-reactivation killing step has been weak. This paper says — quietly — that the natural reactivation pathway is cognate antigen recognition, and that even the proviruses in the most repressive chromatin are accessible through that route. That reframes the cure strategy in two directions. One, therapeutic vaccination — induce strong antigen-specific C-D-four responses that flush the antigen-reactive reservoir — gets a clear mechanistic rationale, and so do T-cell-engagers that crosslink C-D-four reservoir cells to cytotoxic effectors. Two, the ag q-VOA itself is a much better diligence assay for H-I-V cure trials than the standard q-VOA, because it measures the population of provirus that actually behaves like the in-vivo reservoir.

The Blackbird angle. H-I-V cure is a high-stakes, well-funded therapeutic space — the Beat-H-I-V and I-four-C Martin Delaney collaboratories alone are several hundred million dollars over multiple cycles, and the strategic interest from Gilead, ViiV, and Merck on the post-lenacapavir question is real. Johns Hopkins owns the H-I-V latency franchise in a way no other academic center does. Siliciano's lab, Joel Blankson's group, Janet Siliciano's reservoir work — the network around this group is the obvious sourcing zone for cure programs. The ag q-VOA in particular is the kind of platform asset — a defensible assay with clear use in trial diligence — that licenses cleanly. The pull-through here is a J-H-T-V meeting with Francesco Simonetti, who is the corresponding author and the rising figure in this group, on whether there is an I-P filing around the ag q-VOA, what the next set of patients looks like, and whether the assay platform is independently spinnable from a therapeutic cure vehicle. The natural advisor seat is Bob Siliciano himself.

One more on the radar. Albert Lau's lab at Hopkins Biophysics posted a Python library yesterday called T-EntroPy that builds directional protein allostery networks from molecular dynamics simulations using transfer entropy. It's a method paper, not a sourcing lead — the library is open-source and there's no obvious I-P play. But the use case for Blackbird is direct. The G-P-R-fifty-two agonist program with the Lieber Institute is exactly the kind of allosteric G-protein-coupled receptor where understanding directional information flow between the orthosteric pocket and the allosteric pocket is the thing that distinguishes a developable molecule from a non-developable one. The library identifies which residues act as broadcasters and which act as receivers in the protein's intrinsic dynamics, and traces the communication routes between binding sites. Worth Avi knowing that Lau's group has a tool for this kind of analysis sitting one building over from the Blackbird BioHub — and worth pinging Avi if the molecular discovery team is at a point in the G-P-R-fifty-two SAR campaign where mapping the allosteric network of the lead chemotype would change the design loop.
