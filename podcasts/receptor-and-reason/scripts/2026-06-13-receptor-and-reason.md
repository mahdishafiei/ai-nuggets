## Script

Welcome to Receptor and Reason for Saturday, June 13th, 2026. Eleven items today, leaning heavily into receptor biology — there's a striking reassignment of an orphan glutamate-family receptor as a real acetylcholine sensor, a clinical PET study that strengthens the muscarinic story in schizophrenia, an AbbVie pipeline move on a next-generation psychedelic, plus drug discovery on the delta-opioid and tau fronts and circuit-level work on dyskinesia, alcohol, and stress. We close on an agentic knowledge-graph framework for drug repurposing.

We start with what is probably the most surprising receptor paper of the week. A team led by Shashank Dravid at Texas A&M, in Molecular Psychiatry, reports that the orphan delta-family glutamate receptor GluD1 is localized at cholinergic synapses in the striatum of both mice and primates, and behaves functionally as an acetylcholine receptor. GluD1, like its sister GluD2, has been the subject of years of frustration: it has the canonical ionotropic glutamate receptor fold, it organizes synapses through its extracellular domain, and yet glutamate barely gates it. What Dravid's group shows is that in striatal medium spiny neurons, with cholinergic, AMPA, and GABA-A receptors all pharmacologically blocked, applying acetylcholine still elicits a current, and that current is sensitive to the GluD1 channel blocker NASPM. GluD1 knockouts lose cholinergic terminals and lose the corresponding excitatory drive at those synapses. So GluD1 is doing two things: it's organizing the synapse architecturally and it's transducing acetylcholine. If this holds up in independent hands, it reshapes the cholinergic landscape — for years we've spoken about ACh in the striatum as nicotinic and muscarinic; this paper inserts a third receptor class that happens to be misfiled in the glutamate receptor family. The translational hooks for autism and addiction are obvious, since GluD1 sits in copy-number variant hotspots and the cholinergic interneuron is a converging node for those disorders.

That GluD1 finding pairs naturally with the second item, which is an in vivo PET study of muscarinic M1 in schizophrenia, in Biological Psychiatry. Sixteen patients and sixteen matched controls were scanned with the M1-selective ligand 11C-LSN3172176, the same tracer the Lilly-derived M1 PAM program built. Patients showed roughly a 13 to 19 percent drop in M1 receptor availability across cortex and striatum, and a substantial subgroup — close to half by one quantification — had whole-brain deficits larger than 20 percent. Two things about this matter. First, the spatial pattern is exactly where you'd want it for the xanomeline–trospium hypothesis, KarXT, to work: caudate, putamen, frontal and temporal cortex. Second, the heterogeneity is a feature, not noise — it raises the possibility that M1 PET could prospectively pick the patients most likely to benefit from a muscarinic agonist or PAM, the way striatal D2 occupancy stratified the early antipsychotic literature. The sample is small and the effect sizes are modest, but as a biomarker hook for muscarinic psychiatry it's the first replication-quality dataset I've seen.

Staying on the receptor-pipeline theme, AbbVie has started bridging studies for bretisilocin, also known as ABBV-2505, an intramuscular short-duration psychedelic that AJ Cannon discussed this week in a Psychiatric Times conversation. The trial registered as NCT07604558 is a Phase 1 open-label single-dose pharmacokinetic study in Japanese, Han Chinese, and non-Asian healthy adults, twenty-six participants, primary completion in September. Two things to note. First, this is exactly the kind of ethnic-bridging PK study you only run when you're committed to a global development plan — AbbVie isn't just dabbling. Second, the IM short-duration profile is a deliberate move away from the all-day-in-clinic ritual of psilocybin and LSD, and toward something more like a rescue-medication footprint. Whether that maps cleanly to durable antidepressant or anti-anxiety effect remains to be tested in the disease-state trials, but the bridging study tells you the design assumption: psychedelic effect, drug-like dosing.

That's a natural transition to the EEG microstate paper from the Czech group around Tomas Palenicek and Nikola Jajcay, in bioRxiv. Fifteen healthy volunteers, double-blind placebo-controlled crossover with psilocybin, EEG at five time points across the dose. Microstates are the resting-state EEG idea that the scalp topography of large-scale activity collapses into a handful of canonical maps that the brain visits on the order of tens of milliseconds. Under psilocybin at peak intoxication, the number of global field power peaks went up, microstate lifespan dropped, and the frequency of state transitions rose — basically, the brain is hopping between its canonical states faster, but the repertoire is preserved. That last point is the interesting one: this is not the brain entering some exotic microstate library, it's the same maps cycled more rapidly. And crucially, the degree of microstate acceleration at peak intoxication correlated with both acute subjective intensity and self-reported psychological changes twenty-eight days out. The sample is small and the persistence measure is self-report rather than a clinician-rated scale, but as a candidate biomarker for psychedelic-induced state change, this is exactly the kind of EEG signature you want — cheap, scalable, and tied to durable outcome.

A counter-intuitive serotonin item next, from Neuropharmacology. Thalamic 5-HT2A receptor activation induces absence seizures in rats. The Wistar group used bilateral microinjection of the selective 5-HT2A agonist TCB-2 into the ventrobasal thalamus, recorded tonic GABA-A currents in thalamocortical neurons by patch clamp, and observed spike-and-wave discharges with behavioral arrest — confirmed as absence-type by ethosuximide reversal. The mechanism is unusual: 5-HT2A activation suppresses GABA reuptake, which raises extracellular GABA at extrasynaptic sites, which drives tonic GABA-A current up, which drives the thalamocortical loop into absence rhythmicity. So a Gq-coupled excitatory serotonin receptor produces a net hyperinhibitory effect on T-type-gated thalamic relay. The clinical implication that should make psychiatrists pause: 5-HT2A is the dominant psychedelic target, and there are scattered case reports of seizure induction with high-dose psilocybin and LSD. This paper at minimum gives a thalamic mechanism for why a small subset of patients could be vulnerable, particularly anyone with a generalized epilepsy diathesis. It also tells you the brain region matters — cortical 5-HT2A signaling looks very different from thalamic.

Number six is the most advanced new mechanism item of the day: PN6047, a novel delta-opioid receptor agonist, from Amynah Pradhan's group at Washington University in St. Louis, on bioRxiv. PN6047 is a piperidylidene benzamide that has already completed a Phase 1 safety and tolerability study. In this preclinical package, a single injection blocked chronic cephalic allodynia in the nitroglycerin model of chronic migraine and in a post-traumatic headache model. Chronic dosing did not produce medication-overuse headache itself — and, this is the key result for the field, chronic PN6047 actually prevented the development of medication-overuse headache induced by sumatriptan. It also reduced KCl-evoked cortical spreading depression events and delayed CSD onset in a freely-behaving channelrhodopsin model. So you have a compound that hits the delta-opioid receptor selectively, blocks the major migraine model endpoints, doesn't induce tolerance, and actively protects against triptan-induced MOH. The DOR migraine hypothesis has been around since the early 2010s — this is the closest a selective DOR agonist has come to a clean preclinical package paired with a clean Phase 1. CGRP biologics still dominate migraine prevention, but a small-molecule DOR agonist would slot into a very different prescribing niche, especially for patients overusing triptans.

Item seven keeps us in neurodegeneration drug discovery. Paul Seidler's group at USC reports a coumarin-based small-molecule series, lead compound PT-13, that disaggregates pathological tau through what they describe as a stacking-driven co-assembly mechanism — in plain language, the small molecule wedges into the fibril and breaks it apart. The result that will draw the most scrutiny is that disaggregation does not generate soluble oligomeric intermediates. That's been the central worry with tau disaggregators since the field began: if you pull apart fibrils, do you transiently produce more toxic oligomers? Seidler's data say no, at least with their structure-activity series. PT-13 is brain-penetrant, well-tolerated, and in a tauopathy model reduces both fibrillar and oligomeric tau, preserves proteasome capacity, preserves synaptic integrity, and preserves behavior. This is the early-stage preclinical version of what eventually needs to become a Phase 2 in early Alzheimer's or a frontotemporal dementia trial, but as a chemical biology demonstration that tau disaggregation can be a viable strategy without the oligomer hazard, it's a notable proof-of-concept.

Eighth, a circuit-level Parkinson's paper from Mariangela Scarduzio and David Standaert at UAB. They used fiber photometry with the GRAB-ACh sensor in the unilateral 6-OHDA mouse to track striatal acetylcholine dynamics across dopamine loss and L-DOPA treatment. The headline is that the temporal structure of ACh, not its mean level, is the variable that breaks. Under physiological conditions, cholinergic interneurons hold a slow delta-band rhythm, and you can see that rhythm in extracellular ACh. Dopamine depletion disrupts that delta-band rhythmicity and replaces it with irregular higher-frequency phasic bursts. Acute L-DOPA broadly suppresses ACh — across frequencies — and partially restores the imbalance, but it does not restore the slow rhythm. Chronic L-DOPA dosing, the regime that produces dyskinesia, further degrades delta-band coordination during the ON state. And the anti-dyskinetic drug amantadine restores low-frequency temporal structure. The intuition this drives home is that for striatal motor output, dopamine and acetylcholine aren't just opposing levers — they're temporally coordinated, and dyskinesia is what you get when that temporal coordination fails. The framing has potential implications for thinking about M4 PAMs and other cholinergic-targeting strategies for LID: aim at restoring the rhythm, not just at lowering ACh.

Item nine is brief — a dynorphin and alcohol paper from Brendan Walker's group at the University of South Florida. They identify the islands of Calleja, those striatal granule-cell clusters in the ventral striatum, as a previously underappreciated dynorphin-rich region recruited by alcohol dependence. After eight weeks of alcohol vapor exposure, Wistar rats showed increased dynorphin-A immunoreactivity in the islands of Calleja, increased alcohol self-administration, and elevated twenty-two-kilohertz ultrasonic vocalizations — the rodent negative-affect proxy. Crucially, average dynorphin-A neuron size correlated with twenty-two-kilohertz call count in dependent animals only. The dynorphin-kappa opioid story in AUD has typically focused on nucleus accumbens shell and amygdala; the islands of Calleja are essentially a new entry in that map. It's correlative work — no kappa antagonist intervention in this preprint — but it's the kind of anatomical finding that sets up the next round of intersectional dynorphin-Cre experiments.

Tenth, an addiction paper from Molecular Psychiatry on cannabinoids and stress generalization. The design is unusually clean: rats are stressed once with restraint paired with an odor, then three weeks later get vaporized THC plus CBD for five days, then ten days of intravenous THC-CBD self-administration, then abstinence — and the question is whether the cannabinoid exposure makes them generalize the stress response from the original odor to a neutral odor. It does. And the molecular signature in nucleus accumbens core is astrocyte retraction from synapses, drop in synapsin-1 density, and activation of matrix metalloproteinase-2 and -9. The effects are male-only. The take-home is that the PTSD literature on cannabinoids has been confused for a decade because the protective acute effects sit on top of a destructive remodeling that requires repeated exposure to surface. This work gives a tractable molecular axis — astrocyte morphology and MMP activation — and explains why the same drug looks acutely calming and chronically stress-sensitizing.

We close on the day's agentic AI item: CAREPath, from Sangseon Lee's group at Inha University, on bioRxiv. The drug repurposing literature on biomedical knowledge graphs has spent the last two years arguing about path depth. Shallow paths miss biological context. Deep paths blow up combinatorially and end up routing through hub genes — TP53, MYC — that connect to everything and explain nothing. CAREPath proposes a balanced architecture: a DFS-style module that constrains traversal to short disease-gene-drug paths and encodes each path as a structured prompt for a biomedical language model, paired with a BFS-style module that builds entity-level mechanism-context embeddings from one-hop neighborhoods and augments them via pharmacologically related drugs and gene-signature-similar diseases. Across five biomedical knowledge graphs and eighteen baselines, CAREPath gets the best overall AUPRC, up to about four percent better. The semantic short-path encoding is doing most of the work, and the BFS context augmentation provides robustness when evidence is sparse. The case studies reproduce recent FDA-approved indication switches. For computational pharmacology readers, the take is methodological: hybrid DFS-BFS reasoning over a KG with an LLM doing the embedding is becoming a respectable design pattern, and the gains over both pure KG-embedding methods and pure LLM methods are real but modest.
