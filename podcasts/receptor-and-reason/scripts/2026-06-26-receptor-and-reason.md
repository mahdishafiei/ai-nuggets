# Receptor & Reason — 2026-06-26

## Script

Good morning. It's Friday, June 26th, 2026 — Receptor and Reason. Ten items today, opening with a CNS polypharmacy story and closing with a new chloride sensor for inhibitory imaging.

Leading off — a small but interesting drug-repurposing experiment from Ted Allison's group at Alberta, posted yesterday. The setup is the trauma-to-tauopathy connection: post-traumatic seizures appear to drive part of the long tail of chronic traumatic encephalopathy and Alzheimer's risk after TBI, and prior work shows that anti-seizure drugs given acutely can blunt the eventual tau pathology. They run a larval zebrafish TBI model and ask whether levetiracetam alone is enough, or whether an mGluR2 positive allosteric modulator — JNJ-42153605, a tool compound from the Janssen schizophrenia program — adds anything. Three findings. Levetiracetam given right after injury blocks subsequent tau aggregation and cell death with an IC50 around three micromolar. The JNJ mGluR2 PAM by itself is even more potent, with an IC50 in the high nanomolar range. And — the key polypharmacy point — a subeffective dose of the PAM shifts the levetiracetam dose-response curve by about sixteen-fold. The take is that mGluR2 PAMs, which have had a tough decade as schizophrenia monotherapies, may be more useful as adjuncts that sensitize synaptic-vesicle-targeting AEDs in the acute post-TBI window. Worth watching whether anyone moves it into a mammalian closed-head injury model with a longer disease course.

Second — vitamin D3 in the MK-801 mouse model of schizophrenia, from an Indian group. They presupplement mice with 500 IU per kilo per day of D3 before two weeks of MK-801, and they get partial rescue of hyperlocomotion, anxiety-like behavior, and working memory deficits. What's worth pulling out is the molecular detail: D3 normalizes prefrontal expression of the NR1, NR2A, and NR2B subunits, restores alpha-7 nicotinic acetylcholine receptor expression, brings back PSD-95, and knocks down calcineurin. In HEK cells transfected with alpha-7 nAChRs, calcitriol potentiates the single-channel current amplitude — a direct allosteric effect on a ligand-gated channel, which is more interesting than the usual "vitamin D as anti-inflammatory" story. They also find vitamin D deficiency in their Indian schizophrenia patient sample, which is the translational hook. This is preclinical and it's a nutraceutical, so the bar is high — but the direct alpha-7 modulation is a specific, testable claim, and it slots into the NMDA-hypofunction-plus-alpha-7 story that's been the dominant framework for cognitive symptoms.

Third — a nice mechanistic GPCR paper on GPR65, the proton-sensing receptor that's been creeping up the inflammatory-pain target list. The question is whether GPR65 has to internalize to do its job. In fibroblast-like synoviocytes — the cells that line synovial joints and drive joint inflammation — they pharmacologically and genetically block internalization and show that endosomal trafficking is required for nuclear ERK signaling and the transcriptional program that produces sensitizing cytokines. When they block GPR65 internalization in primary mouse synoviocytes, conditioned media from those cells loses its ability to sensitize dorsal root ganglion neurons. So this is endosomal signaling, not plasma-membrane signaling, that drives the pro-nociceptive output. For drug discovery this matters because biased-agonist programs that try to bias toward or away from internalization typically assume you want to retain G-protein signaling at the membrane — here, the trafficking step is itself the pharmacologically relevant event.

Fourth — a single-nucleus RNA-seq atlas of the nucleus accumbens after volitional cocaine self-administration, from Heath Schmidt's group at Penn. Rats run ten days of intravenous cocaine self-administration versus yoked saline. They profile about 37,000 nuclei. The transcriptional response is heavily concentrated in two MSN subclasses: the D1 Ebf1-positive MSNs absorb about forty percent of all the differentially-expressed events on their own, followed by D2 Stk32a-positive MSNs and astrocytes. The pathway signal points at synaptic organization, RAS-MAPK, NMDA-receptor-adjacent signaling, and CREB-dependent transcription. The interpretive headline — accumbens cocaine transcriptomics is not a uniform reward-circuit signature; it's a small number of specific MSN subclasses doing most of the work, with astrocytes pulled in as well. Useful as a cell-type-of-record reference if you're targeting CUD with anything subtype-selective.

Fifth — staying on dopamine but flipping to methodology. A cautionary paper from fiber-photometry users comparing three sensors during acute morphine challenges in rats: GCaMP6f in VTA dopamine neurons, dLight1.3b in the lateral shell of the nucleus accumbens, and GRABDA2h in the same region. All three are widely deployed. GCaMP6f and dLight1.3b behave as expected — morphine increases transient frequency and amplitude. GRABDA2h goes the other direction: morphine *suppresses* its transient activity, while the whole-stream signal increases with reduced variability. They interpret this as sensor saturation — basically, GRABDA2h has high enough affinity that under sustained morphine-driven release, the sensor is pinned and can't report transients anymore. The lesson is simple but worth flagging: if you're doing pharmacology with the high-affinity GRAB sensors, you have to validate against orthogonal sensors at the doses you're using, because a "suppression" result may just be a saturated reporter. Anyone doing photometry-based opioid or stimulant work should read this before designing their next study.

Sixth — a swing at disease modification in Parkinson's via beta-1 integrin. The premise: beta-1 integrin acts as an alternative receptor for GDNF — the dopaminergic survival factor — and also mediates microglial responses to alpha-synuclein. They give unilateral 6-OHDA-lesioned mice an allosteric anti-beta-1 antibody called JB1a, either prophylactically three days before lesion, or therapeutically three or seven days after. Pre-treatment trends toward reduced apomorphine-induced rotation but doesn't reach significance with n equals four. The three-day post-lesion arm is null. The seven-day post-lesion arm cuts apomorphine-induced rotation by about half, bringing rotations down to sham levels. The authors are honest in the abstract: apomorphine-induced rotation reflects post-synaptic D2 supersensitivity rather than direct neuronal preservation, and they didn't have histology to confirm dopaminergic survival. So it's proof-of-concept behavioral data, not a disease-modification claim. But the temporal specificity — late, not early — is interesting and rules out a simple acute neuroprotection story. The right next experiment is the same protocol in a synuclein-driven model with stereology.

Seventh — TCR-repertoire-based diagnosis of idiopathic Parkinson's disease, from a group reconstructing T-cell receptor repertoires from RNA-seq data in two of the largest iPD cohorts available. The historical problem: prior attempts to find a shared PD-specific TCR signature have failed, and the field has largely concluded that iPD patients don't have convergent motifs. Their bet is that this isn't a signal absence — it's a stratification problem. They use three etiologically distinct mouse models as anchors — genetic alpha-synuclein, MPTP-like toxin exposure, and a third — to define molecular subtypes within the human iPD patients, then train subtype-specific multimodal multi-instance classifiers. Within each data-driven subtype, AUCs exceed 0.8. This is a good template more generally — using mechanistic animal models as supervised anchors to slice etiologically heterogeneous clinical populations before training. If this holds up in independent cohorts it's a peripheral blood diagnostic, and arguably more importantly, a way to tell mechanistically-distinct PD patients apart for trial enrichment.

Eighth — patient iPSC-derived cerebral organoids benchmarked against clinical Alzheimer's proteomics. The question is the one everyone using AD organoids should be asking but few do: do organoid proteomes actually look like patient cortex, CSF, or plasma proteomes? They run AD and control organoids — containing neurons, astrocytes, and microglia — on the three platforms that dominate clinical biomarker discovery: mass spectrometry, SomaScan, and Olink. They benchmark against 121 prior studies and over 17,000 plasma, CSF, and cortex samples. Almost every clinically-nominated candidate is detectable in the organoid system. About one in four of the most reproducible disease-associated changes are recapitulated, spanning synaptic, mitochondrial, and proteostatic biology. One in four is below where you'd want it for a target validation system, but it's the first clear quantitative answer to "how well do AD organoids actually mirror the patient proteome." This is the kind of methods reference paper that should sit on the desk of anyone running compound screens in patient organoids.

Ninth — sliding into the agentic-AI tier. A group out of Michigan and collaborators has built a provenance-aware multi-agent system for psychiatric medication safety information. The motivation will be familiar — patients increasingly query the internet for safety information on their psych meds, and the available evidence is split between FDA Adverse Event Reporting System data, which is authoritative but abstract, and patient-reported narratives on Reddit and WebMD, which are experience-near but unvalidated and inconsistent. They unify almost half a million Reddit posts, sixty thousand WebMD reviews, and twenty years of FAERS records for nine antidepressants into a Neo4j knowledge graph grounded in ATC, ICD-10, and MedDRA vocabularies. The entity-recognition pipeline hits F1 of 0.97 against physician annotation. Two findings worth noting. First, the patient platforms agree with each other much more than either agrees with FAERS — Jaccard overlap up to 0.9 between Reddit and WebMD. Second, for sertraline, many adverse events show up in patient-reported sources hundreds of days before the corresponding FDA report. That second finding is the most important one for pharmacovigilance: provenance-aware integration of patient-generated data is potentially a leading indicator of FAERS rather than a noisy duplicate. The system itself is interesting too — keeping every claim traceable to its source means you can present regulatory facts and patient experience to a user without conflating them, which is the right design pattern.

Tenth and last — a new genetically-encoded chloride sensor called iClima, for imaging fast GABAergic chloride currents in vivo. Existing chloride sensors have a long-standing problem — they're pH-sensitive in the physiological range, which contaminates the chloride signal with metabolic and activity-driven pH transients. iClima is engineered with a pKa of 7.84, sitting it above the physiological pH range so it's effectively pH-insensitive. Chloride affinity is 3.5 millimolar at pH 7.2, which is in the right range for resting and dynamic intracellular chloride, and it's photostable enough for sustained in vivo imaging. They show it detecting GABA-A-evoked chloride transients in vitro, sensory-evoked transients in pyramidal neurons of mouse visual cortex in vivo, and — using its spectral separation from GCaMP6f — simultaneous chloride and calcium measurements in the same cell. The big use case is mapping the spatiotemporal dynamics of inhibitory drive at synaptic resolution, including the chloride dysregulation that emerges in seizures and in conditions where the KCC2-NKCC1 balance is disrupted. Watch for this to show up in the developmental epilepsy and refractory-seizure literature over the next year.

That's ten. The CNS-pharm story of the day is the JNJ-mGluR2-PAM-plus-levetiracetam combination for blunting post-TBI tauopathy. The methods item worth bookmarking is the GRABDA2h saturation finding — recalibrate your photometry interpretations accordingly. Have a good Friday.
