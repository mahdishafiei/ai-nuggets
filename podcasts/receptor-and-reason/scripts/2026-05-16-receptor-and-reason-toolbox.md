# 2026-05-16 — Receptor & Reason — Bonus: Drug-Target Toolbox

## Script

Welcome back to Receptor and Reason, Alan. This is a bonus drop for Saturday, May sixteenth, because the bioRxiv pharmacology and neuroscience queues kept producing strong methodology and drug-discovery items after this morning's main episode shipped. The theme that emerged is unmistakable: today is a toolbox day. PET tracers for two different neuroinflammation targets. A negative allosteric modulator of GABA-A alpha-five failing to translate to a better Down syndrome model. Phage-display cyclic peptides against a CAPON adaptor protein. A statin-activated TRPA1 site map. A manganese-chelator pipeline running through zebrafish and knockout mice. A clinical neuromodulation paper that argues we have been dosing prefrontal TMS wrong for a decade. And a multi-agent large language model system that tries to do genome-scale metabolic reasoning. Ten items, all fresh, all from the last week, none overlapping with what shipped this morning. Let us get into it.

Item one is a systematic review and meta-analysis from the group at Rio Grande do Sul, looking at the effects of NMDA receptor antagonists on social behavior in preclinical animal studies. This matters because pharmacological NMDA blockade — most often dizocilpine, the high-affinity channel blocker historically called MK-801, plus ketamine and phencyclidine — is the workhorse rodent model for the negative-symptom domain of schizophrenia. The authors screened the literature without language or date restrictions, ended up with two hundred sixty-four eligible controlled in-vivo studies, and synthesized effect sizes using correlated multilevel random-effects models with cluster-robust variance estimation. The overall signal: NMDA antagonists do reduce social interaction and social preference relative to controls, supporting the construct. But two caveats matter. First, the social preference literature is vulnerable to small-study effects and imprecision, which means publication bias is plausibly inflating that signal. Second, when antipsychotics rescue social behavior in NMDA-antagonist-treated animals, the rescue commonly co-occurs with reduced locomotion during the social interaction test — which is exactly the confound you would worry about. The authors flag that improvements in social measures may partly reflect altered behavioral competition and time allocation rather than selective restoration of social functioning. The takeaway for your audience: if you are using NMDA-antagonist social withdrawal as a translational endpoint for a new compound, paradigm choice and locomotor control are doing more of the work than the field has been pricing in. This is a useful methodological prior to carry into reading any new schizophrenia-model paper.

Item two stays in the GABA receptor space and is, frankly, a translational disappointment worth thinking about carefully. A group at IGBMC and Strasbourg evaluated alpha-five-I-A — a negative allosteric modulator at alpha-five-containing GABA-A receptors, sometimes written as alpha-five-I-A or just 5-I-A — in the Dp-sixteen-one-Yey mouse model of Down syndrome. The rationale here is well-trodden: cognitive deficits in Down syndrome have been attributed to over-inhibition by interneurons, and alpha-five-I-A type compounds rescued cognition in the older Ts65Dn model, which provided much of the preclinical scaffold for clinical trials of alpha-five negative modulators. The Dp-sixteen-one-Yey line carries a more accurate duplication of the orthologous region of human chromosome twenty-one. The authors first characterized the phenotype — spatial memory deficits on the Barnes maze, robust learning deficits in a pattern dissociation task, motor coordination impairments — and then ran the drug. Alpha-five-I-A improved motor coordination, but failed to restore Barnes-maze performance or pattern dissociation. The authors interpret this as possible divergence in pathophysiology between the two mouse models, which dovetails with the well-known struggles of alpha-five-NAMs in human Down syndrome trials. It is the kind of negative result that should weigh more in our priors than it usually does — the corpus of cognitive rescue claims in Ts65Dn may be overfit to that specific aneuploidy model.

Item three crosses over into pharmacogenomics, and it is a beautiful experimental design. The McGill group — Khayachi, Milnerwood, and the Rouleau lab — used patient-derived induced pluripotent stem-cell neurons from lithium-responsive bipolar patients, lithium-non-responsive bipolar patients, and matched controls, and asked whether the neuronal secretome — specifically, extracellular vesicle cargo — carried disease and treatment-response signatures. Unbiased proteomic and microRNA profiling identified ten proteins and thirteen microRNAs that distinguished bipolar EV cargo from control, with distinct sub-signatures separating lithium responders from non-responders. The pathways implicated are the usual suspects in a good way: synaptic function, neurotrophic signaling, and cellular stress responses. The conceptual payoff comes from a network experiment: chronic exposure of control neuron cultures to bipolar-neuron-conditioned media altered the proportion of active neurons and the frequency and amplitude of calcium transients. So the secretome is not just a biomarker readout — it is functionally active. Given that only about thirty percent of bipolar patients fully respond to lithium, and given that we still have no validated clinical pharmacogenomic test for lithium response, an EV-based liquid biopsy panel — even at the proof-of-concept stage — is a serious signal. The corresponding caveat: hiPSC-derived neuron experiments have a long history of producing differential expression that does not survive replication or scale-up. So the path forward is matched plasma EVs in patient cohorts, not more dish work.

Item four pivots from biomarkers to clinical neuromodulation dose-finding. A team at the Max Planck Institute for Human Cognitive and Brain Sciences asked: when we deliver repetitive TMS to dorsolateral prefrontal cortex for depression, what does the dose actually look like at the cortex, and how does the dose interact with functional connectivity to the subgenual cingulate? They combined resting-state functional MRI, finite-element simulation of the TMS-induced electric field, and electrocardiography to measure heart-brain coupling — defined as spectral power in the cardiac signal locked to the TMS pulse frequency — across six DLPFC targets in healthy adults. Two clean findings. First, cortical electric-field dose explained substantially more variance in heart-brain coupling than the conventional motor-threshold-percentage dose that essentially every TMS clinic uses. Second, incorporating DLPFC-to-subgenual-cingulate connectivity further improved the model, and the strongest, most reliable cardiac modulation came from DLPFC regions anti-correlated with the subgenual cingulate — which is exactly the connectivity-guided targeting principle that Fox and Pascual-Leone proposed years ago, but here grounded in a physiological readout rather than a clinical endpoint. There is also a hint of nonlinearity: at confined dose ranges, positively connected sites can also drive coupling. The clinical implication is that motor-threshold dosing is a coarse instrument, and that field-based, connectivity-informed dosing is now sitting on enough evidence that someone with a TMS device and an MRI scanner should be running a head-to-head trial.

Items five and six are PET tracer papers that, taken together, sketch the current state of in-vivo neuroinflammation imaging beyond the saturated TSPO target. Item five, from a multi-institution group with corresponding authorship at Emory, is the preclinical evaluation of fluorine-eighteen-labeled P4B-2412 as a phosphodiesterase 4B-selective PET radioligand. PDE4B hydrolyzes cyclic AMP and is highly expressed in brain regions implicated in neuroinflammation and neurodegeneration; it is also a notoriously hard target to drug selectively without emetogenic effects from PDE4D cross-reactivity. The authors report high radiochemical yield around twenty-seven percent, ninety-nine percent radiochemical purity, favorable molar activity, in-vitro autoradiography showing high PDE4B binding, dynamic PET demonstrating in-vivo specificity, and excellent metabolic stability. It is exactly the preclinical package you want to see before a tracer moves toward IND. The use case is twofold: as a research tool for measuring central PDE4B engagement of new PDE4B-preferring inhibitors, and as a candidate disease-state biomarker for neuroinflammation in CNS drug trials. Worth tracking through first-in-human.

Item six is the companion paper conceptually — a P2X7 receptor radiotracer characterized in a temporal lobe epilepsy model. The Copenhagen group at Rigshospitalet validated tritiated JNJ-64413739, the well-known P2X7 antagonist scaffold, in rat brain by autoradiography — measuring a dissociation constant around three nanomolar with full saturation by ten nanomolar — and then ran the tracer through an intrahippocampal kainic-acid status-epilepticus model at multiple post-insult time points. The interesting findings are temporal, not just regional: P2X7 binding in the hippocampus and thalamic subregions rises within days of status epilepticus, peaks at day thirty, and stays high through day ninety. Temporal and piriform cortex are flat until day thirty and then jump sharply. The corpus callosum follows a similar delayed pattern. The interpretation is that the inflammatory signal P2X7 reflects is a sustained, region-progressive process rather than an acute spike — and that means a P2X7 PET tracer could potentially stratify patients in the latent period of post-traumatic epileptogenesis, which is exactly the window where disease-modifying anti-epileptogenic trials have repeatedly failed because we cannot identify who is converting.

Item seven brings us into structure-aware drug discovery. A group at Weill Cornell, with Gabr as corresponding author, used phage display of a disulfide-constrained cyclic peptide library to identify ligands targeting CAPON — also called NOS1AP — the adaptor protein that scaffolds neuronal nitric oxide synthase and has been independently implicated in Alzheimer's, NMDA-receptor-driven excitotoxicity, and tau pathology. After phage enrichment, ELISA validation, microscale thermophoresis, and biolayer interferometry, they advanced a peptide called CAP1, with low micromolar affinity for CAPON. The functional story is more interesting than the biophysics: CAP1 attenuated amyloid-beta-42 neuronal toxicity, suppressed NMDA-driven nitric oxide production, and reduced pathological tau phosphorylation in neuronal models under AD-relevant stress. They also report preliminary pharmacokinetic-favorable properties — aqueous solubility, plasma stability, measurable membrane permeability — which for a cyclic peptide at the start of optimization is not nothing. The audience-relevant point is that CAPON has been on Alzheimer's target lists for years without a credible chemical probe, and a stapled or constrained-peptide scaffold is exactly the modality where the field has made the most progress against protein-protein interaction targets in CNS.

Item eight is one of those papers that complicates a familiar narrative. A group at KU Leuven in the Talavera lab found that five commonly used statins — simvastatin, lovastatin, fluvastatin, atorvastatin, and pravastatin — directly activate TRPA1, the polymodal nociceptive cation channel, in human and mouse heterologous expression systems. The activation is reversible, concentration-dependent, in the low micromolar range, and crucially is not mediated by the canonical N-terminal cysteines that electrophilic agonists like allyl isothiocyanate target. Molecular docking and single-point mutagenesis revealed two distinct putative binding sites in the pore domain: one near the kink of transmembrane segment five, and one at the interface between TM4 and TM5. The TRPA1 antagonist A-967079, which abrogates the electrophilic agonist response, has weaker and varied effects across the different statins and shows agonist-dependent competition. The pharmacology interpretation is that TRPA1 has at least two non-electrophilic agonist sites and that agonist-specific antagonists are mechanistically plausible. The clinical relevance is more speculative: statin-induced myalgia is a real and poorly understood clinical problem, and TRPA1 is a credible candidate mediator of statin-associated muscle and nerve sensitization. Worth holding alongside the broader statin-pleiotropy literature.

Item nine is a drug-development pipeline paper that reads like a roadmap. A group at King's College London established and validated a screening pipeline for manganese chelators aimed at manganism — the parkinsonian movement disorder arising from environmental manganese overexposure or from loss-of-function variants in the manganese transporters SLC30A10 and SLC39A14. Current clinical practice relies on intravenous calcium-disodium-EDTA, which is burdensome and not viable for long-term use. The authors selected two novel chelators based on manganese-MRI-contrast-agent scaffolds — abbreviated as H3-P-y-C-three-A and H4-EDTA-BTA, plus their methyl ester pro-drug derivatives — and ran them through zebrafish with loss-of-function slc39a14 and through SLC30A10 knockout mice. H3-P-y-C-three-A and its methyl-ester pro-drug were more effective than calcium-EDTA at mobilizing manganese, improved locomotor behavior in zebrafish, and showed selective manganese chelation over physiological zinc and copper in the mouse model. Oral bioavailability was under one percent, but long-term oral dosing of the parent compound modestly reduced liver and brain manganese accumulation, which the authors interpret as a gastrointestinal-chelation contribution. This is a rare disease with a real unmet need, and the in-vitro-to-in-vivo pipeline they describe is reusable for other metal-overload disorders.

Item ten is your weekly agentic-AI capstone. A group at the University of Nebraska, with Helikar as senior author, presents MechAInistic — a multi-agent large language model system that takes natural-language biological questions and converts them into executable workflows on genome-scale constraint-based metabolic models. The architecture follows an Architect-Reviewer pattern: one agent decomposes the question into a plan, another reviews and executes against COBRA-style metabolic model APIs, and the system produces a structured report grounded in the model output and the literature. They demonstrate the system on two drug repurposing use cases. In paired single-cell metabolic models of naive B cells from rheumatoid arthritis patients versus healthy controls, the system flagged metabolic rewiring, prioritized candidate reactions via topological hub filtering and robustness analysis, and surfaced devimistat — the alpha-ketoglutarate-dehydrogenase inhibitor in oncology trials — as a candidate repurposing hit. In a paired CD4-positive Th17 multiple-sclerosis model, the system identified NADP-dependent isocitrate dehydrogenase as the optimal target and proposed ivosidenib — the FDA-approved IDH1 inhibitor — as the repurposing candidate. The candor-worthy caveat: drug-repurposing claims from constraint-based modeling are notoriously over-generated, and devimistat in particular has had a brutal clinical history in oncology. The interesting contribution is not the repurposing hits per se but the demonstration that you can put a competent agentic wrapper on a genuinely mechanistic modeling backend and produce a workflow that a biologist without flux-balance training could plausibly drive. That is the right shape for agentic biology — the agents do the orchestration, the mechanistic models do the science.

That is the toolbox bonus. A meta-analysis that sharpens our priors on schizophrenia-model interpretation. A negative GABA-A alpha-five result that should temper Down-syndrome translational enthusiasm. A patient-derived neuronal secretome panel that, if it scales to plasma, could finally crack lithium response prediction. A field-and-connectivity-based dosing argument for prefrontal TMS. Two PET tracers, one for PDE4B and one for P2X7, pointing at neuroinflammation imaging beyond TSPO. A cyclic peptide modality for CAPON in Alzheimer's. A novel mechanistic site on TRPA1 for statins. A manganese-chelator pipeline for an underserved rare disease. And an agentic wrapper on genome-scale metabolic models with the right division of labor between the language model and the math. Have a good Saturday, Alan. Back to the regular daily cadence tomorrow.
