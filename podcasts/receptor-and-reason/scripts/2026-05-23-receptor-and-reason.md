## Script

Welcome back to Receptor and Reason. Ten items today, opening on a Wyss-Institute paper that quietly removes a load-bearing assumption from the CNS-biologics field — that brain-shuttle receptor targets at the blood-brain barrier are evenly expressed across patients and brain regions. Then a substantial Baylor tau-ablation paper that maps what happens to every major brain cell type when you remove tau in health and in amyloidosis, a Parkinson's mechanism paper from Toulouse that puts the disease-driving locus in microglia rather than dopaminergic neurons, an Edinburgh paper that shows astrocytes literally rewrite the epigenome of developing cortical neurons through a single ion-channel-family switch, two clean iPSC-and-mouse drug-rescue papers on different channelopathies — a Northwestern SCN2A sodium-channel-variant paper and a Vanderbilt small-molecule rescue of GABA-A receptor folding mutants — a Wyoming depression paper that goes after the molecular signature of treatment-resistant illness as a progressive landscape rather than a state, a McGill paper that asks why the adolescent regulatory architecture in females creates baseline vulnerability to depression, an NIH theoretical paper that proposes the synaptic pruning of late adolescence as the structural cause of the psychiatric-disorder onset window, and a closer on a small-molecule drug-design benchmark for large-language-model agents that is finally hard enough to be useful. Let's go.

Lead item, and the one with the most immediate clinical read-through — brain-shuttle target expression levels vary by individual, not by brain region, disease, age, or sex, from the Gorman group at the Wyss Institute at Harvard. The CNS-biologics field has been struggling with the same problem for decades — antibodies, peptide therapeutics, enzyme replacements, and gene-therapy payloads do not cross the blood-brain barrier on their own. The current solution that is moving fastest into the clinic is the brain shuttle — fuse your therapeutic to a binder against an endothelial cell-surface receptor that the barrier already shuttles across, like the transferrin receptor for trontinemab, or CD98 heavy chain, and piggyback your payload through receptor-mediated transcytosis. The unstated assumption is that the shuttle receptor is expressed at similar levels in every patient, in every brain region, and across the diseases you want to treat. If that assumption fails, the dose-response curve becomes a moving target — same dose, very different brain exposure, with the variability invisible in plasma pharmacokinetics. The Wyss team tested the assumption directly — eleven canonical brain-shuttle targets, gene and protein expression, in isolated human brain microvascular endothelial cells and brain microvessels, across eleven cohorts spanning normal brain, Alzheimer's disease, Parkinson's disease, Huntington's disease, and amyotrophic lateral sclerosis. The result has two parts. The reassuring part — expression is remarkably stable across brain regions, sexes, ages, and all four major neurodegenerative diseases. Disease state is not collapsing or amplifying the shuttle receptor pool at the barrier. The load-bearing part — between individuals, expression of the most-used shuttle targets varies by roughly an order of magnitude. The transferrin-receptor variance across donors is large enough that a fixed-dose biologic delivered through a transferrin-receptor shuttle will produce up to ten-fold differences in brain exposure across patients, with no easy plasma readout. Three clinical implications. Dose-finding studies for brain-shuttle therapeutics need to be powered for inter-individual receptor-expression variability, not just body weight and renal clearance. A peripheral diagnostic for shuttle-receptor expression — whether a circulating endothelial cell or a surrogate biomarker — becomes a real product opportunity. And the fact that disease does not move the shuttle target means you can extrapolate dosing from healthy volunteers to patient populations without a major correction. Big lesson, small paper.

Item two, the most substantial tau paper of the week — tau ablation rewires brain cell programs in health and restores function in disease, from the Lasagna-Reeves lab at Baylor College of Medicine. The tau-knockout phenotype has been one of the most studied genetic perturbations in neurodegeneration, with the dominant interpretation being that loss of tau is neutral in health and protective in tau-driven and amyloid-driven disease. The Baylor group asks the cell-resolution version of that question at depth. Single-nucleus RNA sequencing on aged tau-knockout mouse cortex against age-matched wild-type, and the same comparison on a cerebral-amyloid-angiopathy background — a vascular-amyloid model that drives neurodegeneration. Four cell-type findings. Excitatory neurons in tau-knockout cortex shifted into a distinct subtype with a unique glutamatergic signaling program — tau loss is actively rewiring excitatory identity, not sitting neutral. Astrocytes adopted a synaptoprotective state rather than the reactive A-one pattern associated with neuroinflammation. Oligodendrocytes upregulated genes supporting axonal connectivity and plasticity, consistent with the white-matter preservation seen in tau-knockout aging. And microglia engaged structural-remodeling rather than the disease-associated inflammatory signatures of tauopathy and amyloidosis. The disease-model arm is what makes the paper translational. In the cerebral-amyloid-angiopathy background, tau ablation pulled each of these cell types back toward the protective configurations seen in the healthy tau-knockout, and that cell-state restoration tracked with functional rescue. The implication for tau-lowering therapeutics — antisense oligonucleotides, small-molecule destabilizers, immunotherapies — is that the mechanism of benefit may not primarily be about removing toxic tau species from neurons. It may be substantially about releasing astrocytes, oligodendrocytes, and microglia into protective states they cannot reach while tau is intact. That changes the biomarker conversation — instead of soluble tau or neurofilament light, you would want cell-state markers in CSF or imaging that report on the glial response. And if the benefit is glial-state-dependent, the dose-response in patients may be flatter than tau-engagement assays predict. A serious paper.

Item three, a Parkinson's disease paper that lands the disease-driving locus in microglia rather than neurons — microglial Foxo3 shapes dopaminergic vulnerability in Parkinson disease, from the Szelechowski group at the Toulouse Institute for Infectious and Inflammatory Diseases. The conceptual question is one the field has been circling for years. Dopaminergic neurons in the substantia nigra show characteristic stress signatures in Parkinson's — mitochondrial dysfunction, proteotoxic stress from alpha-synuclein aggregation — and the obvious move has been to ask whether the cell-intrinsic stress response in those neurons is what drives their death. The Toulouse group took the question apart at the cell-type level. The stress-response transcription factor Foxo3 — anchor it as a forkhead-box family member that translocates to the nucleus under oxidative and metabolic stress and activates pro-survival or pro-apoptotic gene programs depending on context — gets rapidly induced in dopaminergic-like cells in response to both complex-one inhibition and alpha-synuclein aggregation. Consistent with the field's standing assumption. The surprise is what happens when you delete Foxo3 selectively in neurons versus selectively in microglia. Neuron-specific deletion attenuated early Parkinson-like phenotypes only modestly. Microglia-specific deletion produced substantially larger rescue — meaning the microglial Foxo3 state is the more important determinant of whether the dopaminergic neuron survives. Microglial Foxo3 is acting as a switch between a homeostatic state and a neurotoxic state, and the neurotoxic state is what drives dopaminergic loss. Two drug-discovery implications. The Parkinson's microglial target list has been dominated by broad anti-inflammatories and LRRK-two-pathway compounds. A microglia-selective Foxo3 modulator would be a different shape of asset — closer to the immuno-oncology playbook of cell-state-selective transcription-factor pharmacology. And it changes how you read the failure of recent LRRK-two trials in idiopathic Parkinson's. If the microglial state is downstream of LRRK-two but more proximate to neuron loss, LRRK-two inhibition was attacking the right pathway at the wrong node.

Item four, an astrocyte paper from the Hardingham lab at the University of Edinburgh that takes the astrocyte-shapes-neuron-development story to a deeper layer — astrocytes transform the transcriptomic and epigenetic landscape of developing cortical neurons to promote activity via intrinsic excitability control. The field has known for over a decade that astrocytes secrete factors influencing neuronal maturation, but the genome-wide and epigenome-wide impact has been hard to separate from neuron-intrinsic developmental trajectories. The Edinburgh group separates them. Cortical astrocyte co-culture triggers widespread DNA methylation and chromatin accessibility remodeling in developing cortical neurons — distinct from what neurons do on their own — and the transcriptomic consequences are not additive to the neuron's program, they are a separate trajectory. The mechanism the paper pins down is unexpectedly tractable. The astrocyte-derived signal causes transcriptional repression of inwardly-rectifying potassium channels — the Kir family — in the cortical neuron, and that one move flips intrinsic excitability into a pro-excitatory zone. Neurons start firing earlier in development without first building functional excitatory synapses, which is the textbook prerequisite for activity emergence in vitro. Cell-autonomous homeostasis in the neuron then comes online and modulates Kir expression to bring excitability back. The disease-genetics layer closes the loop. The astrocyte-induced neuronal gene program is significantly enriched for risk genes from schizophrenia, epilepsy, attention-deficit hyperactivity disorder, and Alzheimer's disease. A substantial fraction of psychiatric-disorder risk gene expression in the developing cortex is not being set by the neuron — it is being set by the astrocyte through this Kir-mediated switch. Two implications. Drug screens that use neuron-only iPSC platforms are missing the astrocyte-driven half of the risk-gene regulatory landscape. And Kir-channel modulators — a class with several existing tool compounds, including selective neuronal Kir-two-one modulators in development — become candidates for early-developmental psychiatric-disorder phenotypes.

Item five, a clean human-iPSC-neuron rescue paper for a sodium-channel disorder — excitatory dysfunction and phenotypic rescue in a human neuronal model of SCN2A-related disorders, from the Adney lab at Northwestern. SCN2A — the gene encoding the voltage-gated sodium channel Nav1.2 — sits at the center of a heterogeneous group of disorders with phenotypes ranging from early-infantile epilepsy and developmental delay to autism with later seizure onset. The conventional account distinguishes loss-of-function variants, which present as autism, from gain-of-function variants, which present as early-onset epilepsy. Most of the gain-of-function work to date has been in HEK cells or oocytes, which give channel biophysics but not neuronal behavior. The Northwestern group ran the experiment in iPSC-derived excitatory neurons carrying the SCN2A methionine-to-threonine substitution at position one-eight-seven-nine — a pathogenic missense variant associated with early-onset epilepsy and developmental delay — with isogenic CRISPR-Cas9-corrected controls. Conventional patch-clamp and a high-throughput all-optical electrophysiology platform converged on the same phenotype — disease-variant neurons fired more action potentials at higher stimulus intensities than corrected controls, with characteristic action-potential-shape changes consistent with a gain-of-function sodium current. The therapeutic test is the part that makes this a Receptor and Reason story. Selective Nav1.2 blockers, at clinically achievable concentrations, restored disease-variant firing properties toward the isogenic-control baseline. A clean in-vitro proof of concept for precision pharmacology on a specific SCN2A variant. The clinical conversation needs the next two pieces — does the rescue hold in more mature human neurons, and does Nav1.2 selectivity hold up under chronic dosing. But the all-optical-electrophysiology assay is the kind of throughput the channelopathy-drug-discovery field has been needing to screen variants and compounds at scale.

Item six, a pharmacological rescue paper in the same family — four-phenylbutyrate rescue in GABRA1 variants associated with developmental epileptic encephalopathies, from the Kang lab at Vanderbilt University Medical Center. GABA-A receptors are the dominant inhibitory ionotropic receptors in the CNS and the target of most antiseizure drugs, yet variants in GABRA1, encoding the alpha-one subunit, produce developmental and epileptic encephalopathies that respond poorly to existing GABA-A pharmacology. The reason, as the Vanderbilt group's prior work showed for a different subunit, is that many of these pathogenic missense variants are misfolding mutations — the receptor subunit fails to traffic to the cell surface, so the channel pool available for drug modulation is reduced before any drug has had a chance to act. Four-phenylbutyrate, or PBA — anchor it as a small-molecule chemical chaperone, approved for urea-cycle disorders, with broad protein-folding-rescue activity at clinically achievable doses — was tested for ability to rescue GABRA1 misfolding variants. Multi-scale platform — in silico structural modeling identified candidate variants, HEK-cell expression with flow cytometry and patch clamp confirmed the surface-expression deficit, PBA treatment increased total and surface alpha-one subunit expression and rescued functional GABA-A currents, and a Gabra1 alanine-to-aspartate-three-twenty-two knockin mouse model extended the rescue to the whole animal. The translational implication is that the chaperone-rescue paradigm generalizes across GABA-A subunit variants, not just the specific gamma-two-Q-three-ninety-X variant the group worked on before. Patients with GABRA1-associated DEEs, who currently have no targeted therapy, become candidates for a PBA-based program. The next move is a structured rescue-screening platform that tells a family with a specific GABRA1 variant whether their child's receptor will respond to PBA, and at what dose.

Item seven, a depression paper that takes seriously the clinical reality that major depressive disorder is a progressive illness — molecular characterization of the progressive landscape of depression, from the Shukla group at the University of Wyoming, with Sibille and McCullumsmith collaborators. Most molecular-psychiatry studies of depression compare a depressed episode against remission, or against control, treating the disease as a state that switches on and off. The clinical reality is different — patients accumulate episodes, inter-episode intervals shorten, treatment response degrades, and the illness becomes recurrent and treatment-resistant. The Wyoming group asks whether postmortem brain has a molecular signature that reads out the cumulative-burden axis, not just the state axis. They reanalyzed RNA-seq from postmortem subgenual anterior cingulate cortex and built a model that distinguishes progressive changes — defined as a linear trend across clinical stages from first to recurrent episodes or remissions — from state-specific changes between episode and remission. Two findings carry pharmacological weight. Progressive and state-specific signatures overlap substantially but are not identical — there is a separable progressive component you would miss in a standard case-control comparison. And the progressive component is enriched for cell types and pathways under-explored in depression therapeutics — microglial inflammatory programs, mitochondrial metabolism, and specific GPCR-signaling cassettes — several with existing drug candidates from other indications that could be repositioned. Treatment-resistant depression has historically been framed as a more severe version of the same illness — same biology, but worse. The progressive-landscape framing says treatment-resistant depression has a partially distinct molecular fingerprint, and that fingerprint is where the unmet pharmacological need actually lives.

Item eight, a complementary piece on depression vulnerability that operates at the developmental rather than the disease-state axis — sex-specific hormone-sensitive regulatory architecture in adolescence as a scaffold for depression vulnerability, from the Corina Nagy group at McGill. The longstanding observation is the post-pubertal female depression-vulnerability gradient — males and females have roughly equal depression rates in childhood, then diverge sharply through adolescence into the established roughly two-to-one female-to-male prevalence in adulthood. The McGill group used longitudinal transcriptomic, epigenomic, and gene-regulatory-network analyses across adolescence to characterize the normative developmental architecture before any depression has manifested. Two patterns. Immediate early genes — the activity-dependent transcription-factor cassette that includes c-Fos, Arc, Egr-one — show the strongest sex-biased adolescent signals, with males and females on opposing developmental trajectories. More broadly, males show stronger activity-dependent neuronal signatures, females stronger stress-responsive and immune-related signatures. Extended into a disease-context arm using single-nucleus data from postmortem depressed brain, the female-biased adolescent stress-and-immune cassette overlaps substantially with the molecular signature of depressed brain in adult women. The female adolescent regulatory architecture is not pathological — it is normal developmental wiring — but it is closer to the disease state than the male architecture is, which is the structural reason a comparable stressor at age twenty-five produces depression more often in women than in men. Two pharmacological read-throughs. Prevention pharmacology in adolescent populations is closer to viable than the field has assumed, because the molecular substrate is being laid down in a biomarker-accessible window. And the long-noted female-biased efficacy and side-effect profiles of several antidepressant classes are partially explained by the regulatory-architecture difference — biomarker-stratified trial design would tease that apart.

Item nine, a theoretical paper from Bruno Averbeck at NIMH and Nicolas Brunel — synaptic pruning, myelination, and the emergence of psychiatric disorders in late adolescence. The observation the paper tries to explain is the late-adolescent and early-adult psychiatric-disorder onset window — schizophrenia, bipolar disorder, treatment-resistant depression all show characteristic onset peaks in the same developmental window that overlaps with two well-documented neurobiological changes — substantial pruning of excitatory connections in prefrontal cortex, and continued myelination of long-range axons. The question is whether those two structural changes, on their own, can produce the disease-vulnerability window. The Averbeck-Brunel approach is to build computational models of prefrontal attractor dynamics with three-factor learning rules — Hebbian plasticity gated by a global modulator like dopamine, the standard formal model of reinforcement learning at the circuit level — and let pruning and myelination unfold as they do empirically. Both, when they cross threshold during late adolescence, push the cortical attractor landscape into a regime where strongly encoded memories — including aversive, paranoid, or depressed cognitive patterns — become deeply embedded and difficult to dislodge through normal learning. The model produces psychiatric-like behavior — perseverative thought, rigidity of attractor states, resistance to extinction-style updating — as a generic consequence of the late-adolescent structural transition. The pharmacological read-through is the speculative but generative one. If the pathological attractor state is partly a function of the post-pruning, post-myelination network, the therapeutic move is to push the network back toward a more flexible regime. Two existing classes do exactly that, at least transiently — psychedelic-class 5-HT2A agonists, which soften attractor states based on imaging and behavioral readouts, and ketamine-class NMDA antagonists, which loosen prefrontal recurrent dynamics. The model gives those clinical observations a structural explanation that does not require the receptors themselves to be the seat of the disease — they are levers on the network state, not the source of pathology. That is a more biologically honest framing than the receptor-deficiency models, and it predicts that synaptic-plasticity-enhancing drugs and attractor-softening drugs should be more effective in combination than either alone.

Closing item, on the agentic-AI-for-drug-discovery side — SMDD-Bench, a benchmark that asks whether large-language-model agents can solve real-world small-molecule drug-design tasks. The honest assessment of the LLM-for-drug-discovery field is that benchmarks have been the bottleneck — the existing ones are too easy, single-turn, or trivially solvable by retrieval, which lets every new model claim state-of-the-art without saying much about whether it can actually do drug design. SMDD-Bench is built hard in the right ways. Five-hundred-and-two task instances, multi-turn, long-horizon, agentic — the model needs to plan, execute multiple steps, and produce structured outputs that get verified. Five task types — two-dimensional pharmacophore identification, interaction-point discovery, scaffold hopping, lead optimization, and fragment assembly — across one-hundred-and-two unique protein targets. Each task is guaranteed solvable by a competent medicinal chemist given the same information, so the benchmark rewards capability rather than constraining it. The interesting part is the model comparison. Frontier closed-source models solve a meaningful fraction of the easier task types but degrade sharply on lead optimization and fragment assembly, which require multi-step chemical reasoning across iterations. Open-source models with chemistry-specific fine-tuning do better on narrow tasks but lose ground on cross-domain transfer. The benchmark differentiates models in a way the previous generation of drug-design benchmarks did not. The reason this matters — the agentic drug-discovery workflows the show has been covering, the de-novo peptide pipelines, the structure-prediction-driven hallucination protocols, the multi-agent frontier-model knowledge graphs from yesterday — all need a public, hard, multi-turn benchmark to know whether their gains are real. SMDD-Bench is the first that looks like it might serve that role for the small-molecule side. Watch how the model-developer community responds to it over the next few months — that response is itself a signal about which design programs are operating from the same evidence base.
