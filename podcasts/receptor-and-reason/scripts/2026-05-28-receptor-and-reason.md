## Script

This is Receptor and Reason for Thursday, May twenty-eighth, twenty twenty-six. Ten items today, all fresh. We open on a chemogenetic platform that decodes the physiological roles of specific G-protein-coupled receptors without off-target drug effects, and we close on a state-space model architecture that lets electroencephalography run continuously in real time.

First, an off-target-free chemogenetic platform that decodes physiological roles of target G-protein-coupled receptors, from the Kiyonaka lab at Nagoya University. Conventional chemogenetic tools — designer receptors exclusively activated by designer drugs, the DREADD family — work by mutating a receptor to respond to an otherwise inert synthetic ligand, which is powerful but typically leaves no way to interrogate the wild-type endogenous receptor itself. The group inverts that logic. Instead of building a new receptor that responds to a new drug, they engineer the receptor of interest to be insensitive to a clinical-grade antagonist while preserving full responsiveness to its endogenous agonist. As a proof of concept they target the adenosine A-two-A receptor, a prototypical class-A G-protein-coupled receptor with known involvement in Parkinson's disease and other neurodegenerative conditions. They identify a mutant A-two-A receptor that ignores a clinical A-two-A antagonist but still binds and signals through adenosine, and they pair it with the antagonist as a chemogenetic pair. The application is immediate — they show that A-two-A signaling is critical for neurite outgrowth under hypoxia in neuron-like cells, a finding they can isolate from off-target effects of the antagonist on other adenosine receptor subtypes. The general principle works across other class-A G-protein-coupled receptors. For a field where most of our highest-value drug targets are receptors with messy off-target pharmacology, this is a methodological hand-off worth absorbing, because it gives you a way to ask the in-vivo question — what does the endogenous receptor actually do — without committing to a tool drug.

Second, an atomic interaction conserved for over six hundred million years gates inhibitory neurotransmission, from the Goldschen-Ohm lab at the University of Texas at Austin with collaborators at Karolinska, Iowa, and the University of Texas at Arlington. Pentameric ligand-gated ion channels — the receptor family that includes the GABA-A receptor, the glycine receptor, and the inhibitory invertebrate receptors going back to early bilaterians — share a tyrosine residue in the M-two-to-M-three linker that has been positionally conserved for over six hundred million years. The group hypothesized that this tyrosine forms a hydrogen bond to the backbone of the neighboring Cys-loop that couples the extracellular agonist-binding domain to the transmembrane channel-gating machinery. They tested it the hard way — by combining site-directed mutagenesis with noncanonical amino acid incorporation, which lets them ablate the hydrogen-bond donor or acceptor separately at the backbone level rather than just substituting the side chain. Electrophysiology in Xenopus oocytes shows that breaking this hydrogen bond markedly reduces both agonist sensitivity and maximal channel activation in GABA-A and glycine receptors, with effects localized to specific subunits, and molecular dynamics simulations confirm the gating role. The clinical hook is that human disease-associated variants at this tyrosine cause epilepsy, neurodevelopmental disability, and hyperekplexia, and the paper provides a mechanistic explanation. For pharmacology programs that target inhibitory channels — and this is the family that benzodiazepines, neurosteroid analogs like brexanolone and zuranolone, and many anesthetics act on — the gating linkage being a single hydrogen bond is the kind of structural anchor that allosteric modulator design can lean on.

Third, dysregulation of microRNAs drives premature GABAergic maturation and early neurodevelopmental defects in schizophrenia, from the Freude lab at the University of Copenhagen. Schizophrenia is a neurodevelopmental disorder, but most molecular work in the disease has been on adult post-mortem tissue, which captures end-state pathology rather than the developmental trajectory. The group built three-dimensional dorsal forebrain organoids from induced pluripotent stem cells of six schizophrenia patients and five matched controls, and cultured them out to one hundred and twenty days, which captures the first trimester equivalent of human cortical development. Multi-omics shows nineteen differentially expressed microRNAs in schizophrenia organoids — nine downregulated and tied to neural progenitor proliferation, ten upregulated and tied to neuronal differentiation and synaptic maturation — a signature consistent with a compressed developmental timeline rather than a global block. Fifty-five of the seventy-seven dysregulated messenger RNAs are predicted microRNA targets, and the network converges on early GABAergic lineage specification, with secondary effects on glutamatergic and dopaminergic development. Premature GABAergic maturation as a primary insult is a hypothesis that connects two of the older threads in schizophrenia — the parvalbumin-interneuron deficit and the excitation-inhibition imbalance — to a developmental cause. For pharmacology this is upstream of where current antipsychotic mechanisms act, but it makes the case that microRNA-based modulation during a vulnerable developmental window could be a future intervention point.

Fourth, disruption of the glutamine carrier Slc38a1 causes cognitive impairment, anxiety, and depressive-like behavior, from the Chaudhry group at the University of Oslo. The GABA-synthesis literature has long known that the rate-limiting substrate for GABA biosynthesis in inhibitory neurons is glutamine, supplied through specific solute carriers from neighboring glia. Solute carrier thirty-eight member one — Slc38a1 — accumulates glutamine in subpopulations of GABAergic neurons. The group had previously shown that genetic inactivation of Slc38a1 in mice lowers GABA levels, alters synaptic vesicle morphology, slows gamma oscillations, and reduces cortical processing. Here they push to behavior. Slc38a1 knockout mice show learning and memory deficits in the Morris water maze, increased despair in the forced swim test, and anxiety signatures in the open field, while sociability and social novelty preference are preserved. Metabolic phenotyping and locomotion rule out a peripheral explanation. The clinical anchor is a separately identified pathogenic Slc38a1 variant in a family with depression and suicidal behavior — small but real human evidence that this is not just a mouse phenotype. The reframe the paper is asking the field to consider is that GABAergic deficit in major depression and anxiety may be a substrate-supply problem upstream of receptor pharmacology — which would explain why direct GABA-receptor modulators have a noisy track record in mood disorders and would point toward enzyme- or transporter-level interventions as a complementary pharmacological lane.

Fifth, distinct ensembles in the prelimbic cortex track different measures of motivation for cocaine and water reinforcers, from the Moschak lab at the University of Texas at El Paso. The motivation construct in addiction research is typically assessed across three tasks — progressive ratio for effort, punishment for negative-consequence tolerance, and extinction for persistence in the absence of reward — and recent work has suggested that a shared latent variable explains performance across all three. The group recorded prelimbic cortex activity with in-vivo endoscopic calcium imaging in thirty-two male and female Sprague-Dawley rats running these tasks for either cocaine or water. The first finding is that the shared-latent-variable model fits water-reward behavior cleanly but does not fit cocaine behavior, which is a meaningful caveat for the whole shared-latent line of reasoning. The second finding is mechanistic. They identify two distinct prelimbic populations — cost-sensitive neurons that are selectively engaged during progressive ratio, and reward-sensitive neurons that are selectively engaged during extinction. Individual rats with more of these neurons present during the respective task show higher reward pursuit, and this relationship holds across both cocaine and water rats. The point is that motivation in the prelimbic cortex is not one signal — it is multiple ensembles tracking distinct decision variables, and pharmacological interventions that target prelimbic cortex function in addiction should expect dissociable effects on cost tolerance versus persistence rather than a single motivation knob.

Sixth, chronic stress alters dorsal bed nucleus of the stria terminalis synaptic neurotransmission in a Dravet syndrome mouse model, from the Nobis lab at Vanderbilt University Medical Center. Stress is one of the most commonly reported seizure precipitants in epilepsy, and patients with intractable epilepsies carry an outsized burden of psychiatric comorbidity, but the circuit interaction between chronic stress and the epileptic brain has been poorly characterized. Dravet syndrome — a severe pediatric epilepsy driven by haploinsufficiency in the sodium channel SCN-one-A — is a useful model because the genetic lesion is defined and because patients have well-documented anxiety and behavioral comorbidities. The group put Dravet model mice and wild-type littermates through chronic restraint stress and read out the corticotropin-releasing factor neurons of the dorsal bed nucleus of the stria terminalis, a stress-responsive forebrain node implicated in epilepsy. Stressed Dravet mice show elevated corticosterone, increased mortality in females, and increased anxiety-like behavior. Ex-vivo slice electrophysiology shows that chronic stress increases spontaneous excitatory event frequency onto corticotropin-releasing factor neurons in both genotypes, but selectively increases the amplitude of both spontaneous excitatory and inhibitory currents in Dravet mice. Evoked recordings reveal a genotype-specific stress effect on glutamatergic transmission in this circuit. The general implication is that the bed nucleus of the stria terminalis corticotropin-releasing factor circuit is a maladaptive node where chronic stress and epileptic genotype interact to amplify both seizure vulnerability and psychiatric comorbidity. For pediatric epilepsy pharmacology, where the comorbidity load drives much of the quality-of-life burden and is typically treated as a separate problem, this argues for thinking about the same circuit.

Seventh, a human GBA-L-four-four-four-P transgene drives early and persistent dopamine neurotransmission deficits and alpha-synuclein pathology in a mouse model of early Parkinson's disease, from the Wade-Martins group at the Oxford Parkinson's Disease Centre with the Aerts group at Leiden. Heterozygous mutations in the GBA-one gene — which encodes the lysosomal enzyme glucocerebrosidase — are the most common genetic risk factor for Parkinson's disease, and they are unusual mechanistically because they look like they cause disease through both loss-of-function and gain-of-function effects. Disentangling those two has been hard because existing mouse models use the mouse gene. The group used bacterial-artificial-chromosome recombineering with site-specific integration to incorporate the entire human GBA-one locus — endogenous promoter, all exons and introns, and flanking regions — as a transgene, and crossed it onto a heterozygous mouse Gba background. By eighteen months, the L-four-four-four-P mice show reduced glucocerebrosidase activity and accumulate a high-molecular-weight enzyme form consistent with endoplasmic-reticulum retention and aberrant glycosylation. They also show an early and persistent reduction in dorsal striatal dopamine release on fast-scan cyclic voltammetry, with no dopaminergic cell loss and no defects in dopamine synthesis or reuptake — a release-machinery deficit, not a cell-count deficit. Both L-four-four-four-P transgenic mice and the simple heterozygous knockout accumulate oligomeric alpha-synuclein, but only L-four-four-four-P mice develop behavioral changes. The clean dissection the model enables is that dopamine release deficits and behavioral readouts arise from a gain-of-function mechanism, whereas alpha-synuclein accumulation arises from glucocerebrosidase loss-of-function. For glucocerebrosidase-targeted Parkinson's therapeutics — chaperones for the mutant enzyme, substrate-reduction therapy, and gene therapy — that matters, because the same intervention will not address both arms of the disease equally.

Eighth, an artificial-intelligence-agent-orchestrated grey-box Transformer framework for sparse pharmacokinetic curve reconstruction and pharmacometric model initialization, from the Liu lab at Peking University Third Hospital. Clinical pharmacokinetic modeling has a fundamental data problem — clinical samples are sparse, single-drug models do not generalize across compounds, and reaching a non-linear-mixed-effects model that supports population-pharmacokinetic decisions takes a skilled pharmacometrician days to weeks. The Pharmacokinetic Foundation Model — P-K-F-M — is a grey-box Transformer pre-trained across thirty-two drugs that ingests sparse concentration observations, dosing events, molecular descriptors, and physiological covariates, and reconstructs full concentration-time profiles while preserving an interpretable output structure. On representative oral pharmacokinetic curves, three sparse input points are enough to recover the principal absorption-and-elimination trajectory with coefficients of determination around zero-point-nine-nine for midazolam and verapamil. When reconstructed curves are then handed to the standard non-linear-mixed-effects software, both covariance stability and individual prediction accuracy improve. Contrastive-learning embeddings let them retrieve top-ten physiologically-based pharmacokinetic candidate models, with three-quarters of observations falling within the two-fold range of the chosen model. They also wrap a pharmacometrics-informed agent — the P-M Agent — around the foundation model, which outperforms general-purpose programming agents on a standardized pharmacometric modeling benchmark, with explicit human pharmacometrician sign-off at every step. For computational pharmacology specifically, this is the right shape of an agentic system — domain-pre-trained foundation model as a substrate for sparse-data inference, with a workflow agent on top that requires expert confirmation before downstream use rather than running open-loop.

Ninth, MolLingo, a molecule-native multi-agent system for large-language-model-powered scientific agents, on arXiv from a multi-institution collaboration. The agentic-molecular-design wave has so far split into two camps — single-shot generative models without external tools, and multi-agent frameworks that lack a chemically meaningful internal representation. MolLingo addresses the representation problem directly. They define a Literature Agent, a Chemist Agent, and an Orchestrator coordinating through shared memory, each with domain-specific tool access, and they introduce a synthesis-aware fragmentation scheme — BRICS-based Fragment Enumeration — that decomposes molecules into chemically meaningful building blocks expressed as block-level simplified molecular-input line-entry system strings paired with common chemical names. The point of the fragmentation is that it gives the language model a representation it can reason about at the level of substructures that a medicinal chemist would actually think about, rather than at the level of raw atoms. As an early-stage therapeutic-design case study they ground the Chemist Agent's reasoning in binding-site geometry and residue-level protein context from molecular docking, and report a fourfold docking-score improvement over the base frontier model despite using the same underlying language model, with consistent drug-property optimization gains. The architectural lesson for receptor-targeted drug discovery — the kind of work where a chemist's intuition about isosteres and fragment swaps is exactly what an agent needs to emulate — is that the agent's value comes from the chemically meaningful representation, not from the language model alone.

Tenth and closing, CaMBRAIN, real-time continuous electroencephalography inference with causal state-space models, on arXiv from a multi-institution collaboration. Electroencephalography signals are long — recording sessions span hours — and they have to be processed in real time for clinical applications like seizure detection, depth-of-anesthesia monitoring, and brain-computer interfaces. Current deep-learning electroencephalography models are nearly all attention-based and process in fixed-length sliding windows, which incurs quadratic cost as sequences lengthen and forces the model to lose global context at every window boundary. CaMBRAIN replaces the attention backbone with a causal Mamba-style state-space model — linear in sequence length, unidirectional in time, and explicitly designed for streaming. The training problem they had to solve is that important electroencephalography events — sharp spikes, seizure onsets — are subsecond, separated by minutes-long quiet intervals, and standard self-supervised reconstruction objectives do not train the hidden state to carry the long-range memory that streaming inference needs. They build a multi-stage self-supervised training pipeline aimed at long-range memory retention, hit state-of-the-art on three electroencephalography benchmarks with more than ten-fold higher throughput than existing models, and produce the first model that runs long-horizon continuous inference on variable-length electroencephalography. For the computational-neuropsychopharmacology audience, the relevance is that the next wave of clinical electroencephalography use — antiepileptic dose titration, anesthesia depth control, closed-loop neuromodulation — needs models that can keep running on a single device for hours, and a linear-time streaming backbone is the architecture that makes that practical.

That is the docket for May twenty-eighth. The week ends on a chemogenetic platform that gives us a clean way to interrogate endogenous G-protein-coupled receptors, a six-hundred-million-year-old tyrosine that explains a slate of inhibitory-channel disease mutations, and a pair of agentic systems — one for pharmacokinetics and one for molecular design — that argue the value of these architectures is in the domain-specific representation underneath, not in the language model on top.
