## Script

Welcome to Receptor and Reason for Tuesday, June sixteenth, 2026. Eleven items today, with a strong neuropsychopharmacology pull from bioRxiv — the psychedelic-induced plasticity literature finally getting a direct mechanistic test, an interestingly contrarian finding on neurexin pharmacogenomics involving an FDA-approved antipsychotic, two reward-pharmacology preprints on opposite sides of the addiction-versus-anhedonia axis, a microglial upstream-of-MMP story that fills in a long-missing piece of stress neuroinflammation, and a developmental two-hit model that pins E/I imbalance to specific glutamate and endocannabinoid changes. Then cryo-EM pharmacology that should make people doing GPCR ligand screening sit up, an Alzheimer mechanism that connects tau cooperativity to specific motor proteins and lysosome transport, an ALS surprise about oligodendrocytes, and we close with two computational items — a BBB-permeability model that just edged the state of the art, and a rare-disease diagnostic agent that gets there with one reasoning model and a few public tools rather than another massive ensemble.

The opener is a serotonergic-psychedelic paper on bioRxiv that does something the field has needed for years — a direct mechanistic test of the relaxed-priors hypothesis, in both humans and mice, in the post-acute window after a single dose. The prevailing theoretical frame, sometimes called REBUS, says psychedelics persistently relax top-down predictive signals so that the brain reassesses sensory and emotional content with less prior bias — and that this is what underwrites therapeutic effects lasting days to weeks. The new work tests the prediction directly. In humans, the group recruited people who had taken psilocybin, LSD, or 5-MeO-DMT within the past three weeks, and put them through a saccadic prediction task with EEG. Recent users produced fewer fast saccades and less suppression of EEG delta and theta power to predictively presented stimuli — which is what you would expect if the predictive-coding circuit is loosened. The effect correlated with time since dosing and replicated across two cohorts taking different agonists. In mice, a single one-milligram-per-kilogram dose of psilocybin produced the same loss of predictive suppression in primary visual cortex twenty-four hours later, and this coincided with weakened top-down modulation of V1 from anterior cingulate, plus visible spine growth on the anterior cingulate neurons that project to V1. That last bit is the key — the relaxation of top-down predictive influence is not a global pharmacological aftereffect, it's the consequence of plastic remodeling of feedback circuits in a specific direction. For therapeutic development, this nails down what the post-acute window is doing structurally, and it gives a clean assay — predictive suppression on EEG — that can be ported to the next round of psychedelic trials as a biomarker of mechanistic engagement.

That gives us the post-acute serotonergic story, and the second item gives us an interestingly orthogonal pharmacogenomic one — also on bioRxiv. The team built a behavioral screen in C. elegans for neurexin loss-of-function phenotypes — NRXN1 is the synaptic adhesion molecule associated with autism, schizophrenia, Tourette syndrome, and seizures, and produces over a hundred isoforms — and screened a hundred-and-ninety-compound library for rescue of activity deficits in two isoform-specific deletion alleles. The finding is that monoamine-targeting compounds rescue, but they do so allele-specifically — different neurexin disruptions respond to different drugs. Out of the entire screen, only one compound rescued both alleles with strong effect: olanzapine, the atypical antipsychotic. The team then showed olanzapine also rescues a worm carrying the conserved human autism-associated L18Q missense variant, rescues activity deficits and extends survival in Drosophila neurexin mutants, and outperforms asenapine — another second-generation antipsychotic — on social-feeding behavior even though both improved activity. The take is twofold. First, monoamine modulation is a conserved compensatory axis for neurexin loss across phyla, which is mechanistically interesting given that neurexin isn't itself thought of as a monoamine system component. Second, this is the strongest case I've seen for genuine pharmacogenomic stratification within the NRXN1-deficiency population — the same drug doesn't help every allele, but a specific drug already on the market may help a specific subset, and that's a much more tractable trial design than another fishing expedition.

Item three keeps us on synaptic remodeling, this time in addiction. Also from this morning on bioRxiv — KCNQ2/3, the M-current potassium channels, identified as the molecular brake on the synaptic pathology that drives cocaine seeking. The setup: in nucleus accumbens, D1-receptor-expressing medium spiny neurons become hyperactive after cocaine withdrawal, gain calcium-permeable AMPA receptors at their synapses, and form new dendritic spines — and these changes track cocaine-seeking behavior. What had been missing was the intermediate control variable. The new work shows that KCNQ2/3 deactivation is that variable. Cocaine-conditioned mice fourteen days into withdrawal had elevated spine density, increased surface calcium-permeable AMPARs, and elevated D1 medium-spiny-neuron firing. Repeated dosing with KCNQ2/3 openers — or D1-MSN-specific expression of a constitutively active KCNQ2 — reversed every one of those phenotypes and suppressed cocaine seeking. The positioning the authors propose is that KCNQ2/3 is a master regulator of drug-seeking synaptic remodeling, and channel activation in D1-MSNs is the relapse-prevention target. The translational interest is concrete: retigabine — a KCNQ opener — was an approved anticonvulsant before being withdrawn over skin and retinal pigmentation issues. Xenon's next-generation Kv7 opener azetukalner is well into late-stage trials for focal epilepsy with a much cleaner profile, and Biohaven's BHV-seven-thousand has been in epilepsy trials too. None of those programs have been positioned for addiction yet, but the molecular argument here is good enough that they probably should be.

Item four stays on reward pharmacology but pivots to the inverse problem — anhedonia and motivational deficits, where the question is how to reset glutamatergic tone. The paper is an mGluR-two-and-three antagonist preclinical, also on bioRxiv. The tool compound is LY341495, the workhorse selective mGluR-two-and-three antagonist that's been around for two decades but whose reward-domain pharmacology has stayed surprisingly thin. The team ran two complementary paradigms. In head-fixed temporal conditioning to a sucrose reward, systemic LY341495 dose-dependently reduced both anticipatory and consummatory licking, while preserving the temporal structure of reward anticipation — meaning the mice still knew the reward was coming, they just stopped working for it. In freely moving paradigms, the same drug reduced food intake and reduced social preference without impairing locomotion, gross orofacial motor function, or courtship ultrasonic vocalizations. The interest is the cross-domain effect — mGluR-two-and-three antagonism affects ingestive reward and social reward through what looks like a common motivational mechanism. The clinical relevance is that mGluR-two-and-three antagonists are sitting in development for treatment-resistant depression — including the prodrug TS-one-six-one — exactly on the rationale that they enhance motivation and reward seeking. This preclinical map gives those programs a more refined picture of which reward domains they're likely to move and which they aren't.

Item five connects stress to that same accumbens machinery from a neuroimmune angle. A bioRxiv preprint puts microglial CSF1R signaling upstream of the matrix metalloproteinases that we already know rewire accumbens synapses after stress. The lab had previously shown that stress-induced plasticity in nucleus accumbens core depends on MMP-two and MMP-nine activation and consequent extracellular-matrix remodeling — but what was upstream of the MMPs was unknown. The new work uses PLX3397 — the CSF1R inhibitor pexidartinib, FDA-approved for tenosynovial giant cell tumor — to pharmacologically engage microglia ahead of acute restraint stress in rats. CSF1R inhibition blunted the stress-induced increase in MMP-two-and-nine activity, blunted the increase in microglial CD68-associated phagocytic markers, and dampened the inflammatory transcriptional program — tumor necrosis factor alpha, cannabinoid receptor two, and MMP16 all came down in concert. The mechanistic takeaway is that stress recruits microglia via CSF1R as the upstream trigger for the MMP-driven ECM remodeling that underwrites accumbens synaptic plasticity. The reason that matters: it gives a peripherally accessible target for blunting stress-induced reward-circuit remodeling, and pexidartinib is in the clinic — repurposable in a way that experimental MMP inhibitors and microglia-ablating diets generally aren't.

Item six takes the developmental version of the same question and asks what happens when E-and-I balance is set wrong from the start. A two-hit rat model — maternal immune activation with LPS in late gestation, plus peripubertal unpredictable stress — and a careful neurochemical map of the adult dorsal hippocampus. The findings sit on the wall of psychiatric neurodevelopment: maternal immune activation alone raised the NMDA receptor GluN1 subunit, while peripubertal stress alone shifted the Grin2a-to-Grin2b mRNA ratio in the direction of incomplete NMDA subunit maturation. Combined, GABA levels and the GABA-A gamma-two subunit didn't compensate — the brain stays excitatory-shifted. The endocannabinoid layer was more nuanced. Peripubertal stress raised monoacylglycerol lipase — which degrades the endocannabinoid 2-AG — while maternal immune activation suppressed diacylglycerol lipase alpha, which synthesizes 2-AG, but only in the absence of subsequent stress. The two insults converge on reduced endocannabinoid tone, but they do it from opposite ends — synthesis versus degradation — depending on which hit is delivered. Taurine — which acts at extrasynaptic GABA-A receptors and is one of the most abundant free amino acids in the developing brain — was elevated only in the double-hit condition; glycine was elevated by maternal immune activation independent of stress. The reason this paper matters for drug development is that it gives the field two candidate molecular markers — taurine and glycine — for stratifying developmental versus stress-driven contributions to adult E-and-I imbalance, and it pushes the endocannabinoid system as a specific compensatory target in two-hit psychiatric models.

Item seven is the cryo-EM paper that should change how people think about screening ion channels. A bioRxiv structural-pharmacology piece on TRPM4 — the calcium-activated cation channel that controls a lot of immune, cardiac, and CNS excitability — combining cryo-EM of membrane-derived vesicles and detergent-solubilized channel, with the anthranilic anilide inhibitor PBA bound. The startling finding is that PBA occupies two entirely different pockets at eight degrees and at thirty-seven degrees Celsius. At low temperature, it sits in the previously described inhibitor pocket formed by S3, S4, the S4-S5 linker, and the TRP helix — that's the canonical TRP-channel ligand site. At physiological temperature, it relocates into a different site within the S1 through S4 voltage-sensing-domain bundle, proximal to the calcium regulatory region. Two pockets, same molecule, temperature-dependent which one is occupied. The methodological point is one the field needs to absorb: a lot of TRP-channel cryo-EM is done at four degrees in detergent, and you can get a structurally clean ligand bound in a pocket that is not actually where it binds at body temperature. The receptors-and-reason reading is that membrane-vesicle reconstitution preserves a native-like paralipid environment and reveals lipid binding patterns similar to detergent — so detergent-solubilized TRPM4 is structurally relevant for ligand work — but the temperature dependence of the binding site itself is something that classical SBDD pipelines will miss unless they collect at physiological temperature. For anyone running structure-based campaigns on TRP channels or related polymodal channels, this should change practice.

Item eight is the Alzheimer-tau mechanism piece, also on bioRxiv. Tau is a microtubule-associated protein that normally binds microtubules cooperatively — adjacent tau molecules favor binding next to each other, and this cooperativity organizes tau into cohesive envelopes along the microtubule lattice. These envelopes are increasingly understood to regulate access to the microtubule, including which motor proteins can run. The new work uses tau phosphomutants — both hyperphosphorylation-mimicking and phospho-resistant — in vitro and in live neurons, and asks what cooperativity does for trafficking. Hyperphosphorylated tau loses the envelope behavior — it distributes uniformly along the axon, dissociates faster from microtubules, and stops forming cohesive patches. Functionally, that translates into differential effects on the two main neuronal motor proteins. KIF5C — the workhorse anterograde kinesin — is only weakly inhibited by tau normally and becomes less inhibited under hyperphosphorylation. KIF1A — which carries dense-core vesicles and lysosomes — is strongly inhibited by tau, and hyperphosphorylation makes that inhibition stronger by reducing motor processivity and accelerating motor detachment. Net effect in neurons: lysosome transport is dysregulated, and phospho-resistant tau and hyperphosphorylated tau exert opposite control over lysosome motility — neither matches wild type. The connection to tauopathy is the right one: if the early failure in tau-driven neurodegeneration is the breakdown of organelle trafficking — specifically lysosome trafficking — then any disease-modifying therapy has to either preserve tau cooperativity or compensate for its loss at the motor-protein level. This is the kind of mechanism that gives you something concrete to assay in a screen.

Item nine is the ALS curveball — also bioRxiv. Reduced oligodendroglial support has been implicated in ALS progression for a while, but mechanistically what the oligodendrocytes were doing wrong was murky. The new work uses the SOD1-G37R mouse model of familial ALS and asks specifically whether the mutant superoxide dismutase in the oligodendrocytes themselves matters. Cre-mediated excision of mutant SOD1 in the oligodendrocyte lineage before myelin compaction slowed onset, improved motor performance, and prolonged survival. Silencing the same gene in oligodendrocytes after myelin compaction did not help. So the timing of oligodendrocyte SOD1 expression — before the myelin sheath closes around the axon — is what matters. Electron microscopy then showed why: mutant SOD1 aggregates inside paranodal loops and the inner periaxonal tongue — the myelinic nanochannels, the cytosolic compartments inside the myelin sheath that carry metabolites and motor-driven cargo to the underlying axon. To close the loop, in a separate model — SOD1-G93A — depleting CNP, a structural protein that keeps the myelinic channels open, accelerated disease and shortened survival. The mechanism the paper proposes is that mutant SOD1 aggregation collapses the myelinic transport channels that keep motor axons alive, and that this happens in a window during early myelin compaction that has been hard to access experimentally. The implication for ALS therapeutics is that interventions targeting only motor neurons are addressing one of two cell types that need fixing, and that the oligodendrocyte-targeted window is developmentally constrained. The broader argument — that any aggregation-prone protein expressed in oligodendrocytes might do the same thing through the same channels — extends naturally to other neurodegenerative disease contexts where myelin loss tracks disease progression.

Item ten is on computational pharmacology and a deceptively simple-looking benchmark win for blood-brain-barrier permeability prediction. The system is called TITAN-BBB, on bioRxiv. The architectural idea: instead of choosing between traditional chemical descriptors and deep-learning embeddings, fuse both — tabular molecular descriptors, image-based representations of the molecule, and text-based features from the SMILES — and combine them with attention. The group aggregated the largest BBB-permeability dataset published to date by pulling from multiple sources and standardizing, then trained for both binary classification — does it cross — and continuous regression — how much. Results: eighty-six-and-a-half percent balanced accuracy on classification, beating the previous best by three-point-one points, and a mean absolute error of zero-point-four-three-six on regression, twenty percent better than the prior state of the art. The model and weights are on Hugging Face, the source is on GitHub, and the dataset is also released. For CNS drug discovery campaigns running in-silico filters early — which is essentially all of them now — a three-point gain in balanced accuracy is real, and the openness of the release is the part that makes it adoptable. The interesting follow-on question is how the multi-modal fusion does on the harder problem — predicting passive versus active transport, which is what actually distinguishes useful CNS drugs from the ones that fail in vivo despite a clean in-vitro permeability number.

Closing on agentic AI applied to clinical reasoning, with LiteOdyssey on arXiv — a rare-disease diagnostic agent that takes the opposite design philosophy from what the field has been doing. Most medical AI improves by scaling — more fine-tuning data, more agents in the ensemble, larger retrieval databases. LiteOdyssey asks whether you can match state of the art with a single reasoning agent guided by a clinical-genetics policy and given dynamic access to a few standard biomedical tools — phenotype-ontology lookups, gene-disease databases. On LIRICAL plus the PhenoPacket Store benchmarks — together over twelve hundred cases, with around half ultra-rare diseases under one-in-a-million prevalence — LiteOdyssey achieved fifty-nine-point-three percent Recall-at-one overall. On the harder PhenoPacket subset specifically, it hit sixty-point-seven percent Recall-at-one versus ten-point-seven percent for the same base reasoning model without tools — a six-fold gain from a thin tool layer over a single agent. No fine-tuning, no ensemble, no large case-retrieval database. The reason this matters for the field is twofold. First, it's a counterexample to the assumption that competitive medical reasoning requires custom-built infrastructure — for the rare-disease deployment context, where audit, transparency, and physician review matter more than raw throughput, the lean approach is a better fit. Second, the policy-iteration-with-human-feedback approach to building the reasoning policy is something more groups should be doing — it's the agentic equivalent of clinical-trial protocol development, and it's a credible answer to the question of why our diagnostic agents seem to plateau when we ask them to act on subspecialty knowledge.
