## Script

Good morning, this is Receptor and Reason for Saturday, June 20th, 2026. Nine items today, weighted toward CNS structural pharmacology and agentic AI in drug discovery.

We are going to lead with what is probably the most consequential structural-pharmacology paper of the week, and it is about a transporter most of you have on a side note rather than a front burner: SLC6A15, also called B-zero-A-T-2. It is a brain amino-acid transporter linked to major depressive disorder by GWAS years ago, but its transport and inhibition mechanisms have been unresolved. A bioRxiv preprint out this week solves the cryo-EM structure in five states across the transport cycle — apo, with each of three substrates including proline, and complexed with two inhibitors, loratadine and tiagabine. The substrate result is the kind of finding you would expect: a single phenylalanine in the canonical S1 pocket, Phe-308, rotates and remodels the binding geometry to tune substrate selectivity. Fine. The inhibitor result is more interesting. Loratadine stabilizes an outward-occluded state by allosteric binding at the extracellular S2 pocket — that part is consistent with how loratadine engages other SLC6 members. But tiagabine, when it locks the transporter inward-open, does so cooperatively at three sites: S1 plus two previously unrecognized intracellular cavities, which the authors call S3 and S4. And here is the bit that should make every SLC6 drug discoverer pay attention — sequence and structural analysis indicates S3 and S4 are conserved across the entire SLC6 family. That means SERT, DAT, NET, the GABA transporters, the glycine transporters — they all share intracellular vestibular pockets that nobody has been deliberately drugging, because we did not know they were there. State-selective intracellular modulators for monoamine transporters have been the white whale of SSRI, SNRI, and DAT-blocker design for years; this paper hands the field a structural target list. The honest caveat is that all the structural evidence is in B-zero-A-T-2 itself, and conservation across SLC6 is inferred from sequence and geometry — whether you can actually fit a usefully selective ligand into the S3 or S4 of SERT specifically is the next experiment.

Staying on SLC6 and going from the structure of one family member to the mechanism of the most-prescribed target in the family: a related preprint, updated this week by the Forrest lab at NINDS, finally settles a decades-old question about how chloride helps SERT do its job. The textbook stoichiometry for SERT has been one serotonin, one sodium, one chloride moving in together, one potassium moving out, with chloride understood to be co-transported. Electrophysiological work in recent years had cast doubt on whether chloride actually crosses. The new work combines transport in reconstituted proteoliposomes, conformational accessibility assays on cysteine-substituted residues in the cytoplasmic and extracellular pathways, and molecular dynamics, and the answer is: chloride is not transported. What chloride does is bind to a site that enhances sodium affinity and stabilizes the conserved arginine-glutamate salt bridge that closes the extracellular gate. So the requirement for extracellular chloride that everyone has been teaching for forty years is a conformational requirement, not a co-transport requirement. The practical implication for drug discovery is modest in the short term — SSRIs still bind where they bind — but it does mean the chloride site itself becomes interesting as a state-tuning allosteric handle for SERT specifically, and it forces us to update our reasoning about anion dependence across the rest of the neurotransmitter:sodium symporter family, which now bifurcates between true chloride-symporting amino-acid transporters like glycine and GABA transporters, and the monoamine transporters where chloride is an architectural cofactor only.

Third item, switching from transporter pharmacology to amyloid drug discovery. A bioengineering group has put out AmyBind, which is a computational pipeline for de novo design of mini-protein binders targeting polymorph-specific structural features of amyloid fibrils. The proof of concept is alpha-synuclein. They designed two mini-protein binders against alpha-synuclein fibrils, one of which shows the kind of polymorph specificity that you would want for differential diagnosis of Parkinson's versus multiple system atrophy, where the underlying fibril strains differ structurally. Two important caveats. First, the binders engage the lateral fibril surface, not the growing fibril end, so neither one inhibits fibril elongation in vitro. And second, in cell-based propagation assays, they do not reduce fibril uptake or seeding either. What that means is, as designed, these are imaging and diagnostic agents, not therapeutic ones — polymorph-selective labels rather than disaggregators or anti-seeding therapeutics. That said, structure-guided polymorph specificity is genuinely hard to achieve, and the framework would transfer to tau strains in Alzheimer's-versus-CTE differentiation, or to TDP-43 strains in ALS subtypes. File this under "infrastructure for the next generation of strain-specific neurodegeneration drugs," with the diagnostic application available sooner than the therapeutic one.

While we are on neurodegeneration with an in-silico-led drug-discovery angle, item four is an ALS preprint this week that takes a different computational approach: classical virtual screening, pointed at an unusually clever target choice. TDP-43's mislocalization to mitochondria is one of the major proteinopathy mechanisms in ALS, and it depends on a six-residue mitochondrial localization sequence called M1, which sits buried inside TDP-43's N-terminal domain and is normally solvent-inaccessible. The authors use equilibrium all-atom molecular dynamics to show that M1 is in fact structurally labile — specifically the hydrogen-bonded bridge between two beta strands, beta-4 and beta-5, breaks early in unfolding, making M1 transiently exposed and available for the mitochondrial import machinery. Then they virtually screened both the FDA-approved subset of the ZINC15 database, about 2,100 molecules, and a much larger 515,000-molecule non-approved library, against this exposed M1 conformation. They identified a hit that stays bound to M1 across the molecular dynamics trajectories with favorable free energy — and the neat part is that this molecule was previously characterized as a MAP-kinase-activating protein kinase 2 inhibitor, MAPKAPK2. So you have a drug-repurposing candidate whose existing pharmacology might be either a feature, because MAPKAPK2 inhibition is anti-inflammatory in microglia and would synergize with reducing TDP-43 mitochondrial damage, or a confound. As always with virtual-screen-only hits, this is an MD-anchored binding model awaiting biophysical and cellular validation. But it is a clean illustration of two undervalued ideas combined: a transiently exposed disordered motif as a druggable interface, and repurposing as a way to enter a hard target class.

Fifth, staying with neurodegeneration but pivoting to Huntington's disease and mitochondrial biology. A preprint this week identifies the mitochondrial cytochrome CYP27A1 as the missing link between vitamin D signaling and mitochondrial dysfunction in HD models. The setup: HD patient-derived cells and the 3-nitropropionic acid rat model of HD both show reduced CYP27A1 expression. Vitamin D supplementation restores CYP27A1, improves mitochondrial fusion, rescues complex II function, and reverses cognitive and motor deficits. The mechanistic confirmation is that CYP27A1 overexpression alone is sufficient to recapitulate the protective effect — so it is not just a vitamin D pleiotropy through some other downstream gene. Mechanistically, vitamin D acts via VDR-dependent transcription of CYP27A1 and a handful of other mitochondrial biogenesis genes, creating a feedback rheostat. The implication for HD drug discovery is that you now have a small-molecule-tractable target — CYP27A1 enzymatic activity or its transcriptional activation — that converges with the long-standing complex II dysfunction story in HD. The big caveat, and it is a real one, is that 3-NP is a metabolic toxin model, not a CAG-expansion model. The next obvious validation is YAC128 or zQ175 knock-in mice, where the HD pathophysiology has the right etiology.

OK, switching gears entirely to agentic AI in biology. Item six is Orion, a new computer-using AI agent for biomedical image analysis that came out as a preprint this week. The build is an LLM combined with terminal execution and full graphical interface control in a shared computing environment, which means it can — instead of calling APIs — actually drive desktop scientific software via point-and-click and menus, the way a human would. The benchmarks: over 90 percent accuracy on biomedical database and literature retrieval tasks, learned to operate both CellProfiler and QuPath for cellular and tissue image quantification well enough to produce usable outputs, without bespoke integration code. The most interesting demo is a 100-hour autonomous run on a large-scale perturbation imaging dataset, during which Orion produced 52 research reports, of which human scientist review flagged 22 as plausible mechanistic hypotheses. The reason this matters for our audience: nearly every CNS drug-discovery program runs perturbation imaging at some scale — phenotypic screens in iPSC neurons or organoids especially — and the analysis bottleneck is real. An agent that can drive CellProfiler well enough to produce auditable, human-reviewable hypotheses from these screens is the right shape of automation for that part of the pipeline. The caveat is the obvious one: 22 plausible out of 52 reports is a 42 percent signal rate, and human review remains essential — but for an exploratory hypothesis-generation mode, that is a useful funnel.

Item seven, another AI-for-drug-design preprint, this one targeted at a much more specific molecule class: PROTACs. PROTAC design is a hard generative problem because you are trying to bridge two protein-binder warheads with a linker of the right length and chemistry to enable a productive ternary complex with an E3 ligase. The new framework, DesignMaster, is a diffusion model on top of an E-three-equivariant graph transformer, and the key technical idea is what they call gated multi-condition fusion: they inject linker length and physicochemical property constraints throughout the diffusion process rather than just at the end, which is how earlier methods generated linkers with unrealistic logP or rotatable bond counts. On the PROTAC-DB benchmarks they report a 34 percent improvement in recovery over previous generative methods, and on one published case they report a 52 percent reduction in linker geometry RMSD against the crystallographic ternary complex. For our audience, PROTACs are not yet a big CNS modality because of brain-penetration challenges — molecular weight is the obvious one. But if linker design becomes routine the way scaffold hopping is now, you change what becomes possible for targets like tau and TDP-43 that have been stuck on classical small-molecule approaches.

Item eight, addiction genetics with a parent-of-origin effect that is worth flagging. Researchers compared oxycodone self-administration in two near-isogenic rat substrains — Wistar Kyoto more-immobile and less-immobile, which differ in depression-like behavior and substance-use vulnerability — and importantly, in their reciprocal female F1 crosses. The escalation result is striking: F1 females sired by more-immobile fathers escalated oxycodone intake faster when sessions extended from one hour to four hours, and consumed more total oxycodone across the expanded-access stages, than females from the reciprocal cross. The lick microstructure also indicates that the high-vulnerability females are driven less by drug subjective value and more by cue-directed seeking during drug-unavailable timeout periods. The big claim is a parent-of-origin effect on female oxycodone vulnerability — which, if it replicates in mice or in human pedigree data, is a methodological lever for finding the imprinted or epigenetically inherited loci that mediate addiction risk. Treat this as a hypothesis-generating finding from one F1 generation, but the design is clean and the effect is large.

Closing item, an OCD model paper that is more about neural dynamics than about drug discovery, but worth tracking because of the underlying theoretical claim. The model is chronic quinpirole — that is a D2/D3 dopamine receptor agonist used as a tool compound for stereotypy induction in rats, which is the standard non-pharmacogenic rodent model of OCD. The theoretical hypothesis being tested is that OCD behavioral symptoms correspond to neural network attractor dynamics becoming too stable and insufficiently flexible. Recording from hippocampus and anterior cingulate, the authors report that anterior cingulate firing rate increases gradually within a session, paralleling the within-session escalation of behavioral stereotypy. In the hippocampus, what increases is the temporal stability of firing — not the spatial organization of place fields — and it is linked specifically to theta-rhythm modulation. So both behavior and at least one neural signature track with the attractor-stability prediction. As mechanism this is preliminary; as a methodological template for testing computational psychiatry hypotheses against concrete neural recordings, it is the right shape.
