Welcome back to Receptor and Reason. Ten items today, all bioRxiv. Worth flagging up front — arXiv was rate-limiting on my IP this morning, so the agentic-AI side of the show is thinner than usual; the bioRxiv corpus filled the rest. Psychiatric Times had no fresh items in the window either. Let's go.

Lead item is from a very large consortium effort with senior author Oliver Howes — Reproducible Human Reward Imaging Phenotypes Exhibit Differential Sensitivity to Dopamine D2 Receptor Antagonism. The headline number is the sample — over twelve hundred people across five cohorts, including the IMAGEN imaging consortium and an Apulian psychosis sample. Using reward-anticipation fMRI tasks, they identify two stable phenotypes that mirror the sign-tracker, goal-tracker distinction that's been doing real work in the rodent incentive-salience literature for fifteen years. Sign-tracker-like individuals have dominant ventral-striatal responses to reward-predictive cues; goal-tracker-like individuals respond more at outcome. The phenotype replicates across sites. Now the pharmacology — single-dose and repeated-dose D2/D3 antagonism with risperidone, haloperidol, and amisulpride selectively knocks down the anticipatory ventral-striatal signal in the sign-tracker phenotype, and single-dose antagonism also produces a parallel drop in self-reported energy in those individuals. In contrast, aripiprazole, a D2/D3 partial agonist, raises the anticipatory response and reduces the outcome-phase response in goal-trackers — essentially shifting them toward the sign-tracker pattern. And in the psychosis cohort, antipsychotic D2 affinity tracks with blunted anticipatory signaling and higher negative-symptom burden. Why this matters — the field has been talking about stratifying antipsychotic response by imaging for a long time without much that replicated. A reproducible cue-versus-outcome axis, with a clean dose-response to drugs whose pharmacology we already understand, is the closest thing to a usable stratification biomarker that has come out of reward imaging in years. The obvious next test is prospective — randomize psychosis patients by sign- versus goal-tracker phenotype and see if response prediction holds.

Staying with receptor pharmacology and moving up to the network scale — Role of GABA and NMDA Receptors in Shaping Cortical Timescales and Large-Scale Network Dynamics, out of Düsseldorf, with senior author Gerhard Jocham. They gave sixty healthy participants pharmacological manipulations of GABA-A and NMDA receptors and measured resting-state magnetoencephalography. The framing is the intrinsic-timescale literature — different cortical regions integrate information over different windows, from millisecond sensory in V1 to seconds in prefrontal, and the field has theorized for years that the excitation-inhibition balance is what sets the timescale gradient. This is the cleanest causal test I've seen of that hypothesis. Increasing GABAergic activity lengthened intrinsic timescales across cortex, with the strongest effects in the frontal default mode and dorsal attention networks. They also see a network-level consequence — the default mode network's occupancy probability went up, dorsal attention's went down. NMDA manipulation, somewhat surprisingly, did not produce significant timescale effects. That asymmetry is worth sitting with — much of the schizophrenia-as-NMDA-hypofunction literature assumes glutamatergic excitation sets timescales, and at least at the resting-state MEG level in healthy participants, this study says no, it's the inhibition side that's load-bearing.

Third item, on the pharmacology of psychosis models — Acute Inflammation-Mediated Attenuation of Behavioural Sensitization in Methamphetamine-Sensitized Mice via Distinct COX-2 and TNF-Alpha Pathways. The motivating observation is clinical — schizophrenia is broadly considered an inflammatory disorder, but there are scattered reports that acute infections coincide with transient improvement in positive symptoms. Most animal models use enormous inflammatory doses that crash the whole system, so the question of whether physiologically realistic inflammation modulates psychotomimetic responses has been under-tested. Here they pre-sensitized mice with repeated methamphetamine and then induced acute inflammation either peripherally with low-dose lipopolysaccharide, calibrated to match cytokine levels seen in human patients, or psychologically with two hours of restraint stress. Both suppressed the sensitization response, both depended on toll-like receptor 4, and the mechanisms diverged downstream — peripheral inflammation worked through cyclooxygenase-2, restraint stress through microglia-derived TNF-alpha. Restraint stress also lowered striatal extracellular dopamine; peripheral inflammation did not. This is a nice mechanistic frame for an old clinical puzzle, and the fact that the two routes converge behaviorally but diverge molecularly is the kind of finding that should inform how anti-inflammatory adjuncts get tested in psychosis — COX-2 inhibitors and TNF modulators may not be interchangeable.

Item four, dopaminergic-glutamatergic crosstalk in early Alzheimer's. Out of Concepción, Chile — Selective Impairment of Long-Term Depression in Accumbal D1R+ MSNs Involves Calcium-Permeable AMPA Receptors in Early Alzheimer's Disease. Why this matters for a neuropsych audience — the neuropsychiatric symptoms of AD, the agitation, the reward dysregulation, the disinhibition, precede frank cognitive decline by years, and the mesolimbic circuitry is increasingly implicated. They use a double-transgenic APP/PS1 mouse with tdTomato in dopamine D1-receptor-positive medium spiny neurons of the nucleus accumbens, and they show that long-term depression is selectively impaired in D1-positive MSNs but not D2-positive ones, despite comparable amyloid-beta accumulation in both. The mechanism is shifted AMPA receptor composition — calcium-permeable AMPARs, the GluA2-lacking flavor, are doing more of the work, and the mGluR1/5-dependent LTD pathway that normally regulates AMPAR trafficking is broken. Block the calcium-permeable AMPARs pharmacologically and LTD is rescued. Behaviorally, the mice show increased palatable-reward consumption and altered context preference while associative learning is preserved — a phenotype that maps remarkably well onto early AD neuropsychiatric symptoms. The translational angle here is that calcium-permeable AMPAR blockers are a real pharmacological class, and this study makes a case that the mesolimbic neuropsychiatric phenotype of early AD might be a more tractable drug target than memory deficits.

Item five — Presynaptic GABA-A Receptors Mediate Ethanol-Induced Suppression at a Central Auditory Synapse. Out of Leipzig. The clinical observation everyone has lived through is that alcohol impairs speech perception in noise, but the cellular mechanism has been hand-waved at the receptor level. Here they record at the endbulb of Held — the first central auditory synapse in the cochlear nucleus — in Mongolian gerbils. Low-dose ethanol suppresses sound-evoked firing in vivo, and the suppression is blocked by GABA-A antagonists. Presynaptic patch-clamp shows ethanol potentiates GABA-A currents specifically at the presynaptic terminal, not at the postsynaptic bushy cell. The mechanism is a high intra-terminal chloride concentration that flips GABA-A from inhibitory to depolarizing; the depolarization activates low-threshold Kv1 potassium channels and shunts the presynaptic action potential, which limits calcium influx and glutamate release. So ethanol-induced auditory suppression is a presynaptic shunting phenomenon at a very specific synapse, not a generic CNS depression effect. Two takeaways — first, GABA-A heterogeneity matters at the receptor-subunit level for predicting drug effects, even for a drug as nominally non-selective as ethanol. Second, for anyone modeling alcohol-related cognitive effects with GABA-A potentiators as analogs, the locus matters; the same molecular effect produces different consequences depending on chloride gradient.

Item six and seven are a deep-brain-stimulation pair. Item six — Conserved Fiber Topography of the Anterior Limb of the Internal Capsule in Treatment-Resistant Psychiatric Patients. From Sameer Sheth's group at Baylor. The anterior limb of the internal capsule is the major white-matter highway from prefrontal cortex to thalamus, brainstem, and subthalamic nucleus, and it's the DBS target for treatment-resistant OCD and increasingly for depression. The clinical question is whether the well-characterized topographic organization seen in healthy individuals — basically a medial-to-lateral and dorsal-to-ventral mapping of prefrontal subregions through the capsule — is preserved in the treatment-resistant population, or whether disease pathology has reorganized it. Across eighteen OCD and five depression patients, using diffusion tractography and, for the depression patients, cerebro-cerebral evoked potentials elicited by stimulating the DBS lead, they show the topography is preserved. Ventral ALIC connects to medial ventral prefrontal cortex; dorsal ALIC connects to lateral ventral prefrontal cortex, and this evoked-potential mapping is left-lateralized. Why this matters for the field — DBS programming has historically been a black box of contact-by-contact stimulation parameter searches. If the topography is preserved, individualized tractography can rationalize lead placement and contact selection, and the response variability that's plagued ALIC DBS trials may be partly explainable by where each patient's prefrontal projections actually cross the capsule. This is the foundation for image-guided DBS for psychiatric indications.

Item seven, the second of the DBS pair — Para-Subthalamic Nucleus Adjoins Subthalamic Nucleus and Medial Forebrain Bundle, Major DBS Targets in Parkinson's Disease, OCD, and Depression. The subthalamic nucleus is the workhorse target for Parkinson's DBS and an emerging target for OCD; the superolateral branch of the medial forebrain bundle is a recent target for treatment-resistant depression. These three are clinically adjacent but the spatial-molecular relationships have been murky. This group, anchored by Åsa Wallén-Mackenzie in Uppsala, did fluorescent in situ hybridization across mouse, macaque, and human, plus light-sheet 3D reconstruction in whole mouse brains. The substantive finding is that the para-subthalamic nucleus, a small hypothalamic structure historically lumped or ignored, actually wraps around the subthalamic nucleus and is partly embedded in the medial forebrain bundle. So the three big DBS targets in this region are not anatomically isolated, and effective stimulation likely engages para-STN as a matter of geometry. They also show molecular heterogeneity — including a gradient of the serotonin 2C receptor across these structures, which is interesting because 5-HT2C tone modulates dopamine outflow from this region and is a target for several existing and developmental antipsychotics. So the para-STN may be partly explaining off-target effects of STN DBS, and a 5-HT2C-aware DBS-plus-pharmacology approach is now imaginable.

Item eight, jumping to the agentic-AI side — A Scalable Sign-Aware Multi-Omics Knowledge Graph Foundation Model for Mechanistic Drug Action and Clinical Response Predictions, from Lei Xie's group. The motivating problem — most biomedical knowledge graphs represent biology as unsigned associations. Drug A is connected to protein B, protein B is connected to pathway C, but the graph doesn't know whether those edges are activating or inhibitory. So when you train a graph neural network on top, the network learns proximity but loses mechanism. They build SIGMA-KG, a signed multi-omics atlas that explicitly encodes direction and polarity, integrating chemogenomic perturbation data well beyond the approved-drug set with transcriptomic, proteomic, and clinical data. On top of it they train FLASH, a lightweight signed heterogeneous graph neural network, as a foundation model. Pretrained, no fine-tuning, FLASH matches or beats nine baseline graph neural networks across mode-of-action prediction, drug-induced clinical response, and drug-drug interaction prediction. The translational claim is the strongest part — they used the model for inductive drug repurposing in four complex diseases, and report sixty-nine-point-six percent external clinical validation. That's a number I'd want to see independently audited, because the field has burned a lot of credibility on prospective validation rates that didn't replicate. But the architectural idea is right — preserving sign is necessary for mechanistic interpretability, and structural balance theory from social network analysis is a reasonable way to compose signs across multi-hop paths. This is worth pulling up if you're building anything that has to reason about drug mechanism rather than just associate drug with phenotype.

Item nine, computational pharmacology that's also moving into pre-clinical territory — De Novo Design of Miniprotein Agonists and Antagonists Targeting G Protein-Coupled Receptors, out of David Baker's group. GPCRs are roughly a third of all approved drug targets, but de novo design of protein binders for them has been hard because GPCRs are membrane-embedded and conformationally dynamic — they're a moving target inside a lipid bilayer, not a soluble pocket. This paper reports a workflow combining computational de novo design with a high-throughput cell-based microscopy screen they call receptor diversion. The outputs are real — miniprotein agonists for MRGPRX1, NK1R, and CCR5; antagonists for CXCR4, CCR5, oxytocin receptor, GLP-1R, GIP-R, glucagon receptor, PTH-1R, and CGRP receptor. They confirm atomic-level agreement between designed and experimentally determined structures with cryo-electron microscopy for several. For a neuropsychopharmacology audience the specific receptors here aren't all CNS, but the proof-of-concept is what matters — designed protein agonists and antagonists for GPCRs are now within reach, and the obvious extension is to neuropsychiatric targets. Designed selective biased agonists at 5-HT2A, NK1R for depression, oxytocin receptor for autism social phenotypes, are all plausible next steps. The combined affinity, selectivity, and conformational selectivity that miniproteins can achieve is something small molecules have been struggling to deliver at hard GPCR targets for decades.

Closing on the most clinically suggestive item of the week. BCG Vaccination Mitigates Tau Pathology and Restores Cognitive Function in PS19 Mice, from William Bishai's group at Johns Hopkins. The motivating epidemiology — retrospective studies in bladder cancer patients, who routinely receive BCG as a bladder instillation, report dramatic reductions in subsequent Alzheimer's incidence, with estimates ranging from twelve to seventy-eight percent depending on the study. That's a large effect for a vaccine that's been on the WHO essential medicines list for decades. The question has been whether the association reflects real CNS benefit or selection bias. Here they administer BCG to PS19 tauopathy mice and run the full readout. Hippocampal phospho-tau drops. Microgliosis drops, with selective reduction of activated resident microglia. T2-MRI shows attenuated brain atrophy and glutamate-weighted CEST-MRI signals rise. Novel-object-recognition performance improves. Body weight and survival are preserved. Transcriptomic profiling of the hippocampus shows near-complete normalization of the disease-associated gene-expression signature back to wild-type. The mechanistic story they're building is myeloid reprogramming — peripheral myeloid cells recruited to the brain after BCG show enhanced CD80 expression and enhanced phagocytosis of tau fibrils in primary brain myeloid cell assays, plus increased lactate production consistent with a different metabolic state. This is the kind of finding that, if it holds up in a second model and ideally in a prospective human trial, reframes the prevention conversation for AD around an inexpensive, globally available vaccine. Worth tracking. That's it for today — back tomorrow.
