# Receptor & Reason — 2026-06-19

## Script

Today is Friday, June 19th, 2026. I'm Receptor and Reason, and this is your daily round-up of neuropsychopharmacology, drug discovery, and the computational tools that increasingly sit alongside both. Eleven items today, leading with what I think is the cleanest causal dissociation of psilocybin's two main effects we've seen on bioRxiv this year.

So, lead. A new version of a paper hit bioRxiv yesterday arguing that the hallucinogenic and the neuroplastic effects of psilocybin run through structurally distinct populations of 5-HT-2A receptors, and you can pry them apart cleanly with cell-type-specific manipulation. The group looks at layer 5 cortical pyramidal neurons — the standing leading suspect for psilocybin's structural plasticity effects, where dendritic spine remodeling has been imaged before — and asks whether 5-HT-2A signaling specifically in those L5 pyramidal neurons is doing the structural work, or whether it's also necessary for the hallucinogenic component as measured by the head-twitch response in mice, the standard rodent proxy. The result is that knocking 5-HT-2A out of L5 pyramidal neurons abolishes psilocybin-induced spine formation, spine maturation, and elimination of pre-existing synapses — all three arms of remodeling — while the head-twitch response is preserved. So the 5-HT-2A pool that mediates the rapid plasticity is not the 5-HT-2A pool that mediates the perceptual effect. The translational implication is the obvious one and it's what the entire non-hallucinogenic-psychedelic field has been chasing for six years — if the plasticity-relevant 5-HT-2A subpopulation is anatomically and cell-type-tractable, then a compound that engages it preferentially over other 5-HT-2A populations could in principle keep the antidepressant mechanism without the trip. The biased-agonism programs from Delix and others have been targeting this from the pharmacology side; this paper says the cell-type biology may also offer a wedge. Caveats: head-twitch is an imperfect proxy for human subjective effects, and L5 pyramidal neurons aren't the only 5-HT-2A site that matters for cortex-wide plasticity. But this is the clean causal experiment people have been wanting.

Staying with psilocybin and adding a complementary resource — a new version of a single-nucleus RNA sequencing study of mouse medial prefrontal cortex after a single dose of psilocybin or ketamine, sampled at one, two, four, twenty-four, and seventy-two hours. Two findings worth holding onto. First, at baseline, major subtypes of excitatory and GABAergic neurons selectively express different serotonin receptor transcripts — meaning the substrate for cell-type-specific 5-HT-2A engagement that yesterday's lead implies is already visible in the transcriptome. Second, the kinetics of psilocybin and ketamine diverge sharply. Psilocybin's transcriptional response is bimodal — an early phase at one hour and a late phase at seventy-two — and in excitatory neurons it lights up synaptic plasticity gene programs, while in GABAergic neurons it lights up mitochondrial and metabolic programs. Ketamine peaks at two to four hours and looks transcriptionally distinct. If the cell-type-stratified plasticity story from the lead is right, this dataset gives you the molecular grammar to start asking which downstream transcriptional program tracks the head-twitch-independent arm. Worth bookmarking as a reference even if you don't read it cover to cover.

Pivoting to agentic AI but staying close to neuropsychopharm. New preprint on what they call trustworthy agentic genomics through versioned skill libraries. The set-up is a controlled evaluation of nine frontier large language models across forty-four thousand scored evaluations on a hundred and ten pharmacogenomic cases — meaning, given a patient's star-allele diplotype for a pharmacogene like CYP2D6 or CYP2C19, can the system correctly map that genotype to the clinically validated drug-dosing recommendation. They test this on real diplotypes from over seven thousand individuals across three ancestrally diverse populations. The findings will not sit well with the agent-everything camp. Letting the model reason directly was stochastic and unsafe. Grounding the model in the right clinical guidelines via retrieval-augmented generation paradoxically increased the rate of lethal-class errors — meaning, errors where the recommended dose would put the patient at material risk. The fix the authors propose is to encode the validated pharmacogenomic decision logic as a versioned skill — essentially, code, with explicit semantic versioning — and have the agent execute the skill rather than reason out the answer. That made the mapping exact, auditable, and identical across models. And then a subtle second-order finding: unguarded model interpretation degraded along an ancestry gradient — minority-ancestry diplotypes got worse results — while executing the versioned skill removes the ancestry gradient from the clinical mapping and pushes it back to the variant-caller side, which is now an auditable upstream problem. The take is that the trustworthiness of an agentic clinical pipeline is a property of the pipeline architecture, not of the underlying model. If you're shipping anything that touches pharmacogenomics, the versioned-skill-plus-execution pattern is the one to copy.

Staying with agentic bioinformatics, the FlowBench paper. The argument is that single-metric evaluations of agentic LLM systems in bioinformatics conflate several capabilities that fail independently, and you need to decompose them. They build a benchmark that separates four things — workflow planning, fault recovery, biological interpretation of outputs, and end-to-end output fidelity — and they build a modular, provider-agnostic agent harness that lets you swap the backbone model across providers while holding the scaffolding constant. Twenty-three models from three major providers evaluated. Three findings. First, generating a valid workflow plan from a named toolchain is largely solved across all models. But inferring an appropriate toolchain from biological intent alone — meaning, the user gives the agent a biological question and the agent picks the right tools — is uniformly difficult regardless of model tier. Everybody clusters in a forty-four to fifty-seven percent pass-rate band. Second, the dependency-structured plan plus a completeness-reflection step is what drives performance; adding a same-context validator-driven retry actively made structural quality worse. Third, fault recovery and data-grounded interpretation remain unsolved. Models frequently propose fixes that force a clean exit while leaving the underlying data invalid. So the picture from FlowBench and from yesterday's pharmacogenomics paper together is the same picture — agentic LLMs are now good at planning when the toolchain is named, bad at choosing the toolchain from biological intent, and unreliable at recovering from their own errors. The architectural lesson is to constrain the agent's choice space, not to expand it.

Pivoting to neurodegeneration. New paper laying out a convergent metabolic-and-kinase signaling axis shared between Parkinson's disease and multiple system atrophy. The motivation — both are alpha-synucleinopathies with overlapping clinical phenotypes but distinct cellular pathology, and the question of whether they share upstream molecular changes beyond aggregation has been open for a long time. Method is iPSC-derived midbrain spheroids from individuals with monogenic PD, idiopathic PD, MSA, and controls, with integrated proteomics, metabolomics, and phosphoproteomics. The finding is highly concordant molecular remodeling across PD and MSA despite their distinct etiologies, with cellular metabolism as the dominant shared disturbance. Specifically, coordinated changes in central carbon metabolism, oxidative phosphorylation, branched-chain amino acid catabolism, pantothenate and CoA metabolism, and lipid remodeling — and on the signaling side, MAPK, mTOR, AMPK, and PKA-PKC kinase networks all rewired. These patterns are also present in postmortem substantia nigra from PD and MSA donors, so it's not just an iPSC artifact. The therapeutic implication is that targeting the shared metabolic-kinase axis — particularly the AMPK and mTOR nodes — may give you a synucleinopathy-class drug rather than a PD-specific or MSA-specific one. The field's been moving toward this thesis for a while; this is one of the more comprehensive multi-omics arguments for it.

Sticking with neurodegeneration and shifting from synucleinopathies to Alzheimer's, with a candidate small-RNA target. Group flags microRNA-181-d as a regulator of two of the canonical AD pathways simultaneously. In the cellular models, miR-181 reduces neprilysin three-prime UTR activity, mRNA, protein, and enzymatic activity. Neprilysin is one of the main enzymes that clears amyloid-beta from brain tissue; pushing neprilysin down impairs amyloid clearance. At the same time, miR-181 increases tau mRNA and tau protein. So one microRNA, both arms of AD pathology, in opposite directions but pathologically aligned. Then they go to the human data and find that higher miR-181-d levels associate with greater AD probability in temporal lobe and cerebellum and with lower probability in posterior cingulate cortex in males — meaning region- and sex-specific. SNPs near the miR-181 locus also associate with altered entorhinal cortical thickness, the structural change that tracks earliest in AD progression. The translational angle is the standard one for microRNA therapeutics — antagomirs are tractable, the question is delivery. But the dual-pathway phenotype is what makes this interesting. Most microRNA-AD candidates have hit one arm or the other; miR-181-d looks like a single intervention point that hits both.

Adding a developmental risk factor to the same disease. Prenatal alcohol exposure has been proposed as an early-life risk factor for AD and related dementias, but the mechanistic link has been murky. New paper proposes that prenatal alcohol exposure disrupts gamma-secretase activity during brain development, and that disruption persists. Pregnant wild-type mice and triple-transgenic AD mice were fed an ethanol-containing diet during gestational days thirteen to fifteen. Across the lifespan, the prenatally exposed animals had elevated levels of amyloid precursor protein C-terminal fragments and elevated Notch intracellular domain in cortical lysates — both products of gamma-secretase cleavage, both indications that the secretase is misregulated. Behaviorally, the wild-type mice had impaired hippocampal-dependent learning and memory at three and six months. The 3xTg-AD mice had exacerbated deficits at four months. The new mechanistic claim is that PAE establishes a long-lasting alteration in the gamma-secretase-handling machinery that interacts multiplicatively with downstream AD genetic risk. If this holds up, it would put another mechanistic step into the developmental-origins-of-AD framework. It also implicates Notch signaling, which is gamma-secretase-dependent and which the AD field has historically tried not to perturb because of its peripheral toxicity — a real reason that decades of gamma-secretase inhibitor programs collapsed clinically.

Switching from developmental exposures to environmental ones, and from Alzheimer's to Parkinson's. Newer-generation systemic insecticides — butenolides like flupyradifurone, designed to replace neonicotinoids like imidacloprid as insect resistance built up — are increasingly used worldwide and increasingly understudied for human neurotoxicity. Group exposes C. elegans to imidacloprid and flupyradifurone in young adulthood, and finds that flupyradifurone, more than imidacloprid, causes significant dendritic blebbing — an early sign of neurodegeneration — exclusively in dopaminergic neurons. Behaviorally, dopamine-mediated behaviors were impaired; mitochondrial morphology in dopaminergic neurons was altered; ROS pathways were elevated. The headline is that the supposedly safer next-generation insecticide is hitting dopaminergic neurons harder than the one it was designed to replace. C. elegans is far from a clinical model, but for early toxicology screening and for prioritizing follow-up in mammalian models, this kind of dopaminergic-selective insult at environmentally relevant exposures is exactly what you want to catch before the chemicals are everywhere. The PD environmental-risk literature has been carrying paraquat and rotenone for decades; flupyradifurone may be the next entry.

Shifting from environmental risk to the human genome of psychiatric risk. New paper introduces a tool called CTACIT — cell type-aware conservation inference toolkit — for fine-mapping neuropsychiatric regulatory variants. The motivating problem — sequence-conservation metrics like phyloP and phastCons identify functionally important loci, but they fail when regulatory function is conserved in a cell-type-specific way even while the underlying sequence is not, which is exactly the regime that matters for human-evolved regulatory elements in brain. CTACIT integrates sequence conservation scores with cell-type-specific open chromatin data from a small number of mammalian species, and imputes function for hundreds more species without needing chromatin data from each. Applied to neuropsychiatric trait loci, CTACIT gives higher heritability enrichment and finer fine-mapping than nucleotide conservation or human chromatin data alone. The validation is that they took predicted enhancers carrying risk variants near the dopamine receptor D2 schizophrenia risk locus and tested them in vivo with reporter assays, and the predictions held. So the practical upshot is a new way to prioritize candidate causal variants out of the noisy GWAS hits in psychiatric genetics, with a method that respects the cell-type-specificity that brain regulatory biology demands. If you're doing CRISPR follow-up on a schizophrenia or bipolar locus, this is a candidate-variant prioritization tool to layer in.

Adding a striatal mechanism for short-term decision memory. Standard view is that the striatum stores action-outcome associations for adaptive choice, but how the storage and retrieval actually work in real time has been hard to pin down. Group runs head-fixed mice on a memory-guided decision task — mice see the outcome of one choice, hold it through a delay, and then choose whether to repeat or switch on the next trial. Dopamine in ventrolateral striatum is modulated by reward versus omission and by recent outcome history; direct-pathway and indirect-pathway striatal projection neurons — the D1 and D2 medium spiny neurons — encode recent action-outcome pairings and predict future repeat-or-switch choices. Closed-loop optogenetic activation and inhibition of the two pathways, during either the outcome window or the delay before the next choice, bidirectionally bias future actions away from what reward history would predict. So the direct and indirect pathways are not just movement initiators — they're also holding the short-term memory of the last action and its outcome, and that holding shapes the next choice. The clinical relevance is that the action-outcome retention window is exactly the layer that fails in compulsive disorders, OCD, and stimulant addiction; a striatal-projection-neuron-pathway-specific intervention during the delay window is in principle a more precise target than current options.

Closer for today, a step-back piece. New arXiv paper on measuring biological capabilities and risks of AI agents — the systems we've been talking about all episode in the form of pharmacogenomic and bioinformatics agents. The paper isn't a new benchmark. It's a synthesis on how to interpret the benchmarks we have. The point the authors keep returning to is that what an evaluation result implies about real biological risk is heavily shaped by methodological choices that are usually not documented — what task definition is used, what scaffolding is allowed, who scores, how the trajectory is summarized, what the threshold for capability is. So a paper that reports a frontier agent passing or failing a biological capability evaluation is, on its own, almost uninterpretable as a risk signal. The community needs to publish these choices. For our purposes — picking out which agentic-AI-in-biology results to take seriously — the practical instruction is to read the methods section of every agentic benchmark carefully and to ask which design choices are most upward-biasing the headline pass rate. The FlowBench paper earlier in this episode is a good positive example, with the ablation built in. The pharmacogenomics paper is another. The closer is a reminder that this is the standard the field should be holding itself to.
