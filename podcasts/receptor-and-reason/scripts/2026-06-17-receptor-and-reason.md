Welcome to Receptor and Reason for Wednesday, June seventeenth, 2026. Eleven items today across structural opioid pharmacology, depression imaging, AI-driven neuroimmune drug discovery, two complementary schizophrenia papers — one molecular-developmental, one synaptic-morphological — environmental neurotoxicology that lands on a calcium-handling mechanism, glymphatic transport under different anesthetic regimes, and a four-item computational tail covering ADMET prediction, agentic software optimization, agentic antimicrobial peptide design, and a theoretical learning paper that takes a step toward reconciling local plasticity with backpropagation. The bioRxiv pull was unusually rich today, so the tier-one neuropsychopharm content dominates, with a smaller AI cluster at the end.

The opener is the enhanced-sampling molecular-dynamics paper on the mu-opioid receptor, with buprenorphine bound. This is exactly the kind of paper that matters for opioid-use-disorder pharmacology and for the broader biased-agonism agenda, because it puts a thermodynamic framework around something the field has only been able to talk about qualitatively for years. The background is that biased signaling and partial agonism at G-protein-coupled receptors are the two big strategic levers for making opioid analgesics that retain G-protein-mediated efficacy without the beta-arrestin-mediated side effects — respiratory depression, tolerance, and dependence. Recent cryo-EM of MOR has shown clear structural differences between G-protein-bound and beta-arrestin-bound active states, but those structures are taken in the presence of effector. The new work asks what conformational landscape the receptor itself populates in the absence of effector, and how different ligands redistribute that landscape. The team ran extensive enhanced-sampling simulations of MOR bound to two ligands: buprenorphine, the FDA-approved partial agonist used for opioid-use-disorder treatment, and endomorphin-1, an endogenous full agonist that's been treated as unbiased. The finding is clean. Endomorphin-1 lets the receptor sample two distinct ensembles of active-like conformations, one compatible with G-protein recruitment and one compatible with beta-arrestin-1 recruitment. Buprenorphine restricts access to that second ensemble, holding the receptor in a narrower subset of active states that look G-protein-preferring. They also map allosteric pathways connecting the ligand binding pocket to the intracellular conformations. The take is that ligand efficacy and signaling preference at MOR emerge from selective redistribution of the receptor's conformational populations even without the effector protein attached — meaning you can in principle screen for bias from receptor-only simulations, before going to expensive functional cellular assays. For people designing the next generation of opioid agonists, this is the kind of grounding the field has been short on.

Item two stays on the GPCR-and-psychiatry axis but pivots from opioid to serotonin, and from molecular dynamics to whole-brain imaging. Recall yesterday's item that gave us a mechanistic test of the relaxed-priors hypothesis for psychedelics. Today's paper, also bioRxiv, comes at the post-acute psilocybin question from a different angle — comparing psilocybin head-to-head against the SSRI escitalopram in a double-blind randomized controlled trial for major depression, using pre- and post-treatment resting-state fMRI from the COMP360 PsiConnect cohort. The methodological novelty is the analysis layer. Instead of standard functional connectivity, the team builds whole-brain models of effective connectivity for each patient and then quantifies how far brain dynamics deviate from the fluctuation-dissipation theorem — which is the statistical-physics relation that holds for systems near equilibrium and breaks for systems driven away from it. The deviation magnitude gives you a one-number summary of how non-equilibrium the brain's hierarchical dynamics are. The headline result is opposite reconfigurations of this measure for the two treatments. Psilocybin and escitalopram move the brain off-equilibrium in opposite directions, with corresponding changes in segregation and asymmetry across the cortical hierarchy. Equally interesting is the baseline-prediction result: baseline FDT-deviation distinguished responders from non-responders within each treatment arm. That gives clinicians a candidate stratification biomarker — a pretreatment scan that may tell you which patient will respond to psilocybin versus to SSRI. This adds a structural-dynamics frame on top of yesterday's predictive-coding picture, and it strengthens the case that psilocybin and SSRIs are not interchangeable interventions that happen to share an endpoint — they engage different parts of the cortical dynamical landscape.

Item three is an AI-driven small-molecule drug-discovery story aimed at the neuroimmune compartment of Alzheimer's. About a week ago we covered an affinity-selection mass-spec discovery of the first reported small-molecule LILRB4 inhibitors — that's the inhibitory microglial receptor that suppresses amyloid clearance through ApoE-dependent signaling. Today, a different group reports a second, AI-driven campaign against the same target, with a different chemotype and substantially deeper biological characterization. They screened approximately five hundred million compounds in silico for binding to the LILRB4 interdomain pocket that mediates ApoE engagement, then validated the top hits with orthogonal biophysical assays, finding nanomolar binders that competitively block ApoE. Structural and mutational analyses confirmed the pocket. The biology then goes further than the prior paper. In human iPSC-derived microglia, LILRB4 inhibition suppresses SHP1 and SHP2 phosphatase signaling — that's the inhibitory branch — attenuates NF-kappa-B activation, and restores amyloid-beta uptake. The lead compound has clean pharmacokinetics and robust brain penetration. Oral dosing in the five-by-FAD mouse model improves cognitive performance, reduces amyloid burden, and dampens neuroinflammation. The reason this matters is that within ten days the field has gone from "is LILRB4 druggable" — yes, ASMS shows it is — to "here's a brain-penetrant AI-discovered lead with cognitive efficacy in a transgenic Alzheimer's model." Two completely independent chemical scaffolds against the same neuroimmune checkpoint, both functional, both via different discovery modalities. The Alzheimer's microglia-checkpoint thesis is rapidly becoming concrete.

Item four moves from microglial small molecules to the molecular biology of schizophrenia risk, with a developmental-dynamics paper on bioRxiv. The methodology is the interesting move here. Post-mortem brain gene-expression data is a snapshot at a moment in time. The group converts those snapshots into a lifetime series using a "liquidity" metric — a measure of how rapidly gene co-expression networks reorganize across time, computed across postmortem samples from multiple developmental stages. Normal prefrontal cortical development gets increasingly liquid through the first two postnatal decades, with sharp transitions at birth and adolescence. The schizophrenia finding is that neurotypical individuals — people who don't have the diagnosis, but who carry elevated polygenic risk — show a generalized delay in this developmental liquidity, with the strongest convergence on GABA-A receptor functional maturation. Specifically, the high-polygenic-risk neurotypicals show an elevated expression ratio between NKCC1 and KCC2 — the two chloride co-transporters that determine the equilibrium potential of chloride and therefore whether GABA-A is depolarizing or hyperpolarizing — relative to low-risk individuals. The same chloride-transporter imbalance is seen in patients with schizophrenia. So schizophrenia genetic risk is associated with delayed maturation of the perinatal GABA switch, and that signature is detectable in the brains of high-risk individuals who never developed clinical disease. Two implications worth noting. First, this is mechanistically coherent with the long-held excitation-inhibition-balance model of schizophrenia in a way that no transcriptomic snapshot has been before — it pins the abnormality to a specific developmental transition that happens in utero and just after birth. Second, the chloride-switch is a candidate target. Bumetanide, a low-affinity NKCC1 inhibitor, has been tested for autism and Fragile X with mixed results, but the rationale for testing it as a risk-stratified intervention in schizophrenia-high-risk youth has just been strengthened.

Item five is the morphological-synaptic complement to that genomic story. Same disorder, different layer — bioRxiv neuroscience again. The team did a population-level analysis of dendritic spine nanostructure in cultured neurons derived from multiple mouse models of neuropsychiatric disorders — schizophrenia and autism. The technique sorts individual spines into morphological subpopulations and asks whether disease models cluster by the distribution of those subpopulations. They do. Schizophrenia-associated models cluster together based on an enriched subpopulation of small-volume spines and reduced growth of nascent spines. Autism-spectrum models cluster together based on an enriched subpopulation of large-volume spines. So the textbook claim — schizophrenia has fewer or smaller spines, autism has more or larger spines — gets confirmed at the level of phenotype distribution, not just average size. The molecular pickup is Ecrg4, a gene encoding small secretory peptides. Ecrg4 is elevated in the schizophrenia-associated models, and knockdown rescues the small-spine phenotype and impaired spine dynamics. The functional confirmation makes this a candidate target, not just a marker. The combination with item four is the point. Item four says high-risk individuals carry a delayed GABA-A maturation signature; item five says schizophrenia mouse models converge on a reduced-spine-volume phenotype with Ecrg4 as a mechanistic driver. These two papers landed within four days of each other on bioRxiv from independent groups, and they're both telling you that schizophrenia is a developmental synaptic disorder — at the chloride-switch level developmentally, and at the spine-morphology level mechanistically — and they're pointing at intervention windows much earlier than current clinical practice acknowledges.

Item six takes us into environmental neurotoxicology with a mechanism that should interest anyone working on astrocyte calcium handling. Mancozeb is a manganese ethylene-bis-dithiocarbamate fungicide used at very high tonnage in agriculture worldwide. Concern about its neurotoxicity has been mostly Parkinson-linked and dopamine-system-focused. The new paper, on bioRxiv, asks what mancozeb does to astrocytes in vivo and in vitro. Chronic oral mancozeb at half a microgram per kilogram per day — well below the human acceptable daily intake — reduced GFAP expression in hippocampus and corpus callosum, indicating astrocyte atrophy. At thirty micrograms per kilogram per day, the acceptable daily intake, mancozeb produced hippocampal astrocyte atrophy and hyperlocomotor activity in open field tests within a week. The mechanism is store-operated calcium entry. Mancozeb specifically inhibits SOCE in astrocytes by targeting the Orai1-STIM1 complex — knockdown of either Orai1 or STIM1 abolishes the inhibitory effect, while TRPA1 knockdown does not. The downstream consequence is depletion of endoplasmic-reticulum calcium stores and loss of P2Y1 receptor agonist-induced calcium transients. Electrophysiologically, mancozeb selectively reduces inhibitory postsynaptic current frequency without affecting excitatory currents, consistent with reduced astrocyte-mediated GABA release. Two reasons this matters. First, the dose level — the acceptable daily intake produced behavioral phenotypes within a week, which is a high-priority data point for chemical risk assessment. Second, the mechanism nominates Orai1-STIM1 SOCE as the convergent intracellular target for at least one class of environmental neurotoxicant, which is testable for other agents. The astrocyte calcium-signaling field has been a relatively niche corner of CNS pharmacology, and this is the kind of mechanistic anchor that may bring it into broader drug-discovery scope, since the SOCE machinery is itself druggable.

Item seven is a methodological glymphatic-transport paper that's worth a short pass for the anesthesia-and-clearance crowd. Glymphatic CSF transport is highly sensitive to brain state — alpha-2 adrenergic agonist anesthesia enhances it, isoflurane suppresses it, and a long-standing critique has been that the apparent isoflurane suppression might reflect rapid tracer clearance from the basal cisterns rather than genuine inhibition. The new paper addresses that critique by comparing conventional short-duration cisterna-magna tracer injections against prolonged low-rate infusions, while keeping total tracer dose and anesthesia duration matched. Across both injection paradigms, ketamine-dexmedetomidine consistently produced substantially greater perivascular CSF influx than isoflurane, with no difference in tracer accumulation in blood or cervical lymph nodes. The conclusion is that the isoflurane suppression of glymphatic transport is real, not an artifact of injection kinetics. The relevance for CNS drug delivery and for the broader question of how anesthetic regime affects intrathecal therapeutic distribution should be obvious — for clinical pharmacology programs running intrathecal or intranasal CNS delivery in conscious-sedation contexts, the alpha-2 agonist class is the right anesthetic backbone if you want perivascular distribution, and isoflurane is the wrong choice.

Item eight is the first of four computational items, on the ADMET-prediction front. ADMETron is an industry-built SaaS platform from Innoplexus's OntoSight family that combines RNN-derived embeddings from SMILES strings with classical physicochemical descriptors, feeds both into gradient-boosting machines, and outputs predictions across thirty-four ADMET endpoints — physicochemical properties, absorption, CYP450 inhibition, hERG blockade, mutagenicity, and so on. The platform took second place on the Therapeutics Data Commons ADMET benchmark for Ames mutagenicity, with AUROC around point-eight-seven, and second place for LD50 with mean-absolute-error of point-five-seven-three. Beyond the model itself, the paper highlights what the SaaS layer adds — real-time multi-compound comparison through an interactive radar visualization, broader endpoint coverage than open online ADMET platforms, and integration with medicinal-chemistry assessments. For people running early-stage CNS lead optimization, the relevant question is whether this matches or beats the ADMET-AI-Predictor and SwissADME-class tools you already use. The benchmark numbers suggest yes — modestly — and the multi-endpoint visualization is the differentiator. It's a competent industry platform, and the broader signal in the literature is that ADMET prediction is becoming a commoditized layer in computational pharmacology, much like docking did over the past decade.

Item nine is the agentic-AI paper that should make computational-biology infrastructure engineers pay attention. AutoZyme, on bioRxiv, is an autonomous agentic framework for optimizing bioinformatics software. Given a target function — say, a Scanpy or Seurat routine — AutoZyme builds benchmarks against the existing implementation, identifies bottlenecks, and iteratively tests code changes, retaining only changes that improve runtime while preserving output identity. Evaluated on forty-five functions across Seurat, Scanpy and related single-cell-genomics packages, AutoZyme improved runtime in over ninety-five percent of cases. Across thirty-eight functions specifically, the median runtime reduction was eight-and-a-half-fold, with the largest exceeding six-hundred-fold. The optimized functions are distributed through AutoZyme-Library as drop-in replacements. Two things worth flagging. First, this is the same agentic-AI substrate pattern we've been seeing across biology — the agent doesn't do science, it manages infrastructure, and the science teams reap the speedup. Second, the equivalence-preservation check is the load-bearing piece. Without it the system would just produce silently broken faster code. The verification harness — running the original and modified function against fixed inputs and asserting output identity — is what makes the throughput-versus-correctness tradeoff acceptable. For groups running single-cell analyses at atlas scale or pharmacology pipelines on millions of compounds, drop-in faster replacements at this magnitude meaningfully shift what's feasible. And it's an interesting reframing of where agentic capability creates value — not as a scientific reasoner but as a tireless software optimizer.

Item ten is on arXiv — agentic generative drug discovery applied to peptide therapeutics. AMPGAN v3 is the third iteration of a conditional GAN for antimicrobial-peptide design from the same group, and the new version handles two things that prior generative AMP models couldn't: D-amino acids and terminal modifications like amidation. These are the non-natural building blocks that distinguish real-world peptide drugs from academic peptide sequences — they're necessary for protease resistance, half-life, and structure stabilization. The architectural piece is dual-discriminator multi-objective GAN training. The new piece is PepCraft, a multi-agent orchestration layer with a Planning Agent that coordinates specialized executors for generation, filtering, and verification. Five candidate peptides across three structural classes were tested in vitro, with two showing activity against Gram-positive strains and the best candidate hitting MIC of eight micrograms per milliliter against Bacillus subtilis. The hit rate is consistent with prior AMP discovery campaigns; the novelty is that the agent composes the design-filter-verify loop end-to-end with the generative model handling non-canonical chemistry. For broader drug discovery the read-across is the agentic peptide-engineering pattern. When the generative model knows about non-natural amino acids and the agent handles the design-test-iterate loop, you start to see the architecture that could plausibly scale to therapeutic-peptide design beyond antimicrobials.

Closing item is a theoretical neuroscience paper that tries to reconcile something the field has been quietly stuck on for thirty years. Hebbian-like learning is what synapses actually do, as far as electron-microscopy and electrophysiology can tell. Backpropagation — non-local credit assignment — is what computational models need to solve complex cognitive tasks. The dichotomy has been awkward. The new bioRxiv paper notices that recent cortical electron microscopy data show synapses with a spine apparatus — a calcium reservoir found preferentially in large spines — undergo different plasticity dynamics than smaller spines without one. The proposal is a recurrent neural network where each synapse uses one of two plasticity rules depending on its current strength: a Hebbian-like rule for weak synapses, a credit-assignment rule for strong ones. They call this a plasticity-switching RNN. It learns working-memory and other cognitive tasks in fewer trials than backpropagation-only RNNs, despite fewer synapses using credit assignment at any moment. Three mechanisms underlie the gain — credit assignment samples multiple parameter configurations, Hebbian plasticity creates a task-relevant initialization for the credit-assignment phase, and the switch prevents Hebbian synapses from drifting into unfavorable regions. The interaction produces lower-rank, more feedforward recurrent structure — which gives the paper a testable connectomic prediction, since lower-rank-recurrent versus higher-rank-recurrent topology is something the field can now measure with serial-section EM. The broader argument is that the spine apparatus may be the biological switch by which the brain decides which synapses participate in credit assignment, and that this is a far more efficient learning architecture than either pure Hebbian or pure backpropagation. For computational pharmacology, the relevance is indirect but real — if learning rules in the brain are state-dependent and switching at the synapse level, then drugs that influence calcium dynamics in spines may have learning-rule-shifting effects that look qualitatively different from simple plasticity gain or loss.
