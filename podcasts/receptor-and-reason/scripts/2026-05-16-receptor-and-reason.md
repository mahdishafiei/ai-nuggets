# Receptor and Reason — May 16, 2026

## Script

Good morning, Alan. This is Receptor and Reason for Saturday, May 16, 2026 — your daily roundup of neuropsychopharmacology, computational pharmacology, and agentic AI in biology. Ten items today. Two threads dominate. The first is developmental neuropsychopharmacology — prenatal antidepressant exposure imaged on fetal MRI, maternal diet rewiring serotonergic reward circuits through microglia, and a sex-specific cannabinoid mechanism for disrupting fear memory reconsolidation. The second is cannabinoid system pharmacology more broadly, with the first in-vivo PET measurements of fatty acid amide hydrolase dynamics during cannabis abstinence. Then a clean clinical-neurochemistry mapping paper, a noradrenergic mesoscale imaging study, a repurposing mechanism for simvastatin in Alzheimer's, and the renewed lithium conversation.

Opening item, and this is the strongest clinical contribution from Neuropsychopharmacology this week, published online a week ago — prenatal serotonin reuptake inhibitor exposure and maternal depression symptoms are associated with altered fetal brain and placental development. The motivating question is one of the longest-running controversies in perinatal psychiatry — what, if anything, do SSRIs and SNRIs do to the developing fetal brain, separate from the effect of untreated maternal depression. The previous literature is mostly postnatal — cohort studies of childhood behavior or pediatric MRI a few years after birth — and the depression-versus-exposure confound has been almost impossible to dissect. This group does the in-utero version. Fetal MRI between 20 and 40 weeks of gestation in 182 pregnant women — 62 on serotonin reuptake inhibitors and 120 controls — with motion-corrected 3D reconstructions, deep-learning segmentation of hippocampal and cortical structures, and diffusion measurements of the placenta. The exposed fetuses had smaller hippocampal volumes, reduced cortical gyrification, reduced cortical curvedness, and reduced surface area. Crucially, when they stratified by maternal depression severity using the Edinburgh Postnatal Depression Scale, the exposed-versus-unexposed difference in hippocampal volume was present for women with low and moderate depression scores but vanished for women with high scores — that is, exposure-associated reductions appear where unmedicated maternal depression alone was not severe. Meanwhile, in the unmedicated arm, fetuses of women with the highest depression scores had smaller hippocampal volumes than fetuses of women with low scores. Placental volume and diffusion were both increased in the exposed group, and placental size correlated with brain volume. The interpretive shape is that this is genuine fetal structural pharmacology, not confounded by depression severity. The clinical significance is undetermined — the authors are explicit that the structural findings do not yet map to long-term neurodevelopmental outcomes — but this is the cleanest in-utero structural evidence to date, and it will reshape the perinatal-psychiatry conversation about benefit-risk framing.

Item two pairs thematically — maternal exposure shaping offspring neural circuit assembly through a different mechanism. From bioRxiv neuroscience three days ago, maternal high-fat diet drives sex-specific microglia remodeling of serotonergic reward circuits. The setup is mouse, but the model is mechanistic and worth the audience's attention because the mechanism is microglial — a cellular layer that until five years ago was almost entirely missing from circuit-assembly accounts of mesolimbic development. Dams on a high-fat diet during pregnancy and lactation produce offspring with increased serotonergic fiber density in the nucleus accumbens, but only in male offspring, with this hyperinnervation visible by postnatal day fourteen. The proximal cause is reduced microglial phagocytosis of serotonin projections during this critical window — microglia normally prune excess serotonin terminals, and the mHFD environment turns that pruning down. The hyperinnervation persists into adulthood. Functionally, the affected males show elevated nucleus accumbens serotonin release and accelerated reward-motivated learning, a phenotype the authors reproduce with chemogenetic activation of accumbens-projecting serotonin neurons. So you get a circuit-level account that connects maternal metabolic state to a sex-specific reward-circuit phenotype, with microglia as the intermediary. The translational implications are speculative but pointed — early-life metabolic inflammation as a developmental programming signal that shapes adult serotonergic reward function, with sex-specific vulnerability.

Item three. Also from Neuropsychopharmacology online ahead of print a week ago, sex-specific role of microglia in delta-9-tetrahydrocannabinol-induced disruption of fear memory reconsolidation. This bridges the microglia thread from the previous item into cannabinoid pharmacology. The clinical hook is real — THC has been investigated as a fear-memory-reconsolidation disruptor for PTSD, with the idea that giving it in the window after fear recall could weaken the reconsolidated trace. The known pharmacology is CB1-mediated. What this paper adds is two things. First, peroxisome-proliferator-activated-receptor-gamma — PPAR-gamma — turns out to be a parallel transducer of THC's effect at the relevant dose, alongside CB1. Second, microglia in the dorsal hippocampus mediate the disruption, and the receptor split is sex-dependent. In male rats, the effect requires both CB1 and PPAR-gamma at hippocampal microglia. In females, the effect requires CB1 alone. Chemogenetic inhibition of dorsal-hippocampal microglia or selective antagonism at either receptor in males abolishes the reconsolidation-disrupting effect. The implication is that THC's PTSD-relevant action has a neuroimmune component, not just a synaptic-cannabinoid one, and the sex-specific receptor split would translate into different dose-response shapes in clinical trials. It also bears on the broader question of why PPAR-gamma agonists, which are well-established peripheral metabolic drugs, keep showing up in CNS pharmacology screens — pioglitazone for example has cropped up in addiction and mood-disorder trials with intriguing-but-uneven signals.

Item four stays in the cannabinoid system and is the most methodologically novel piece of in-human translational work this week. Also from Neuropsychopharmacology online a week ago, PET imaging of fatty acid amide hydrolase in chronic cannabis users during short-term abstinence. Quick anchor — fatty acid amide hydrolase, FAAH, is the primary enzyme that degrades anandamide, the major endogenous cannabinoid agonist at CB1, and FAAH inhibitors have been a drug-development target for cannabis use disorder and for anxiety for years. The clinical attraction is that raising endogenous anandamide should buffer the loss of exogenous cannabinoid tone during withdrawal, a mechanism that nicotine replacement and methadone use through different chemistry. Trial results have been mixed — and one trial, the BIA 10-2474 phase 1, was infamously catastrophic for non-FAAH reasons. The bottleneck is that nobody has directly measured what FAAH actually does in the human brain during the withdrawal window. This study uses a positron-emission-tomography tracer called carbon-11 CURB, which binds FAAH irreversibly, in fourteen people with cannabis use disorder, scanned overnight after last use and again three to seven days later. FAAH binding increases by about ten percent across the whole brain in that interval, with the largest change in ventral striatum at about eleven percent. The interpretation is that during early abstinence the enzyme is upregulating, which would lower anandamide tone, and the time course of upregulation tracks withdrawal symptoms and craving. Translationally, this is a strong rationale for timing FAAH inhibition to the first week of abstinence rather than dosing chronically, and it supplies a pharmacodynamic biomarker that human trials of FAAH inhibitors have lacked. The sample is small and the dynamics will need replication, but this is the kind of in-vivo enzymology that the cannabis-use-disorder field has been asking for.

Item five — also fresh, also clinical, and the cleanest cross-modal mapping study of the week. From bioRxiv neuroscience yesterday, neurotransmitter circuits of the human language network and their disruption in post-stroke aphasia. The premise is that the language network has been mapped functionally and structurally for decades, but the neurotransmitter architecture has never been charted because no single imaging modality captures it. This group combines large-scale functional MRI, atlas-based PET-derived receptor and transporter maps from prior open datasets, and structural connectomics. They find that the language network is organized by connection class — cortico-cortical pathways are dominated by serotonergic and glutamatergic signaling, thalamo-cortical projections are predominantly cholinergic, and anterior temporal connections show GABAergic enrichment. In 239 stroke patients, aphasia is associated with distinct neurochemical disruption profiles depending on lesion location — lobar lesions show postsynaptic serotonergic injury, subcortical aphasia shows preferential presynaptic cholinergic or serotonergic disruption. And the part that lifts this from descriptive to actionable is a post-hoc reanalysis of four prior pharmacological trials in aphasia recovery, showing that the treatment-to-neurotransmitter-disruption match predicts behavioral improvement. The translational shape is a personalized-pharmacology framework for post-stroke aphasia — which has been a notoriously hard-to-treat domain — with neurotransmitter mapping as the patient-stratification handle. The same framework should generalize to other lesion-induced clinical syndromes where mixed neurotransmitter pharmacology has been tried without stratification.

Item six. From bioRxiv neuroscience five days ago, sex-dependent effects of glutamatergic disruption on the dopaminergic neuron subtype vulnerable in Parkinson's disease. This is the second adolescent-or-developmental glutamate-on-dopamine paper this week — yesterday's was the Grin2a knockout in dopamine neurons producing a schizophrenia phenotype, and this one is the Grin1 knockout in a defined dopamine subtype, with a different behavioral phenotype. The subtype is ALDH1A1-positive dopamine neurons — anchor here is that this is the population that's preferentially lost in Parkinson's disease, defined by expression of aldehyde dehydrogenase 1A1, which is involved in dopamine metabolism and probably contributes to their selective vulnerability. The authors delete Grin1, which encodes the obligatory NMDA-receptor subunit, in this subtype. Conditional knockouts have normal motor function, but females show enhanced operant reward acquisition and a striking post-food-restriction overfeeding phenotype with transient weight gain. Mapping the regional contribution, the VTA subset of ALDH1A1-positive neurons drives the feeding effect — substantia nigra knockdown does not. Transcriptomics shows sex-specific changes in mitochondrial and energy metabolism genes, more pronounced in females. The clinically interesting framing is that compulsive eating is a well-documented impulse-control side effect of dopaminergic Parkinson's therapy, particularly in women, and this paper offers a substrate — disrupted NMDA input onto the specific dopamine neuron subtype that's losing function in the disease, with female-biased mitochondrial vulnerability. It also pairs cleanly with yesterday's Grin2a story to argue that NMDA-dopamine coupling is sex-specific and subtype-specific in ways the field has under-modeled.

Item seven is a clean neuromodulator-imaging methods piece. From bioRxiv neuroscience three days ago, spatiotemporally dynamic noradrenergic regulation of cortical networks. The standard model of locus-coeruleus noradrenaline has been a global arousal signal coupled to pupil dynamics and sleep-wake state. Recent work has been chipping at that, suggesting modular partitioning of the locus coeruleus into projection-specific subpopulations, but the imaging tools to see norepinephrine release across the cortex in real time have only matured in the last two years. This group uses dual-color mesoscopic imaging in mice — a genetically-encoded norepinephrine sensor paired with either a calcium sensor or an acetylcholine sensor — across the entire dorsal cortex, with simultaneous high-density electrophysiology. The pattern of cortical norepinephrine signaling varies with behavioral state and predicts enhanced functional connectivity. Simultaneous noradrenaline and acetylcholine imaging shows that the two systems are spatiotemporally coupled but with distinct dynamics. Sleep deprivation disrupts both. The contribution is less a single conceptual flip and more a methods-and-evidence platform — once you can image two neuromodulators across the whole cortex simultaneously with cellular-scale temporal resolution, every prior framing of "noradrenergic state" becomes testable in a way it wasn't before. The audience-relevance angle is that essentially every cortically-acting psychiatric drug operates against this noradrenergic-cholinergic-acetylcholine backdrop, and this kind of imaging is going to anchor the next generation of pharmacological-mechanism studies.

Item eight is a repurposing-mechanism paper. From the same issue of Neuropsychopharmacology yesterday, simvastatin rescues cognitive impairment in an amyloid-beta-induced Alzheimer's model through the HDAC2-BDNF signaling pathway. Statins and dementia have a long history — observational studies for years suggested statin use lowered dementia risk, randomized trials have been more equivocal, and the mechanistic explanation has alternated between cholesterol-lowering effects on amyloid metabolism and pleiotropic anti-inflammatory or neurotrophic effects. This paper makes the case for a specific epigenetic mechanism. In a mouse Alzheimer's model induced by intracerebroventricular amyloid-beta peptide injection, the amyloid raises HDAC2 expression in the dorsal hippocampus, reduces histone H4 acetylation at the BDNF promoter, and reduces BDNF levels. Simvastatin reverses all three and rescues the cognitive deficit. Viral overexpression of HDAC2 abolishes the simvastatin benefit, which is the clean causal handle. The shape this gives the field is that the cognitive-protective effect of statins — to whatever degree it's real in humans — is plausibly downstream of HDAC2 inhibition at BDNF, not of cholesterol lowering. That's a different druggable target — HDAC2-selective inhibitors are an active discovery space — and it predicts that any drug that opens H4 acetylation at the BDNF promoter would carry the same benefit. The trial-design implication for human statin-and-cognition work is to stratify by HDAC2 activity or by BDNF rather than by lipid response.

Item nine moves to clinical-pharmacology framing. Earlier this week Psychiatric Times published a review on low-dose lithium as a new frontier in mental-health treatment, with commentary from clinicians who shaped the original lithium dosing literature, and it ties together a story that has been building for a year. The new piece is a synthesis, not original data, but the synthesis is the news because it makes the case that low-dose lithium — meaning doses well below the bipolar-maintenance range — is the right framing for an emerging set of indications. The two pillars are the dementia-prevention literature, where a Nature 2025 paper documented endogenous lithium depletion in aged brains and its rescue with low-dose supplementation in mice, and the suicide-prevention literature, where ecological correlations between groundwater lithium concentration and suicide rate have been replicated across multiple geographic settings. The mechanism candidate is GSK3-beta inhibition, with downstream effects on neurotrophic and neuroprotective signaling, neuronal autophagy of misfolded protein aggregates, and circadian gene expression. The audience-relevance angle is that lithium-as-neuroprotective-tonic is starting to look like a distinct pharmacological category from lithium-as-mood-stabilizer, with a different therapeutic window and a different patient population. Whether any of this becomes prescribable practice depends on a clinical-trial agenda that has not really started yet, but the conceptual reframing is the thing to watch.

Last item, the lightest, and the agentic-AI-in-biology pick of the week. From arXiv on Thursday, NeuroAtlas — a benchmark for foundation models in clinical EEG and brain-computer interfaces. The motivating problem is that EEG-specific foundation models have proliferated over the last eighteen months — pretrained representation extractors for clinical and BCI applications — but published evaluations differ in datasets, in preprocessing, and in metrics, which has made it hard to tell which architectures actually work. NeuroAtlas aggregates 42 datasets, totaling 260,000 hours of EEG, across clinical use cases — epilepsy, sleep medicine, brain age estimation — and standard BCI tasks. Three findings worth flagging. First, EEG-specific foundation models do not consistently outperform generic time-series foundation models that have no EEG-specific architecture or pretraining — a result that mirrors what's been seen in other biomedical modalities. Second, standard machine-learning metrics are insufficient for clinical utility — for epilepsy you need event-level decision metrics, for sleep you need hypnogram-derived features, for brain age you need a properly-calibrated brain-age gap. The benchmark provides those domain-specific evaluations. Third, model rankings vary substantially within domains, meaning a single ranked leaderboard isn't a useful summary. The pharmacological-research relevance is that EEG biomarkers are increasingly used as pharmacodynamic readouts in CNS drug development — for antipsychotic and antidepressant mechanism-of-action work, sleep-pharmacology trials, and TMS-combined trials — and the foundation-model layer underneath those readouts is now standardizable rather than ad hoc.

That's ten for today. The prenatal SRI fetal-MRI study is the week's most consequential clinical contribution and will reshape how perinatal psychiatry frames the benefit-risk conversation. The FAAH PET work is the methodologically strongest mechanism piece. And the language-network neurochemical mapping is the most novel cross-modal methods contribution — worth tracking whether the personalized-pharmacology framing it proposes generalizes. See you tomorrow.
