First up — and this is the opener because if it replicates, it forces a textbook rewrite — out of bioRxiv biophysics this past Tuesday, a paper titled "GluDs are ionotropic dopamine receptors tuned by G-proteins." The glutamate delta receptors, GluD1 and GluD2, have been the orphans of the ionotropic glutamate receptor family for forty years — they have the architecture, they sit in the synapse, but no one has been able to demonstrate clean ligand-gated ion channel activity for them. There was actually a PNAS piece in 2024 arguing definitively that GluDs are not ligand-gated. This new paper makes the opposite case using cryo-EM, lipid bilayer recordings, mutagenesis, and patch clamp: dopamine, not glutamate, is a direct agonist of GluDs and elicits ionotropic current. They show dopamine binding induces clamshell closure at the ligand-binding domain through a distinct molecular interface from glutamate, and that the channel activity is gated by G-proteins acting as molecular switches. Free Gβγ subunits inhibit the dopamine-evoked current; Gα subunits or inactive heterotrimers permit it. A neurodegeneration-associated point mutation uncouples this G-protein tuning. The implications, if it holds, are large. We've classified D1 through D5 as the dopamine receptors and treated dopamine as a purely metabotropic signal. An ionotropic dopamine receptor — gated and tuned by adjacent metabotropic signaling — is a different category of object, and it changes how you think about fast dopamine effects in striatum and elsewhere. Important to flag this is one preprint against a recent counter-claim; the field will need to reproduce it carefully. But the experimental package is broad enough that it's not easily waved off.

Item two stays in dopamine-receptor territory and pivots to a specific clinical question. New on bioRxiv neuroscience this Sunday, on D3 dopamine receptors and the aversive side effects of quetiapine. Second-generation antipsychotics have a side-effect-driven compliance problem, and the variability across compounds in that side-effect profile has been hard to mechanistically resolve. This group identifies a D3 receptor-expressing subpopulation of nucleus accumbens neurons — embedded within the D1 receptor population, not separate from it — that defines a transcriptionally and projection-defined subtype. Selectively activating this D3-positive subpopulation reproduces the aversive component of quetiapine's effect in mice; selectively blocking D3 signaling in that subpopulation blunts the aversion without losing the antipsychotic-relevant effects on other readouts. The translational hook is the same one D3 has had for a decade — D3-preferring or D3-selective ligands could decouple efficacy from the dysphoric and sedative components that drive discontinuation. The new contribution is that it's not just receptor-level selectivity that matters but the projection-target identity of the D3-expressing neurons. That's the kind of granularity that lets you ask whether existing D3-preferring tools — cariprazine on the partial agonist side, the older D3 antagonist tool compounds — are engaging the right neuronal population or the wrong one.

Item three keeps the G-protein-coupled receptor pharmacology focus and moves to neurotensin. From bioRxiv this past weekend, biased signaling at the neurotensin receptor type 1, NTSR1, differentially regulating inhibitory transmission in the extended amygdala and suppressing motivated feeding. The tool compound is SBI-553, a β-arrestin-biased allosteric modulator of NTSR1 that recruits arrestin while attenuating Gq-mediated signaling — this is the Sanford Burnham series that's been the workhorse for proving the biased-agonism concept in the neurotensin system, with the cocaine and opioid-reward applications previously published. The new piece extends that to consummatory behavior: in slice, NTS and SBI-553 differentially modulate GABAergic transmission in the central nucleus of the amygdala and the bed nucleus of the stria terminalis, and in vivo SBI-553 suppresses motivated feeding without the obvious confounds of generalized motoric or motivational suppression. The reason this matters beyond feeding is that NTSR1-biased agonism is becoming a credible drug-discovery handle for stimulant use disorder and binge-type eating disorder — both reward-pathology indications — and the case for biased-versus-balanced agonism rests on this kind of region-by-region pharmacological dissection. The G-protein versus arrestin question doesn't have a clean answer at the systems level, and papers like this push toward an empirically derived rather than ideological position.

Item four moves from receptor pharmacology to circuit-level mechanism. From bioRxiv neuroscience last Friday, the cerebellum-to-VTA projection in chronic-stress-induced depression. This is a follow-up to earlier work from the same group showing that deep cerebellar nuclei project to the ventral tegmental area and that this projection contributes to depression-like behavior after chronic restraint stress. The new paper goes after the mechanism. Chronic stress drives an activity-dependent reduction in vesicular glutamate transporter 2 — VGLUT2 — at the cerebellar terminals onto VTA dopamine neurons, which reduces miniature excitatory postsynaptic currents and downstream activation. They show this is reversible by manipulating VGLUT2 levels and tracks behaviorally with the depressive phenotype. Why this is interesting beyond circuit cartography: the cerebellum has been one of those structures everyone agrees is somehow involved in affect but that has resisted clean inclusion in the depression-circuit pharmacology. A presynaptic VGLUT2-trafficking step is in principle a druggable mechanism — there are no good clinical VGLUT2 modulators, but the substrate is well-defined and the activity-dependent regulation looks targetable. It also reframes how you might interpret cerebellar engagement in TMS protocols, which have started moving away from purely DLPFC targets.

Item five is the closest thing to clinical news this week, and it's a mixed report. Vistagen Therapeutics announced preliminary positive data on Tuesday from the open-label extension of their PALISADE-3 Phase 3 trial of fasedienol for acute social anxiety disorder. Fasedienol — for context, a synthetic pherine administered intranasally that's hypothesized to engage the chemosensory nasal-to-limbic pathway and produce a fast anxiolytic effect, mechanism distinct from any benzodiazepine or SSRI — failed its primary and secondary endpoints in the randomized portion of PALISADE-3 back in December. The new data are from the open-label extension portion: clinically relevant improvement in social anxiety over four months on both clinician-administered and patient-reported scales, discontinuation due to adverse events 2.6%, no discontinuations attributed to fasedienol itself. The interpretive question is the obvious one — open-label extension data in a population that just failed a randomized public-speaking-challenge trial is not a substitute for a positive Phase 3, and Vistagen knows that. Their pitch is that this together with the earlier positive PALISADE-2 and the still-pending PALISADE-4 — last patient visit completed last week, topline expected this quarter — could constitute substantial evidence for an NDA. The honest read is that the field has spent a decade chasing rapid-onset anxiolytics that aren't benzodiazepine-class and the pherine mechanism is unusual enough to be either real or a long noise tail. PALISADE-4 is the decisive readout; this is a preview.

Item six is a psychedelics paper, and it's a structural mechanism paper rather than a behavioral one. From bioRxiv neuroscience Monday, a single dose of LSD persistently reduces pain affect in rats — specifically the affective rather than sensory component. Local administration in the anterior cingulate cortex recapitulates the effect; administration in primary somatosensory cortex does not. Neuropixels recordings show LSD suppresses stimulus-evoked nociceptive responses in the ACC, which reduces the encoding of aversive value. Ex vivo, intrinsic excitability of ACC neurons actually goes up. In vivo, maximum stimulus-evoked firing goes down. So the cell-intrinsic excitability change is opposite to the network-level response — a circuit-gated rather than cell-intrinsic effect on aversive encoding. Why this matters: the psychedelic-for-chronic-pain hypothesis has been advanced mostly on hand-waving about anti-inflammatory mechanisms and 5-HT2A-driven cortical plasticity. This is a mechanism paper that puts the affective-pain effect in a specific circuit — ACC, not somatosensory — and gives a candidate readout for translation. Worth watching alongside the chronic-pain arms in the second-wave psychedelic trials, which are still slow to materialize.

Item seven keeps the addiction-and-reward block going. From bioRxiv this past Sunday, dual GLP-1 plus FGF21 agonism in voluntary alcohol consumption. The compound is GLP1-ELP-FGF21 — a long-acting bispecific fusion linking a GLP-1 receptor agonist to FGF21 through an elastin-like-polypeptide linker. Both agents individually have been shown to reduce alcohol intake in rodents, via complementary metabolic and reward-pathway mechanisms, but the dual hasn't been characterized. A single dose reduces voluntary alcohol intake for at least 72 hours in male mice, shifts alcohol-versus-water preference, and modulates nucleus accumbens dopamine in voltammetry. The GLP-1 piece is the obvious commercial elephant in the room — semaglutide for alcohol use disorder is in active clinical investigation, and the early signals from registry and observational data have been intriguing if noisy. The FGF21 angle is less obvious but more biologically tied to alcohol — FGF21 is a liver-secreted hormone whose levels rise with alcohol consumption and which has its own central reward-modulating effects. Combining them into a single molecule is a logical preclinical move. The honest reservation is that single-dose, 72-hour-reduction-in-mice is roughly six rungs below a meaningful clinical signal, and the GLP-1 mental health story is still negotiating itself — the Lancet Psychiatry national cohort out of Sweden earlier this year showed semaglutide-specific reductions in worsening mental illness while other GLP-1s didn't show the same, which complicates straightforward read-throughs. Still, dual agonism is the design choice that's working in metabolic disease, and watching it migrate into reward pharmacology is interesting.

Now the agentic AI block. Item eight, from bioRxiv bioinformatics last Friday, Open-Rosalind — a tool-first biomedical LLM agent framework with a process-aware benchmarking suite. The pitch is structurally different from most of the bio-agent papers we've been seeing. The argument is that flexibility — which helps general-purpose agents — actively hurts in biomedical research, where you need accountability for the trace, not just the answer. So Open-Rosalind is built around four operational principles: evidence-grounded outputs, trace completeness, workflow-constrained execution, and explicit tool mediation for factual claims. The benchmark — Open-Rosalind BioBench — evaluates not just whether the final answer is correct but whether the reasoning trace is complete and each factual claim was supported by an explicit tool call rather than from the language model's parametric knowledge. That's the right framing. Most existing biomed-agent benchmarks measure end-to-end success and miss the situations where the agent guessed correctly without actually verifying. If you've been reading ClaroAI-Bench from earlier this week — and the biomedical-agentic-AI feed covered it — Open-Rosalind is the complementary methodological piece. ClaroAI-Bench asks "can the agent reproduce a published paper end-to-end?" Open-Rosalind asks "did the agent earn each claim along the way?" Both are necessary, and the field is starting to converge on the right evaluation dimensions.

Item nine extends the AI-in-drug-discovery thread to virtual cell models. From bioRxiv yesterday, a paper just called "Cell-Level Virtual Screening." The premise is that molecular models — your standard ligand-target affinity predictors — are insufficient to predict higher-order biological effects, which is why drug discovery accumulates late-stage failures. Virtual cells, which predict gene expression responses to drug perturbations, have been proposed as a solution, but they're weakly validated as screening tools — gene expression is an intermediate, not a clinical endpoint. This paper actually asks the basic operational question: how well does ranking compounds by virtual-cell-predicted activity correlate with ranking them by measured cellular phenotype? They build out an evaluation harness, compare virtual-cell screens against compound-level baselines, and identify which compound classes and which cell types the virtual-cell approach actually adds value for. The contribution is methodological honesty more than headline performance. The virtual cell space — Tahoe, the Recursion Phenom platform, the various academic prototypes — has been moving fast and validating slowly, and a paper that takes seriously the question of whether the predictions track downstream useful endpoints rather than just other predictions is useful for the field. Won't be the last word, but it's the right framing.

Last item, the lightest. From bioRxiv pharmacology and toxicology Tuesday, ramelteon — the MT1 and MT2 melatonin receptor agonist used clinically for insomnia, sold as Rozerem — facilitating hippocampal ripple occurrence and amplitude in mice. Sharp-wave ripples during non-REM sleep are the canonical neural substrate of memory consolidation, and ramelteon has had unexpected cognitive-enhancing whispers around it for years that have never been mechanistically nailed down. Chronic local field potential recordings show that after intraperitoneal ramelteon, both the rate and the amplitude of hippocampal ripples during NREM sleep increase relative to vehicle, without disrupting overall sleep architecture. The reason this is interesting beyond the bench: the melatonin-receptor agonist space is essentially ramelteon, agomelatine, tasimelteon, and one or two niche compounds, and the marketed indications are sleep timing and seasonal depression. A ripple-facilitating cognitive-consolidation hypothesis is a different framing entirely. If it survives translation — and ripples are recordable in humans with intracranial electrodes and increasingly with refined scalp methods — then ramelteon becomes interesting as a pro-cognitive adjunct, not a sleep aid. Plausibly more interesting than its currently marketed use. Soft signal, but worth tracking.
