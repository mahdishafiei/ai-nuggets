## Script

Welcome back to Receptor and Reason. Ten items today, with a center of gravity on the neurodegeneration-and-inflammation axis — a Phase 2 readout for a TNF-targeted biologic in inflammation-defined Alzheimer's, a GLP-1 mechanism paper in a genetic Parkinson's model, an oligodendrocyte-subtype story in clinical Parkinson's progression, and a microglial GPCR that gates the disease-associated microglial state in Alzheimer's. From there we pivot to ligand-discovery methodology with a Shoichet–Roth collaboration on random-background controls in ligand optimization, a pointed rebuttal of a dopamine-neuron GPCR atlas, a developmental pharmacology rescue in Down syndrome, a clinical-psychology piece on anhedonia buffering early-life unpredictability, a protein-language-model-suggested mutation that amplifies optogenetic photocurrent, and a closing methodological provocation on the shape of pharmacokinetic curves. Let's go.

Lead item, and the most newsworthy clinical readout of the week — Phase 2 MINDFul results on XPro1595 for early Alzheimer's disease with inflammation, reported through Psychiatric Times. XPro1595, generic name pegipanermin, is a dominant-negative TNF inhibitor — engineered to selectively block soluble tumor necrosis factor without disturbing transmembrane TNF signaling, which is the pharmacological move that distinguishes it from etanercept-class biologics that hit both. The hypothesis the field has been chasing for a decade is that a subset of Alzheimer's patients have a meaningful inflammatory component driving cognitive decline, and that selectively quieting soluble TNF can slow progression in that biomarker-defined subset. MINDFul is the Phase 2 test of exactly that hypothesis — patients enriched for an inflammatory signature, randomized to XPro1595 or placebo, with cognition and biomarkers as endpoints. The trial trends toward cognitive and biomarker improvement in the inflammation-defined population. That is a careful phrase — trends toward, not statistically definitive — and the read-through depends on how strong the secondaries are, how the effect compares across the subgroups, and how the safety profile holds. But the strategic shape of this readout matters. If the inflammation-stratified subset really does behave differently, then the field's enrichment-trial template for Alzheimer's — which has been amyloid-PET-centric for fifteen years — gains a parallel inflammatory-biomarker arm. That changes the design of every late-stage CNS anti-inflammatory program that's been waiting in the wings. Watch the Phase 3 design announcement; the question is whether the company keeps the inflammatory-biomarker enrichment or widens to all-comers.

Item two, a mechanistic neurodegeneration paper that fits the GLP-1 narrative cleanly — GLP-1 analogs restore inflammatory, mitochondrial, and intercellular signaling networks in a SncaG51D knock-in mouse model of Parkinson's disease, from the Zoghbi group at Baylor. SncaG51D is a missense mutation in the gene encoding alpha-synuclein, the protein whose aggregation is the defining molecular feature of Parkinson's; the G51D knock-in mouse develops a slow, prodromal-then-motor phenotype that recapitulates the disease arc. The pharmacological question is one the field has gotten progressively more excited about over the last two years on the back of GLP-1 trials in idiopathic Parkinson's — does glucagon-like-peptide-1 receptor agonism, the same class as semaglutide and liraglutide, intervene on the molecular pathology rather than just the symptoms. The Zoghbi group treated the G51D mice with a GLP-1 analog and ran transcriptomic and proteomic profiling against vehicle-treated mutants and wild-type controls. The result is multi-system. GLP-1 treatment partially restores inflammatory signaling, mitochondrial network gene expression, and intercellular signaling pathways toward the wild-type signature. The signal isn't selective for one mechanism — it's the kind of multi-pathway normalization that you'd expect from an upstream metabolic intervention, which is consistent with what the Phase 2 clinical signals have suggested. Why this matters — the lixisenatide and exenatide Phase 2 trials in Parkinson's have produced mixed but provocative motor-symptom signals, and the field has not had a satisfying genetic-model mechanistic story to anchor those. This is one. The next experiment is the obvious one — do you see the same multi-pathway normalization in the LRRK2 and GBA mouse models, where the upstream genetic lesions are different but converge on the same lysosomal-mitochondrial-inflammatory axis. If yes, GLP-1 agonism is a genuinely disease-modifying handle across genetic Parkinson's. If no, it's a SncaG51D-specific effect and the translation story narrows.

Item three, staying with Parkinson's but pivoting to a non-neuronal cell type that's been quietly accumulating attention — oligodendrocyte subtype diversity underlines clinical progression in Parkinson's disease, from a Mainz–Saarland consortium led by the Groppa group. Most Parkinson's molecular pathology work has stayed neuron-centric, with dopaminergic neurons in the substantia nigra as the primary cell of interest and oligodendrocyte involvement treated as a multiple-system-atrophy-adjacent footnote. They did the single-cell work on both a synucleinopathy mouse model and post-mortem human Parkinson's brain, and they segment oligodendrocytes into transcriptional subtypes with distinct disease-stage dynamics. Specific oligodendrocyte subtypes are depleted or expanded with clinical progression in the human samples, with parallel signatures in the mouse model. The clinically actionable angle is that one subset of the oligodendrocyte response appears to track the rate of motor progression rather than just disease presence, suggesting these cells may be a progression biomarker rather than just a pathology marker. The broader implication is consistent with where the field has been heading — Parkinson's is not just a dopaminergic-neuron disease, it's a glial disease with neuronal manifestations, and the therapeutic candidates that engage glial biology, including the GLP-1 story from the previous item, are the ones to watch.

Item four, and the GPCR-and-microglia thread that connects neatly to the Alzheimer's inflammation story from item one — the G-protein-coupled receptor GPR34 promotes the homeostatic state of microglia and restrains the disease-associated microglial response in an Alzheimer's model, an updated preprint from a Sheng-and-Stevens-affiliated team at the Broad. GPR34 is a lysophosphatidylserine-responsive GPCR enriched in microglia, downregulated in Alzheimer's, multiple sclerosis, and several other neurodegenerative contexts. The field has had this expression pattern in hand for a while but no clean causal story. They knocked GPR34 out, crossed into an Alzheimer's amyloid model, and ran the single-cell-RNA-sequencing readout on the microglial population. The result is a clean homeostatic-versus-disease-associated rebalancing. GPR34 loss accelerates the transition from homeostatic microglia into the disease-associated-microglia state, with corresponding changes in plaque-adjacent microglial behavior and downstream amyloid pathology. The pharmacological implication is the right kind of provocation. GPR34 is a druggable GPCR — it's a class A receptor with known endogenous ligands — and the expression-pattern data plus this functional knockout result implies that a positive allosteric modulator or biased agonist that retains microglia in the homeostatic state could plausibly slow disease progression. That's a more selectively druggable target than the broad anti-inflammatory programs the Alzheimer's field has run with, and it's worth watching whether anyone in industry picks it up.

Item five, a methodology paper from a Shoichet–Roth–Manglik–Fraser–Basbaum consortium that ought to land harder in the computational pharmacology conversation than its understated title suggests — Toward a Random Background for Ligand Optimization. The setup is the practical pain point in lead optimization. When you've found a hit against a target and you're optimizing analogs, you measure incremental affinity gains against the parent. The implicit assumption is that the parent is a meaningful baseline. But the analog series is enriched for compounds that are similar to the parent in ways that may correlate with target engagement for reasons that aren't actually about the optimization decisions you're making. They propose random property-matched backgrounds — sets of compounds matched to the analog series on molecular weight, polarity, and other physicochemical descriptors, but otherwise random — as a fair-comparison reference for the analogs' performance. The empirical demonstrations are across opioid receptors, cannabinoid receptors, and several other GPCRs and enzymes, and the result is sobering. A substantial fraction of optimization campaigns produce affinity improvements that are statistically indistinguishable from drawing random property-matched compounds and re-testing. The optimization wasn't optimizing; it was sampling. The constructive contribution is the random-background framework, which any computational pharmacology group running structure-based design or molecular dynamics-driven lead optimization can drop into their workflow. The harder-edged contribution is the implicit critique. If you're not running this kind of random-background control on your campaign, you may be selectively reporting your hit-and-miss as signal-and-noise. Expect this to be cited in every responsible methods section for the next two years.

Item six, a sharp methodological pushback in dopamine-neuron molecular taxonomy — Nmur1 and Cckar fail to support functional genetic access in adult dopamine neurons and challenge GPCR atlas assignments, from a Steele-and-Leinninger-affiliated group. There's been a recent push to use single-cell GPCR transcriptomic profiles to define dopaminergic neuron subtypes, with the idea that the GPCR landscape carves the population into functionally and pharmacologically distinct subgroups — and that Cre-driver mouse lines built on those GPCRs would give you genetic access to specific subsets for circuit work. Two of the candidate markers in the recent atlas were neuromedin U receptor 1, abbreviated Nmur1, and cholecystokinin A receptor, abbreviated Cckar. The Steele group bought or generated the corresponding Cre lines and tried to use them. The lines do not produce functional genetic access to dopamine neurons in adults — the recombination pattern is either too sparse, too leaky, or expressed in non-dopaminergic populations. The mechanistic interpretation is that the original transcriptomic signal for these receptors in adult dopamine neurons was either developmental, technical, or near the detection floor — and not a reliable handle for in-vivo functional dissection. The reason this matters beyond the specific markers is methodological. As GPCR-and-neuropeptide-receptor atlases proliferate and groups use them to design Cre-driver experiments, the gap between transcriptomic detection and reliable functional driver-line expression is going to be a recurring failure mode. Worth keeping a healthy skepticism when an atlas paper claims a GPCR carves a population; the downstream genetic-tool validation is the work that turns an atlas claim into useful neuroscience.

Item seven, a developmental pharmacology paper with a long-horizon design that's exactly what the field has been missing — long-term neurodevelopmental and cognitive outcomes following prenatal inhibition of DYRK1A by Leucettine L41 in mouse models of Down syndrome, from the Hérault group at IGBMC in Strasbourg. DYRK1A is the dual-specificity tyrosine-phosphorylation-regulated kinase 1A, a chromosome-21-encoded kinase whose gene dosage is increased in Down syndrome and whose hyperactivity has been implicated in the cognitive and neurodevelopmental phenotype. Leucettine L41 is a marine-alkaloid-derived DYRK1A inhibitor — a tool compound with reasonable selectivity for the kinase. The clinical-development question that has hung over the DYRK1A field is whether transient prenatal pharmacological correction of the kinase imbalance can produce durable postnatal benefit, or whether the developmental window of vulnerability is too narrow. They ran prenatal-only Leucettine L41 dosing in a Down syndrome mouse model, then followed the offspring through to adulthood and ran cognitive and neuroanatomical endpoints. Prenatal DYRK1A inhibition produces measurable long-term neurodevelopmental and cognitive improvements in the offspring, even after the drug is washed out before birth. The result is encouraging both as a proof-of-principle for the window-of-treatment hypothesis and as motivation for the next-generation DYRK1A inhibitor programs that are starting to mature in industry. The translational caveat is the usual one — prenatal pharmacology in humans carries safety hurdles that the mouse model doesn't capture — but the postnatal-rescue story might be the clinically tractable framing, and that's the natural next experiment.

Item eight, a clinical-psychology piece with a counterintuitive framing — anhedonia buffers the effects of early-life unpredictability on threat-reward decision-making, from a Yassa–Bornstein collaboration with Baram, Hartley, and others. Early-life unpredictability — the construct that captures inconsistent caregiving and chaotic developmental environments — has been linked to a pattern of poor decision-making under threat-and-reward conflict in adulthood. Separately, anhedonia — diminished capacity to experience or anticipate pleasure — is a transdiagnostic symptom of depression and one of its most treatment-resistant features. The expectation would be that the two compound each other: a developmentally vulnerable adult who also has anhedonia would make worse decisions under threat-reward conflict than either alone. They ran a large behavioral study with retrospective unpredictability assessment and current anhedonia measurement, and found the opposite. Anhedonia buffers the effects of early-life unpredictability on the threat-reward decision-making pattern. The interpretive frame is provocative — the reward-blunting that defines anhedonia clinically may serve a compensatory regulatory function in adults whose developmental history makes them vulnerable to maladaptive reward-driven choices under threat. For the psychopharmacology of depression with developmental-trauma background, this is the kind of finding that should make clinicians careful about which symptoms the treatment is targeting. The standard SSRI-and-bupropion-style intervention that restores reward sensitivity may, in the developmentally-unpredictable subgroup, restore the maladaptive decision-making pattern that the anhedonia was suppressing. Worth carrying into any conversation about ketamine, psilocybin, or fast-acting antidepressant interventions in patients with adverse-childhood-experience histories.

Item nine, on the optogenetic-tool engineering side with a protein-language-model angle worth pulling on — a mutation at position 300 recommended by protein language models gives ChrimsonR amplified photocurrent response, from a Boyden-and-Forest-affiliated team at Georgia Tech and MIT. ChrimsonR is a red-shifted channelrhodopsin variant widely used for deep-tissue optogenetic activation. The engineering goal is brighter photocurrent at lower light power. The traditional path is structure-guided mutagenesis on the retinal-binding pocket, which is slow and inefficient. The new path is to use protein language models — the same class of foundation models that have transformed structure prediction — to suggest functionally consequential mutations directly from sequence context. The model nominates position 300 with a specific mutation, ChrimsonR-E300. They build it, express it, and measure. The mutant has amplified photocurrent response over the wild-type variant, with a usable kinetic profile. This is a small paper with a big methodological implication. Protein language models are starting to suggest single-residue mutations that materially improve channel function in a real working tool — without explicit structural modeling, without screening libraries. The same approach is plausibly tractable for receptors of pharmacological interest, including the G-protein-coupled receptor optimization problem for biased ligands and stabilized active states. If you're running tool-engineering campaigns in your lab, this is the methodology to evaluate now, before the field commodifies it.

Closing item, and a methodology provocation in computational pharmacology — Topological Pharmacokinetics: Reading the Shape of Drug Disposition from Data. The conventional pharmacokinetic toolbox reduces a concentration-time curve to scalar summaries — area under the curve, maximum concentration, clearance, half-life. The summary loses the shape information. The proposal here is that the topology of the concentration-time curve — its persistent geometric and topological features under techniques borrowed from topological data analysis — carries mechanistic information about drug disposition that the scalars discard. The framework distinguishes drugs with similar areas-under-the-curve but different disposition mechanisms, and they argue it could detect formulation differences, drug-drug interaction patterns, and individual pharmacokinetic outliers that conventional summaries miss. Whether this becomes a regulatory or industry-grade tool depends on validation work that this paper doesn't quite do — they show the framework works on the cases they look at, but the translation to model-informed drug development isn't yet there. The reason to flag it is that the field has been doing pharmacokinetics with the same scalar summaries for fifty years, and the data-rich era of dense pharmacokinetic sampling and physiologically-based pharmacokinetic modeling has not seriously revisited the summary-statistic question. This is one of the few papers that does. Worth keeping in the methods queue. That's the slate. Back tomorrow.
