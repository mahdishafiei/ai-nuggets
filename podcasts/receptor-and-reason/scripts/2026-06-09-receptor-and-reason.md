## Script

Welcome to Receptor and Reason. Tuesday, June ninth, twenty twenty-six. Ten items today, with a heavy bioRxiv preprint week and a couple of agentic AI items from arXiv. I'll open with what I think is the most architecturally interesting result on the table, then move through stress and addiction circuits, neurodegeneration, brain injury repair, and close with computational pharmacology and a surprising neuromodulation finding.

The opener is from Mark von Zastrow's group at UCSF, and it tackles a question that has been quietly nagging the neuromodulation field for the last several years. Modern transcriptomics has revealed that individual neurons co-express far more neuromodulatory G-protein-coupled receptors than anyone expected. A single hippocampal pyramidal neuron expresses dozens of receptors that all converge on a much smaller number of G protein transducers. The standard worry is that this should impose an information bottleneck. If four different receptors all signal through the same cyclic AMP cascade, how does the cell tell them apart? The von Zastrow group focused on exactly that case, four Gs-coupled receptors natively co-expressed in essentially all hippocampal pyramidal neurons, all elevating cyclic AMP. And on acute activation, the cyclic AMP elevations look basically identical. But downstream, the effects diverge dramatically. The receptors differ in their ability to drive transcriptional responses, and they differ profoundly in temporal stability, with neuropeptide receptors remaining responsive for hours while monoamine receptors rapidly desensitize. The conclusion is that the effective chemical bandwidth of GPCR neuromodulation is not actually capped by transducer count. Temporal coding, kinetics of desensitization, downstream divergence, all of these open a much richer multiplex. For drug discovery this matters because it reframes how we think about polypharmacology and biased agonism. Two ligands that produce identical acute second-messenger profiles can still drive very different cellular outcomes if they engage receptors with different desensitization kinetics. That is not a new idea in isolation, but seeing it laid out across four co-expressed receptors converging on the same cascade is a clean architectural argument.

Staying with receptor pharmacology, Kasia Radwanska's lab at the Nencki Institute reports convergent mouse and human evidence that estrogen receptor alpha in the basolateral amygdala drives alcohol craving in females. Female mice in their IntelliCage paradigm stratify cleanly into alcohol-use-disorder-prone and resistant phenotypes. In the prone group, amygdala transcriptomics identified ER-alpha as a top transcription factor for differentially expressed genes, and although bulk receptor levels did not change with alcohol exposure, inter-individual variation in receptor protein levels positively correlated with cue-induced alcohol seeking. Local knockdown of the receptor in the basolateral amygdala reduced excitatory synaptic transmission, attenuated motivation, blocked cue-induced seeking, and impaired the cue-associated memory recall, without touching anxiety-like behavior. Ovariectomy produced parallel effects, so endogenous ovarian hormones are doing real work here. On the human side, three ESR-one polymorphisms tracked with binge drinking, consumption days, and craving in a longitudinal clinical cohort, and ESR-one transcript was elevated in blood from women diagnosed with alcohol use disorder. The female-specific framing here is the part that drug discovery has historically been bad at, and a circuit-defined estrogen-receptor target in the amygdala is a more tractable handle than a generic systemic estrogen signaling story.

Liping Wang's group at the Shenzhen Institutes of Advanced Technology has a neuroimmune paper that links REM sleep to stress susceptibility through a peripheral-to-central pathway. The chain runs from chronic stress to increased splenic nerve activity, then recruitment of circulating monocytes into the brain through the choroid plexus, then regionally restricted microglial remodeling in the external globus pallidus toward a pro-inflammatory state, then IL-six and gp-one-thirty signaling onto parvalbumin-positive neurons in the same region, which become persistently more excitable. The behavioral payoff is that activity of those parvalbumin neurons during REM sleep, but not during wakefulness, bidirectionally regulates anxiety-like and defensive behaviors. The framing is unusual because it positions REM sleep itself as the gating physiological state for a neuroimmune circuit, not as a downstream readout of stress. If that holds up, it nominates REM-sleep-locked interventions as a category for stress-related anxiety, which is an angle that has been more talked about than mechanistically grounded. The caveat is that the GPe parvalbumin specificity is a strong claim that the field will want replicated outside this group.

Adjacent in topic, Fereshteh Nugent's lab at the Uniformed Services University of the Health Sciences characterizes a previously unrecognized intrinsic population of corticotropin-releasing-factor-expressing neurons in the lateral habenula. The field knew the lateral habenula receives CRF inputs but the sources were poorly defined. These intrinsic CRF neurons are mostly VGLUT-two positive, with a rostral subpopulation co-expressing GAD-two. Chemogenetic activation does not change place preference or generic anxiety-like behavior, but it biases defensive strategies toward passive action-locking during the visual looming shadow test, with prolonged escape latencies in males and prolonged post-escape shelter stays in females. The sex dimorphism is structural as well as behavioral. Males have more of these neurons and they are intrinsically more excitable; females have stronger local excitatory connectivity. As a circuit, this is interesting because it puts intrinsic CRF signaling, not just CRF input from elsewhere, inside the lateral habenula, which is already a central hub for stress-related computation. Defensive strategy switching has been a recurrent target for PTSD-adjacent therapeutics, and a sex-dimorphic intrinsic CRF cell type is a much more specific handle than a generic CRF receptor antagonist program.

From Yohei Kondo's group at Nagoya University, a computational neurodegeneration paper that is genuinely useful as a tool. The problem they tackle is that tau spreads heterogeneously across the human brain, and inferring spatially resolved propagation kinetics from tau PET data is a high-dimensional inverse problem that has been intractable at whole-brain scale. They build a differentiable reaction-diffusion framework that takes tau PET data plus MRI-informed forward simulation and uses error backpropagation to reconstruct voxel-wise tau amplification rates across the human brain. The inferred maps reveal structured non-uniform amplification patterns that are not visible in static tau burden maps. There is cross-individual consistency and meaningful inter-individual variability, and integrating transcriptomic data identifies gene-expression programs that track regional amplification. The pharmacology angle is that tau-targeting therapeutics are nominally targeting a propagation process, and a method that gives you per-voxel propagation kinetics rather than per-voxel burden is the kind of biomarker substrate that anti-tau trials have been wanting. Whether differentiable simulation generalizes from research cohorts to clinical-trial settings is still open, but as a piece of methodology this is a clear step up from regression-style tau-burden analyses.

Sticking with Alzheimer's, a paper from Fumiaki Ito's group at the Institute of Prophylactic Pharmacology on a five-residue catalytic peptide that cleaves amyloid-beta. The peptide is SKGQA. The framing is "catalytide", meaning small synthetic peptides that mimic the proteolytic activity of full serine proteases despite being orders of magnitude smaller. They use HADDOCK docking plus molecular dynamics simulations to show that SKGQA does not work through a static active site. Instead it operates through a dynamic process where the amyloid-beta substrate itself serves as a scaffold that stabilizes serine-protease-like active geometries out of a flexible conformational ensemble of the peptide. They distinguish a stable-binding mode and a stochastic-attack mode, which together explain how a peptide this small could cleave at multiple sites. The therapeutic argument they make is about access. Larger proteases struggle to penetrate dense amyloid environments, and a five-mer should diffuse freely. This is preclinical and the leap from molecular dynamics plus docking to in-vivo amyloid clearance is large, but it is one of the more interesting recent computational-pharmacology bets on amyloid-degrading agents, and the substrate-induced anchoring mechanism is a useful concept for thinking about other amyloid-targeting catalytic peptides.

Richard Lee's group at Harvard reports that recombinant growth differentiation factor eleven, a circulating TGF-beta family protein, improves neurological recovery in mouse models of intracerebral hemorrhage and traumatic brain injury. GDF-eleven has prior data in ischemic stroke; this paper extends it to hemorrhagic stroke induced by intrastriatal collagenase, and to closed-head TBI from a pneumatic impactor. The treated animals show improved neurobehavioral scores on three independent assays out to twenty-eight days, increased vascular area, increased neuronal density, and reduced microglial and macrophage density in the hemorrhage model. In the TBI model, rotarod latency is improved by day six and the composite score by day twenty-eight. There are no approved therapies for functional recovery from either condition, so the bar for a regenerative biologic is low in terms of competition and high in terms of skepticism. GDF-eleven has had a complicated history in the regeneration literature, with replication disputes from the parabiosis era, but the brain-injury body of work is now multiple independent papers, and a single recombinant protein hitting two anatomically and mechanistically distinct injury models with a consistent functional readout is at least worth a careful look from translational neurology.

A quick stop in hippocampal circuit pharmacology from Jelena Radulovic's lab at Albert Einstein. They ask whether CA-two contributes to trace fear conditioning. The result has a non-obvious twist. Chronic inhibition of CA-two decreases cue-associated freezing, but acute inhibition of the CA-two to CA-one projection specifically increases freezing. Combined with differential c-Fos patterns, this says that global and projection-specific perturbations of CA-two have opposing effects on the same task, which is the kind of dissociation that bites you when you try to drug a hippocampal subfield. Fiber photometry shows that CA-two-to-CA-one activity shifts from tone responsiveness during conditioning to expected-shock activation during recall, consistent with learning-driven remodeling. For PTSD-relevant trace fear circuits, the takeaway is that CA-two is a contributor, and projection-specific manipulation gives a different answer from cellular-level manipulation. Drug-target selection in this region needs to be aware of that.

Two arXiv items now, both posted yesterday. The first is from the Noah AI team, titled "AI Scientists Are Only as Good as Their Evidence", and it is a stratified ablation of a production drug-asset valuation agent. They run three arms. Arm A is a plain web-only large language model analyst. Arm B adds public structured tools, a fourteen-dimension valuation playbook, a verifier, an objectivity policy, and a red-team. Arm C adds a proprietary curated corpus of pipeline, trial, and deal intelligence. Across a thirteen-asset benchmark, arm B improves calibration and audit discipline, tier-in-range accuracy goes from zero-point-eight-zero to zero-point-eight-nine, objectivity goes from three-point-one-six to three-point-three. But under capability-superset accounting, arm A and arm B recover only zero-point-two-five and zero-point-three-eight of the curated gold competitive record, while arm C recovers zero-point-nine-six. On a long-tail subset the gap is even larger. They introduce a completeness-aware decision-utility metric, defined as decision quality times gold coverage, and on that arm C scores seven-point-four-three against one-point-seven-six and two-point-five-seven for the other arms. The framing the authors push is that for knowledge-intensive scientific decisions the limiting factor is not the model or the scaffolding but the evidence substrate the agent can access. Reasoning scaffolds improve calibration and discipline but they cannot exceed a ceiling set by what is in the corpus. This is a useful counterweight to the current wave of pure scaffolding papers. The caveat is that the entire result is from a single vendor's production agent on a thirteen-asset benchmark, and "proprietary corpus matters more than reasoning" is exactly what a vendor of a proprietary corpus would want to show. The internal accounting still matters because the ablation is controlled, but expect external replication to take a while.

The second arXiv item is on closed-loop LLM molecular design from a group focused on what they call analysis-driven iteration. The framing is sharp. Current LLM molecular design loops generate a candidate, score it with a single scalar, and reject or accept. That is informed trial-and-error, not reasoning. They replace the scalar with the full physicochemical rationale from first-principles calculations, feeding orbital energies, atomic charges, and electron densities back into a retrieval-augmented self-reflection module. On HOMO-LUMO gap targets between one and five electron volts, the deviation drops to as low as zero-point-zero-zero-zero-three electron volts, with a hundred percent success rate on moderate tasks. The framework generalizes to dipole moment design and works across five different LLM backbones. The substantive point is that when the model is told not only that a molecule failed but why, in mechanistic terms, the loop becomes genuinely causal rather than stochastic. This is a clean demonstration on an electronic-structure target where ground truth is computable from first principles. Whether it extends to bioactivity targets, where the ground truth is empirical and noisy, is the open question. But as a proof of concept for replacing scalar feedback with mechanistic rationale, the result is convincing.

To close, a lighter item that is mechanistically pretty. Natale Sciolino's group at the University of Connecticut shows that phasic activation of the locus coeruleus reshapes population coding of taste in the primary gustatory cortex. They use miniscope calcium imaging in awake mice combined with optogenetic LC activation, and they look at how three taste attributes are encoded, palatability, mixture ratio, and intensity. Phasic LC activation increases correlations between gustatory cortex responses and palatability, and expands the dynamic range of the population representation along a palatability-relevant axis. The expansion is driven primarily by an aversive shift in the representation of every tastant except sucrose, the most palatable stimulus. For mixture ratio and concentration the population geometry both stretches and rotates. Tonic LC activation does not produce the same expansion. The bottom line is that phasic norepinephrine release does not just gain-modulate sensory cortex, it reshapes the geometry of how taste attributes are represented. For affect-relevant sensory coding, that is the right kind of result. Whether the same principle generalizes to LC modulation of other primary sensory cortices, or whether gustatory cortex is uniquely positioned because of its affective tie-in, is what to watch next.
