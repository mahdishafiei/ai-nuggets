## Script

Welcome to Receptor and Reason. Wednesday, June tenth, twenty twenty-six. Eleven items today. Heavy on receptor pharmacology and addiction circuits in the first half, then schizophrenia and Parkinson's mechanism, a new neuroimmune drug target, deep brain stimulation, and an agentic AI close. I'll open with a paper that takes a fresh swing at a question the SSRI literature has been picking at for thirty years.

That opener is from Valentina Mosienko's group at the University of Bristol, on how fluoxetine actually elevates cyclic AMP in astrocytes. The puzzle is familiar. Selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors are the first-line treatment for depression, prescriptions have grown about fifty percent in the last decade, but synaptic serotonin elevation alone does not explain the therapeutic delay, the inter-patient variance, or the downstream pathway changes. There has been growing evidence that astrocytes are part of the story, and specifically that astrocytic cyclic AMP signaling, which is downregulated in depression, gets restored by SSRIs. What was missing was the wiring. This paper provides it. In primary rat astrocytes carrying FRET-based cyclic AMP sensors, fluoxetine elevates intracellular cyclic AMP by twenty-eight percent without changing calcium. That increase is blocked by either a 5-HT2B antagonist or an adenosine 2B antagonist, which is the first clue. Then, using an extracellular ATP sensor, they show fluoxetine pushes astrocytes to release ATP, and that release depends on 5-HT2B. Then they deplete microglia, and the cyclic AMP elevation goes away while extracellular ATP rises. The whole cascade is: SSRI engages astrocyte 5-HT2B, astrocyte releases ATP, microglia convert ATP to adenosine, adenosine engages astrocyte adenosine 2B, cyclic AMP goes up. So fluoxetine's astrocyte effect is not direct on the astrocyte. It requires glial crosstalk. For drug discovery this matters because it nominates 5-HT2B on astrocytes and adenosine 2B as parts of the active mechanism, and it puts microglia in the loop in a way that has implications for any psychiatric population with altered microglial state. The caveat is primary culture, not in vivo, and 5-HT2B has its own cardiovascular liability profile, but the mechanistic clarity is the contribution.

Staying with receptor pharmacology, Loren Martin's lab at the University of Toronto Mississauga has a clean dissection of where placebo analgesia lives in the brain. They build a mouse conditioning paradigm after peripheral nerve injury, where pairing morphine with environmental cues produces, after a washout, robust pain suppression in the absence of drug. That gives them a tractable mouse model of conditioned placebo analgesia. Whole-brain c-Fos mapping shows distributed cortical and subcortical changes, with a functional connectivity reorganization that shifts hubs and selectively rewires inter-regional coupling. Causal manipulation narrows the circuit. Excitatory activation of the anterior cingulate cortex blocks placebo analgesia, while inhibitory manipulation has no effect. Basomedial amygdala and paraventricular thalamus are not necessary. The clinching experiment uses cell-type-specific access to mu-opioid-receptor-expressing neurons in anterior cingulate, and shows that this specific population is required for the placebo response. So endogenous opioid signaling in a specific cortical cell type gates placebo analgesia, and other nominated regions are not in the causal path. For pain pharmacology this is useful because placebo response is a confound and a target at the same time. A cortical opioid circuit that is necessary for placebo is something you could potentially augment, or learn to avoid in trial design.

Next, from Alexey Ostroumov's lab at Georgetown, a paper on how acute stress potentiates operant reward learning through a circuit-specific change in midbrain GABA transmission. The prior work from this group had shown that acute stress downregulates the potassium-chloride cotransporter KCC2, a developmental regulator of chloride gradients in inhibitory transmission, specifically in GABA neurons of the ventral tegmental area, and that this depolarizing shift enhances mesolimbic dopamine signaling and Pavlovian cue-reward learning. The new paper extends this to instrumental learning. Prior acute restraint stress enhances acquisition of operant sucrose self-administration in both male and female rats. Mechanistically, GABA release events onto dopamine neurons become more temporally coincident, and excitability of GABAergic inputs rises, but only onto dopamine neurons projecting to the nucleus accumbens lateral shell and the dorsomedial striatum. Circuit specificity within mesolimbic and nigrostriatal systems is one of the harder things to demonstrate convincingly, and they have it here. The rescue uses CLP290, a KCC2 enhancer that has been used as a tool compound for studying chloride homeostasis, and it normalizes both the inhibitory transmission and the stress-induced learning potentiation. As a piece of addiction pharmacology this is a clean target validation for KCC2 enhancers in stress-driven reward learning, which is one of the relapse-relevant phenotypes that addiction therapeutics have struggled to drug.

Picking up the addiction thread, Alexander Chubykin's group at Purdue has a paper in Neuropsychopharmacology on the GABAergic neurons of the ventrolateral periaqueductal gray and their role in fentanyl withdrawal. Whole-brain Fos mapping during fentanyl withdrawal flags the periaqueductal gray as a central hub, and within it the ventrolateral subdivision is where the action concentrates. GABAergic neurons there show increased Fos and increased inhibitory synaptic transmission during withdrawal, mapped with channelrhodopsin-assisted circuit mapping. Chemogenetic inhibition of those GABA neurons alleviates withdrawal-induced hyperalgesia, the pain-sensitization symptom that drives a lot of relapse in clinical opioid use disorder. The piece is mechanistically tight and the translational hook is real, because hyperalgesia during medication-assisted treatment is a problem that doesn't currently have a non-opioid pharmacological lever. A vlPAG GABA-specific intervention would obviously require a delivery solution, but the target validation is now in place.

A short item with strong audience appeal from Bin Gu's group at Ohio State, also in Neuropsychopharmacology, on cannabidiol in Angelman syndrome. CBD is heavily prescribed off-label across neurodevelopmental disorders, often ahead of any controlled data. In a Ube3a-deficient mouse model of Angelman, chronic CBD increased REM sleep during the dark cycle, restored the so-called siesta phase that is disrupted in this model, improved sleep homeostasis, and reduced spontaneous seizures after flurothyl kindling. This is a small, focused phenotypic paper, but the question it answers is one that pediatric neurology has been waiting on. Angelman has prominent sleep and seizure components, and the chronic CBD signal on both, in a genetic model, is informative for trial design. It is also an example of CBD pharmacology moving past the post-Epidiolex haze, where everyone agreed CBD does something in some seizure syndromes but the syndrome-specific story remained anecdotal.

Now to a paper that is going to shape thinking about schizophrenia mechanism for the next several years. Hyejung Won and Eva Anton at the University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill perform in vivo CRISPR droplet sequencing in the postnatal mouse neocortex, knocking down twelve schizophrenia risk genes previously connected by careful fine-mapping to functionally validated common risk variants. Single-cell sequencing on the perturbed cells finds three thousand differentially expressed genes that recapitulate transcriptional changes seen in postmortem schizophrenia brains, so the in vivo perturbation is at least partially recovering the real disease signal. Integrating across analytical frameworks, the convergent program that comes out is ciliary. Primary cilia, the small antenna-like organelles that most neurons and glia carry, run gene programs that are repeatedly altered across these twelve genes. Perturbing key contributors to those ciliary programs changes ciliary structure. Schizophrenia genetics has been trying to identify convergent biology for two decades, and the cilium has not been on most short lists. The implication is that synapse-independent signaling, the kind that runs through primary cilia for things like sonic hedgehog and certain G-protein-coupled receptor cascades, is part of the schizophrenia mechanism. That is a different drug discovery surface than the synaptic and glutamatergic frames the field has been working from. It is early, the perturbation is in mouse cortex, but the analytical convergence across DEG clustering, factor analysis, and gene regulatory network inference is the kind of triangulation that tends to hold up.

Adjacent in neurogenetics but in Parkinson's, Wolfdieter Springer and Fabienne Fiesel at the Mayo Clinic publish on PINK1 loss in astrocytes. PINK1 is the canonical mitophagy kinase whose loss causes recessive Parkinson's, and the field's default model has been that this loss matters because dopaminergic neurons can't clear damaged mitochondria. This paper shows that astrocytes also have robust PINK1 activity, and that human PINK1 mutant astrocytes undergo what the authors call homeostatic collapse, with bulk transcriptomic and protein-level changes. Co-culture experiments show that these dysfunctional astrocytes kill neurons through non-cell-autonomous mechanisms, meaning the neurons themselves don't need to lose PINK1 to die. The rescuing intervention is pharmacological enhancement of autophagy, which suppresses the inflammatory secretome and reduces neuronal damage. For Parkinson's drug discovery this is a meaningful reframing because mitophagy enhancers are usually pitched at neurons, and the data here say at least part of the disease-relevant pathology runs through glia. Whether any of the current mitophagy enhancers in development engage astrocytes adequately is now a relevant question.

A drug discovery item from Moustafa Gabr's group at Weill Cornell Medicine on LILRB4, which goes by ILT3. It's a myeloid immune-checkpoint receptor that, in microglia, restricts amyloid clearance through an ApoE-dependent signaling axis. ApoE genotype is the strongest common genetic risk factor for late-onset Alzheimer's, so a microglial receptor that gates ApoE-driven amyloid clearance is a natural drug target. The paper uses affinity selection mass spectrometry against an Enamine fragment library, identifies direct binders to ILT3, and the lead compound, called LT12, binds with nanomolar affinity by microscale thermophoresis and surface plasmon resonance. They map the binding pocket computationally and validate it with mutagenesis. Functionally, LT12 disrupts the ILT3-ApoE interaction, dampens SHP1 and SHP2 signaling, attenuates NF-kappa-B activation, reduces IL-1-beta secretion, and restores amyloid beta uptake in human iPSC-derived microglia. In 5xFAD mice, LT12 improves cognition, reduces amyloid burden, and attenuates neuroinflammation. This is a first-in-class small-molecule ILT3 inhibitor and the company-grade biophysics is unusually clean for an academic paper. The neuroimmune-checkpoint frame puts it adjacent to the TREM2 program in Alzheimer's, where modulating microglial state to favor clearance is the operating hypothesis. It is preclinical and 5xFAD has well-known limitations as an AD model, but the target validation package is one of the more thorough ones to land this year.

Now a piece of clinical neuromodulation from Alik Widge's group at the University of Minnesota, on which prefrontal pathways actually carry the cognitive-control benefit of deep brain stimulation at the ventral internal capsule and ventral striatum. The VCVS target is used in DBS for several treatment-refractory psychiatric conditions, and the clinical response is variable. Four DBS patients did the multi-source interference task, a cognitive control measure, under twenty-seven stimulation settings varying by side, electrode, amplitude, and frequency. Personalized pathway-activation models quantified axonal recruitment across eleven prefrontal fiber bundles per hemisphere. The lateralization result is striking. Right-sided stimulation improves reaction time more than left-sided. The pathways that predict faster RT are right dorsal fibers connecting to dorsolateral and dorsomedial prefrontal cortex. No left-sided pathways significantly predict improvement. For psychiatric DBS programming, where the trial-and-error space is enormous and the patient cost of a bad setting is high, having a small set of imaging-defined target pathways to optimize toward is the kind of result that should translate fairly directly into clinic. The caveat is n equals four, but the within-subject design across twenty-seven settings is what makes this informative beyond the patient count.

To the agentic AI items. The first is LinkD, from Kuan-lin Huang's group at the Icahn School of Medicine at Mount Sinai, an integrated drug-repurposing platform unifying four lines of evidence. The first is binding affinity, computed by a diffusion-based model across fourteen thousand nine hundred eighty-one drugs and twenty thousand three hundred eighty-five human targets, which they report ranks first on eight of nine standard drug-target affinity benchmarks, with the biggest gains in cold-start conditions. The second is proteome-wide selectivity scoring combined with molecular docking, which recovers about ninety-five percent of known drug-target pairs. The third is phenotypic validation against drug-sensitivity and CRISPR-dependency data from nine hundred sixty cancer cell lines. The fourth is population-scale clinical evidence using eleven-point-five million individuals from Mount Sinai records and the UK Biobank. The agentic layer, called LinkD-Agent, orchestrates all four. Their headline real-world result is that LinkD-prioritized beta-blockers, propranolol and carvedilol, were associated with reduced five-year prostate-cancer incidence relative to metoprolol, with hazard ratios of zero-point-eight-two and zero-point-nine-two, corroborated by docking against the beta-2 adrenergic receptor and growth inhibition in a prostate cancer cell line. The architectural argument is that prior repurposing platforms used a single line of evidence and rarely validated across biological scales. The cross-scale integration here, in particular hooking population-scale clinical outcomes into the same agentic pipeline as molecular docking, is the part that is genuinely new. The caveat the field will pick at is that retrospective hazard-ratio reductions can absorb a lot of confounding, and any real propranolol prostate cancer signal will need prospective testing, but the framework is one of the more serious agentic drug-repurposing systems we've seen.

To close, an arXiv item titled Dep-LLM, on training-free depression detection from clinical interviews using off-the-shelf foundation models. The clinical task they are after is automatic depression detection from long, multi-topic interview transcripts, where the relevant signal is sparse and the labeled data is limited because of clinical privacy. They build a three-stage pipeline. First, a chain-of-thought module decomposes the long dialogue into five clinically aligned themes and produces evidence-grounded rationales for each, addressing the long-context dependency problem. Second, a confidence module quantifies the epistemic reliability of each rationale from token-level entropy and modulates within-label and across-theme contributions to amplify trustworthy signals and suppress uncertain ones, with no extra training. Third, the final diagnosis integrates the multi-factor signals weighted by confidence. The result is that the framework beats the zero-shot baseline on essentially all twenty-one foundation models tested across nine metrics on the standard depression-interview corpora, and outperforms supervised domain-specific models and the latest closed-source commercial models, again without training. The interesting design choice here is treating the LLM less as a diagnostic agent and more as a structured reasoning engine that is forced to surface evidence per theme and then weight its own contributions by entropy. For computational psychiatry, the test that matters next is generalization to real clinical interviews outside the DAIC corpora, but as a training-free baseline that beats trained specialized systems, this is a meaningful data point on what's achievable when scaffolding is doing the heavy lifting rather than fine-tuning.
