## Script

Welcome to the Scripps Biomedical Brief for Sunday, June twenty-first. Four fresh bioRxiv preprints — a new chemical-probe inhibitor of the essential motor protein that powers malaria, toxoplasma, cryptosporidium, and babesia invasion; a modular riboswitch architecture that decouples ligand sensing from transcriptional control; an atypical NF-kappa-B family member that doubles as both a tumor-infiltration and anti-exhaustion lever for CD8 T cells; and a structurally encoded spontaneous post-translational modification that buys a high-affinity binding protein twenty degrees of thermostability.

First, from Gary Ward's group at Vermont with Nicholas Westwood at Saint Andrews and Julia Schaletzky at Berkeley, identification of UCB-9721 as a potent inhibitor of MyoA, the unconventional class XIV myosin motor that drives the gliding motility apicomplexan parasites use to invade host cells. The team screened over fifty thousand small molecules against Toxoplasma MyoA actin-activated ATPase activity, and the top hit was nearly forty-fold more potent than KNX-002, the previously best-described chemical probe in this class, both as an enzyme inhibitor and at blocking parasite motility, with no detectable mammalian-cell toxicity. The compound is also active against Plasmodium falciparum, Cryptosporidium parvum, and Babesia duncani, meaning one chemical handle reaches across malaria, toxoplasmosis, cryptosporidiosis, and babesiosis. Comparative docking, targeted MyoA mutagenesis, and a directed structure-activity series pinpoint why the new scaffold gets the potency jump — the sulfonamide group hydrogen-bonds with arginine 249 and glutamate 275 inside the MyoA pocket through a structured water network, and breaking those contacts collapses activity. For neglected-tropical-disease drug discovery this is an unusual win, because the target itself has no close mammalian homologue with the same mechanism, the chemistry is tractable, and pan-phylum activity is rare enough to be worth a real medicinal-chemistry campaign rather than just another tool compound.

Second, from Julius Lucks's group at Northwestern, a new transcriptional riboswitch architecture they call tug-of-war. Standard transcriptional riboswitches rely on sequence overlap between the ligand-binding aptamer and the downstream expression platform, and that overlap is the historical bottleneck for engineering them, because any change to one half tends to break the other. The new design removes the constraint entirely. A ligand-stabilized RNA structure placed immediately upstream of an intrinsic terminator suppresses termination by physically competing with the nascent terminator hairpin for occupancy of the RNA polymerase exit channel — antitermination by steric occlusion. They built four working tug-of-war riboswitches by mixing and matching different aptamers with different terminators, and the bioinformatic follow-up shows about ten percent of natural ZTP riboswitches, almost all in gram-positive bacteria, are already using this mechanism in the wild. For programs that want ligand-controlled gene circuits in cell or gene therapy, modularity is the headline — you can now port a known aptamer onto a known terminator without engineering each junction by hand.

Third, from Patrick Hogan and Anjana Rao at the La Jolla Institute for Immunology, the atypical I-kappa-B factor I-kappa-B-delta, encoded by Nfkbid, as a transcriptional lever that simultaneously addresses two of the cardinal failures of CD8 T cells inside solid tumors — they don't accumulate, and the ones that do are exhausted. Nfkbid is an NFAT target gene expressed in CD8 effectors and at modest levels in tumor-infiltrating lymphocytes. Genetic depletion of Nfkbid impairs TIL accumulation and worsens tumor growth; ectopic overexpression expands TILs, suppresses exhaustion-associated transcription factors and inhibitory receptors, and elevates cytotoxic molecule production, with measurable improvement in tumor control. The structurally clean part is that the two phenotypes split between two parts of the protein. The shared core spanning the ankyrin-repeat domain that contacts NF-kappa-B is sufficient to drive accumulation. The roughly one-hundred-fifty-residue N-terminal region is what's needed for the anti-exhaustion and effector-function piece. For CAR-T programs that have been chasing these two bottlenecks with separate engineering approaches, this is a single transgenic handle that engages both, with domain-level specificity for which output to amplify.

Fourth, from Paola Laurino's group at OIST with Ben Clifton, a paper that turns a spontaneous post-translational modification into an engineering feature. They show that a high-affinity solute-binding protein from the abundant marine bacterium Pelagibacter contains an unusually stable succinimide residue formed by spontaneous cyclization of asparagine 269. The protein needs that succinimide. An alanine substitution drops the melting temperature by more than twenty degrees and reduces binding affinity for the substrate, the amino-acid derivative 5-oxoproline, by fifteen to fifty fold. High-resolution crystallography and tandem mass spectrometry independently confirm the modification in situ. Across SAR11 homologues the position is occupied by either asparagine or aspartate, and the aspartate variant also partially cyclizes into the same succinimide. The implication for biologics design is concrete — succinimide is normally treated as a degradation product to be suppressed in formulation, but in the right structural context it is evidently a stable, encodable handle for boosting both affinity and thermostability without engineered disulfides, crosslinks, or non-canonical amino acids.
