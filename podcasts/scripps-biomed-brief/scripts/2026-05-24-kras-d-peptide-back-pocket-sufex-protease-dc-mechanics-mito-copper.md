## Script

Good morning. Today's briefing covers four moves spanning a new RAS modality, a new protease chemical-biology platform, a mechanically programmed cell therapy, and a structural redefinition of mitochondrial copper biology: an all-D macrocyclic peptide that engages a cryptic KRAS back pocket distinct from the switch-two site, a sulfur-fluoride electrophile platform that builds covalent probes against non-catalytic protease residues, matrix stress-relaxation programming of dendritic cell function, and copper as a structural stabilizer of the TCA cycle lipoylation machinery.

First, Matthew Mitcheltree and colleagues at Merck Sharp and Dohme report that mirror-image mRNA display has yielded all-D macrocyclic peptide ligands targeting a previously unexploited cryptic back pocket on KRAS — distinct from the switch-two pocket that every approved RAS-targeting compound and every advanced RAS macrocycle engages. The strategy is the load-bearing piece. Conventional mRNA display screens libraries of natural L-peptides against a wild-type target; mirror-image display screens an L-peptide library against the chemically synthesized mirror-image D-protein target, and the hits, re-synthesized as their mirror image, become all-D ligands of the native L-target — proteolytically stable by construction and accessible to epitopes that natural-chirality peptides cannot reach. Applied to KRAS, the campaign delivered nanomolar peptide binders that engage KRAS in both the inactive guanosine-diphosphate-bound state and the active guanosine-triphosphate-bound state with equal affinity, exploit a single-residue difference among RAS isoforms to bind KRAS selectively over HRAS and NRAS, and inhibit oncogenic signaling in KRAS-mutant cells through a mechanism that does not depend on switch-two engagement. The strategic significance is the combination axis. Switch-two-pocket-targeting drugs — sotorasib, adagrasib, the pan-RAS-on macrocycle from Revolution, the molecular glue approach from BridgeBio — all share an epitope where resistance mutations cluster; a structurally orthogonal binding site that hits both nucleotide states gives a partner for combination therapy and a fallback after switch-two resistance, and mirror-image display as a general platform extends to any cytosolic target that today escapes proteolytically stable peptide pharmacology.

Second, Maximilian Lakemeyer's group at the University of Bath, with Maria Barrueco and collaborators, introduces covalent macrocyclic activity-based probes that selectively label non-catalytic residues in serine protease active sites — a generalization of the activity-based-probe toolkit beyond the conserved catalytic serine that dominates current protease chemoproteomics. The team combined phage-display selection of macrocyclic peptides against a target protease with systematic electrophile scanning that installs sulfur-six-fluoride electrophiles — sometimes written as SuFEx warheads — at defined positions on the macrocyclic scaffold. The geometry of the macrocycle positions the electrophile such that it engages alternative nucleophiles, specifically lysines and tyrosines, rather than the catalytic serine. Applied to plasma kallikrein and to urokinase plasminogen activator, the platform yielded probes with rapid and complete covalent labeling, and small changes in electrophile chemistry produced tuneable differences in covalent kinetics — including a benzenesulfonyl fluoride derivative that drove fast and complete modification of an active-site lysine, robust even in complex biological samples. The translational read is that selectivity across closely related serine proteases — historically a major obstacle for the catalytic-serine-targeted toolkit because the catalytic serine is identical — becomes accessible when the warhead engages a residue that varies among family members.

Third, Yu-Chia Chen and Kyle Vining at the University of Pennsylvania report that the stress-relaxation rate of the matrix in which dendritic cells are cultured durably programs their functional state — even after the mechanical cue is removed. Stem cell-derived dendritic cells cultured in fast-relaxing matrices acquired enhanced antigen presentation, faster migration, and higher expression of T-cell-recruiting chemokines, while slow-relaxing matrices biased the cells toward pro-inflammatory cytokine secretion with gene signatures of lipid accumulation and stress response. The mechanical programming is conserved across human and murine dendritic cells, transduced through phosphoinositide-three-kinase signaling and branched-actin polymerization downstream of integrated interleukin-four and granulocyte-macrophage colony-stimulating factor signaling. The functional consequence in vivo is direct. Adoptive transfer of fast-relaxing-matrix-licensed dendritic cells loaded with ovalbumin generated higher draining-lymph-node CD8 T cell responses with more antigen-specific T cells and higher restimulation potential than dendritic cells cultured on conventional plastic. This is a manufacturing-stage lever for dendritic cell therapy in oncology and tolerance applications — separate from the antigen-loading and maturation-cocktail levers already in clinical use.

Finally, Sourik Ghosh and Will Wei at the University of Pennsylvania reframe the mechanistic basis of mitochondrial copper biology — and the cuproptosis-style cancer therapy field built on it. The team shows that mitochondrial copper, delivered by the inner-membrane transporter solute carrier two-five-A-three, is required to maintain the stability of the lipoylated subunits of the major TCA cycle dehydrogenases and the lipoylation enzymes lipoic acid synthetase and lipoyltransferase one. Loss of the transporter or pharmacological copper depletion selectively destabilizes these proteins, and the effect is not reproduced by acutely inhibiting the electron transport chain — indicating copper acts as a structural cofactor of the lipoylation machinery, not through respiratory chain function. Chemical probes and synthetic peptide approaches show that copper directly engages the reduced lipoyl moiety, and the metabolic consequences propagate the way one would predict. Pyruvate dehydrogenase and alpha-ketoglutarate dehydrogenase activity drop, the TCA cycle rewires, the cell becomes dependent on pyruvate carboxylase for anaplerosis, aspartate is depleted, mTOR complex one signaling falls, and proliferation slows. Selective copper redelivery to the mitochondria restores everything. This is the mechanistic backbone the cuproptosis literature has been missing — and it provides a more refined target hypothesis for copper-ionophore and copper-chelator programs in oncology than the original "lipoylated protein aggregation kills cells" framing.

That is the brief for today. Four moves — an all-D macrocyclic peptide engaging a cryptic KRAS back pocket distinct from switch-two, a SuFEx-electrophile macrocyclic activity-based probe platform that targets non-catalytic protease residues, matrix stress-relaxation programming of dendritic cell state for cell therapy manufacturing, and mitochondrial copper as a structural cofactor of the TCA lipoylation machinery that rewrites the cuproptosis target rationale. Have a productive day.
