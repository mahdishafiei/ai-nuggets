## Script

Welcome to the Scripps Biomedical Brief for Monday, June twenty-second. Four fresh bioRxiv preprints — all posted in the last twenty-four hours, so the bodies haven't rendered yet and the takes below are grounded in the abstracts and metadata. A first-in-class CDK4-selective molecular glue degrader whose recognition is parked on endogenous tumor suppressors, the first crystal structure of a fungal mannitol-biosynthesis enzyme with a sub-nanomolar covalent inhibitor that synergizes with voriconazole, a refined mechanistic story that recasts cardiac stress-granule coarsening as the actual arrhythmia trigger, and a systemic prime-boost viral-vector vaccination that drives brain tissue-resident memory T cells against checkpoint-blockade-refractory glioblastoma.

First, from Patrick Zanon, Bachuki Shashikadze and colleagues with Henrik Daub at NEOsphere Biotechnologies, the discovery of NE26394, a first-in-class molecular glue degrader that selectively eliminates CDK4. The discovery engine is the headline as much as the molecule. Cereblon-directed molecular glues have historically come from medicinal-chemistry intuition or serendipity, and what NEOsphere ran instead is a high-throughput mass-spectrometry-based proteomics screen across thousands of cereblon-directed compounds, reading out neosubstrate degradation directly. NE26394 emerged from that screen with selectivity for CDK4 over CDK6 — something the clinical CDK4/6 inhibitors palbociclib, ribociclib and abemaciclib have never delivered, and the source of the on-target CDK6-driven neutropenia that limits their dosing. The mechanism is the second interesting part. Cereblon does not recognize CDK4 directly. CDK4 is handed to the ligase by endogenous INK4-family tumor-suppressor proteins — p16, p15, p18, p19 — which are themselves co-recruited into the ternary complex. The degrader is reading a cellular state, not just a sequence. In CDK4-dependent cancer models, NE26394 phenocopies the anti-proliferative RB-E2F pathway perturbation of the clinical CDK4 inhibitors. The combination of CDK4-over-CDK6 selectivity, degradation over substrate-competitive inhibition, and a rational MS-based discovery workflow is unusually well-aligned with the targeted-protein-degradation roadmap.

Second, from Sophia Nguyen, Isaac Pinner and colleagues with Blagojce Jovcevski and John Bruning at the University of Adelaide, the first crystal structure of mannitol-2-dehydrogenase from Aspergillus fumigatus, together with a covalent chemical probe at sub-nanomolar potency. Mannitol is central to fungal stress tolerance and virulence, and the biosynthetic enzymes have no close human equivalents, but the absence of structural information has held back medicinal chemistry on this pathway. The Adelaide group solved the apo enzyme at one-point-eight angstroms and the NADH-bound complex at two-point-one angstroms, mapping a large central cavity lined with positively charged residues that accommodates the cofactor, with a phenylalanine forty-five pi-stacking the nicotinamide ring. They then characterized one-four-benzoquinone, a cysteine-modifying small molecule, and report a half-maximal inhibitory concentration of one-point-two nanomolar. Intact mass spectrometry and proteomics show that the compound modifies up to five cysteine residues on the enzyme. In whole-cell antifungal assays the compound is active on its own and synergizes with voriconazole, the front-line antifungal in this disease. The compound itself isn't a clinical candidate — one-four-benzoquinone is too promiscuous and toxic — but the structure plus the covalent-warhead-on-cysteine playbook is exactly what a real antifungal medicinal-chemistry campaign needs to start, against a target with a fungal-specific pathway and no host counterpart.

Third, from Hayden Struckman, Ian Field and colleagues with Jonathan Silva at Washington University in Saint Louis, a study that refines where, mechanistically, cardiac stress granules cause arrhythmia. Stressed cardiomyocytes accumulate biomolecular condensates known as stress granules, and previous work has flagged them as broadly proarrhythmic. Across acute and chronic oxidative-stress models, Silva's group shows that the granules preferentially form at z-lines and intercalated discs, the mechanically critical sites of cardiomyocyte architecture, and that the actual pathological inflection point is not granule formation but granule coarsening — the merging of small granules into larger ones. Early nascent granules are broadly cytoprotective. Coarsened granules, by contrast, disrupt alpha-actinin and L-type calcium-channel — that is, Cav1.2 — nanodomains, and shorten the action-potential duration. The disruption is microtubule-dependent, and pharmacologic arrest of coarsening with nocodazole preserves the calcium-channel nanodomain organization and restores normal action-potential morphology. The translational reframe is that the therapeutic window is on the dynamics, not the existence, of the granules, and that targeting microtubule-mediated coarsening rather than the condensates themselves may rescue the proarrhythmic remodeling without removing the cytoprotective early signal.

Fourth, from Emma Steffke, Lela Latifi and colleagues with Masaki Terabe at the National Cancer Institute, a viral-vector prime-boost vaccination strategy that drives durable anti-glioblastoma immunity through brain tissue-resident memory T cells. Glioblastoma is unresponsive to immune-checkpoint blockade, and the working hypothesis here is that the limit is initial priming rather than effector function. The group used a heterologous prime-boost — the simian adenovirus ChAdOx1 followed by modified vaccinia Ankara — and conferred therapeutic efficacy against orthotopic SB28 murine glioblastoma, a model that is normally checkpoint-blockade-refractory. The vaccination was effective against the established tumor antigen P1A and, importantly, against a newly identified glioblastoma-associated antigen they call Gpr149. Adding checkpoint blockade on top gave no additional benefit. The infiltrating CD8 T cells in the tumor-challenged brains were dominated by a CD103-positive, CD69-positive tissue-resident memory phenotype that was polyfunctional and persistent, and intracranial adoptive transfer of these T-R-M cells alone protected naive recipients from subsequent tumor challenge. For neuro-oncology programs that have repeatedly failed to translate systemic immunotherapy into glioblastoma, this places the therapeutic burden on driving brain-resident memory directly, with a clinically usable viral-vector platform.
