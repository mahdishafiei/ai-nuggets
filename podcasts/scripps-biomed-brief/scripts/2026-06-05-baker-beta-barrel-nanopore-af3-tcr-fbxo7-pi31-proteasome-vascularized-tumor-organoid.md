# Scripps Biomedical Brief — 2026-06-05

## Script

Good morning. Today's brief: a generative method for designing programmable asymmetric transmembrane beta-barrel nanopores from David Baker's group, generalized prediction of T-cell receptor antigen specificity from sequence using AlphaFold three, cryo-E-M-resolved regulation of the twenty S proteasome core particle by the Parkinsonism-associated proteins F-B-X-O seven and P-I thirty-one from Brenda Schulman's group, and a patient-specific vascularized tumor-organoid platform with B-two-microglobulin-knockout universal endothelium for immune-checkpoint-response stratification from Shyni Varghese's group at Duke.

First, from Anvay Philomin, Rashmi Sonigra and colleagues in David Baker's group at the University of Washington, a rapid generative pipeline for de novo monomeric transmembrane beta-barrel design that combines diffusion-based backbone generation conditioned on beta-barrel structural features with transmembrane-barrel-optimized sequence design. Native homo-oligomeric beta-barrels are the workhorse of nanopore sensing, sequencing, and separation, but their uniform symmetric lumen limits spatial resolution and the localized interactions required for analyte discrimination and selective filtration; energy-based monomeric designs have been built one at a time but each requires extensive expert input and does not scale. The team characterized forty-eight designs spanning ten to sixteen strands with measurable conductances corresponding to pore diameters of zero point seven to one point five nanometers, and solved crystal structures of two designs that match the designed models at atomic-level agreement. They then demonstrated the versatility of the method by designing nanopores with copper-binding sites for selective ion sensing, larger pores that support D-N-A translocation, and longer pores with increased hydrophobic thickness that mediate ion transport across three-dimensional networks of monoglyceride bilayers and synthetic block-copolymer-lipid hybrid membranes. The result is a programmable design substrate for monomeric transmembrane beta-barrels with custom inner-surface chemistry, geometry, and host-membrane compatibility — a generative platform for biosensors, single-molecule chemistry, sequencing, separations, and synthetic transmembrane circuits.

Second, from Lucas Woods, Bradley Neff and colleagues with John Altin at the Translational Genomics Research Institute and Cristian Tomasetti, AlphaFold three predicts the structures of M-H-C / peptide / T-cell-receptor triads with sufficient accuracy that generalized prediction of T-cell-receptor specificity from sequence becomes possible — against epitopes the model has never seen. The M-H-C / peptide / T-cell-receptor interface is the most diverse trimolecular interaction known in biology and the central trigger for adaptive immunity; T-cell receptors are now routinely sequenced at scale, but decoding their cognate antigens has remained the bottleneck, and prior in-silico methods could not predict against unseen epitopes, limiting their applicability to a tiny fraction of cases. The team applied AlphaFold three to more than nine thousand T-cell receptors mapped to over one thousand distinct epitopes restricted by more than seventy M-H-C class one and class two alleles, identified structural features that distinguish cognate triads from controls, and built a downstream model that achieves median A-U-Cs of zero point eight one to zero point nine two on validation triads unseen by AlphaFold three or by the feature-selection step. Generalized sequence-to-specificity prediction unlocks T-cell-receptor repertoire decoding, neoantigen-directed cell-therapy design, autoimmune-target identification, and infection-and-vaccine-response analysis at a scale that experimental sorting and tetramer screening cannot reach.

Third, from Frank Adolf, Ellen Goodall and colleagues with Brenda Schulman's group at the Max Planck Institute of Biochemistry, John Hanna at Brigham and Women's, and Wade Harper at Harvard Medical School, a cryo-E-M-resolved structural mechanism for how the Parkinsonism-associated proteins F-B-X-O seven and P-I thirty-one regulate the twenty S proteasome core particle. F-B-X-O seven and P-I thirty-one variants are linked to rare Parkinsonian syndromes, implicating their dysfunction in neurodegeneration, but how they engage the proteasome at the structural level was not understood. The team showed that each protein can associate with the proteasome independently, with multiple domains of F-B-X-O seven contributing; the F-B-X-O seven C-terminal domain engages multiple subunits inside the core particle and blocks the beta-five peptidase activity, while P-I thirty-one engages all three catalytic sites within the twenty S core and provides the previously unknown structural basis for beta-one inhibition. Disease-associated variants in both proteins disrupt proteasome regulation and the assembly of the S-K-P-one / F-B-X-O seven / P-I thirty-one complex. The work rewrites the inventory of endogenous proteasome modulators relevant in neurons and opens a structurally tractable axis for small-molecule or molecular-glue interventions to phenocopy or correct disease-associated dysregulation in Parkinson's disease.

Fourth, from Naveen Natesh, Soumen Maity and colleagues in Shyni Varghese's group at Duke University, a vascularized patient-specific tumor-organoid platform that combines self-organizing microvascular networks built from beta-two-microglobulin-knockout endothelial cells with patient-derived tumor organoids and autologous tumor-infiltrating lymphocytes — a universal endothelium engineered to minimize allo-recognition and make the platform broadly compatible across patient samples. With patient-specific, lymphocyte-incorporated models, the team quantitatively measured T-cell infiltration under immune-checkpoint blockade and stratified responders from non-responders consistent with the patients' clinical outcomes. Single-cell R-N-A sequencing identified tumor-intrinsic and immune-associated programs underlying responder versus non-responder status, including tumor-driven hyperangiogenic signaling as a barrier to T-cell extravasation. Pharmacological co-targeting of P-D-one and V-E-G-F restored T-cell infiltration in non-responder organoids and shifted them from an immune-excluded to an immune-inflamed state, providing both a mechanistic rationale and a patient-level preclinical platform for the design of combination immunotherapy.

That's the brief. Four reasons today to be optimistic about generative de novo design for transmembrane beta-barrel nanopores as a programmable substrate for sensors and separations, generalized M-H-C / peptide / T-cell-receptor specificity prediction from sequence using AlphaFold three, structural mechanism for F-B-X-O seven and P-I thirty-one regulation of the twenty S proteasome as a new axis for Parkinson's-disease intervention, and patient-specific vascularized tumor organoids with universal endothelium as a predictive platform for combination immunotherapy.
