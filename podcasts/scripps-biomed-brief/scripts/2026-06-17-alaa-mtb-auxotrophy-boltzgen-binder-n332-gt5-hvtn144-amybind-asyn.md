# Scripps Biomedical Brief — 2026-06-17

## Script

Good morning. Today's brief is four bioRxiv preprints — a drug-repurposing-derived antitubercular that engineers alanine auxotrophy into Mycobacterium tuberculosis by irreversibly inhibiting a metabolic enzyme the bacterium cannot supplement from the host, an all-atom generative binder-design model from MIT that designs proteins and peptides across modalities and targets in a single unified system, the bioprocess paper for the N332-GT5 germline-targeting HIV vaccine immunogen now in HVTN144 first-in-human dosing out of Bill Schief's Scripps program, and a de novo protein design pipeline that produces polymorph-specific mini-protein binders for alpha-synuclein amyloid fibrils.

First, from Daniel Flaherty's group at Purdue and Matthew Chojnacki's group, a new metabolic vulnerability in Mycobacterium tuberculosis. A drug-repurposing screen identified TI-374, with sub-micromolar potency against Mtb. Resistance mapping, supplementation rescue, and direct biochemistry converged on alanine aminotransferase — encoded by Rv0337c, called AlaA — and the compound is an irreversible inhibitor. Blocking AlaA induces alanine auxotrophy, and the bacterium evidently cannot scavenge enough alanine from the host: TI-374 remained active against intracellular Mtb in macrophages despite excess endogenous alanine, and genetic deletion of alaA attenuated Mtb survival in a murine infection model. Medicinal chemistry then took TI-374 to TI-801 with low-nanomolar potency. The take is two-fold — a host-relevant new metabolic vulnerability for a pathogen where new mechanisms of action are urgently needed, and a tractable covalent scaffold ready for further optimization. The pairing of a knockout phenotype with an irreversible chemical probe at low-nanomolar potency is exactly the validation arc the antitubercular pipeline has needed.

Second, from Hannes Stark and colleagues with Tommi Jaakkola, Regina Barzilay, and Gabriele Corso at MIT, with Bill DeGrado, David Sabatini, and others, BoltzGen — an all-atom generative model unifying binder design with structure prediction in a single network. The architectural argument is that binder design and structure prediction are the same problem run in opposite directions, and folding-grade structural reasoning baked into the generative process should produce binders whose binding mode the model itself can defend. Inference is controlled by a design specification language covering covalent bonds, structure constraints, and binding sites. Eight experimental campaigns across 26 targets validate the platform with functional and affinity readouts. Modalities range from nanobodies to disulfide-bonded peptides; targets range from disordered proteins to small molecules. The headline result is nanobody binders for targets with low structural similarity to anything in the training set with a known bound complex — a real test of generalization rather than recall. Weights, data, and code are released. For an audience watching whether generative models can finally produce credible mini-protein and nanobody chemical matter across diverse target classes from a single model, this is the most ambitious single-system claim yet.

Third, from Sam Pallerla and colleagues with the Bill Schief group at Scripps Research and IAVI, the end-to-end bioprocess and cGMP manufacturing of the N332-GT5 envelope trimer — the germline-targeting HIV-1 immunogen now in first-in-human dosing in HVTN144. A stable CHO line built on ATUM's Leap-In transposon platform reached titers above 200 milligrams per liter and held genetic stability through sixty population doublings. The process scaled from Ambr250 mini-bioreactors to 200-liter single-use systems with consistent quality, and a three-step purification — affinity capture, multimodal polishing, viral clearance — yielded better than 99 percent trimer with native-like antigenicity, site-specific glycan occupancy matching design, and viral clearance exceeding eighteen and eleven logs. Why this matters for this audience: the eOD-GT8 and now N332-GT5 program is the most rigorous test we have of whether sequential germline-targeting immunogens can guide a naive B-cell repertoire toward broadly neutralizing antibody lineages in humans. Clinical material for the next sequential dose has to be structurally faithful to the design — and this is the paper showing the manufacturing platform meets that bar at clinical scale.

Fourth, from Bruno Correia's group at EPFL, AmyBind — a computational pipeline for de novo design of mini-protein binders that target polymorph-specific structural features of amyloid fibrils. Two designs targeting alpha-synuclein fibrils bound the fibril, and one of them discriminated between polymorphs. Neither inhibited fibril elongation in vitro, which fits their designed lateral binding mode, and in cell-based models both showed polymorph-specific colocalization without altering uptake or seeding. The near-term application is fibril-selective imaging probes for differential diagnosis of synucleinopathies, where distinct polymorphs may correlate with different clinical syndromes. The longer-term opening is that polymorph specificity becomes a designable property — a route into amyloid-targeted therapeutics with selectivity that small-molecule PET tracers and pan-amyloid antibodies have struggled to deliver.

That is the brief for June seventeenth. Detailed links and references are in today's show notes. Until tomorrow.
