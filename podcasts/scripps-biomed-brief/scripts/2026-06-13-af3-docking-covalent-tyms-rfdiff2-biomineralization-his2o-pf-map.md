# Scripps Biomedical Brief — 2026-06-13

## Script

Good morning. Today's brief is four bioRxiv preprints sitting squarely at the interface of chemistry, computation, and cell biology — an honest stress test of AlphaFold 3 as a docking engine, a phenotype-first covalent fragment screen that turns up a synthetic-lethal handle on a classic target, de novo proteins that template specific carbonate polymorphs, and a probe-free proximity labeling chemistry.

First, from Jiankun Lyu's group at Rockefeller — version two posted yesterday — what is probably the most rigorous prospective test to date of AlphaFold 3 as a small-molecule discovery engine. AlphaFold 3 can co-fold proteins with ligands; the open question has been whether that translates into finding genuinely new chemical matter, or merely into re-posing what is already known. The answer is nuanced. On retrospective enrichment against decoys, AlphaFold 3 looked dominant — but the authors show that the apparent advantage is largely an artifact of hidden ligand-only biases in computational decoy sets. In three large experimental datasets that lack those biases — the sigma-2 receptor, the D4 dopamine receptor, and AmpC beta-lactamase — the physics-based docking program DOCK3 actually achieved stronger overall enrichment, with AlphaFold 3 contributing mainly to early enrichment. Pose reproduction on more than eight thousand post-training-cutoff complexes correlated strongly with training-set similarity, meaning the model is partly memorizing atomic positions rather than learning general principles of recognition. The headline is a prospective head-to-head against the sigma-2 receptor, novel to AlphaFold 3's training set. AlphaFold 3 hit a thirteen percent rate and pulled out a thirteen-nanomolar binder, with a crystal structure confirming a near-native predicted pose — but DOCK3 in parallel delivered roughly twice the hit rate from the same library. The take-home is that AlphaFold 3 is best deployed as a complementary engine to physics-based docking, useful particularly as a post-docking filter, rather than as a replacement. For structure-based campaigns at Scripps and elsewhere, that is a realistic envelope.

Second, from Simon Boulton's group at the Francis Crick Institute, a phenotype-first covalent fragment screen that turns up an unexpected synthetic-lethal handle on a classic drug target. The team screened roughly five hundred cysteine-reactive fragments against an isogenic pair of cells — ATRX wild-type and ATRX-knockout — looking for differential cell killing rather than for binding. A single chloroacetamide, which they call PP12, selectively impaired the ATRX-deficient cells. Instead of chasing the binder through chemoproteomics alone, they cross-referenced competitive click-chemoproteomic hits against genome-wide CRISPR synthetic-lethal datasets and converged on thymidylate synthase. Target validation came from a co-crystal structure, competition with 5-fluorouracil at the active site, and a measurable drop in cellular d-T-M-P. Mechanistically, thymidylate synthase inhibition induces replication stress that is selectively cytotoxic to the ATRX-deficient cells in a manner dependent on FAM-one-eleven-A and SLFN-eleven. The broader point is the workflow. Phenotype first, then deconvolute the target by combining covalent chemoproteomics with CRISPR genetic-interaction data. It is a generalizable template for finding context-specific covalent ligands without committing upfront to a target hypothesis — and a reminder that the substrate space accessible to covalent fragments is much wider than the binding-pocket landscape of classical medicinal chemistry.

Third, from Paul Kwon and colleagues with David Baker at Washington, de novo protein design extended to biomineralization. By fine-tuning RFdiffusion-2 on repeat-protein scaffolds, the team designed reconfigurable two-dimensional protein arrays that template specific calcium carbonate polymorphs — calcite from one set of designs and metastable aragonite under nucleation conditions that otherwise produce phase mixtures. They also designed lattice-matched proteins that template cobalt carbonate, and showed that a D3 protein cage confines homogeneous cobalt carbonate nanocrystals inside its interior. That hybrid cage-mineral composite functioned as an electrocatalyst for alkaline water splitting. For a drug-discovery audience the relevance is indirect but real: designed protein interfaces are now controlling phase, polymorph, and morphology at the inorganic-organic boundary, opening a credible route to functional protein-mineral composites for sensing, delivery, and catalysis. It is one more demonstration that the design field has graduated from making soluble proteins to engineering material properties.

Finally, a chemical-biology tool from Tae-Hyuk Kwon and Hyun-Woo Rhee with a Korean consortium. Photocatalytic proximity labeling has been limited by its reliance on singlet oxygen and exogenous trapping probes — a constraint that biases coverage in dynamic compartments like endosomes and exosomes where probe access is uneven. Their new photocatalyst, IDM, instead drives water oxidation to generate hydroxyl and superoxide radicals, which dioxidize proximal histidines into a persistent lactam tautomer that remains electrophilic after cell lysis. Decoupling labeling from live-cell probe access let them recover a vesicle-trafficking subproteome that conventional probe-dependent workflows had missed. A nice example of a chemistry-first design choice expanding what proximity proteomics can resolve.

That is the brief for June thirteenth. Detailed links and references are in today's show notes. Until tomorrow.
