# Scripps Biomedical Brief — 2026-06-08

## Script

Good morning. Today's brief: an itch G-protein-coupled receptor identified as an unexpected melanoma driver from Xinzhong Dong's group at Johns Hopkins, isolation of the endogenous double-stranded R-N-As that actually arm M-D-A-five filaments from Sun Hur and Roger Greenberg's groups at Boston Children's and Penn with a direct line to D-D-X-three-X mutant cancers, a heme-driven tissue-level stress framework for excess proteolytic activity from Ruslan Medzhitov's group at Yale, and a humanized-mouse demonstration that immune-checkpoint-inhibitor colitis is driven by autoantibodies acting through Fc gamma receptors from Michelle Krogsgaard's group at N-Y-U Langone.

First, from Naina Gour, Aishwarya Atakkatan and colleagues with Xinzhong Dong at the Johns Hopkins University School of Medicine, Mas-Related G-protein-coupled receptor X-four — a sensory-neuron-restricted receptor previously characterized as the receptor for cholestatic itch — identified as an unexpected oncogenic driver in melanoma. M-R-G-P-R-X-four is upregulated in melanoma and is confined to invasive, neural-crest-like and pre-epithelial-mesenchymal-transition states that define dedifferentiation and therapy resistance. Ectopic expression of M-R-G-P-R-X-four in mouse melanocytes drove one hundred percent penetrant, highly metastatic melanoma — a demonstration of oncogenic behavior, not just association. Mechanistically, the receptor activates P-I-three-K, A-K-T, and M-A-P-K signaling in a basal, ligand-independent manner. Multi-omic profiling links its expression to a mesenchymal and neural-crest-like program defining a distinct invasive niche, with broad transcriptional overlap with B-R-A-F- and N-R-A-S-driven tumors plus an additional extracellular-matrix-rich invasive program. The receptor also remodels the tumor microenvironment toward an immunosuppressive, checkpoint-high state enriched in suppressive myeloid cells. Pharmacologic inhibition of M-R-G-P-R-X-four suppressed basal signaling and limited melanoma growth and invasion in their hands — nominating a tractable, ligand-independent somatosensory G-P-C-R as a melanoma therapeutic target sitting upstream of the very phenotypic state that drives therapy resistance.

Second, from Jiazhang Chen, Zhe Mo and colleagues with Sun Hur at Boston Children's Hospital and Roger Greenberg at the University of Pennsylvania, the long-standing question of which endogenous double-stranded R-N-As actually arm M-D-A-five — answered by direct affinity purification of cellular M-D-A-five filaments followed by R-N-A sequencing. A-D-A-R-one converts adenosine to inosine in endogenous double-stranded R-N-As to prevent excessive M-D-A-five-driven interferon-stimulated gene expression, but only a small fraction of A-D-A-R-one substrates activate M-D-A-five, and cellular M-D-A-five filaments themselves had never been isolated. Greater than eighty-four percent of double-stranded R-N-As suppressed by combined D-D-X-three-X helicase and A-D-A-R-one base-editing activity were present in M-D-A-five filaments — versus less than one percent of substrates acted on by A-D-A-R-one alone. These dual-substrate, immunogenic R-N-As are inverted-repeat structures embedded in three-prime untranslated regions with high base-pair complementarity and long intervening sequences between repeats, with a minor contribution from sense-and-antisense intermolecular duplexes. Critically, the majority of these dual-substrate immunogenic R-N-As were hyperedited in D-D-X-three-X-mutant cancers. A-D-A-R-one inhibition is one of the most active small-molecule modalities in oncology right now; defining the specific M-D-A-five-loading substrate pool — and a D-D-X-three-X-mutant cancer subset where editing is dialed up — gives the field a much sharper handle on patient stratification and on the failure modes of A-D-A-R-one inhibition.

Third, from Karen Agaronyan, Allison Greaney and colleagues with Ruslan Medzhitov at Yale University, what has been a poorly defined concept — tissue-level stress — framed as a discrete and conserved injury signature. Using the lung and a panel of diverse proteases, the team identifies a unifying signature of proteolytic stress marked by vascular disruption, red-blood-cell extravasation, and heme release, which triggers oxidative stress. Alveolar macrophages act as the primary sensors of this response, activating an N-R-F-two-dependent heme detoxification program, while fibroblasts produce protease inhibitors that limit the damage. Repeated exposure induces tissue adaptation that protects against subsequent injury and infection. For diseases driven by excess extracellular protease activity — chronic obstructive pulmonary disease, rheumatoid arthritis, and a long list of others — this defines a tractable, pharmacologically addressable axis at the level of heme detoxification, N-R-F-two induction, and protease-inhibitor biology, rather than chasing individual proteases one at a time.

Fourth, from Irina Voloshyna, Yuri Patskovsky and colleagues with Michelle Krogsgaard at the N-Y-U Langone Medical Center, immune-checkpoint-inhibitor colitis recast as an autoantibody-driven, Fc-gamma-receptor-dependent process. The group transferred immunoglobulin G from melanoma patients treated with pembrolizumab, nivolumab, or ipilimumab — with or without severe colitis — into wild-type mice or mice with humanized Fc gamma receptors, all of which received comparable checkpoint blockade. Wild-type recipients showed no colonic changes. In contrast, humanized-Fc-gamma-receptor mice receiving immunoglobulin G from colitic patients developed colon inflammation marked by submucosal lymphocyte infiltration, goblet-cell loss, and elevated circulating interleukin six, seventeen, and twenty-two. Single-cell R-N-A sequencing identified an immunoglobulin-G-regulated inflammatory network spanning interferon-gamma-producing type-one innate lymphoid cells, T-helper-one and cytotoxic T cells, interleukin-one-beta M-one macrophages, plasmablasts, and interleukin-twenty-two-producing lymphoid-tissue-inducer cells. Patient serum autoantibody profiling further identified the chemokine receptors C-C-R-five and C-X-C-R-four as candidate immune-related targets of the colitis-associated autoantibodies. This reframes immune-related adverse events from generic T-cell collateral damage into a defined autoantibody-Fc-gamma-receptor axis — and C-C-R-five and C-X-C-R-four are both already drugged, opening immediate translational angles for prophylaxis or treatment.

That's the brief. Four reasons today to be optimistic about a tractable G-protein-coupled receptor sitting upstream of melanoma's invasive neural-crest-like state, a defined pool of endogenous double-stranded R-N-As that arm M-D-A-five with a D-D-X-three-X cancer connection, a heme-N-R-F-two framework for tissue-level proteolytic stress, and a Fc-gamma-receptor mechanism for checkpoint colitis with C-C-R-five and C-X-C-R-four as ready-to-target candidate autoantigens. Until tomorrow.
