# Scripps Biomedical Brief — 2026-06-09

## Script

Good morning. Today's brief: synthetic protease-activated G-protein-coupled receptors from Alice Ting's group at Stanford rewiring dorsal-root-ganglion neurons to convert a pain-inducing protease signal into analgesia, a thermodynamically grounded drug-target specificity foundation model from Sai Reddy's group at E-T-H Zurich computing binding compatibility directly from sequence, a miniaturized zwitterionic subcutaneous cell-encapsulation platform from Minglin Ma's group at Cornell sustaining a broadly neutralizing anti-H-I-V antibody for thirty-six weeks in mice, and a deep-mutational-scanning map of the C-F-T-R variant selectivity and off-target landscape of V-X-one-twenty-one — the new C-F-T-R corrector inside Alyftrek — from Jonathan Schlebach's group at Purdue.

First, from Matthew Ravalin, Nicholas Kalogriopoulos and colleagues with Alice Ting at Stanford, a fully synthetic platform for rewiring cellular behavior around extracellular protease activity. Extracellular proteases govern coagulation, inflammation, cell migration, and pain, and their dysregulation is common in disease — but the existing toolset for sensing protease activity in a programmable way is thin. The Ting team builds synthetic protease-activated receptors — SynPARs — from engineered, auto-inhibited G-protein-coupled receptors in which proteolytic relief of autoinhibition allows activation by either an exogenous or a tethered agonist. The same scaffold can drive transgene expression, a real-time fluorescence readout, or endogenous G-protein signaling, and is modular across diverse secreted proteases. They establish a cell-based library selection that optimizes the protease recognition sequence for any target enzyme, and then take the platform into mice. In the dorsal root ganglion, where extracellular trypsin produces hyperalgesia, SynPAR-equipped neurons translate that same trypsin signal into an analgesic response — a clean demonstration that a pain-inducing protease can be rewired into a pain-suppressing input at the level of individual sensory neurons. This is a new class of programmable cell-based and gene-based medicine with a directly drugged biology, sitting on top of a G-P-C-R chassis that is already well understood from a chemical-biology standpoint.

Second, from Sai Reddy at E-T-H Zurich, a transformer-as-Boltzmann foundation model for drug-target specificity that computes molecular binding compatibility as a thermodynamic quantity directly from sequence. The key identity is mathematical — transformer softmax attention is isomorphic to the Boltzmann distribution governing binding at thermal equilibrium — and that identity prescribes a single sequence-native architecture. The drug-target Specificity Foundation Model, d-t-S-F-M, was trained on roughly seven hundred fifteen thousand measured drug-protein interactions across five hundred twenty-three thousand compounds and twenty-three thousand proteins, with AlphaFold three used as an independent structural verifier sharing no architecture, training data, or representational basis with the model. The encoder retrieves a drug's target and a target's drug at ninety-five percent and eighty-nine percent recall at ten, and ranks the known off-targets of clinical kinase inhibitors at a median of thirtieth out of nearly five thousand genes — the top zero-point-six percent of a proteome-scale screen, validated against a chemoproteomic panel. In the target-to-drug direction it screens a five-hundred-twenty-thousand-compound library and identifies forty-six novel candidates against three immunology targets that pass AlphaFold three structural gating. A cross-attentive generative decoder produces novel molecules against sixteen targets, with seven-hundred-one of twelve-hundred designed candidates matching the AlphaFold three structural confidence of the approved drug. Wet-lab validation is the immediate next step, but a sequence-native, thermodynamically grounded foundation model that handles off-target prediction, repurposing, and de novo generative design from one architecture is the most interesting piece of structural-A-I drug-discovery infrastructure to appear in some time.

Third, from Minseok Lee, Bo Wang and colleagues with Minglin Ma at Cornell, a miniaturized subcutaneous cellular-implant platform that finally puts cell encapsulation on a clinically credible footing for sustained biologic delivery in resource-limited settings. The device is a thin cylindrical fiber combining clonal mesenchymal stromal cells engineered to produce P-G-T-one-twenty-one — a broadly neutralizing anti-H-I-V-one antibody — with a zwitterionic polyurethane nanofibrous membrane that suppresses the foreign body response. Subcutaneously implanted in multiple mouse models, the device sustained P-G-T-one-twenty-one in serum for up to thirty-six weeks, and devices remained functional after cryopreservation, opening an off-the-shelf manufacturing path. The applicator-based, minimally invasive implantation and retrieval procedures were demonstrated in a clinically relevant minipig model. The same chassis is, in principle, target-agnostic — swap the antibody, swap the protein. For passive immunization against H-I-V, and beyond it for any chronic biologic that today requires repeat injection in settings where cold chain and clinical infrastructure are limiting, this is a meaningful advance in the cell-encapsulation modality.

Fourth, from Akshat Jhangiani, Joel Olson and colleagues with Jonathan Schlebach at Purdue University, the first comprehensive map of how V-X-one-twenty-one — the new C-F-T-R corrector inside Alyftrek — performs across the rare-variant landscape of cystic fibrosis. The C-F-T-R gene carries more than twelve hundred disease-associated variants, and pharmacological responsiveness across the long tail of rare variants varies dramatically. Using deep mutational scanning, the team surveyed the impact of V-X-one-twenty-one on the plasma-membrane expression of two hundred thirty-two rare C-F-T-R variants. V-X-one-twenty-one generally enhances variant expression more strongly than its precursor V-X-four-forty-five and is most potent toward variants with mutations in the first membrane-spanning domain. One variant, Y-one-zero-three-two-C, showed diminished proteostatic and functional selectivity for V-X-one-twenty-one relative to V-X-four-forty-five — and computational docking traces the loss to disruption of favorable interactions between the native tyrosine side chain and V-X-one-twenty-one. Crucially, photo-crosslinking experiments show that V-X-one-twenty-one avoids a key off-target interaction that burdens V-X-four-forty-five. Two practical takeaways: a rational, variant-resolved framework for matching specific rare C-F-T-R alleles to the right corrector chemotype, and a chemical-biology demonstration that V-X-one-twenty-one's improved profile reflects a cleaner on-target binding pose rather than just bulk potency.

That's the brief. Four reasons today to be optimistic about the chemical-biology and drug-discovery toolkit: a synthetic protease-activated G-P-C-R platform that rewires pain to analgesia in vivo, a thermodynamically grounded foundation model that unifies drug-target specificity from sequence, a zwitterionic subcutaneous cellular implant sustaining a broadly neutralizing antibody for thirty-six weeks, and a deep-mutational-scanning map giving variant-resolved guidance on the newest C-F-T-R corrector. Until tomorrow.
