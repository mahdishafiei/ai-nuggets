## Script

Welcome to the Scripps Biomedical Brief for Wednesday, June twenty-fourth. Four fresh bioRxiv preprints today — two of them are still abstract-only because bioRxiv hasn't rendered the bodies yet, and I'll flag those. From Jennifer Doudna's group, T cells engineered as programmable delivery vehicles for genome-editing machinery. From the Mougous and Bhardwaj labs at U-W, de novo designed orally available miniproteins that neutralize a colorectal-cancer-linked bacterial toxin in vivo. A delivery system that uses membrane-localized liquid-liquid phase separation to walk small molecules, siRNA, and antibodies across the plasma membrane and even across the intact cornea. And a vector-engineering fix that makes in vivo CAR-T meaningfully safer.

First, from Kevin Wasko, Mara Maker and colleagues with Jennifer Doudna at U-C Berkeley, going off the abstract here — the preprint posted yesterday and the body isn't up yet — T cells engineered as a programmable delivery system for genome editing molecules. The premise is that the cell-type targeting problem in therapeutic editing is currently solved badly, mostly by tuning lipid nanoparticle tropism or AAV serotype, both of which give you population-level enrichment rather than logic. The Doudna group's claim is that you can instead use T cells themselves — which already do tissue infiltration and programmed antigen recognition — as the delivery vehicle. They show engineered T-lymphoid lines that produce editing enzymes and transfer them to target cells in response to a programmable ligand, with both cell-contact-dependent and contact-independent modes, and demonstrate the circuit in primary human T cells. The framing is a customizable genetic circuit for macromolecular delivery whose targeting comes from intercellular recognition rather than from biophysics. If it generalizes the way the abstract suggests, this turns adoptive cell therapy and gene editing from two parallel modalities into one, where the cell product is both the engineered effector and the delivery surface for editing the patient's own tissue.

Second, from Pooja Srinivas, Victor Adebomi and colleagues with Gaurav Bhardwaj and Joseph Mougous at the University of Washington, also abstract-only as of this morning, orally available de novo designed miniprotein inhibitors of Bacteroides fragilis toxin. Enterotoxigenic B. fragilis colonizes a substantial fraction of human guts and its toxin — a metzincin protease that cleaves E-cadherin and triggers proinflammatory signaling — has been linked to colorectal cancer development. The U-W team used de novo protein design to generate inhibitors that bind distal to the active site and physically block the toxin from engaging its claudin-four receptor. They stabilized the designs with engineered disulfides, gave them to mice in drinking water as well as locally to the cecum, and separately had an engineered live biotherapeutic secrete the inhibitor in situ. Across all three delivery modes the toxin was neutralized and B-F-T-associated gut pathology, including tumor formation, was prevented. The headline here is not just that designed miniproteins worked in vivo, but that they survived gut conditions well enough to be dosed orally and through a live bug — a non-antibiotic, mechanism-specific way to disarm a pathogen without depleting the rest of the microbiome.

Third, from a team led by Jingyi Yu and colleagues, phase-tuned interfacial condensates that drive transcellular access. The trick is to confine liquid-liquid phase separation to the plasma-membrane interface itself by co-condensing a cell-penetrating peptide with silk fibroin. Tuning the C-P-P-to-silk-fibroin stoichiometry pushes the condensate between liquid-like, which crosses the membrane, and solid-like, which only coats it. Cryo-T-E-M, S-T-E-D, and in situ cryo-F-I-B-cryo-T-E-M resolved nucleation at the membrane within thirty seconds and intracellular entry within five minutes — orders of magnitude faster than prior phase-separation-based delivery — and the same condensates ferried small molecules, nucleic acids, and antibodies at roughly tenfold lower C-P-P doses. The translational hook is the eye: by acquiring cytoplasmic access and then re-exiting, the condensates carried betaxolol across the intact cornea, dropping intraocular pressure at a four-point-two-fold lower drug dose, and shuttled siRNA into the anterior chamber. Phase state, not cargo identity, is the design knob.

Fourth, from a Shenzhen Celconta-led team, CLEAN-V — a CAR-less lentiviral vector for in vivo CAR-T. The known risk with in-vivo-delivered C-A-R lentiviruses is that the C-A-R protein expressed in the packaging cell ends up incorporated into the virion envelope, which then transduces the very tumor cells the therapy is meant to target. CLEAN-V sticks an E-R retention signal on the C-A-R during packaging so it never reaches the cell surface and so is excluded from budding virions. The resulting particles show near-complete loss of C-A-R-mediated tumor cell transduction, drop straight into existing third-generation L-V-V four- or five-plasmid workflows, and still produce C-A-R-T cells with preserved phenotype and function. For a modality whose biggest non-clinical liability is exactly this off-target tumor-transduction failure mode, this is a clean, manufacturing-compatible engineering fix.
