# Scripps Biomedical Brief — 2026-06-11

## Script

Good morning. Today's brief: a mechanistic and pharmacologically actionable picture of how the lupus-associated solute carrier S-L-C-fifteen-A-four licenses endolysosomal toll-like-receptor inflammation, from Christopher Parker's group at Scripps Research; a modular biologic-plus-small-molecule platform from Laura Dassama's group at Stanford that installs targeted post-translational modifications on disordered, undruggable proteins like K-RAS, p-fifty-three and B-C-L-eleven-A in live cells; a charge-based screen of more than fifteen thousand peptides from Bradley Pentelute's group at M-I-T that yields an arginine-depleted cell-penetrating peptide for muscle delivery of antisense exon-skipping oligonucleotides in Duchenne muscular dystrophy; and a continuous-glucose-monitor-informed pair of microneedle patches from Giovanni Traverso and Robert Langer at M-I-T that finally make glucagon a kinetically appropriate, on-demand therapy outside the emergency setting.

First, from Tin-Yun Chiu, Patrick Hall and colleagues with Christopher Parker at Scripps Research, a previously uncharacterized molecular interface that couples endolysosomal toll-like receptor signaling to m-T-O-R-C-one and to the inflammatory cytokine output that drives lupus. The endolysosomal solute carrier S-L-C-fifteen-A-four is essential for T-L-R-seven, eight, and nine signaling and is one of the most robust genetic susceptibility loci for systemic lupus erythematosus, but how it does its job has been incomplete. The Parker group identifies a direct physical interaction between S-L-C-fifteen-A-four and L-A-M-T-O-R-one, the core scaffold subunit of the lysosomal Ragulator complex. Structural modeling and site-directed mutagenesis show that an alpha-helix of L-A-M-T-O-R-one engages the substrate-binding pocket of S-L-C-fifteen-A-four, and that disrupting this interface broadly suppresses T-L-R-seven through nine cytokine production, m-T-O-R activation, and downstream I-R-F-five and I-R-F-seven signaling. For drug discovery, this is the most concrete picture yet of a tractable, well-defined small-molecule binding pocket at the heart of the endolysosomal-T-L-R-to-m-T-O-R axis in lupus — and a clean rationale for chemical disruption of the S-L-C-fifteen-A-four-L-A-M-T-O-R-one interface as a path to disease-modifying therapy.

Second, from Feng Shen, Octavia Merino-Chavez and colleagues with Laura Dassama at Stanford, a chemical-biology platform for installing targeted post-translational modifications on proteins that conventional chemically-induced proximity has been unable to reach. Existing degrader and recruiter strategies require a small-molecule binder for the protein of interest — a non-starter for disordered or pocket-less targets. The NanoBridge instead uses biologic binders — single-domain antibodies and peptide ligands — to transiently tag a protein of interest with F-K-B-P-twelve-F-thirty-six-V, and then deploys heterobifunctional small molecules to recruit endogenous P-T-M enzymes onto that handle. Across three notoriously hard targets — the cancer driver K-RAS, the tumor suppressor p-fifty-three, and the fetal-hemoglobin regulator B-C-L-eleven-A — the platform achieves rapid, reversible, temporally controlled targeted degradation, phosphorylation, and acetylation of the endogenous protein, with no exogenous P-T-M writer needed. The bigger picture is a generalizable strategy that opens up multiplex P-T-M editing on proteins that the small-molecule field has essentially given up on.

Third, from Charlotte Farquhar, Nina Dow and colleagues with Bradley Pentelute at M-I-T, a high-throughput discovery engine for cell-penetrating peptides that fixes the dose-limiting toxicity problem of conventional arginine-rich C-P-Ps. The approved phosphorodiamidate morpholino oligomer exon-skippers for Duchenne muscular dystrophy are limited in the clinic by poor muscle uptake; arginine-rich C-P-P conjugates improve delivery but cause renal injury at the doses needed. The Pentelute team built a charge-based chromatographic enrichment platform able to screen more than fifteen thousand synthetic peptides — including sequences with non-canonical, abiotic amino acids — and used it to de-novo discover arginine-depleted C-P-Ps. Four leads gave efficient nuclear morpholino delivery with roughly tenfold lower in-vitro toxicity than penetratin; the top peptide, designated C-X-P-one, conjugated to the morpholino, produced more exon skipping in dystrophic m-d-x mice than the standard arginine-six glycine conjugate at the same dose, with a clean tissue-level pharmacokinetic-pharmacodynamic relationship. A new chemical class of muscle-tropic delivery vehicles for the antisense modality with a real translational story behind it.

Fourth, from Junwei Li, Caleb Byrne and colleagues with Giovanni Traverso and Robert Langer at M-I-T, a continuous-glucose-monitor-informed redesign of glucagon therapy. Mining continuous-glucose-monitor data from one-thousand-one-hundred-thirty-five type-one-diabetes patients across two-hundred-forty-six patient-years, the team shows that twenty to thirty percent of hypoglycemic episodes are follow-on events after under-treatment of a prior event, and that independent hypoglycemic episodes last roughly seventy-nine to one-hundred-eight minutes. Guided by these kinetics, they engineer two thermostable, high-density solid glucagon formulations into microneedle patches: an on-demand patch for rapid prevention or treatment of mild daytime hypoglycemia, and an enzyme-driven, hypoglycemia-responsive patch that releases glucagon autonomously during sleep. Both formats showed good in-vitro stability, loading, and release kinetics, and reversed hypoglycemia in diabetic animal models — a credible path to making glucagon a routine, patient-friendly modality rather than the emergency-only injection it has been for a century.

That's the brief. Four reasons today to be optimistic: a Scripps-led molecular interface that turns a lupus genetic risk gene into a defined, druggable target; a Stanford platform that brings targeted post-translational modification to the disordered, undruggable end of the proteome; an M-I-T peptide screen that may finally give the antisense field a non-toxic muscle-delivery vehicle for Duchenne; and an M-I-T microneedle redesign that finally aligns glucagon kinetics with the way hypoglycemia actually happens in patients. Until tomorrow.
