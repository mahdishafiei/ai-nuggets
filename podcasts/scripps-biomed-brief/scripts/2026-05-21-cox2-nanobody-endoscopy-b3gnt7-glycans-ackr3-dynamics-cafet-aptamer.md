# Scripps Biomedical Brief — 2026-05-21

## Episode

A COX-2-targeted nanobody lights up early colorectal adenomas under fluorescence endoscopy; the glycosyltransferase B3GNT7 turns out to be the lynchpin of colonic mucus protection in colitis; molecular dynamics simulations explain why the atypical chemokine receptor ACKR3 refuses to couple to G proteins; and a charge-amplified field-effect-transistor aptamer biosensor pulls small molecules out of undiluted plasma at picomolar concentrations.

## Script

Good morning. Today's briefing pulls four threads from the past forty-eight hours of preprints: a targeted molecular imaging probe for early colorectal lesions, a glycan-installing enzyme that turns out to be central to colonic mucus defense, a structural-dynamics explanation for why one chemokine receptor refuses to couple to G proteins, and an aptamer biosensor that pulls picomolar small molecules straight out of undiluted plasma.

First, Larry Marnett's group at Vanderbilt, joined by Robert Coffey and Craig Duvall, has built the first COX-2-targeted nanobody for in-vivo molecular endoscopy of colorectal adenomas. The team raised an alpaca-derived library of seventy-three nanobody clones against COX-2, then took the lead candidate, F9, and used proteomic mapping to engineer a site-controlled fluorophore attachment by mutating two surface lysines so that the dye lands cleanly at the amino terminus without disturbing the COX-2 binding pocket. The conjugate has a seventeen-minute plasma half-life, no acute toxicity through forty milligrams per kilogram, and lights up tumors selectively in the azoxymethane–DSS mouse colorectal model on noninvasive fluorescence endoscopy. This is interesting in two ways: it is a clean demonstration that you can lysine-engineer a nanobody to control conjugation geometry on a small scaffold, and it converts thirty years of mechanistic COX-2 work into a screening tool for exactly the lesions colonoscopy currently misses — aberrant crypt foci, hyperplastic polyps, and microadenomas.

Second, Jennifer Kohler at UT Southwestern, working with Lance Hooper's group, has identified B3GNT7 as the critical glycosyltransferase that builds the sulfated polyLacNAc — keratan sulfate — extensions on colonic mucin O-glycans. B3GNT7 is uniquely tuned to prefer a sulfated acceptor substrate, distinguishing it from every other family member, and its expression is dramatically lower in ulcerative colitis tissue. In a human goblet cell model the enzyme is required for production of polyLacNAc-modified mucus; in vivo, intestinal B3GNT7 deficiency leaves mice markedly more susceptible to chemically induced colitis and to enteric infection. The implication is that the mucus protective deficit seen in ulcerative colitis is not just a downstream consequence of inflammation. There is a specific, druggable glycan layer whose loss appears causally upstream of barrier failure, which nominates B3GNT7 stabilization, or downstream sulfated polyLacNAc supplementation, as a fresh therapeutic axis in inflammatory bowel disease.

Third, Irina Kufareva's group at the Skaggs School at UC San Diego ran ten-microsecond all-atom molecular dynamics simulations to ask why the atypical chemokine receptor ACKR3 is one hundred percent biased toward beta-arrestin and refuses to engage G proteins, despite binding the same agonist as CXCR4. The answer is in the dynamics. CXCR4 transitions cleanly between stable active and inactive states depending on whether wild-type CXCL12 or an antagonist variant is bound. ACKR3, by contrast, holds a persistently active transmembrane helix seven and a variable, loosely constrained transmembrane helix six, and it lacks the residue interaction networks in the transmembrane core that gate full activation in CXCR4. The receptor is, in effect, conformationally pre-disposed to a beta-arrestin-competent geometry but cannot lock in a G-protein-competent one. They validated the simulation predictions with BRET assays on ACKR3 mutants. For anyone designing biased ligands or thinking about scavenger receptor pharmacology in HIV, cancer metastasis, or regenerative medicine, this gives a concrete structural handle on what ACKR3 bias actually means.

Finally, Tom Soh's lab at Stanford reports a charge-amplified field-effect-transistor aptamer biosensor, which they call CAFET, that detects 3-hydroxykynurenine and progesterone at picomolar concentrations in undiluted human plasma. That sensitivity sits four orders of magnitude below the aptamer's own dissociation constant. The trick is a strand-displacement aptamer switch: target binding releases a pre-hybridized displacement strand from within the Debye length of the transistor surface, which produces a sharp net-charge change that drives a large FET current response, and an optional charge label on the displacement strand amplifies the signal further. The architecture is modular and aptamer-agnostic, which means the bottleneck for any new analyte moves from sensor engineering to aptamer selection. That is potentially enabling for low-abundance neuroactive metabolites, hormone biomarkers, and point-of-care therapeutic drug monitoring.

That is the brief for today. Four moves across imaging, glycobiology, GPCR pharmacology, and biosensing — a chemistry-controlled COX-2 nanobody endoscopy probe, a glycosyltransferase as a new IBD target, a dynamics explanation for chemokine receptor bias, and a picomolar small-molecule sensor in plasma. Have a productive day.
