For today's spotlight, we're going deep on Regenxbio's RGX-202 Duchenne data — because it's the biggest individual catalyst the gene therapy field has produced this year, and yet the market response was brutal. Stock down more than thirty-five percent on a Phase 3 hit. That tension — pivotal-grade efficacy paired with a safety profile that the market read as disqualifying — is what makes this an interesting case to unpack. There are real lessons here for any biologic program that wants to thread an accelerated approval, including some of Calibr's own.

Let me start with what was reported.

Regenxbio enrolled thirty-one ambulatory boys with Duchenne, age one and older, in the pivotal portion of the AFFINITY Duchenne trial. They all received a single intravenous dose of RGX-202 at two times ten to the fourteenth genome copies per kilogram. The primary endpoint was microdystrophin expression at week twelve, measured by muscle biopsy. The bar Regenxbio had negotiated with the FDA was ten percent of normal microdystrophin — a surrogate the agency had pre-agreed could support accelerated approval based on the established correlation between dystrophin expression and disease modification.

The trial cleared that bar comfortably. Twenty-eight of thirty boys with evaluable biopsies hit at least ten percent microdystrophin. Mean expression was about seventy-one percent across the cohort. In boys over age eight — the group that's typically further along in disease progression and harder to rescue — mean expression was around forty-two percent. The p value on the primary was less than oh-point-zero-zero-zero-one. By any reasonable read of the surrogate-endpoint framework, this is a win.

The functional data — which Regenxbio is presenting as supportive rather than as the basis for filing — comes from nine boys with a full year of follow-up. On the North Star Ambulatory Assessment, on timed function tests like time-to-stand and the ten-meter walk-run, and on stair climbing, the year-one cohort showed statistically significant improvements relative to natural-history controls. And the company demonstrated a statistically significant within-trial correlation between individual microdystrophin levels and individual functional response — which is exactly the bridging analysis the FDA wants to see to justify an accelerated approval based on the surrogate.

That's the clean part of the story. Now the complication.

Two patients had serious adverse events. One was cardiac inflammation — myocarditis, in plain English — and one was an asymptomatic but laboratory-confirmed liver injury. Both resolved within weeks without long-term sequelae, and both occurred in the context of an AAV gene therapy known to cause transient liver and immune-mediated effects. By the bar of cardiac and hepatic SAEs in AAV-based DMD therapy, neither is unprecedented. Mean transaminase and GGT and total bilirubin levels stayed within the upper limit of normal across the cohort. The company emphasized this. The market did not care.

The reason the market did not care is the elephant in the room — Sarepta's Elevidys.

Let me lay out the Elevidys context, because you can't read RGX-202's safety profile in isolation. Sarepta's Elevidys was the first approved DMD gene therapy, AAVrh74-delivered, with a different and smaller microdystrophin construct. After launch, three patients on Elevidys died — two pediatric non-ambulatory DMD patients from acute liver failure, and one adult on a related Sarepta limb-girdle gene therapy. The FDA ultimately suspended distribution, placed clinical trials on hold for the broader Sarepta gene therapy program, and added a boxed warning. Distribution was later allowed to resume, but only in ambulatory patients aged four and up. Roughly ninety percent of treating physicians kept prescribing Elevidys for ambulatory patients, but the franchise took a real hit.

That history is the lens the market is using to read RGX-202. One myocarditis case and one liver injury — both resolved — would have been a footnote in 2023. In 2026, after Elevidys, any cardiac or hepatic signal in DMD gene therapy is presumed to be the beginning of a tail risk. Investors are pricing the possibility that the SAE rate goes up as the dosed population grows, or that one fatal event redirects the regulatory pathway.

Now, Regenxbio's competitive positioning is built around two technical claims about why RGX-202 should be cleaner than Elevidys. The first is the capsid — AAV8 rather than AAVrh74, and a manufacturing process they describe as eighty percent or higher full-capsid purity. Capsid purity matters because empty capsids contribute to the immune load without delivering payload, and a higher full ratio at the same total dose means less off-target immune activation. The second is the microdystrophin construct itself — RGX-202 expresses a larger microdystrophin than Elevidys, designed to retain more of the functional domains of full-length dystrophin. The premise is that more of the protein gets you more function per unit of expressed protein.

The data so far is consistent with that thesis but doesn't prove it. Mean microdystrophin expression at seventy-one percent is high — substantially above Elevidys-era expression levels — and the functional response in the year-one subgroup is real. But two SAEs in thirty-one patients is approximately the SAE rate Elevidys reported in its earlier trials. Whether the eventual on-market safety profile diverges is going to depend on what happens at scale.

The regulatory wrinkle is the timing.

Regenxbio's CEO Curran Simpson told investors the company plans to file under the surrogate-endpoint pathway based on microdystrophin expression, and that the FDA has signaled the bridging correlation in the trial supports that pathway. He used the phrase, quote, mandate on rare disease flexibility, end quote — referring to the policy direction the FDA had been moving in. The problem is that the same morning Regenxbio reported, the broader market was processing Marty Makary's resignation as FDA Commissioner. The rare-disease-flexibility regime Makary had been championing — the broader use of accelerated approval, the lighter pivotal trial requirements, the surrogate endpoints — is now in flux. The interim leadership has not signaled where it lands, and the next confirmed commissioner is a question mark.

So Regenxbio is filing into a pathway whose policy backbone just lost its main internal champion. Curran Simpson saying he expects the FDA will still honor the rare disease flexibility framework is a reasonable bet, but it's an explicit bet on regulatory continuity that the market is no longer willing to underwrite at face value. That is the second half of why the stock moved.

Now, what does this mean for Calibr?

The direct overlap is not Duchenne — Calibr doesn't have a DMD gene therapy. But there's a set of structural lessons that translate.

First, on surrogate endpoints. Calibr's MASH biologic is moving through IND-enabling work, and the MASH regulatory pathway is itself an accelerated approval framework built on surrogates — Madrigal got Rezdiffra approved on a histological surrogate, and that approval was conditional on a confirmatory outcomes trial. The lesson from RGX-202 is that a surrogate endpoint hits cleanly in trials only takes you so far if the post-hoc bridging story is weak. Regenxbio's within-trial correlation analysis — individual expression mapped to individual function — is the kind of pre-specified bridging work that defends the surrogate when the safety profile gets contested. For any Calibr biologic going for accelerated approval, building that bridging analysis into the pivotal trial design is not optional. It's the part that survives a regulatory regime change.

Second, on safety profile in the post-Elevidys era. Anyone running a first-in-class or first-in-class-mechanism biologic in a rare disease should assume the safety bar has moved. SAEs that would have been a footnote three years ago — transient myocarditis, asymptomatic transaminase elevation, immune-mediated effects — are now presumptive franchise risk in the market's read. The implication is that your pre-clinical safety package, your immune monitoring protocol, your dose-finding strategy all have to anticipate that any signal will be priced as a tail risk. The defensive answer is dose-escalation discipline and over-collection of mechanistic biomarkers — cardiac MRI, troponin, full transaminase panels with bridging biology — so you can characterize a signal as transient and mechanistically benign before the market does it for you.

Third, on the policy environment. The Makary resignation puts every rare-disease accelerated approval submission into a higher-uncertainty regime for the rest of 2026. For Calibr's IND-enabling programs, the practical implication is that the FDA interaction strategy should not assume regulatory continuity. Pre-IND meetings, Type B written responses, alignment on surrogates — get the agreements on paper, with the current career staff, before any new political leadership arrives. The flexibility framework is a written set of guidances, not a personality, but the practical execution of it can shift with leadership.

And fourth, on the in vivo cell engineering thesis we discussed yesterday — Tessera, eighty-five percent in vivo HSC editing, the trajectory toward in vivo CAR-T. The RGX-202 story is a useful reminder that even when the platform works, the clinical readout is gated by what the FDA and the market think safety should look like. The most elegant platform technology in the world still ships through a regulatory and commercial filter that responds to what happened to the previous generation of products. Plan for that filter.

One final note on positioning. Regenxbio is signaling they may wait to file rather than rush into the regulatory void left by the Makary transition. Curran Simpson's framing was that they want to engage with new leadership on the pathway before committing the submission. That's defensible and probably correct. But it also concedes the possibility that the surrogate-endpoint pathway gets reinterpreted under new leadership in a way that requires additional clinical evidence — a confirmatory functional trial, longer safety follow-up, or expanded enrollment. If that happens, the launch slips from 2027 to 2028 or beyond, and the competitive position against Elevidys — and against Pfizer's fordadistrogene moxeparvovec, which is still in the regulatory pipeline — looks different.

The bottom line: RGX-202 looks like a real drug on the efficacy data. The market is pricing it as a real drug with first-in-class regulatory risk in a transitional FDA. For Calibr's purposes, the takeaways are bridging analyses, defensive safety packages, written FDA alignment now, and a planning horizon that doesn't assume Sarepta-era flexibility persists by default.
