Good morning Travis. This is your Calibr-Skaggs Daily Briefing for Wednesday, May twenty-seventh, twenty twenty-six. Six headlines today.

First, AstraZeneca. This morning, the company announced that the FDA has extended the PDUFA decision date for camizestrant, the next-generation oral selective estrogen receptor degrader, in combination with a CDK four-six inhibitor as first-line therapy for hormone-receptor-positive, HER2-negative advanced breast cancer with an emergent ESR1 mutation. The extension follows an April Oncologic Drugs Advisory Committee vote of three to six against the benefit-risk profile, based on the SERENA-6 Phase 3 trial. AstraZeneca submitted additional circulating tumor DNA clearance data linked to longer-term efficacy outcomes, and those data will be presented at ASCO on Monday June second. We go deeper on this story, the oral SERD competitive landscape with vepdegestrant already approved earlier this month, and the broader regulatory implications for circulating-tumor-DNA-guided early-switch trial designs in today's spotlight.

Second, Apogee Therapeutics. The company is reporting Part B sixteen-week data from the Phase 2 APEX trial of zumilokibart in moderate-to-severe atopic dermatitis on this morning's eight-a.m. Eastern call. Zumilokibart, also known as APG777, is a half-life-extended anti-interleukin-thirteen antibody designed to support three- to six-month dosing intervals — a sharp differentiator against Sanofi's Dupixent at every-two-week dosing. Part A had already hit hard: seventy-one percent reduction in Eczema Area and Severity Index from baseline at sixteen weeks, EASI-seventy-five of sixty-seven percent versus twenty-five percent on placebo, and at fifty-two weeks, three- and six-month maintenance dosing held seventy-five and eighty-five percent of EASI-seventy-five responders. Part B is the dose-optimization cohort across three hundred forty-seven patients, and it gates the Phase 3 start in the second half of this year. Apogee is positioning for a 2029 launch in what the company pegs at a fifty-billion-dollar atopic dermatitis market. Zumilokibart was developed by Paragon Therapeutics and spun into Apogee in 2022 by Fairmount and Venrock — not a Foresite portfolio name, but a relevant template for half-life-engineered biologics.

Third, Eli Lilly. Yesterday Lilly announced three vaccine-developer acquisitions totaling up to three-point-eight-three billion dollars — Curevo at up to one-point-five billion, LimmaTech Biologics at up to seven hundred eighty million, and Vaccine Company at up to one-point-five-five billion. Curevo's lead is amezosvatein, a synthetic-adjuvant subunit shingles vaccine with Phase 2 data showing better tolerability than GSK's Shingrix at comparable efficacy. LimmaTech is in Phase 1 for a Staphylococcus aureus vaccine with additional programs against Neisseria gonorrhoeae and Chlamydia trachomatis — a bet on rising antimicrobial resistance. Vaccine Company brings an in-vivo nanoparticle platform that enables endogenous antigen production, conceptually adjacent to the in-vivo nucleic-acid delivery work we covered yesterday with VERVE-102. With Verve, Orna, Kelonia, Engage Bio, and now this vaccine triple, Lilly is signaling that prevention is becoming a core franchise alongside obesity and oncology — and that the underlying in-vivo delivery technology is the platform thesis.

Fourth, Kardigan. The clinical-stage cardiovascular biotech filed for a Nasdaq Global Market IPO this morning under the ticker symbol K-A-R-D. Kardigan is the second act from the former MyoKardia management team — that's the team Bristol Myers Squibb paid thirteen-point-one billion dollars to acquire in 2020. The pipeline has three late-stage assets: danicamtiv, an oral cardiac myosin activator for genetic dilated cardiomyopathy; ataciguat, an oral soluble guanylate cyclase activator for moderate calcific aortic valve stenosis; and tonlamarsen, a liver-directed antisense oligonucleotide for post-hospitalization blood pressure management in acute severe hypertension. The company has raised five hundred fifty-four million dollars privately, and the Prolaio digital-health platform Kardigan acquired last year provides a wearables-driven trial-enrichment substrate. Underwriters are JPMorgan, Jefferies, Leerink, and TD Cowen.

Fifth, Olympus and BioProtect. Yesterday Olympus announced the acquisition of Israel-based BioProtect for two hundred seventy million dollars. BioProtect's lead product, Balloon Spacer, is a biodegradable hydrogel-equivalent device that creates consistent spacing between the prostate and rectum during prostate cancer radiation therapy, protecting healthy tissue. The deal extends Olympus's urology and prostate cancer franchise on the device side rather than the therapeutic side, and is expected to close by end of the second quarter.

Sixth, Kura Oncology. The company released ASCO 2026 oral-presentation data for darlifarnib plus adagrasib in heavily pretreated KRAS-G12C-mutated solid tumors. Darlifarnib is Kura's next-generation farnesyl transferase inhibitor, and the combination showed meaningful antitumor activity in pancreatic adenocarcinoma and non-small-cell lung cancer with prior KRAS inhibitor exposure, plus in KRAS-naive colorectal cancer. The full data set presents Saturday May thirtieth in Chicago. This positions farnesyl transferase inhibition as a combination chassis for the KRAS class — relevant context as daraxonrasib continues to read out from RASolute earlier this month.
