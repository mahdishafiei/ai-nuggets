Good morning, Travis. It's Thursday, May 14th, 2026, and this is your Calibr-Skaggs Daily Briefing. Here are today's top stories in biotech and pharma.

Number one. The big science news of the day comes out of ASGCT in Boston, where Tessera Therapeutics reported the strongest in vivo gene editing data the field has yet seen. A single intravenous infusion of their RNA Gene Writer, delivered in a proprietary lipid nanoparticle, edited roughly eighty-five percent of circulating blood cells in their sickle cell model — sustained, off-the-shelf, no chemo conditioning, no ex vivo cell engineering. Tessera is a Flagship Pioneering company founded in 2018, and at this same meeting they're also presenting in vivo CAR-T data using targeted LNPs to generate functional CD19 and CD20 CAR-Ts in animals — a presentation scheduled for late this morning Eastern time. We'll go deep on Tessera, what the platform means for the in vivo cell engineering thesis, and what Calibr should take from it in today's spotlight.

Number two. BeOne Medicines won FDA accelerated approval for Beqalzi — generic name sonrotoclax — a next-generation BCL2 inhibitor for adults with relapsed or refractory mantle cell lymphoma after at least two prior lines including a BTK inhibitor. This is the first new BCL2 inhibitor approved in a decade and the first BCL2 ever specifically approved in mantle cell lymphoma — AbbVie and Roche's Venclexta has only been used off-label in this setting. Approval is based on a fifty-two percent overall response rate and a sixteen percent complete response rate, with median duration of response just under sixteen months. BeOne — formerly BeiGene before its 2024 rebrand — was developing this drug as a head-on Venclexta competitor, and getting the MCL niche first is a real wedge. Watch for the broader CLL and AML positioning to follow.

Number three. Eli Lilly released the SURMOUNT-MAINTAIN and ATTAIN-MAINTAIN trial results at the European Congress on Obesity, with simultaneous publication in The Lancet and Nature Medicine. The headline: patients who reached their target weight on injectable tirzepatide can step down — either to a lower Zepbound dose or onto the oral pill Foundayo — and keep most of the weight off. At one year on Foundayo, patients who'd switched from semaglutide kept all but about one kilogram of their loss; switching from injectable Zepbound to Foundayo kept all but about five kilograms. Continuing on the maximum tolerated dose of Zepbound through week one-twelve preserved essentially all of the loss. The strategic message: Lilly is openly building a maintenance phase into the GLP-1 franchise — chronic-injection induction, oral maintenance, and a clinical justification for shifting the long-tail of the population onto a lower-cost dose. That's the right way to defend a forty-billion-dollar franchise against the patent cliff.

Number four. Kyverna Therapeutics initiated a rolling BLA submission for miv-cel — the autoimmune CD19 CAR-T formerly known as KYV-101 — in stiff-person syndrome, after FDA alignment that their single-arm Phase 2 KYSA-8 data can support a filing. This is the first CAR-T therapy of any kind to reach a BLA submission in autoimmune disease. The clinical signal is striking — twenty-six patients showed a median forty-six percent improvement on the Timed twenty-five-foot Walk test at week sixteen. FDA is asking for additional analysis of Kyverna's natural-history study, but no new patient data, and Kyverna plans to complete the rolling filing by Q4 with launch targeted for 2027 under RMAT designation and priority review. For the in vivo CAR-T thesis, this matters because Kyverna is the proof-of-concept that CD19 CAR-T can work outside oncology — making the case for an in vivo delivery format that opens autoimmune accessibility.

Number five. Galapagos has officially completed its transformation. Effective May eighth, the Belgian biotech changed its name to Lakefront Biotherapeutics — capping a multi-year strategic unwind that began when the original Gilead partnership turned bitter, accelerated when the cell therapy pivot under prior management failed to deliver, and concluded with shareholders approving a search-and-develop reorientation tied to a binding collaboration with Gilead's acquisition of Ouro Medicines. Lakefront now holds joint development rights to gamgertamig, a T cell engager for severe autoimmune disease, gets a twenty to twenty-three percent royalty on future Gilead sales, and retains around two billion euros in cash. The cell therapy operation is being wound down with up to one hundred seventy-five million euros in one-time costs. It is a remarkable arc for a company that traded above three hundred euros a share in 2018.

Number six. Takeda announced fiscal year 2026 plans to cut around forty-five hundred jobs globally as part of a one point three billion dollar restructuring drive targeting roughly two hundred billion yen in annual savings by fiscal 2028. The cuts are part of an ongoing slim-down by incoming CEO Julie Kim, following the announcement of six hundred thirty-four U.S. layoffs centered on the Cambridge, Massachusetts headquarters last month. The deeper context: Takeda is still working through the consequences of the sixty-two billion dollar Shire acquisition from 2019. The new strategy emphasizes centralized corporate functions, deprioritization of subscale therapeutic areas — yesterday it was the fourth nausea-and-vomiting program in three years — and a sharper focus on the GI and rare-disease franchises that came in with Shire.

Number seven. AstraZeneca reported a complicated Phase 3 result for eneboparatide, the PTH-1 receptor agonist they paid eight hundred million dollars upfront to Amolyt in 2024 to acquire. The CALYPSO trial in chronic hypoparathyroidism hit its primary endpoint — about thirty-one percent of patients on eneboparatide achieved normalized serum calcium and independence from active vitamin D and oral calcium at twenty-four weeks, versus about six percent on placebo. But the response rate fell short of the bar set by Ascendis Pharma's already-approved Yorvipath, and the company disclosed immunogenicity findings that explain part of the gap. Translation: AstraZeneca's eight-hundred-million-dollar bet has a clear path to approval as a second-in-class agent, but it isn't going to dislodge Yorvipath as the standard of care without a meaningful clinical differentiator.

And a quick eighth before we wrap. The European Commission approved Pfizer's Hympavzi for hemophilia A or B with inhibitors in adults and adolescents — the only once-weekly subcutaneous in either inhibitor population in Europe — based on the BASIS trial showing a ninety-three percent reduction in mean treated annualized bleeding rate versus on-demand bypassing agents. U.S. FDA review on the same indication is ongoing.
