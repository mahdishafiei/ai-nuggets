JNJ-9530, a CD20 mono CAR-T, and JNJ-4496, the CD19 slash CD20 bispecific CAR-T, are both being shelved. What makes this stunning is that just ten months ago at the European Hematology Association meeting in Milan, J and J reported a ninety-six percent objective response rate and a seventy-seven percent complete response rate at the recommended Phase 2 dose for JNJ-4496. Number two — the FDA approved Pfizer and Arvinas's vepdegestrant on Friday, branded as Veppanu. This is a milestone moment for drug development: it's the first-ever PROTAC, a heterobifunctional protein degrader, to win FDA approval. Indication is estrogen-receptor-positive, HER2-negative, ESR1-mutated advanced or metastatic breast cancer. The Phase 3 VERITAC-2 data showed median progression-free survival of five months versus two-point-one months for fulvestrant — modest in absolute terms but a meaningful hazard ratio of zero-point-five-seven. The bigger story is the modality validation: targeted protein degradation just crossed the regulatory threshold.

Third — AstraZeneca had a tough week of its own. An FDA advisory committee rejected the proposed labeling for camizestrant, AstraZeneca's oral selective estrogen receptor degrader, or oral SERD. AstraZeneca's commercial ambitions in the SERD space just took a serious hit, on the same day Pfizer and Arvinas got their PROTAC across the finish line.

Number four — Amgen's Q1 earnings call on Thursday came with two pipeline funerals. Chief Scientific Officer Jay Bradner announced Amgen is killing anvumetostat — the PRMT5 inhibitor in lung, pancreatic, and other solid tumors — after four Phase 1b and Phase 2 trials failed to clear the bar for further investment. They're also dropping adezkibart, the FLT3-ligand antibody for Sjögren's syndrome that came over in the twenty-seven-point-eight billion dollar Horizon acquisition. Adezkibart failed an interim futility analysis in Phase 2. That's a notable autoimmune setback worth flagging.

Fifth — the FDA is escalating its push to pull Tavneos, Amgen's avacopan for ANCA-associated vasculitis, off the market. Amgen filed a label update Friday in response. Recall the backstory: in March, the FDA disclosed seventy-six cases of drug-induced liver injury, including eight deaths, plus the prior finding that unblinded study personnel had manipulated the pivotal trial results. CDER formally proposed withdrawal in late April. Amgen is digging in.

Number six — Summit Therapeutics also stumbled. The Phase 3 HARMONi-3 interim PFS analysis for ivonescimab — the PD-1 by VEGF bispecific — missed its predefined statistical threshold in the squamous non-small cell lung cancer cohort. The data monitoring committee said keep going, no safety concerns, and the alpha-spend was conservative — but the market read it as a self-inflicted stumble and the stock tanked. The November FDA decision in EGFR-mutated non-squamous NSCLC is now under a brighter spotlight.

Total deal value reaches around two-point-zero-two billion dollars with milestones. Yet another China-to-West licensing deal, and a clear bet that combination immunotherapy and anti-angiogenesis will move toward single-molecule trispecifics.
