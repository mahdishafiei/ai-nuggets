Good morning, Travis. It's Saturday, May 16th, 2026, and this is your Calibr-Skaggs Daily Briefing. Here are today's top stories in biotech and pharma.

Number one. The FDA leadership crisis deepened on Friday. Tracy Beth Høeg, the acting director of the Center for Drug Evaluation and Research, was fired. According to her own account on social media, two FDA officials arrived at her office and gave her the choice to resign or be terminated. She refused to resign and was fired. The officials told her the order came from, quote, way above their pay grade. Michael Davis, the deputy director — a former CDER psychiatry team leader who spent the last three years as chief medical officer of a psychedelics-focused nonprofit before returning to the agency last year — is now acting CDER director. Combined with Marty Makary's resignation on Monday and Vinay Prasad's departure from CBER at the end of April, every senior leadership seat at the FDA is now sitting on either an acting director or a vacancy. We'll go deep on what this means for ongoing reviews and IND-enabling timelines in today's spotlight.

Number two. The FDA approved Roche's Tecentriq in combination with Natera's Signatera circulating tumor DNA test for post-surgical bladder cancer. This is the first immunotherapy label tied directly to a tissue-agnostic minimal residual disease assay — meaning the eligibility decision is driven by molecular evidence of microscopic residual disease rather than imaging or staging alone. Useful precedent to watch for any program where post-surgical recurrence risk can be stratified molecularly, including some of the more advanced solid tumor work going on across the campus.

Number three. AstraZeneca reported that perioperative Imfinzi plus Pfizer and Astellas's Padcev hit on both event-free survival and overall survival in muscle-invasive bladder cancer in the NIAGARA-style Phase 3 setup. Both endpoints were statistically significant and clinically meaningful. The combination essentially layers PD-L1 blockade onto a Nectin-4 antibody-drug conjugate in the curative-intent perioperative window, and the overall survival signal is what makes this likely to redefine the standard of care.

Number four. BeOne Medicines — the rebranded BeiGene — won FDA accelerated approval for Beqalzi in relapsed or refractory mantle cell lymphoma after at least two prior lines. The drug is a BCL-2 inhibitor, putting it directly into competition with AbbVie's venetoclax franchise. Response rate around fifty-two percent with sixteen percent complete remissions in the registrational cohort. The strategic story is that BeOne now has a US-approved BCL-2 inhibitor of its own — not licensed in, but discovered and developed in-house — which is a notable milestone for a China-origin pipeline reaching US registration on internal assets.

Number five. Shionogi's zatolmilast missed its primary endpoint in two Phase 3 trials in fragile X syndrome — one adult cohort, one adolescent cohort. The drug is a selective PDE4D inhibitor that was supposed to enhance neuronal connectivity by raising cyclic AMP. The cognition composite from the NIH toolbox failed in both studies, though the adult trial did show signals on a fragile X numerical rating scale for language and daily function. Safety was clean. Shionogi is keeping the open-label extension running and continuing exploratory analyses, but the path forward in fragile X is now unclear. The company is also testing zatolmilast in Alzheimer's and in Jordan syndrome.

Number six. Takeda announced a four thousand five hundred person layoff as part of a restructuring aimed at cutting one point three billion dollars in annual costs by 2028. The framing is that the company wants to flatten management layers ahead of expected approvals in narcolepsy, psoriasis, and polycythemia vera — three potential blockbusters. Worth noting alongside Novartis trimming a select number of roles in its biomedical research arm in the same week. The pattern across both is that even companies with strong near-term pipelines are tightening cost structures into 2028 patent-cliff planning.

Number seven. The American Society of Gene and Cell Therapy, together with the Orphan Therapeutics Accelerator, launched a marketplace called CGTxchange for shelved cell and gene therapy programs. The platform vets and scores discontinued assets — preclinical and clinical efficacy, safety, market opportunity, regulatory path — then connects them with impact investors and venture philanthropists. The launch follows Janet Woodcock's recent estimate that over a thousand CGT programs have been shelved for purely economic reasons. The two anchor examples in the announcement are Novo Nordisk's cell therapy shutdown last October and Takeda's parallel exit from cell therapy that same month.

And a quick eighth. Three hundred biotech executives and investors have signed a petition urging the administration to nominate Rick Pazdur — the longtime head of FDA oncology — as the next permanent FDA commissioner. The signatory list reads as a who's-who of the venture and operator class, and the framing is explicitly about restoring confidence in scientific rigor at the agency. No word from the White House yet on the actual shortlist.
