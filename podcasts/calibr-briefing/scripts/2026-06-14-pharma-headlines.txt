Good morning. It's Sunday, June fourteenth, twenty twenty-six, and this is your Calibr-Skaggs Daily Briefing. The European Hematology Association annual meeting in Stockholm closes today, and the late-breaking oral sessions made today the in-vivo-CAR-T inflection point in oncology. The lead story — and today's spotlight — is Legend Biotech's first-in-human proof of concept for L-B-two-five-zero-one, a CD-nineteen-by-CD-twenty dual-targeting in-vivo CAR-T in relapsed-or-refractory non-Hodgkin lymphoma. Six items in the roundup.

First, the Legend Biotech L-B-two-five-zero-one data, just out this morning in Stockholm. From the dose-level-two cohort — six patients, median three prior lines of therapy, just under sixty percent refractory to their most recent treatment — Legend reported a one-hundred-percent objective response rate, an eighty-three-percent complete response rate, and all six responses ongoing at the data cutoff. The therapy is a single intravenous infusion of a self-inactivating lentiviral vector — Legend brands the platform TaVec — that selectively transduces T cells in the patient's bloodstream and arms them in vivo with a tandem CD-nineteen-and-CD-twenty chimeric antigen receptor. No lymphodepleting chemotherapy was given. Plasma viral copy peaked right after infusion and was undetectable inside twenty-four hours, and the resulting CAR-T cells were detectable in peripheral blood out to one hundred sixteen days. No dose-limiting toxicities, no serious adverse events, no I-C-A-N-S, no deaths. Cytokine release syndrome appeared in two-thirds of patients but capped at grade two, started on day eleven on average rather than days one through three as in ex-vivo CAR-T, and resolved in roughly four and a half days without any glucocorticoids. From the company that built CARVYKTI with Johnson and Johnson, this is the strongest first-in-human in-vivo CAR-T efficacy-and-safety package in oncology to date. Second, Eli Lilly's Ajax Therapeutics acquisition produced its first clinical readout, also at EHA in Stockholm yesterday. A-J-one-eleven-zero-nine-five is the first Type-Two JAK-two inhibitor in clinical development — designed to bind the inactive conformation of the JAK-two kinase rather than the active conformation that Jakafi and the other Type-One JAK inhibitors bind, with the goal of producing deeper disease modification than the symptomatic relief Type-One drugs deliver. In twenty-three previously treated myelofibrosis patients in the A-J-X-one-zero-one Phase one study, seventy percent achieved at least a thirty-five-percent spleen volume reduction at week twelve, seventy percent hit at least a fifty-percent symptom score reduction, and twenty-one of twenty-three patients showed reductions in JAK-two driver-mutation variant allele frequency. Historical S-V-R-thirty-five rates with Type-One JAK inhibitors in second-line myelofibrosis are zero to thirty-two percent, and Incyte's CALR-targeted antibody — the most-watched competing program — produced a best S-V-R-thirty-five of thirty-nine percent in a larger sixty-nine-patient cohort. Grade-three-or-greater anemia hit half the patients and grade-three-or-greater thrombocytopenia hit thirty percent, so the safety pattern is consistent with on-target JAK-two pharmacology and the seventy-five-milligram dose has been picked for Phase three expansion. Lilly paid up to two-point-three billion dollars for Ajax in April. Jake Van Naarden told Fierce Biotech the medicine started working right out of the gate.

Third, Lilly also presented late-breaking Phase three data at EHA for Jaypirca — pirtobrutinib, the non-covalent Bruton tyrosine kinase inhibitor — in the BRUIN C-L-L-three-two-two study in previously treated chronic lymphocytic leukemia. Adding Jaypirca to a two-year venetoclax-and-rituximab time-limited regimen reduced the risk of disease progression or death by forty-five percent versus venetoclax-and-rituximab alone. The trial was enriched for prior covalent BTK exposure, which makes the readout directly relevant to the modern community-oncology C-L-L treatment line, and the time-limited design avoids the indefinite-treatment-burden problem that defines the covalent-BTK paradigm.

Fourth, the F-D-A approved Sanofi's Tzield — teplizumab, the anti-CD-three monoclonal antibody — for children aged eight and older with stage-three type-one diabetes, formally expanding the pediatric label beyond the existing adult indication. The approval reportedly cleared the agency despite an internal disagreement, with former C-D-E-R director Tracy Beth Høeg opposing the staff recommendation. Sanofi had previously requested withdrawal from the expedited pathway after that objection. Teplizumab remains the only disease-modifying biologic approved for type-one diabetes, and the pediatric label expansion meaningfully broadens its addressable population at exactly the population point where presymptomatic disease modification has the most years of life to alter.

Fifth, Novartis presented biomarker data this week for delpacibart braxlosiran — del-brax, an antibody-oligonucleotide conjugate that came over in the company's twelve-billion-dollar Avidity Biosciences acquisition late last year. In a Phase one-and-two study of about ninety patients with facioscapulohumeral muscular dystrophy, del-brax achieved its primary biomarker endpoint, lowering expression of the DUX-four-regulated transcript that is the disease driver and reducing creatine kinase as a marker of ongoing muscle damage. Novartis is enrolling a Phase three study measuring muscle strength and ten-meter walk-and-run performance and plans accelerated-approval discussions with global regulators. Del-brax is one of three antibody-oligonucleotide-conjugate programs Novartis picked up in the Avidity deal, alongside del-desiran in myotonic dystrophy and del-zota in Duchenne. It remains the highest-profile non-viral muscle-targeted nucleic-acid delivery program in late-stage development and a structural pressure on the A-A-V gene-therapy lane in muscular dystrophy.

Sixth and last for the day, Genentech executed another round of research-and-development layoffs this week, ousting three Roche vice presidents — Vishva Dixit, who has led the physiological chemistry unit for twenty-nine years; Man-Wah Tan, who has led the infectious disease group for sixteen years; and Todd McDevitt, who has run the cell therapy unit for the last three years — and shuttering the physiological chemistry and infectious disease research groups outright. Endpoints first reported the move. The cumulative twenty-six layoff total at Genentech is now approaching three hundred fifty positions. The signal is that Roche is rebalancing G-RED — Genentech Research and Early Development — toward oncology and neuroscience and out of infection biology and certain platform-chemistry functions. This is the most visible big-pharma research-prioritization move of the spring.
