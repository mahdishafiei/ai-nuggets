Good morning. It's Thursday, June eleventh, twenty twenty-six, and this is your Calibr-Skaggs Daily Briefing. The lead story today is a sizable expansion of the Novartis collaboration with Belgian-American molecular-glue biotech Orionis Biosciences — forty million dollars upfront plus up to one-point-four billion dollars in milestones plus tiered royalties, announced yesterday morning. The deal is the second formal partnership between the two companies, after a four-year pact from twenty twenty, and it is the third nine-or-ten-figure molecular-glue partnership Orionis has signed in the last three years. That deal is today's spotlight. Six items in the roundup.

First, the Novartis-and-Orionis expansion. Orionis is the Cambridge-and-Ghent biotech founded around the chemical-biology work of Jan Tavernier at VIB-Ghent University and led by chief executive Nikolai Kley, formerly of Forma Therapeutics. The company's Allo-Glue platform is built to discover monovalent small-molecule glues — chemical compounds that bring two proteins together in a productive interaction without the bifunctional architecture that defines PROTACs and conventional targeted protein degraders. The chemistry can be used either to recruit any E-three ligase the cell expresses and degrade the target, or to assemble a non-degradative protein complex that allosterically modulates target function. The Novartis expansion gives Novartis multi-year access to the platform plus the AI-driven discovery engine that powers Allo-Glue, across multiple disease areas, on top of the original twenty-twenty collaboration. Orionis already runs a parallel two-deal partnership with Roche's Genentech that closed its second expansion last May — one hundred five million dollars upfront, up to two billion dollars in milestones. Three Big Pharma signatures on the same chemistry platform in three years is the pattern the spotlight unpacks.

Second, Sanofi yesterday morning halted the MOBILIZE Phase three trial of riliprubart in refractory chronic inflammatory demyelinating polyneuropathy after the independent data-monitoring committee called futility at the planned interim. Riliprubart is Sanofi's selective humanized anti-activated-C-one-s monoclonal antibody — a classical-complement-pathway blocker — and the MOBILIZE study had been positioned as the registration trial in patients refractory to standard-of-care. No safety signals, just insufficient efficacy. The companion VITALIZE Phase three in I-V-immunoglobulin-treated C-I-D-P remains open under review, but the MOBILIZE result almost certainly takes one of Sanofi's two main complement-platform plays off the board. The setback comes two months after the Tolebrutinib withdrawal and a quarter after the Itepekimab Phase three miss, and it adds to a string of late-stage failures from a pipeline that the new chief-executive transition was supposed to be rebuilding.

Third, Eli Lilly and Sweden's AlzeCure Pharma announced a worldwide out-licensing agreement on Lilly's next Alzheimer's small molecule yesterday morning. Lilly gets global rights to A-C-D-six-eighty, AlzeCure's preclinical gamma-secretase modulator from the Alzstatin platform, for ten million dollars upfront plus over one billion dollars in development and commercial milestones plus tiered mid-single-digit royalties. A-C-D-six-eighty is a small molecule that modulates the gamma-secretase complex to shift its activity away from generating amyloid-beta-forty-two — the aggregation-prone pathogenic species — toward the shorter benign amyloid-beta-thirty-seven and amyloid-beta-thirty-eight fragments. The deal positions Lilly to layer a chronic oral disease-modifying small molecule beneath donanemab — already on the U-S market as Kisunla — and beneath its remternetug subcutaneous antibody, which is expected to file later this year. AlzeCure shares surged three hundred percent on the announcement; for Lilly, the cost is small and the strategic value is the long-term oral-modifier position that the antibody franchises cannot fill.

Fourth, the obesity-pipeline headline out of the American Diabetes Association Scientific Sessions this week. Novo Nordisk's chief scientific officer Martin Lange acknowledged in remarks yesterday that the jury is, in his words, still out on whether the CagriSema fixed-dose combination can match Lilly's retatrutide on weight loss. The pivotal CagriSema obesity data shown last quarter delivered roughly twenty-three percent placebo-adjusted weight loss at eighty-four weeks; the TRIUMPH-one retatrutide data Lilly presented at the same meeting delivered an average of twenty-eight-point-three percent weight loss at the twelve-milligram dose at week eighty, and the longer-term extension carried that to roughly thirty-point-three percent at one-hundred-and-four weeks in the higher-B-M-I subset. Novo is launching the R-E-D-E-F-I-N-E-eleven extension trial to test whether dose re-escalation and longer durations can close the gap. For Calibr's MASH and cardiometabolic interests, the read-through is that the obesity-and-metabolic franchise gradient is now clearly tilted toward the multi-mechanism agonists, which has implications for how the F-G-F-twenty-one-class assets — Akero's efruxifermin in Novo Nordisk's hands and B-O-S-five-eighty in G-S-K's hands — are positioned for combination strategies into that anchor backbone.

Fifth, two notable financings in the gene-delivery and immunology spaces. SonoThera, the South San Francisco ultrasound-mediated genetic-medicines company, closed an oversubscribed one-hundred-twenty-five-million-dollar Series B led by Vida Ventures with participation from the corporate venture arms of U-C-B, Bayer, Otsuka, and Johnson and Johnson. The platform combines diagnostic-grade ultrasound and clinically approved microbubble contrast agents with proprietary D-N-A and R-N-A payloads — a non-viral, non-invasive, outpatient procedure approach to delivering large genetic payloads, including full-length dystrophin constructs for Duchenne muscular dystrophy. First clinical trial is targeted for twenty twenty-seven. Separately, Ethyreal Bio launched out of stealth yesterday morning with a one-hundred-one-million-dollar combined Series A and B, co-led by Atlas Venture, Medicxi, Nandi Life Sciences, Checkpoint Capital, and Avoro Capital, to advance E-T-H-Y-zero-zero-one, a monoclonal antibody designed to block autoantibody-driven activation of the thyroid-stimulating-hormone receptor — the shared pathogenic driver of Graves' disease and thyroid eye disease. First-in-human trials are planned for the second half of this year, with preclinical data set for oral presentation at the Endocrine Society meeting on Sunday.

Sixth, two closing items. Disc Medicine said it has reached alignment with the F-D-A on a clear regulatory path for bitopertin, its glycine-transporter inhibitor for erythropoietic protoporphyria — the rare blood-disease asset that has been in regulatory limbo since the F-D-A's negative complete-response action last year. And a Fierce Biotech layoff-tracker update overnight notes that May industry headcount reductions hit nearly seven thousand positions, with Takeda and BioNTech together driving the majority — the broader pharma hiring picture remains slow, with companies prioritizing operating leverage over investment in research scale.
