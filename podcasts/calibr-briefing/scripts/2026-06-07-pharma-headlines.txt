Good morning. It's Sunday, June seventh, twenty twenty-six, and this is your Calibr-Skaggs Daily Briefing. Sunday roundup runs light — three items today, all of them from the American Diabetes Association's eighty-sixth Scientific Sessions in New Orleans, which run through Tuesday. The dominant clinical story this weekend is incretins, and the spotlight this afternoon goes deep on what Eli Lilly's phase three retatrutide readouts from yesterday do to the obesity-to-M-A-S-H competitive landscape that Calibr's M-A-S-H biologic is being positioned into.

First, the retatrutide phase three data set, presented yesterday afternoon at A-D-A. Retatrutide is Lilly's once-weekly investigational triple agonist of the G-I-P, G-L-P-one, and glucagon receptors — a third receptor versus tirzepatide's two. The TRIUMPH-one obesity trial reported that the twelve-milligram dose drove an average twenty-eight-point-three percent body-weight reduction at eighty weeks, with the extension cohort in patients with a body mass index at or above thirty-five reaching thirty-point-three percent weight loss at one hundred four weeks. Sixty-five percent of participants on the high dose dropped into a body mass index below thirty — meaning out of the obese category entirely. The TRANSCEND-T-two-D-one diabetes trial reported up to a two-point-zero percent A-one-C reduction with sixteen-point-eight percent weight loss in adults with type two diabetes, with forty-six percent of treated patients achieving an A-one-C below five-point-seven, which is the normoglycemic threshold. Two nested basket trials — TRIUMPH-O-A in knee osteoarthritis and TRIUMPH-O-S-A in obstructive sleep apnea — showed a seventy-three percent reduction in the W-O-M-A-C pain score and a sixty-one percent reduction in apnea-hypopnea events per hour respectively. Discontinuation rates on the twelve-milligram dose were eleven-point-three percent in obesity and five-point-one percent in diabetes — at the high end of the class but not dramatically so. The spotlight goes deep on the M-A-S-H implication, because retatrutide's phase three M-A-S-H trial, TRIUMPH-three, is also reading out later this year, and the phase two precedent was an eighty-six percent liver-fat reduction. That is the competitive context that any FGF-twenty-one M-A-S-H biologic — including Calibr's — now has to plan around.

Second, also at A-D-A, Novo Nordisk's phase two data for zenagamtide — the drug previously known internally as amycretin — the subcutaneous co-agonist of G-L-P-one and amylin. In two hundred sixty-two adults with type two diabetes inadequately controlled on metformin with or without an SGLT-two inhibitor, six doses ranging from zero-point-four milligrams to forty milligrams were tested over thirty-six weeks. The highest dose drove an average fourteen-point-five percent body weight loss versus two-point-six percent on placebo, with significant A-one-C reductions across all doses meeting the primary endpoint. Novo Nordisk announced plans to advance zenagamtide into phase three for type two diabetes within twenty twenty-six. This matters because Novo's earlier disappointing CagriSema phase three readout last year had raised the question of whether the amylin axis could deliver clinically meaningful efficacy as a co-agonist. Zenagamtide answers that question affirmatively in type two diabetes — but the obesity-monotherapy phase three is the readout the field is still waiting for, and the Lilly retatrutide number from yesterday now sets a brutal comparator.

Third, the Roche and Zealand Pharma petrelintide phase two ZUPREME-one data set, also presented at A-D-A this weekend. Petrelintide is a once-weekly subcutaneous amylin analog being developed by Zealand under a co-development and co-commercialization deal with Roche announced last year. The trial enrolled adults with overweight or obesity across five active doses. Petrelintide produced up to ten-point-seven percent body weight loss from baseline at forty-two weeks versus one-point-seven percent for placebo, with gastrointestinal adverse event rates that the companies described as generally similar to placebo. The differentiation argument is tolerability — near-placebo G-I safety in a class where nausea and discontinuations have been the dominant tolerability problem. The trade-off is the weight-loss ceiling. Ten-point-seven percent at forty-two weeks does not approach the twenty-five-to-thirty percent that Lilly's triple agonist now puts in the room as the new standard. The Roche and Zealand strategic positioning is the combination — a phase two multi-arm trial of fixed-dose enicepatide and petrelintide combinations starts later this year, and that's where the platform's competitive shape will actually take form. Combination-with-incretin is, structurally, becoming the only viable positioning for a non-incretin obesity asset.

That's the Sunday roundup.
