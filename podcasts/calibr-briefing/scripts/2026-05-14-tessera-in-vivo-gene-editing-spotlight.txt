Welcome back, Travis. For today's spotlight, we're going deep on Tessera Therapeutics — because what they presented yesterday and what they're presenting in Boston later this morning are, in my view, the most significant proof-of-concept data the in vivo cell engineering field has produced. And for a program like Calibr's in vivo CAR-T work, the question isn't whether to pay attention. The question is what specifically to take from it.

Let me start with what was disclosed.

Tessera is a Flagship Pioneering company founded in 2018. They've been working on what they call RNA Gene Writers — engineered enzymes that, when delivered as RNA inside a lipid nanoparticle, can write or rewrite specific sequences into the genome of a target cell. The closest analogy is to CRISPR or base editing, but the mechanism is different — the enzyme makes a programmable cut-and-paste change rather than a single base substitution or a double-strand break. The relevance is that it can in principle correct point mutations like the sickle cell mutation in beta-globin without the off-target risk profile of nuclease-based editing.

Yesterday, Endpoints reported that Tessera has shown a single intravenous infusion of their Gene Writer, packaged in a proprietary LNP, can edit roughly eighty-five percent of circulating blood cells in their model — and crucially, the edited cells include long-term hematopoietic stem cells, which is what gives the effect durability. The earlier non-human primate data, presented at ASCH and ASGCT in late 2025, showed long-term stem cell editing in the forty-to-sixty percent range with one or two doses and peak rewriting efficiency around seventy-six percent. The eighty-five-percent number is downstream — what happens in circulating cells once edited stem cells start contributing progeny. The point is the same: this is the first in vivo platform that produces editing rates in hematopoietic stem cells that approach what ex vivo cell therapies achieve.

Three technical pieces are worth knowing.

First, the delivery. Tessera's LNP is not a conventional liver-tropic LNP — the kind that delivers Onpattro, or that Verve uses for in vivo PCSK9 editing. Their formulation targets bone marrow. The disclosed biodistribution figure is a roughly fifty-two-fold higher concentration in bone marrow versus liver in their NHP studies. That number is the entire ball game for a stem cell platform. Bone marrow is where long-term hematopoietic stem cells live, and liver is where every conventional ionizable LNP wants to deposit by default. Getting fifty-two-fold inversion of the natural biodistribution is the engineering achievement.

Second, no preconditioning. Casgevy and Lyfgenia — the two approved sickle cell gene therapies from Vertex-CRISPR and bluebird — both require harvesting a patient's stem cells, editing them ex vivo, fully ablating the marrow with busulfan or melphalan, and then re-infusing the edited cells. The combined regimen takes months, costs roughly two to three million dollars, and the conditioning chemo carries fertility, infection, and secondary malignancy risk severe enough that uptake of both products has badly disappointed the companies that built them. Tessera's pitch is that an IV infusion of LNPs replaces all of that. No apheresis. No conditioning. No transplant. And critically — it can be redosed, because the LNP doesn't trigger pre-existing immunity the way AAV does.

Third, and this is what they're presenting in Boston at eleven forty-five Eastern this morning — the same delivery platform is being repurposed for in vivo CAR-T. Alberto De Iaco from Tessera is showing data using targeted LNPs to deliver the Gene Writer to T cells in vivo, generating functional CAR-T cells against CD19 and CD20 in animal models. Same chassis, different cargo, different targeting moiety on the LNP surface. They're treating the platform as a stem cell editor and a T-cell editor from the same delivery substrate. That is exactly the architecture the in vivo CAR-T field is converging on.

Now let me put this in competitive context, because Tessera is not alone. The in vivo cell engineering space has consolidated into a recognizable set of players over the last twelve months.

Capstan Therapeutics was acquired by AbbVie last summer for roughly two billion dollars, and they're the most advanced in vivo CAR-T pure-play — CD8-targeted LNPs delivering mRNA-encoded CAR sequences. Umoja Biopharma has its VivoVec lentiviral platform, a different delivery modality but the same in vivo CAR-T thesis. Orna Therapeutics is working on circular RNA in vivo CAR-T. ViaNautis Bio and Deliver Biosciences — Deliver got Fortis's inaugural grant earlier this month — are both pushing on lipid-based delivery. Beam Therapeutics and Verve Therapeutics are running the parallel race on base editing, with Lilly now owning Verve after that acquisition closed last year. Outside the public companies, Capstan's old founders and several Stanford-spinout efforts are still in stealth.

What Tessera does that the others can't — yet — is target two different stem-and-T-cell compartments from the same platform. That is the competitive bet. If they pull it off, the platform isn't a sickle cell drug. It's a delivery substrate that gets applied to whatever cell type matters.

Now let me get to what this should mean for Calibr's in vivo CAR-T program. Five specific implications.

One. Targeted LNP delivery is the only credible path. The Tessera data, the AbbVie–Capstan acquisition price, the Umoja, Orna, and Deliver Biosciences architectures — every serious in vivo cell engineering platform is now built around a tropism-engineered LNP, not around a conventional LNP plus a payload. For Calibr, this means the in vivo CAR-T IND-enabling package is going to be judged primarily on the targeting moiety, the off-target liver distribution, and the bone marrow or lymphoid biodistribution numbers. The Tessera fifty-two-fold marrow-versus-liver figure should be the benchmark to beat for hematopoietic targeting, and if Calibr's chosen targeting strategy isn't producing that kind of inversion, the platform is not yet IND-enabling.

Two. Redosability is a competitive line in the sand. The Tessera platform — and every LNP-based platform — can redose, while AAV-based in vivo gene therapy cannot, because of neutralizing-antibody buildup. For Calibr, this is both an opportunity and a constraint. The opportunity is that redosability lets you titrate response, manage waning effect, and treat populations who can't be cured in one shot. The constraint is that the regulatory bar for safety becomes higher — the agency will demand a clean repeat-dose package, longer tox studies, and a clearer story on adaptive-immunity buildup against the LNP itself or the gene writer payload. The recent Capsida gene therapy death — still under investigation — is a reminder that the regulatory backdrop here is not forgiving.

Three. The therapeutic-bar conversation just shifted. For a long time, the in vivo CAR-T pitch has been hand-waved as "almost as good as ex vivo CAR-T, but more accessible." With Kyverna initiating a rolling BLA for the first autoimmune CAR-T this week, the comparator is no longer hypothetical — Kyverna's KYSA-8 produced a forty-six percent median improvement on the Timed twenty-five-foot Walk in stiff-person syndrome, a result the FDA accepted as registrational on a single-arm basis. Any in vivo CAR-T program — including Calibr's — is going to be benchmarked clinically against autoimmune CAR-T results that exist in the real world. The in vivo platform doesn't have to match ex vivo on efficacy, but the gap can't be large, and the accessibility advantage has to be quantifiable in patients who would otherwise never get apheresis-based therapy.

Four. The MASH and FGF21 work is unaffected by this — but the Calibr cross-platform story benefits. The Tessera data lands at a moment when investors, partners, and major-pharma BD teams are scrutinizing in vivo cell engineering valuations again. A platform-company narrative that includes both a multi-mechanism MASH biologic and a credible in vivo CAR-T program reads as exactly the multi-modality positioning that's attracted premium dealmaking — Bristol Myers's Hengrui deal earlier this week was about exactly this kind of multi-asset bundling. The Tessera datapoint reinforces that in vivo CAR-T is moving from "platform thesis" to "platform with proof of concept," which is the inflection that drives BD interest.

Five. Watch the next twelve months on three specific things. First, whether Tessera files an IND for the sickle cell program in 2026 or 2027 — the eighty-five percent figure makes a 2026 IND plausible, which would put Tessera in front of FDA on in vivo hematopoietic stem cell editing before anyone else. Second, whether the in vivo CAR-T data being presented today reads as durable T-cell engraftment — many groups have shown transient CAR expression but the persistent-cell-pool question is still open. And third, whether Capstan or any of the AbbVie-, Lilly-, or Roche-owned in vivo platforms move into the autoimmune clinic in 2026. The first one of these to dose patients reframes the entire competitive landscape, and the optimal positioning for Calibr's program depends on where the field sits when you reach IND-enabling.

To summarize. Tessera has shown the closest thing to a true in vivo equivalent of ex vivo hematopoietic stem cell gene therapy that the field has produced, with a delivery system that — at fifty-two-fold marrow-versus-liver tropism — sets the engineering benchmark for any in vivo cell platform. The same delivery substrate is being applied to in vivo CAR-T. The implication for Calibr is not that you should change strategy. It is that the engineering bar for IND-enabling has just been raised, and the benchmarking should be done explicitly against numbers like these, not against the field as it looked twelve months ago.

That's the spotlight. The platform race in in vivo cell engineering is real, the targeting-LNP architecture has won, and Tessera just put the strongest data on the board. Plan around it. Back tomorrow.
