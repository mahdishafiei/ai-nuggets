First — and this is the big one we'll spotlight in a few minutes — Intellia Therapeutics reported positive Phase 3 results on Monday, April 27th, for lonvoguran ziclumeran, or lonvo-z, in hereditary angioedema. The HAELO trial hit its primary endpoint with an eighty-seven percent reduction in HAE attacks versus placebo over six months, and sixty-two percent of treated patients were entirely attack-free and therapy-free, against eleven percent on placebo. No serious adverse events on the lonvo-z arm. Intellia has begun a rolling BLA submission and is targeting a US launch in the first half of 2027. This is the first-ever Phase 3 success for an in vivo CRISPR gene editing therapy. We'll go deep on what this signals for the in vivo CAR-T field and Calibr's switchable platform in the spotlight.

Number two — closely related, and not by accident. Italy's Chiesi Group on Wednesday, April 29th, agreed to acquire KalVista Pharmaceuticals for one-point-nine billion dollars in cash, twenty-seven dollars per share. The asset they're paying for is Ekterly, brand name for sebetralstat — the first oral on-demand HAE treatment, FDA-approved last July. So in the same week, the HAE space saw a Phase 3 readout for a one-time gene-editing cure and a one-point-nine-billion-dollar cash deal for an oral on-demand small molecule. The market is essentially saying: the future of HAE is going to be a stratified one. Some patients want a one-and-done CRISPR fix. Others will want a pill in their pocket. Both segments are big enough to support investment.

Third — Bristol Myers Squibb disclosed Thursday that it discontinued the Phase 3 KRYSTAL-10 confirmatory trial for Krazati — adagrasib — in second-line KRAS G12C-mutant colorectal cancer. The Krazati plus Erbitux combo had won FDA accelerated approval back in 2024 on Phase 1-slash-2 ORR data, with continued approval contingent on a confirmatory readout. That readout failed. The colorectal indication now faces formal withdrawal or FDA-initiated removal. The lung cancer label is unaffected. Two takeaways: one, this is another data point on accelerated-approval reckoning under the current FDA — confirmatory failures are not getting waved through. Two, this puts more pressure on Mirati's KRAS franchise economics inside BMS, which BMS bought for five-point-eight billion dollars in 2023.

Number four — Boehringer Ingelheim on Tuesday, April 28th, reported topline Phase 3 SYNCHRONIZE-1 data for survodutide, the GLP-1 plus glucagon dual agonist they've been developing with Zealand. Average weight loss was sixteen-point-six percent at seventy-six weeks versus three-point-two percent placebo. That's Wegovy-like territory for a non-Lilly, non-Novo asset. Worth flagging here because dual and triple agonists in obesity are starting to converge with the FGF21-MASH story — both are reshaping metabolic disease as a multi-receptor combination market. Calibr's MASH biologic is dual-acting against multiple receptors, which puts it in the right design space for where the field is moving.

Fifth — Eli Lilly on Friday agreed to acquire Ajax Therapeutics for up to two-point-three billion dollars, including milestones, for an oral selective JAK2 inhibitor in myelofibrosis. Lilly is rebuilding its hematology and immune-mediated disease portfolio aggressively. This is the third major Lilly deal in oncology and immunology in roughly six weeks, after the three-point-two-five-billion Kelonia in vivo CAR-T deal and continued GLP-1 buildouts. Lilly is not slowing.

Sixth — a regulatory and policy item that matters for anyone with China-sourced clinical assets. The House appropriations committee called late last week for the FDA to reject clinical trial data from China, Russia, Iran, and North Korea in IND and NDA applications. This is on top of the existing FDA freeze on cross-border cell therapy work and heightened scrutiny of single-country China data packages. The read-through for Calibr and AbbVie's sCAR-T program: the J-and-J asset that just got killed, JNJ-4496, was originally licensed in from China's AbelZeta as C-CAR039. The geopolitical risk on China-originated assets is escalating, not de-escalating. Programs with US and EU clinical packages have a meaningfully cleaner path right now.

And seventh — quick FDA leadership note. Vinay Prasad has stepped down as director of CBER for the second time in under a year, effective end of April. Doctor Prasad's deputy has taken the chair while a permanent successor is sought. Worth tracking, since CBER is the gating body for cell and gene therapy approvals, and a leadership vacuum at CBER changes the political tempo for in vivo CAR-T, gene editing, and cell therapy regulatory paths.
