Hey Travis, it's Nigel with your Calibr-Skaggs Daily Briefing for Saturday, May 9th, 2026. Quiet weekend tape, but the back end of the week was anything but quiet. The big story is Daiichi Sankyo posting a nine-hundred-and-fifty-million-dollar extraordinary loss tied to ADC manufacturing overcapacity — the first major admission from a big pharma that the antibody-drug-conjugate buildout has gotten ahead of demand. Trump signed off on a plan to fire FDA Commissioner Marty Makary. The Eisai-Biogen subcutaneous Leqembi got pushed three months. And there is a fresh wave of M&A — Bayer-Perfuse, Roche-PathAI, Angelini-Catalyst — that landed in a single week. Here's the rundown.

Number one — and this is today's spotlight. Daiichi Sankyo announced yesterday afternoon Tokyo time that it is recording a one-hundred-and-forty-nine-point-four-billion-yen extraordinary loss — roughly nine hundred and fifty million US dollars — for the fiscal year ending March 31st, 2026, against revenues of about thirteen-point-four billion dollars. The structure of the charge is striking. About seventy-six billion yen — a little over four hundred and eighty million dollars — is a payment to contract manufacturers to walk away from minimum-purchase obligations on dedicated ADC production lines. Another nineteen-point-three billion yen is an impairment on a planned upgrade at the Odawara plant in Japan, plus termination fees on construction contracts. The root cause is that Daiichi locked in maximum-demand manufacturing capacity for its DXd antibody-drug-conjugate franchise — Enhertu, Datroway, patritumab deruxtecan, the next-gen mPBD platform — and the demand simply did not materialize on the timeline assumed. Datroway's lung cancer launch slipped. The patritumab MSD partnership unwound after the breast cancer Phase 3 missed. Internal pipeline assets like DS-9606 were discontinued. The lesson is sharper than the dollar figure suggests, and it has direct read-through to anyone planning capacity for a novel modality. Spotlight in a few minutes.

Number two — President Trump has signed off on a plan to fire FDA Commissioner Marty Makary, according to coordinated reporting Friday from the Wall Street Journal, Bloomberg, and Politico. The plan is reportedly not yet final, but Makary's tenure has been tense throughout — disagreements with the White House over flavored vape approvals, a slow-walked mifepristone safety review that angered anti-abortion advocates and Republican lawmakers, and significant departures of career FDA staff that have hollowed out the senior review ranks. There is no announced successor. The compounding factor is that neither of the agency's two main biopharma-regulating divisions — CBER for biologics and CDER for drugs — currently has permanent leadership, and a commissioner-level vacancy on top of that is the kind of regulatory turbulence that translates into real timeline risk for any sponsor with an active IND or BLA in review. For Calibr's planning horizon, the in vivo CAR-T IND-enabling work and the MASH biologic IND filing should be calibrated against potentially slower review responsiveness and more conservative information-request patterns at CBER through the transition. Watch for a successor announcement and any read-through to the Project Optimus and accelerated-approval policy reviews that have been in flux this year.

Number three — Eisai and Biogen disclosed Friday that the FDA has extended its priority review of Leqembi Iqlik, the subcutaneous starting-dose formulation of lecanemab, by three months. The new PDUFA date is August 24th, 2026, pushed from May 24th. The extension was triggered by the FDA classifying additional information the companies submitted as a major amendment to the application; the agency has reportedly raised no concerns to date about approvability. The strategic stakes are real — subcutaneous starting dose would let patients begin therapy at home with a one-time visit infrastructure rather than a multi-week intravenous induction, and would meaningfully reduce the bottleneck that has held back the broader anti-amyloid launch. RBC Capital flagged the financial impact as limited because the meaningful reimbursement step-up is not expected until 2027 in any case. For the Alzheimer's competitive set — Lilly's Kisunla and the next wave of anti-amyloids — this is a procedural delay rather than a substantive setback.

Number four — three M&A deals landed within roughly seventy-two hours this week. Bayer announced Wednesday it will acquire Perfuse Therapeutics for up to two-point-four-five billion dollars — three hundred million up front, the rest milestone-based — for a Phase 2 intravitreal sustained-release implant in glaucoma and diabetic retinopathy. This is Bayer's first significant drug acquisition since AskBio in 2020 and is squarely about ophthalmology lifecycle management as Eylea drifts toward biosimilar exposure. Roche announced Thursday it is acquiring PathAI for up to roughly one-point-zero-five billion dollars — seven hundred and fifty million up front, three hundred million in milestones — for the digital pathology and AI-companion-diagnostic platform that Roche has been partnered with since 2021. And Angelini Pharma agreed to acquire Catalyst Pharmaceuticals for four-point-one billion dollars at thirty-one-fifty per share, a twenty-eight-percent premium, for the Firdapse, Fycompa, and Agamree rare-CNS portfolio. The aggregate signal is that the M&A market that everyone has been waiting for in 2026 is now functionally open — three deals, three different therapeutic areas, three different deal structures, all clearing in a single week.

Number five — enGene Therapeutics shares crashed Thursday and Friday after the company posted updated LEGEND pivotal data for detalimogene voraplasmid in non-muscle-invasive bladder cancer. The headline numbers are a fifty-four-percent any-time complete response rate and forty-three-percent CR at six months — but the durability disappointed badly, with twelve-month duration of response estimated at twenty-five percent. The competitive read-through is that this widens the gap to J&J's Inlexzo and to CG Oncology's cretostimogene grenadenorepvec, both of which have shown stronger durability profiles. Leerink and others cut sales forecasts. The company is still planning a BLA filing in the second half of 2026 and will present the data at the American Urological Association meeting on May 15th. The structural takeaway for the gene therapy field is that response-rate momentum can mask durability weakness in the early data, and the durability tail is now where competitive differentiation is being decided.
