Good morning. It's Thursday, June fourth, twenty twenty-six, and this is your Calibr-Skaggs Daily Briefing. Wednesday delivered a heavy news flow — five items merit attention this morning, and the connective tissue is that the in vivo modality platform race continues to consolidate around a single pharma name. We go deep on the most important of these in today's spotlight.

First, and this is the headline story of the week, Eli Lilly signed a global research and licensing collaboration with Ascidian Therapeutics worth up to one-point-nine billion dollars to develop RNA exon editors for inherited monogenic kidney diseases. Terms include an undisclosed upfront, up to one-point-nine billion dollars in combined development, regulatory, and commercial milestones, and tiered worldwide royalties. Lilly receives exclusive target-specific rights to Ascidian's exon-editing platform for an undisclosed set of kidney targets, with options to expand into additional indications. Ascidian leads discovery and selected preclinical work; Lilly takes over preclinical scale-up, clinical development, manufacturing, and commercialization. Ascidian was incubated in twenty twenty by Apple Tree Partners and formally launched in twenty twenty-two with fifty million dollars in Series A funding. Chief executive Michael Ehlers came to Ascidian with senior research-and-development leadership at Biogen and Pfizer behind him. The platform edits messenger RNA at the kilobase scale through trans-splicing rather than making permanent changes to DNA, which is the central differentiator from base editing and prime editing approaches. We go deep on what this transaction means in today's spotlight.

Second, Alnylam Pharmaceuticals signed its first major artificial-intelligence drug discovery deal, with Inceptive Nucleics. Terms: thirty million dollars upfront in a combination of cash and Inceptive equity, up to two billion dollars in preclinical, regulatory, and commercial milestones, and tiered royalties. The deal pairs Alnylam's two-decades-deep RNA-interference chemistry and target portfolio with Inceptive's foundation-model platform for nucleic-acid design. The two companies will jointly explore sequence space and novel chemical modifications to improve siRNA potency, durability, and tissue distribution, with Inceptive's models predicting top-performing candidates that Alnylam takes forward. Inceptive is the artificial-intelligence biotech founded by Jakob Uszkoreit, one of the original co-authors of the transformer-architecture paper at Google. This is the first time Alnylam has paid for an external artificial-intelligence platform on top of its in-house design infrastructure, which is itself a tell about where competitive advantage in nucleic-acid medicines is shifting.

Third, Regeneron and CytomX Therapeutics expanded their conditional bispecific oncology collaboration. CytomX gets thirty-seven million dollars in target-nomination payments for two new programs immediately, with Regeneron holding options on up to six additional future targets. Total deal value across nomination payments, development and commercial milestones, and royalties could reach approximately four billion dollars. The technology pairs CytomX's Probody masking platform, which keeps a bispecific antibody silent in healthy tissue and selectively unmasks it in the tumor microenvironment through tumor-associated protease cleavage, with Regeneron's Veloci-Bi bispecific antibody scaffold. The expansion is the third major commitment from a top-tier pharma to conditional-activation biology this year and continues to validate the masked-antibody approach as a solution to the therapeutic-index problem that has historically limited bispecific potency.

Fourth, Eli Lilly and Boehringer Ingelheim each announced major reductions in planned German investments in response to Germany's pending healthcare cost-cutting reform. Lilly is halving its planned two-point-three-billion-euro investment in its Alzey manufacturing facility, which produces injected obesity drugs and is due to come online next year — head count at the site drops from a planned one thousand employees to approximately five hundred. Boehringer is cutting nine hundred million euros from its domestic German spending. The reform under discussion in Berlin would mandate deep discounts on branded medicines to reduce the country's healthcare cost overrun. The strategic read: pricing reform in Germany, on the heels of the Inflation Reduction Act negotiated-price program in the United States, is now visibly redirecting global pharma capital allocation toward jurisdictions with intact pricing power. Watch for the rest of European industry to make similar announcements over the next month.

Fifth, Legend Biotech released preliminary phase-one data ahead of its late-breaking oral presentation at the European Hematology Association meeting in Stockholm on June fourteenth. The compound is LB twenty-five-oh-one, an in vivo dual-targeting CD-nineteen and CD-twenty CAR-T therapy delivered as a single intravenous infusion without any preceding lymphodepleting chemotherapy. In dose level two, the objective response rate was one hundred percent across six patients with relapsed-refractory non-Hodgkin lymphoma, with five of those six reaching a complete response. All responses were ongoing at data cutoff. No dose-limiting toxicities, no serious adverse events, no deaths, no ICANS, and cytokine release syndrome and infusion reactions all at grade two or below. The in vivo CAR-T field now has three companies — Kelonia under Lilly, Capstan under AbbVie, and Legend with LB twenty-five-oh-one — generating clinical data without the lymphodepletion step that has been a major access and toxicity bottleneck for autologous CAR-T. That is structurally important for the modality.
