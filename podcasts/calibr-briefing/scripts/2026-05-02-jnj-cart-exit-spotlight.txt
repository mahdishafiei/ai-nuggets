We need to talk about Johnson and Johnson's surprise decision to walk away from both of its experimental B-cell lymphoma CAR-T programs — JNJ-9530 and JNJ-4496. Because what looks like a single portfolio cut is really a signal about where the bar now sits in second-line and later large B-cell lymphoma. And that signal matters directly for Calibr's CLBR001 plus SWI019 program with AbbVie.

Let me set up what happened. On Thursday, April 30th, J and J quietly disclosed it was discontinuing two CAR-T cell therapy programs in relapsed or refractory large B-cell lymphoma — known as LBCL. JNJ-9530 was a CD20 mono CAR-T. JNJ-4496, also known as JNJ-90014496, formerly C-CAR039, was a CD19 slash CD20 dual-targeting CAR-T originally licensed in from China's AbelZeta. The official statement: portfolio priorities and an assessment of the evolving large B-cell lymphoma treatment landscape. No safety signal. No efficacy collapse. Just — done. Patients on study get continued care under protocol.

Now here's why that's so striking. Less than a year ago, at the European Hematology Association Congress in Milan in June 2025, J and J presented Phase 1b data on JNJ-4496 that was, frankly, exceptional. Across fifty-one patients in the dose-finding study, the overall objective response rate at the recommended Phase 2 dose of seventy-five million CAR T cells was ninety-six percent. Complete responses came in at seventy-seven percent. In the second-line subgroup, ORR was one hundred percent and CRs were eighty percent. No grade three or four cytokine release syndrome. J and J's R and D head, on stage, called it — quote — best-in-disease, with the best complete response rates that have been reported so far.

To put that in context: Yescarta runs around an eighty-three percent ORR in this setting. Breyanzi around seventy-three. So a ninety-six percent ORR with seventy-seven percent CRs would have been, on paper, the most active CAR-T ever shown in LBCL. And the dual CD19 plus CD20 design specifically addressed antigen escape — the leading mechanism of relapse on existing CD19-only CAR-Ts. This wasn't a marginal asset. It was, as J and J themselves framed it, a generational opportunity.

So why kill it?

The answer isn't hidden in the data. It's in the math. Yescarta and Breyanzi together generated several billion dollars in 2025 sales, and the LBCL CAR-T market is projected to grow at roughly fourteen percent annually through the early 2030s. But the commercial economics of that market have shifted dramatically. Manufacturing remains expensive, slot capacity at certified treatment centers is constrained, and the practical bottleneck is no longer efficacy — it's logistics, vein-to-vein time, bridging therapy, and access. A new entrant, even with truly best-in-class CR rates, is now looking at a launch into a saturated three-product autologous CD19 market — Yescarta, Breyanzi, Kymriah — plus the looming threat of in vivo CAR-T platforms from Lilly, AstraZeneca, AbbVie, and others. The peak revenue projection just doesn't justify the development cost and the cell therapy infrastructure investment.

In other words, J and J ran the numbers and concluded that even an eighty percent CR rate isn't enough — not because the drug doesn't work, but because the slot at the table isn't worth the price of admission anymore.

That is the read-through that matters for Calibr.

Calibr's lead sCAR-T program, CLBR001 plus SWI019, is in Phase 1 with AbbVie for B-cell malignancies including lymphoma, plus solid tumors and autoimmune. So the obvious question is: if J and J just bailed on a best-in-disease CD19 slash CD20 CAR-T in LBCL, what does that mean for Calibr's lymphoma strategy?

Three things, and they actually point in a favorable direction.

First — the J and J exit confirms that the commercial moat in third-line-and-beyond LBCL is now all about differentiation that translates to access, not just response rates. Calibr's switchable architecture isn't trying to win on raw CR percentage. It's trying to solve the durability and toxicity-management problems that all autologous CAR-Ts share. The SWI019 switch lets clinicians titrate CAR activity in real time — dial it up for tumor pressure, dial it down for cytokine release syndrome, pause it during infection. That's a clinical operations advantage, not just a clinical efficacy story. In a market where efficacy is no longer the binding constraint, control becomes the differentiator.

Second — the J and J pullback reduces the field of well-funded competitors in next-gen LBCL CAR-T. A best-in-class dual-targeting program just exited the development pipeline. The other late-stage entrants in this space were largely Chinese assets being licensed in. With J and J out, AbbVie's Calibr-partnered sCAR-T faces a thinner late-stage competitive field for switchable CAR-T in B-cell malignancies — which it already had effective monopoly on, given the proprietary switch chemistry.

Third, and most strategically — the real value of the Calibr-AbbVie sCAR-T program was never going to be third-line LBCL alone. The entire point of the switchable platform is that a single CAR-T product can be reformatted for multiple indications by changing the switch. The autoimmune indications under AbbVie — think lupus, myositis, scleroderma — are where the commercial moat is still wide open. There's no autologous CAR-T approved for autoimmune disease yet. The total addressable population is dramatically larger than DLBCL. And the manufacturing economics scale because you can use the same CAR-T cell product. J and J killing two oncology programs doesn't touch the autoimmune wedge at all — and arguably increases the urgency for AbbVie to push Calibr's program into autoimmune as the primary value driver.

There's a fourth implication worth flagging, and it ties back to last week's Lilly slash Kelonia spotlight. The conventional autologous CAR-T format is now under pressure from two directions simultaneously — commercial saturation in oncology (which J and J just confirmed) and disruption from in vivo platforms (which Lilly's seven billion dollar Kelonia bet just confirmed). Any CAR-T program betting on raw efficacy in third-line lymphoma is increasingly squeezed. Programs with switchable control — that can be used in autoimmune, in solid tumors, in repeat dosing scenarios where in vivo platforms can't easily go — those are the ones that look more defensible over a five-to-ten year horizon. Calibr's sCAR-T sits in that defensible zone.

Let me be honest about the contrary view. There's a more pessimistic reading of the J and J exit, which goes something like this: if a major pharma can't justify the development cost of a best-in-disease CAR-T for LBCL even with extraordinary response data, that signals the entire autologous CAR-T category is becoming structurally unattractive. AbbVie could come to a similar conclusion about CLBR001 plus SWI019 in B-cell malignancies, and choose to refocus the program exclusively on autoimmune. That's not necessarily bad — autoimmune is where the upside is — but it would mean the lymphoma piece of the program effectively narrows.
