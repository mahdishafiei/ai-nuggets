Good morning, Travis. It's Friday, May 15th, 2026, and this is your Calibr-Skaggs Daily Briefing. Here are today's top stories in biotech and pharma.

Number one. The biggest story of the day, and our spotlight: Regenxbio reported pivotal Phase 3 data from the AFFINITY Duchenne trial of RGX-202, their AAV8 microdystrophin gene therapy. The primary endpoint hit cleanly — twenty-eight of thirty boys reached at least ten percent of normal microdystrophin at week twelve, mean expression around seventy-one percent across the cohort. Functional data from the nine boys with a year of follow-up was statistically significant on the North Star Ambulatory Assessment and on timed function tests. But two serious adverse events — one cardiac inflammation, one asymptomatic liver injury, both resolved within weeks — were enough to cut the stock by more than thirty-five percent. We'll go deep on the competitive math against Sarepta's Elevidys, the surrogate-endpoint pathway, and what this means for Calibr's biologic IND-enabling work in today's spotlight.

Number two. Aardvark Therapeutics disclosed that the FDA has placed a full clinical hold on its lead program, ARD-101, in Prader-Willi syndrome. This follows the company's voluntary pause back on February twenty-seventh after detecting reversible cardiac findings at higher doses. Aardvark plans to unblind data from both the Phase 3 HERO trial and the open-label extension to inform next steps. As of the pause, sixty-eight patients had been dosed in HERO and nineteen in the OLE. The company still holds around ninety-one million in cash, enough to fund operations into mid-2027 — but the entire pipeline is now effectively paused, and the stock reflects that.

Number three. Alumis is abandoning lonigutamab, the subcutaneous IGF-1R monoclonal it picked up in the Acelyrin merger, which was being positioned as a head-on competitor to Amgen's Tepezza in thyroid eye disease. The official framing is a reassessment of commercial viability — but the subtext is that Amgen has now reported positive Phase 3 data on a subcutaneous Tepezza formulation of its own, removing the convenience differentiation Acelyrin had been counting on. Alumis is keeping its TYK2 inhibitor ESK-001 in psoriasis as the lead value driver. Worth noting because Acelyrin was a Foresite-affiliated story before the merger — and this closes the chapter on that asset.

Number four. Cabaletta Bio closed a one hundred fifty million dollar registered direct offering with a striking investor list — Bain Capital Life Sciences, Adage, Cormorant, and notably Eli Lilly. Lilly also placed Francisco Ramírez-Valle, their head of immunology research, on Cabaletta's scientific advisory board. The strategic read is that Lilly is positioning around CD19 CAR-T in autoimmune disease without acquiring the asset outright. At the same time, Cabaletta presented six-to-nine-month data from RESET-PV at ASGCT showing drug-free clinical responses in half of pemphigus vulgaris patients on the lowest dose of rese-cel without preconditioning chemotherapy. The preconditioning-free piece is the headline — if it holds, it dramatically widens the eligible patient population.

Number five. Encoded Therapeutics presented expanded data from the POLARIS Phase 1/2 trials of ETX101, their AAV9 gene regulation therapy for SCN1A-positive Dravet syndrome, at the ASGCT Presidential Symposium. A seventy-six percent reduction in seizures, durable through fifty-two weeks, with dose-dependent antiseizure effect across four dose levels. The developmental story is the most striking part — kids treated before age two showed cognitive trajectories generally consistent with neurotypical development through one year of follow-up, diverging cleanly from the developmental stagnation seen in natural-history controls. No treatment-related serious adverse events across the trial. Encoded is moving to a pivotal trial.

Number six. Signant Health acquired Ametris, formerly known as ActiGraph, for an undisclosed sum. The strategic frame is end-to-end electronic clinical outcome assessment combined with wearable-derived digital outcome measures — patient-reported outcomes plus continuous sensor data, integrated in one platform. Worth tracking because the FDA has been progressively more open to wearable-derived endpoints in registrational trials, and the combined platform is positioning to capture that shift. Useful for anyone in Calibr's clinical operations group thinking about future trial design — particularly for chronic disease indications where activity, sleep, or autonomic signals add real efficacy texture beyond a quarterly clinic visit.

Number seven. AC Immune announced that Dr Andrea Pfeifer will retire as CEO at the June eleventh annual general meeting, after twenty-three years leading the company she co-founded. Martin Zügel, the board chair, will serve as interim CEO. Pfeifer is staying on as honorary chair of the board and co-chair of the scientific advisory board. AC Immune is a Swiss-listed biotech focused on Alzheimer's and Parkinson's vaccines and antibodies — the pipeline goes on, but the founder transition matters for a company whose identity has been so closely tied to one person.

And a quick eighth before we wrap. Abeona Therapeutics in-licensed a new engineered T cell therapy called ABO-701, targeting PSMA in metastatic prostate cancer, and simultaneously deprioritized its in-house ophthalmology programs. ABO-701 is described as a first-in-class PSMA-targeting cell therapy engineered to overcome the failure modes of T cell therapies in solid tumors. IND filing targeted for the second half of 2027. Abeona is the team behind Zevaskyn, the recently launched epidermolysis bullosa gene-modified cell therapy, so they have execution credibility — but the cell-therapy-into-solid-tumors graveyard is real, and this is now their flagship.
