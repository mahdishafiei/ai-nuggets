Hey Travis, it's Nigel. Welcome to today's spotlight. We need to talk about Intellia Therapeutics' Phase 3 HAELO readout in hereditary angioedema, because what looks like a single rare-disease win is, in fact, the moment in vivo gene editing crossed a regulatory threshold the entire CRISPR field has been chasing for a decade. And the implications go straight through to in vivo CAR-T, in vivo cell therapy more broadly, and the strategic positioning of Calibr's switchable sCAR-T platform with AbbVie.

Let me set up what happened. On Monday, April 27th, Intellia announced topline Phase 3 results for lonvoguran ziclumeran — call it lonvo-z — in adults with hereditary angioedema, or HAE. HAE is a rare genetic disorder where patients suffer recurrent, sometimes life-threatening swelling attacks driven by uncontrolled bradykinin production. The current standard of care is chronic prophylaxis — Takhzyro, Berotralstat, Cinryze — plus on-demand acute therapy. Patients dose for life.

Lonvo-z is different. It's a one-time intravenous infusion of a CRISPR-Cas9 system delivered via lipid nanoparticle to the liver. It permanently inactivates the kallikrein B1 gene, which is the upstream driver of bradykinin generation. One infusion, lifelong reduction. That's the proposition.

The Phase 3 HAELO data delivered. Primary endpoint was the rate of monthly HAE attacks. Lonvo-z hit it with an eighty-seven percent reduction over a six-month follow-up window versus placebo. The composite secondary — attack-free and therapy-free — was achieved by sixty-two percent of treated patients versus eleven percent of placebo. No serious adverse events in the lonvo-z arm. Treatment-emergent adverse events were limited to mild infusion reactions, headache, and fatigue. Intellia has initiated a rolling BLA, with the goal of a US launch in the first half of 2027.

Now here's the broader context. This is the first time an in vivo CRISPR gene editing therapy has cleared a Phase 3 trial. Not ex vivo, where cells are extracted, edited in a manufacturing facility, and reinfused — that path was pioneered by Casgevy from Vertex and CRISPR Therapeutics for sickle cell disease. This is in vivo, where the editing machinery is delivered systemically, finds the right tissue, and modifies the patient's genome inside their body. Until last week, no in vivo gene-editing therapy had a positive Phase 3. Now there is one.

Three things matter for Calibr.

First — the in vivo delivery platform just received a massive validation event. The lipid nanoparticle infrastructure that Intellia uses to deliver CRISPR to the liver is the same class of technology — refined, but the same family — that companies like CREATE Medicines, Capstan, Circio-Acuitas, and Lilly's Kelonia are using for in vivo CAR-T programs. When the in vivo CRISPR field hits a Phase 3 win, in vivo CAR-T programs that previously looked highly speculative now have a much shorter conceptual leap to make. The big-pharma chequebooks are going to get bigger and faster. Lilly already paid three-point-two-five billion for Kelonia. AstraZeneca paid for EsoBiotec. AbbVie has Calibr. The next Lilly-Kelonia-scale acquisition probably gets done in the next twelve months, and the bidding war just got more expensive.

Second — and this is the strategic nuance — in vivo CRISPR for permanent gene knockout is a fundamentally different product than in vivo CAR-T. Lonvo-z deletes a gene, and you're done. The therapeutic effect is durable because the edit is permanent. There is no off-switch, but there also doesn't need to be one — the kallikrein gene knockout doesn't require titration. You either need less kallikrein, or you don't.

In vivo CAR-T is the opposite. You're putting an active killing program into a patient's T cells. The CAR is alive, expanding, attacking, secreting cytokines. The lethal toxicity in autologous CAR-T comes from cytokine release syndrome, neurotoxicity, on-target off-tumor effects. Going in vivo doesn't fix any of that — it arguably makes it worse, because you can't precondition, you can't stop the manufacturing process, you can't pull the cells out and recheck. Lonvo-z's safety profile is essentially clean because gene knockout in liver cells is, fundamentally, a very different perturbation than activating a T-cell killing program.

That distinction is exactly the wedge that justifies switchable architecture. Calibr's CLBR001 plus SWI019 platform has a built-in answer to the question that lipid-nanoparticle in vivo CAR-T cannot easily solve: how do you titrate, how do you stop, how do you redose? With a switch peptide that controls CAR activity from outside the patient, you have an answer. With raw mRNA-LNP delivery of a CAR construct, you do not.

Third — the regulatory pathway lonvo-z is opening. Intellia's BLA gives FDA's CBER its first realistic shot at writing the in vivo gene editing approval template. Manufacturing standards, comparability protocols, off-target characterization, durability follow-up requirements, post-marketing surveillance — all of that gets codified for the first time. Every in vivo CAR-T program coming behind it benefits from that template. And it's worth noting — relevant to today's headlines — that CBER currently has a leadership vacuum. Vinay Prasad's exit at the end of April means the agency is going to be writing this template under deputy leadership, which historically is when CBER moves slower and more conservatively. That's a six-to-twelve-month tailwind for whoever has the cleanest in vivo data package, and a headwind for anyone trying to push aggressive label expansion.

Now here's the contrary view, because we should be honest. The pessimistic read on Intellia's HAELO win for the CAR-T field goes something like this: if in vivo CRISPR works for permanent gene correction, and in vivo CAR-T arrives at robust efficacy with even a modestly tolerable safety profile, then the value of any switchable mechanism collapses. Patients won't want a sCAR-T plus a continued switch peptide regimen if a single-shot LNP-CAR is on the market with comparable response rates. The switch becomes a feature in search of a problem.

I think that view is wrong, but it's worth taking seriously. Two reasons it doesn't hold. One — for autoimmune disease, which is the highest-value indication for Calibr's program, you don't actually want permanent CAR-T persistence. You want a controlled, time-limited B-cell depletion. Switchable architecture is structurally better for autoimmune than either persistent autologous CAR-T or single-shot in vivo CAR-T, because the disease itself wants pulsed dosing. Two — for solid tumors and combination immunotherapy, the on-off control of switchable CARs lets you sequence CAR activity with checkpoint inhibitors, with bispecifics, with chemotherapy, in a way that uncontrolled in vivo CAR expression simply cannot match.

The watch items from here. One — Intellia's BLA timeline and any FDA filing review feedback through the second half of 2026. Two — does Lilly accelerate its in vivo CAR-T disclosures off the Kelonia platform, or does AstraZeneca disclose more EsoBiotec data, in response to the Intellia validation event? Three — does AbbVie make any sCAR-T platform disclosures at the next earnings call, particularly on autoimmune positioning. Four — and this is the underrated one — watch the in vivo cell therapy IPO window through the second half of 2026. Successful Intellia launch is going to drag a wave of new in vivo cell therapy companies onto public markets, which both validates the category and intensifies competition for capital and clinical talent.

The bottom line, Travis. The Intellia HAELO win is the proof point the in vivo platform field has been waiting for. It does not threaten Calibr's switchable sCAR-T thesis — if anything, it sharpens it, because it confirms that the in vivo delivery problem is solvable and that the next bottleneck for the field will be control, not delivery. Calibr's switch architecture is built for exactly that bottleneck. The strategic question for AbbVie is no longer whether to lean in on switchable sCAR-T. The question is how fast to move into autoimmune, where the platform's structural advantages are clearest, and where in vivo CAR-T has the weakest competitive answer.
