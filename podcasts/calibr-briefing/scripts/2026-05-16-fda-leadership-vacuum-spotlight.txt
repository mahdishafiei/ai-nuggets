Let me set the scene. Four days ago, on Tuesday, Marty Makary resigned as FDA commissioner. We covered that in the spotlight. The framing on Tuesday was that Makary's departure was a controlled transition — he'd been the public face of the agency, but the operational layer underneath him was holding. That assumption is now wrong.

On Friday, May fifteenth, Tracy Beth Høeg — the acting director of the Center for Drug Evaluation and Research, the division that reviews every small molecule and most therapeutic drug applications in the country — was fired. Not asked to resign. Fired. According to her own statement posted that evening, two FDA officials walked into her office and told her she could resign or be terminated. She declined to resign. They told her the order had come from, quote, way above their pay grade — which in practice means above the FDA, above HHS, and at the level of the White House. She was out by the end of the day.

The deputy director, Michael Davis, immediately stepped in as acting CDER director. Let's be specific about who he is, because the answer matters. From 2016 to 2022, Davis worked at CDER as a clinical team leader in the Division of Psychiatry, where he reviewed psychiatric drugs. He left the agency in 2022 to serve as chief medical officer of a nonprofit developing psychedelic compounds for major depression and PTSD. He returned to the FDA in 2025 as CDER deputy director, and now, as of Friday afternoon, he is the acting head of the drug center.

That is a specific kind of profile. He has CDER review experience, but at the division-team-leader level — not at the senior leadership level. And his most recent operational work was at a single-asset nonprofit. He has not run a center. He has not overseen a hundreds-of-applications portfolio. He has not been in the room when the agency negotiates with the White House over priority review designations or rare disease flexibility decisions. He is now the person making those calls.

Now zoom out. The picture across the FDA right now looks like this. Commissioner — vacant, with an acting placeholder. CDER, the drug center — acting director who is one rung below where he needs to be. CBER, the biologics center — also acting, run by Katherine Szarama, who became acting director on April thirtieth when Vinay Prasad departed for the second time. Szarama joined CBER as deputy only in December, after a year as a program manager at the Advanced Research Projects Agency for Health. Like Davis, she has technical credibility but not center-leadership experience. Makary said before he left that a new permanent CBER director would be named, quote, in the coming weeks. That was three weeks ago. Nothing has been announced.

Stack those three vacancies together and the situation is this: every senior policy-making seat at the FDA is sitting on an acting director, and the agency itself appears to be operating under direct political control from above HHS. The Pazdur petition from three hundred biotech executives that's been circulating this week is, in part, a response to exactly this — an attempt to push the administration toward a credible, scientifically rigorous permanent commissioner before the operational damage compounds.

Now let me get to what this actually means for Calibr. There are three layers to think about.

Layer one is the IND-enabling work. The MASH biologic — the dual-acting molecule moving toward IND — is a biologic, which means CBER review. CBER is currently run by Katherine Szarama as acting director, with no permanent successor named. The good news is that the operational review staff at CBER haven't changed. The IND review division you'd interact with — the clinical reviewers, the CMC reviewers, the pharmacology-toxicology reviewers — those people are still in their seats. INDs don't get reviewed by the center director. They get reviewed by division-level teams who follow established guidance. Your timeline for the MASH biologic IND is not directly affected by Friday's firing.

What is affected is anything that requires a senior leadership decision. That includes pre-IND meetings where the company is asking for an unusual development path. It includes breakthrough therapy designations. It includes accelerated approval pathway negotiations. It includes rare disease flexibility — which is exactly what Sarepta got for Elevidys in Duchenne, the same flexibility regime Regenxbio is now trying to invoke after yesterday's pivotal Duchenne readout. Senior-level decisions are now being made by acting directors who have less institutional cover and may be more risk-averse than a confirmed permanent director would be. That risk-aversion typically pushes toward more conservative review outcomes, longer review clocks, and more requests for additional data.

Layer two is the regulatory science direction. The current administration has been signaling, through both Makary and now through the senior personnel choices, an openness to non-traditional development paths — surrogate endpoints in rare disease, expanded use of real-world data, faster moves to market. Makary's resignation removes one of the strongest voices for that approach. Høeg, despite her controversies on vaccines and antidepressants, was actually a proponent of expedited rare disease pathways. Davis as acting CDER head and Szarama at CBER are both new enough that we don't yet know where they sit on these questions. If Calibr's MASH biologic ends up needing to negotiate around a non-traditional endpoint — for example, anything beyond histology in a non-cirrhotic MASH trial — those conversations are now being held with people who don't yet have the political backing to take aggressive positions.

Layer three is the political weather. The phrase from those officials walking into Høeg's office — quote, way above our pay grade — is the part of this story that biotech CEOs and CSOs need to internalize. The FDA is not operating as an independent technical agency right now. It is operating under direct political direction from somewhere above HHS Secretary RFK Junior. For Calibr's planning purposes, that means regulatory strategy can no longer be reduced to scientific argument plus precedent plus a guidance document. There is now a political variable that affects review outcomes and timelines, and the variable is not predictable from the FDA's own published positions.

So what's the practical takeaway? Three things.

First, file what's ready. Anything where the science is solid and the regulatory path is well-precedented should move forward at full pace. Operational review continues unaffected.

Second, slow down on any item that requires senior-leadership novelty. Pre-IND meetings asking for unusual flexibility, novel endpoint discussions, breakthrough therapy applications — those probably benefit from waiting until at least one permanent center director is confirmed. Locking in a position with an acting director who may be replaced in three months creates risk that the position gets re-litigated.

Third, watch the CBER seat. Calibr's near-term clinical bet — the MASH biologic — sits with CBER. Whoever is named permanent CBER director matters enormously for that program. If a credible scientific leader is announced in the next sixty days, the regulatory uncertainty premium drops substantially. If not, the planning assumption should be that CBER stays in acting mode through the rest of 2026, and IND-stage decisions get made under that constraint.

One more thing worth flagging. The combination of Makary's resignation, Høeg's firing, and the still-vacant permanent CBER seat is now starting to affect deal pricing. Endpoints reported yesterday that biopharma M&A is shifting toward smaller, more targeted acquisitions and away from megadeals. The implicit driver is regulatory uncertainty — buyers don't want to pay megadeal premiums on assets whose registration path now has an extra unknown attached to it. That is going to ripple into the MASH space, where Akero's effruxifermin is in pivotal trials and the deal multiples for next-wave assets were already being set by the Novo-Akero comp from October. If acquirers start pricing in a regulatory discount, the next FGF21 or next dual-mechanism MASH asset gets re-priced downward. That has implications for Calibr's strategic options at the IND-readout and Phase 2 inflection points.

Friday's firing is a real inflection — not because operational drug review broke, but because the FDA's senior leadership now has no permanent occupants, and the political layer running it is making decisions without scientific cover. Plan your near-term regulatory work accordingly, watch the CBER director announcement, and keep an eye on whether the Pazdur petition gains traction.
