Hey Travis, it's Nigel. Welcome to today's spotlight. Daiichi Sankyo announced Friday afternoon Tokyo time that it is recording a one-hundred-and-forty-nine-point-four-billion-yen extraordinary loss — about nine hundred and fifty million US dollars — for the fiscal year ending March 31st, 2026. The headline framing in the trade press is a manufacturing write-down. The deeper story is that the largest dedicated antibody-drug-conjugate franchise in the industry has just publicly conceded that the multi-year capacity buildout it committed to during the post-DESTINY-Breast-04 commercial peak has gotten ahead of demand, and the financial consequence of that miscalibration is now booked. There is a clean lesson here for anyone planning capacity on a novel modality with multi-year manufacturing lead times — and for the Calibr in vivo CAR-T trajectory specifically, the lesson is sharper than a casual read of the Daiichi headline would suggest. Let me walk through it carefully.

First, the financial mechanics. The nine-hundred-and-fifty-million-dollar loss is structured as two charges and a residual hedge. About seventy-six billion yen — roughly four hundred and eighty million dollars — is being paid to contract manufacturing organizations to walk away from minimum-purchase commitments and dedicated production-line allocations on Daiichi's DXd antibody-drug-conjugate platform. The CMO contracts in question were signed in the 2023 to 2024 window, when the Enhertu launch trajectory and the AstraZeneca partnership were generating consensus forecasts that pointed to a multi-blockbuster ADC platform supporting Enhertu plus Datroway plus patritumab deruxtecan plus the next-wave mPBD-payload assets. The contracts included not only volume commitments but, critically, dedicated production lines — meaning the CMOs had to allocate physical fermenters, conjugation suites, and fill-finish capacity to Daiichi exclusively, and Daiichi paid for that exclusivity whether or not it utilized it. The second charge is a nineteen-point-three-billion-yen impairment on a planned in-house upgrade at the Odawara plant in Japan, plus contractual termination fees on construction contracts. And the residual hedge is the company's disclosure that it cannot yet provision for medium- to long-term differences between the minimum purchase obligations and the revised supply plan because the demand picture remains too uncertain. Read that as: there may be more write-downs to come.

Second, the demand-side narrative — what actually went wrong. Three things converged. One, Datroway, the datopotamab deruxtecan TROP2-directed ADC, hit a launch slip. The lung cancer indication — historically the bigger of Datroway's two approvals — saw the Phase 3 Avanzar readout pushed from second half 2025 to second half 2026. The breast cancer launch has been steady but smaller than the lung cancer commercial profile would have been. Two, patritumab deruxtecan, the HER3 ADC partnered with MSD, had its FDA filing withdrawn after a disappointing breast cancer Phase 3, which evaporated a substantial portion of the forecasted MSD-partnered demand. Three, internal pipeline assets including DS-9606, the claudin-6-directed ADC that was meant to be the lead asset on the modified-pyrrolobenzodiazepine payload platform, were discontinued in February as part of a strategic portfolio review. Each of these was forecastable individually as a risk in 2023; what was not forecastable was the joint probability — that all three of the secondary engines on a multi-asset capacity plan would slip or fail in the same eighteen-month window. The original demand forecast that anchored the CMO contracts assumed near-best-case execution across the portfolio. The actual execution has been Enhertu carrying the full load.

Third, the AstraZeneca partnership economics. Enhertu is a fifty-fifty profit-share between Daiichi Sankyo and AstraZeneca under the 2019 collaboration. Datroway is on similar economics. The capacity-planning miscalibration falls disproportionately on Daiichi because Daiichi controls the manufacturing — AstraZeneca's commercial responsibility means it bears its share of cost-of-goods through transfer pricing but does not write down the manufacturing infrastructure when it is underutilized. AstraZeneca has not commented on the Daiichi disclosure. The structural read for the partnership is that the manufacturing risk is now visibly asymmetric — AstraZeneca takes its share of profit on the upside but Daiichi alone takes the write-down on the capacity side. That asymmetry will be a topic in any future ADC partnership negotiation across the industry, and it is something Calibr should be aware of when modeling the optimal commercial structure for the in vivo CAR-T platform — manufacturing-bearing partner versus commercial-only partner is not a neutral choice on novel modalities with high fixed-cost lead times.

Fourth, and this is the part that connects directly to Calibr. The Daiichi miscalibration is a specific kind of error — call it the maximum-demand fallacy. The company's own disclosure language makes this explicit: they adopted a policy of securing manufacturing capacity sufficient to cover maximum demand without risk adjustment, with the highest priority placed on ensuring stable supply. That sounds reasonable when written down — patients before profits, capacity before capacity constraints — but it is mathematically the wrong objective function when the upstream pipeline has multiple independent failure modes and when the manufacturing lead time is multi-year. The correct planning posture is closer to expected-demand-with-a-confidence-band, and the contracts should be structured with optionality — staged build-outs, opt-in volumes, capacity that can be re-purposed across modalities or partners — rather than dedicated maximum-allocation lines. ADC manufacturing is a relatively mature modality where the industry has now learned this lesson once. In vivo CAR-T is not. The lipid nanoparticle and viral vector capacity that an in vivo CAR-T launch requires is being built right now, in 2026, by a small number of CMOs and by a handful of vertically-integrated developers, and the contracts being signed today look uncomfortably like the Daiichi contracts of 2023. Long-term volume commitments. Dedicated suites. Pay-for-capacity-not-utilization terms. The Calibr in vivo CAR-T program is far enough from commercial that the contract horizon is shorter, but the partner-selection and capacity-commitment posture should bake in the Daiichi lesson explicitly. Build optionality. Avoid maximum-demand contracts. Stage commitments to clinical inflection points, not to peak-sales forecasts.

Fifth, the read-through to the broader ADC bubble. Daiichi is the most honest of the major ADC players because it has the most concentrated franchise — when they take a write-down it shows up clearly. AstraZeneca is hedged across a much broader oncology portfolio. Pfizer-Seagen, ImmunoGen-AbbVie, the Merck-Daiichi partnerships, the smaller players like Genmab, ADC Therapeutics, and Mersana have each made commitments to ADC manufacturing that were calibrated to a 2023-vintage forecast that has now visibly missed. None of them have written down yet. Watch the next two earnings cycles for further admissions. The investor framing of the ADC space has been that it is the next great oncology modality and the demand will absorb whatever supply is built; the Daiichi disclosure is the first concrete signal that the absorption is slower than the supply curve assumed.

Sixth, what to watch over the coming weeks. AstraZeneca's next earnings call will be the test of whether the partner is going to update its own supply chain commentary. The Avanzar Phase 3 readout for Datroway in second half 2026 is the largest single demand inflection in the Daiichi portfolio — if it hits, the demand picture rebalances and the write-down is a stranded one-time cost; if it misses, there is a second wave of capacity to walk away from. The Daiichi successor announcement on the next-generation mPBD platform is also a catalyst — does the company double down on the platform with a fresh asset to replace DS-9606, or does it concede the platform was over-invested. And on the Calibr side, the practical action is a partnership posture review for the in vivo CAR-T capacity-commitment ladder. The right framing to bring to that review is not how much capacity do we need to support peak commercial demand. The right framing is what is the smallest contract set that lets us hit each clinical inflection without prepaying capacity that we will write down if any one of three programs misses.
