Good morning. It's Monday, June eighth, twenty twenty-six, and this is your Calibr-Skaggs Daily Briefing. Today is the closing day of the American Diabetes Association's eighty-sixth Scientific Sessions in New Orleans, and the headlines split between one large transaction announced this morning out of Roche and Nurix and the final tranche of A-D-A clinical data. Six items today. The spotlight this afternoon goes deep on the Roche and Nurix deal — a seven-hundred-million-dollar-upfront, up-to-two-point-three-billion-dollar total, B-T-K-degrader co-development collaboration that's a substantial validation event for the targeted-protein-degradation modality and is now expanding that modality from oncology into immunology and neurology in the same transaction.

First, the deal itself. Nurix Therapeutics and Roche this morning announced a global co-development and co-commercialization collaboration on bexobrutideg, Nurix's investigational oral B-T-K degrader, previously known as N-X-fifty-nine forty-eight. Nurix receives seven hundred million dollars upfront, with up to two point three billion dollars in development, regulatory, and sales milestones, plus low- to high-teens royalties on ex-United-States sales. Development cost is split sixty Roche, forty Nurix. The two companies will co-commercialize in the United States and split profits and losses equally. Bexobrutideg already has pivotal phase two-three trials underway in chronic lymphocytic leukemia, and the agreement covers further trials in B-cell malignancies more broadly, in chronic spontaneous urticaria where phase two work is planned, and in multiple sclerosis where phase two work is also planned. The clinical context is that Nurix's phase one-A and -B program in heavily pretreated relapsed-refractory C-L-L reported a seventy-eight-point-six percent objective response rate including responses in patients with T-P-fifty-three mutations, P-L-C gamma two mutations, prior covalent and non-covalent B-T-K inhibitor resistance, and central nervous system involvement. The deep dive on what this transaction signals for the targeted-protein-degradation modality and for Calibr's exposure to that modality runs this afternoon.

Second, also out today: Eli Lilly's full safety and tolerability dataset for retatrutide from the phase three TRIUMPH-one obesity trial was presented in detail at A-D-A this morning. The headline efficacy number — twenty-eight-point-three percent body-weight reduction at eighty weeks — was disclosed Saturday, but today's session put numbers on the tolerability tradeoff. Discontinuation due to adverse events on the twelve-milligram dose was eleven-point-three percent versus four-point-nine percent on placebo. Gastrointestinal adverse events — nausea, vomiting, diarrhea — were the dominant driver and ran higher than published rates for semaglutide and tirzepatide at comparable exposure. There was also a dose-dependent dysesthesia signal — a paresthesia-and-tingling neurologic adverse event that increased with dose level, and is not characteristic of the G-L-P-one or G-I-P arms of the molecule but may be coming from the glucagon receptor component. Dropout was meaningfully higher at the twelve-milligram dose than at the nine-milligram dose, which sharpens the question of whether the marginal efficacy gain above nine milligrams is worth the tolerability cost in routine practice.

Third, Pfizer's monthly G-L-P-one agonist berobenatide presented expanded phase two-B data from the VESPER program at A-D-A this morning. Berobenatide is an ultra-long-acting injectable G-L-P-one engineered for once-monthly dosing. The VESPER-three obesity trial reported up to twelve-point-three percent placebo-adjusted body-weight reduction at twenty-eight weeks on the four-point-eight-milligram monthly dose. The VESPER-one open-label extension reported fifteen-point-nine percent non-placebo-adjusted weight loss at thirty-two weeks with no observed plateau. The VESPER-two trial in type two diabetes reported a two-point-two percent A-one-C reduction at twenty-eight weeks on the one-point-six-milligram weekly dose versus zero-point-two percent on placebo. Pfizer has launched a ten-study phase three program including the VESPER-six pivotal maintenance trial, with both monotherapy and combination arms. Monthly subcutaneous dosing is the differentiation argument against the weekly incretins — adherence in the post-novelty period of G-L-P-one therapy is going to be where the next round of commercial sorting happens, and a monthly injectable that holds its efficacy is the cleanest version of that bet.

Fourth, AstraZeneca presented full phase two-B data for elecoglipron — A-Z-D-five-thousand-four — its oral small-molecule G-L-P-one receptor agonist this afternoon at A-D-A. The VISTA obesity trial and the SOLSTICE type-two-diabetes trial both met primary endpoints, with SOLSTICE comparing elecoglipron against placebo and against semaglutide as active comparator. Phase three is initiating in both indications. Oral small-molecule G-L-P-one is the segment in which Lilly's orforglipron has the lead — and the segment where the Pfizer danuglipron failure last year and Roche's C-T-three-six-eighty-three deprioritization left an open question about whether non-peptide oral G-L-P-ones can match the injectable peptides on efficacy and tolerability. AstraZeneca putting two clean phase two-B readouts on the board is the first credible second entrant.

Fifth, Lilly's oral G-L-P-one orforglipron — branded Foundayo — gets a phase three symposium this afternoon at A-D-A. The ACHIEVE-one diabetes readout showed up to a one-point-six percent A-one-C reduction with seven-point-nine percent weight loss at forty weeks. The ATTAIN-two obesity-and-diabetes readout reported up to ten-point-five percent weight loss at the highest dose. Lilly has filed Foundayo for obesity globally and is targeting type-two-diabetes submissions later this year. The interesting thing about Foundayo is that, unlike the peptide G-L-P-ones, it has no food or water restrictions on dosing.

Sixth, the published version of Boehringer Ingelheim's phase three survodutide data — the SYNCHRONIZE-one obesity trial and the SYNCHRONIZE-M-A-S-L-D liver-disease trial — landed yesterday in the New England Journal of Medicine and in Nature Medicine respectively. SYNCHRONIZE-one reported sixteen-point-six percent weight loss at seventy-six weeks in adults with obesity without type two diabetes. SYNCHRONIZE-M-A-S-L-D reported a thirty-four percent reduction in visceral fat and a sixty-three percent reduction in liver fat with limited lean-mass loss, and liver-fat normalization in six out of ten participants at forty-eight weeks. Survodutide is a glucagon and G-L-P-one dual agonist — the same two-receptor combination as the glucagon-G-L-P-one arms of retatrutide, without the G-I-P arm. The M-A-S-L-D liver-fat numbers are highly relevant to the M-A-S-H landscape that yesterday's spotlight covered. Glucagon-receptor activation is doing real work on hepatic triglyceride clearance in both molecules, and it sets the comparator for any FGF-twenty-one-class M-A-S-H biologic that's not running on top of an incretin backbone.
