The spotlight today is the Novartis-and-Orionis molecular-glue collaboration expansion announced yesterday morning — forty million dollars upfront plus up to one-point-four billion dollars in research, development, and commercial milestones plus tiered royalties, in exchange for multi-year access to Orionis's Allo-Glue platform and its A-I-driven discovery engine across multiple disease areas. The deal extends the four-year collaboration the two companies struck in twenty twenty, and it lands seven weeks after the second Genentech-Orionis collaboration expansion that priced at one hundred five million dollars upfront plus up to two billion dollars in milestones. The story is the chemistry, the platform, the three-deal valuation comparable, and the read-through to Calibr's chemistry-platform capability set. Four threads.

The first thread is the chemistry — what monovalent molecular glues actually are, and how they differ from the heterobifunctional degraders we covered Monday in the Nurix-and-Roche bexobrutideg spotlight. A targeted-protein-degradation molecule like bexobrutideg or any classical PROTAC is built from two distinct binders held apart by a linker — one warhead engages the target protein, the other warhead engages an E-three ligase like cereblon or V-H-L, and the linker positions them across an extended molecular distance. The resulting ternary complex recruits the cell's ubiquitin-proteasome machinery and degrades the target. The chemistry is powerful but constrained by molecular weight and physicochemical properties — heterobifunctional degraders are large molecules, frequently above one thousand daltons, often requiring oral-bioavailability tradeoffs and elaborate medicinal-chemistry optimization. Monovalent molecular glues — the chemistry class that defines the Orionis platform — are different. They are small, single-warhead compounds, typically in the conventional small-molecule molecular-weight range, that bind a single protein surface and re-shape it just enough to create a new protein-protein interaction at an adjacent interface. The classical examples include the immunomodulatory drugs lenalidomide and pomalidomide, which glue specific neo-substrates into cereblon for degradation, and the molecular-glue mechanism is now understood to underpin a meaningful fraction of phenotypic-screen hits that historically had no clear mechanism. The Allo-Glue platform is designed to discover these monovalent glues at scale and on demand, in two modes. One mode is degradative — the molecule glues the target protein into any E-three ligase the cell expresses, and the proteasome destroys it. The other mode is non-degradative — the molecule glues the target into a non-ligase partner protein and allosterically modulates the target's function without consuming it. That second mode opens addressable target classes — kinases, transcription factors, scaffolding proteins — where direct active-site inhibition has failed and where degradation may not be biologically desirable. Two chemical-biology modalities from one platform, both addressing the protein-protein-interaction problem from a starting point closer to the conventional small-molecule properties Big Pharma already knows how to develop.

The second thread is the platform — what Orionis has built around the chemistry. The Allo-Glue platform integrates four components. First, a chemical-biology readout system that detects induced protein-protein interactions inside living cells across a high-throughput screening format, which is the data-generating substrate. Second, an A-I-driven discovery engine that ingests target structures, ligase profiles, and chemical-library data to predict productive glue interactions and to optimize hit-to-lead chemistry without requiring a known binding pocket on either partner. Third, custom robotic automation that runs the cell-based assays at a scale needed to test against any target and any ligase the disease biology demands. Fourth, an in-house chemical library biased toward the small-molecule glue chemotype. The combination claims to do something most chemistry platforms have not — generate molecular glues against arbitrary protein pairs, including target-and-ligase combinations that human medicinal chemists could not have predicted from structure-based design. That target-agnostic and ligase-agnostic capability is what the three Big Pharma signatures are paying for. Calibr listeners should hear two implications in that platform stack. First, the A-I-driven discovery engine is not a marketing layer on top of conventional chemistry — it is the structural element that turns the cellular induced-proximity assay into a high-content data source, which is exactly the configuration where the current generation of A-I tools delivers value. Second, the chemical-biology readout system is the kind of capability that medicinal-chemistry-anchored organizations can build, but it requires sustained investment and a willingness to commit to induced-proximity chemistry as a strategic modality rather than a tactical screen.

The third thread is the dealmaking pattern — three Big Pharma signatures in three years, what the structure of each deal tells you, and where the comparable comes out. Orionis closed the first Novartis collaboration in twenty twenty against historically elusive targets, financial terms undisclosed at the time. In September twenty twenty-three, the company struck its first Genentech collaboration, also at undisclosed terms but covering oncology and neurodegeneration. In May of twenty twenty-five, Orionis extended the Genentech relationship into a second collaboration — one hundred five million dollars upfront plus up to two billion dollars in milestones plus tiered royalties, focused on small-molecule monovalent glue medicines for oncology, with Orionis owning the discovery and optimization phase and Genentech owning preclinical-through-commercialization. Now in twenty twenty-six, Novartis extends with the deal terms we opened on — forty million dollars upfront plus up to one-point-four billion dollars in milestones plus royalties, multi-year access to the Allo-Glue platform across multiple disease areas. Three partners. Two with extensions. North of three-and-a-half billion dollars in committed milestone value across the franchise. Standalone pipeline includes O-R-B-zero-one-one, a solid-tumor asset that binds and activates type-one conventional dendritic cells to drive antigen presentation, and O-R-B-zero-two-one, an engineered cytokine designed to direct cytokine activation to specific immune-cell subsets — both lines built on the parallel A-Kine biologics platform that Orionis has been advancing. What the three-deal pattern signals is that the molecular-glue modality has moved from speculative single-deal validation to portfolio-level partner adoption. Novartis is willing to commit to its second platform-access deal, which means the prior collaboration must have generated either advanced preclinical assets or genuine target-class learnings — Big Pharma does not extend an undisclosed-terms exploratory deal into a fourteen-hundred-million-dollar milestone package without that. Genentech extending into a second deal at one hundred five million dollars upfront — more than Novartis's upfront on this one — reflects the parent-Roche group's specific strategic priority around oncology induced-proximity. The pricing pattern across the three deals also tells you how the molecular-glue modality is being priced. Upfront cash is modest for platform deals — tens of millions rather than hundreds. Milestone value is the centerpiece, sized to reflect that the assets the platform generates are themselves novel-mechanism small molecules with full development paths ahead. That structure is the chemistry-platform deal model that Calibr would face if it chose to externalize chemistry capability through similar partnerships.

The fourth thread is the read-through to Calibr-Skaggs. Three connections. First, on the chemistry. Calibr's small-molecule and chemoproteomics capability set — high-throughput medicinal chemistry, cellular target-engagement assays, chemoproteomic profiling, structure-based design — substantially overlaps the toolkit the Allo-Glue platform requires. The Calibr chemoproteomics franchise is, in particular, the closest direct analog to the in-cell readout system that drives the Orionis discovery engine. Translating the Calibr stack toward induced-proximity chemistry is, in principle, an extension of capability rather than a wholesale platform build. The Novartis-and-Orionis deal raises the financial ceiling on chemistry-platform assets pointed at induced-proximity work, which strengthens the case for that translation. Second, on the A-I-in-drug-discovery thesis. Calibr has been tracking the major A-I-discovery players — Xaira, Iambic, Revolution Medicines, and others — across the headlines and the spotlight. The Orionis platform is a slightly different shape of A-I-discovery argument. Rather than a foundation-model approach trained on broad chemistry-and-biology corpora, Allo-Glue is a discovery engine purpose-built around induced-proximity chemistry and trained on in-cell protein-interaction data generated at platform scale. The pattern that emerges across these deals is that A-I-driven discovery platforms anchored on a specific high-value chemistry problem — induced proximity, antibody design, peptide stapling — are the ones drawing the largest Big Pharma checks, not general-purpose chemistry A-I. Third, on the modality landscape. Molecular glues now sit on the same modality menu as P-R-O-T-A-C degraders that we covered Monday with Nurix-and-Roche, antibody-Helicon conjugates we covered yesterday with Parabilis-and-Regeneron, and the antibody-drug conjugates and immune-cell engagers and engineered cytokines that fill the rest of the protein-engineering page. The current Big-Pharma signal is consistent across these modality clusters — pay full multi-billion-dollar platform-deal terms for credible solutions to undruggable, intracellular, or protein-protein-interaction problems. For any Calibr-affiliated chemistry program that addresses that problem, the comparable now includes Nurix-and-Roche on degraders, Parabilis-and-Regeneron on stapled peptides, and Novartis-and-Orionis on monovalent glues, all priced inside a three-week window.

The bottom line: the Novartis-and-Orionis collaboration extension is the third major industry signature on the Allo-Glue platform in three years, lifts cumulative committed value across the Orionis franchise above three-and-a-half billion dollars, and places monovalent molecular glues on the same Big-Pharma modality-investment tier as P-R-O-T-A-C degraders and stabilized alpha-helical peptides. The chemistry is closer to conventional small-molecule pharmacology than its heterobifunctional cousins. The platform pairs cellular induced-proximity readouts with a purpose-built A-I discovery engine, which is the configuration that current A-I chemistry tools execute most reliably. And the chemistry-platform deal model that Orionis is running — modest upfront, large milestone, multi-year multi-disease scope — defines a template that Calibr-Skaggs and similar academic-anchored chemistry organizations should track as the reference comparable for externalizing chemistry capability against induced-proximity targets.
